# Group 

<MathBox title="Binary operation" boxType="definition">
Let $A$ be a set. A binary operation on $A$ is a function $F: A\times A \to A$. That is, to each ordered pair $(a,b) \in A\times A$, the operation $F$ assigns a unique element $F(a, b) \in A$. Instead of the functional notation $F(a,b)$ we commonly uses infix notation such as
- $a\circ b$
- $a * b$
- $a\cdot b$
- $ab$ (juxtaposition)
- $aFb$

The requirement $F(a,b) \in A$ for all $a, b \in A$ is called the closure property (or closure law) of the operation. Since $F$ has codomain $A$, every binary operation on $A$ satisfies the closure property by definition.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Group" boxType="definition" tag="2">
A group is a pair $(G, *)$, where $G$ is a non-empty set and $*: G \times G \to G$ is a binary operation, which satisfies the axioms:
1. **Closure:** $a, b\in G \implies a * b \in G$
2. **Associativity:**
$$
\begin{equation*}
  (a * b) * c = a * (b * c) \in G, \; \forall a,b,c\in\mathbb{G}
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

3. **Existence of an identity element:** There exists $e\in G$ such that for every $g\in G$
$$
\begin{equation*}
  g * e = e * g = g, \; \forall g\in G
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$ 

4. **Existence of inverses:** For each $g\in G$ there exists $g^{-1}\in G$ called the inverse of $g$, which has the property
$$
\begin{equation*}
  g * g^{-1} = e
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

A group $(G, *)$ is *Abelian* or *commutative* if $*$ also satisfies 
- $a * b = b * a$ for all $a,b \in G$ **(commutativity)** 

Note that the closure property follows implicitly from the fact that $*$ is a binary operation on $G$, and is thus sometimes not listed as a group axiom. The group operation is usually referred to as *(group) multiplication*, in which case the operation of two group elements is called a *product*. The group operation $*$ is also usually denoted by $\circ$ or $\cdot$. To simplify notation, it is common to omit the group operation symbol.

If the group operation is addition, the group is called *additive* and the inverse of $a$ is the negative $-a$. Similarly, if the group operation is multiplication, the group is called *multiplicative* and the inverse of $a$ is denoted $a^{-1}$.

A group $(G, *)$ is *discrete* if its underlying set $G$ is countable. Otherwise if $G$ is uncountable the group is *continuous*. Furthermore, if $G$ is equipped with a smooth manifold structure such that the group operations are smooth maps, then the group is a *Lie group*.

<details>
<summary>Details</summary>

The existence of inverse elements implies that the cancellation laws holds in groups

$$
\begin{align*}
  ab =& ac \implies b = c \\
  ba =& ca \implies b = c
\end{align*}
$$

Conversely, if $G$ is finite, cancellation laws imply the existence of inverse elements. The map $G \ni x\mapsto ax \in G$ is injective, and hence bijective by counting. In particular, $e$ is in the image, and so $a$ has a right inverse. Similarly, it has a left inverse, and by uniqueness the two inverses are equal.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Integers under addition form a group' boxType="example">
The set of integers $\Z = \Set{ n }_{n=-\infty}^\infty$ with standard addition forms an infinite discrete Abelian group, denoted $(\Z, +)$. Addition on $\Z$ is associative and commutative with identity element $e = 0$ and inverse elements $a^{-1} = -a$ for all $a\in\Z$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Uniqueness of group identity and inverses' boxType="proposition" tag="1">
The unit element $e\in G$ of a group $(G,*)$ is unique, and for any $g\in G$ its inverse $g^{-1}$ is also unique.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**Uniqueness of group identity**

Suppose there is an identity element $\tilde{e} \in G$ such that for every $g \in G$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  g\tilde{e} =& g \tag{\label{equation-1}} \\
  gg^{-1} =& \tilde{e} \tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{align*}
$$

This implies

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^{-1} \tilde{e} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-1}} g^{-1} \implies& gg^{-1} \tilde{e} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} gg^{-1} = e \\
  \implies& e \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-2}} \tilde{e}\tilde{e} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-1}} \tilde{e}
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $e$ is unique.

**Uniqueness of group inverses**

Suppose $h_1$ and $h_2$ are two inverses of $g\in G$, i.e.

$$
\begin{align*}
  gh_1 = e = gh_2
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{align*}
$$

Then it follows for $i\in\set{1,2}$ that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} ge \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} gh_i h_i^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-3}} = eh_i^{-1}
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and thus

$$
  h_i g \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-4}} = h_i eh^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} h_i h_i^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} e
$$

Consequently, we also have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  h_1 g = e = h_2 g
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which finally implies

$$
\begin{align*}
  h_1 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}}& h_1 e \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} h_1 gg^{1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-5}}& h_2 gg^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} h_2 e \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}}& h_2
\end{align*}
$$

</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Properties of group inverses' boxType="proposition">
Let $(G,*)$ be a group with unit element $e$. Then
1. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  e^{-1} = e
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and for all $a,b \in G$
2.
$$
\begin{equation*}
  (a*b)^{-1} = b^{-1} * a^{-1}
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

3. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  (a^{-1})^{-1} = a
\tag{\label{equation-11}}
\end{equation*}
$$

4. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  b = a \iff b^{-1} = a^{-1}
\tag{\label{equation-12}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** From $\eqref{equation-7}$ we have for $g = e$ that $ee = e$ and from $\eqref{equation-8}$ we see that then $e = e^{-1}$.

**(2):** By associativity we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  ab (b^{-1} a^{-1}) =& abb^{-1} a^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} aea^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}}& aa^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} e
\end{align*}
$$

**(3):** Note that $\eqref{equation-8}$ implies $e = g^{-1} (g^{-1})^{-1}$. Multiplying both sides with $g$ and using that, because of $\eqref{equation-7}$, we have on the left side $ge = g$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  g =& gg^{-1} (g^{-1})^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} e(g^{-1})^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-9}} e^{-1} (g^{-1})^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-10}}& (g^{-1} e)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} (g^{-1})^{-1}
\end{align*}
$$

**(4):** We have

$$
\begin{align*}
  h = g \implies& hg^{-1} = gg^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} e \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-8}}& h^{-1} = g^{-1} \tag{\label{equation-13}} \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-13}}& (h^{-1})^{-1} = (g^{-1})^{-1} \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-11}} h = g
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Left inverses' boxType="proposition">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group with identity element $e\in G$. Then for all $g\in G$
1. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  g^{-1} * g = e
\tag{\label{equation-15}}
\end{equation*}
$$

2. $e * g = g$

and for any $h, k \in G$

3. $g * h = g * k \iff h = k \iff h * g = k * g$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** Note that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (g^{-1} g)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-10}} g^{-1} (g^{-1})^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-11}} g^{-1} g
\tag{\label{equation-14}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which implies

$$
\begin{align*}
  e \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}}& g^{-1} g(g^{-1} g)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-14}} g^{-1} gg^{-1} g \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}}& g^{-1} eg \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} g^{-1} g
\end{align*}
$$

**(2):** Note that

$$
\begin{align*}
  eg \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-12}}& \left((eg)^{-1}\right)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-10}} (g^{-1} e^{-1})^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-9}}& (g^{-1} e)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} (g^{-1})^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-12}}& g
\end{align*}
$$

**(3):** Let $gh = gk$ for some $g,h,k \in G$. Multiplying both sides from the left by $g^{-1}$ and using $\eqref{equation-15}$ implies $h = k$. Multiplying this in turn by $g$ from the left yields $gh = gk$. Hence, $gh = gk$ if and only if $h = k$. The proof of the equivalence of $hg = kg$ with $h = k$ is similar.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="lemma">
Let $N\in\N_+$. Then the set $G = \set{0,\dots,N-1}$ with the group operation given by addition modulo $N$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  a +_{\Z_N} b := (a + b): \bmod N
\tag{\label{equation-16}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is a finite Abelian group $(G, +_{\Z_N})$ denoted by $\Z_N$.

Moreover, for a prime $p$ the set $G \setminus\set{0} = \set{1,\dots,p-1}$ with group multiplication is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  a \cdot_{\Z_p} b := (ab) \bmod p
\tag{\label{equation-17}}
\end{equation*}
$$

also constitutes a finite Abelian group $(G\setminus\set{0}, *\cdot_{\Z_p})$, which is denoted $\Z_p^\times$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

First, consider the additive group $\Z_N$. Since $(a + b) \bmod N \in\set{0,\dots,N-1}$ the group operation given in $\eqref{equation-16}$ is a map $G\times G\to G$ that is associative. The neutral element is $e_+ = 0$ and the inverse of $a\in G\setminus\set{0}$ is given by $a^{-1} = N - a \in G$ since

$$
  a +_{\Z_N} a^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-16}} (a + N - a) \bmod N = 0 = e_+
$$

Clearly, $G$ is finite and $a +_{\Z_N} b = b +_{\Z_N}$ a.

Now consider $\Z_p^\times$, where $p$ is a prime, and let $a, b\in\set{1,\dots,p-1}$ such that $a \bmod p \neq 0 \neq b \bmod p$. It follows that $(ab) \bmod p \neq 0$. Consequently. $(ab) \bmod p \in\set{2,\dots,N-1}$, and the group operation in $\eqref{equation-17}$ is a map $G\setminus\set{0} \times G\setminus\set{0} \to G\setminus\set{0}$ that is associative. The neutral element of this group is $e = 1$ since for any $a\in\set{1,\dots,p-1}$ we have $a \cdot_{\Z_p} e = a\bmod p = a$. Moreover, since for any such $a$ we have $\gcd(a,p) = 1$, we know using the extended Euclid algorithm that we can always fin $x, y\in\Z$ such that $ax + py = 1$. It folows that $x\bmod p = a^{-1} \bmod p \in G\setminus\set{0}$ such that

$$
  a \cdot_{\Z_p} (x \bmod p) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-16}} = (a(x\bmod p)) \bmod p = 1
$$

Hence, every $a \in G\setminus\set{0}$ has an inverse under $\cdot_{\Z_p}$ in $G\setminus\set{0}$.

Obviously, $a\cdot\Z_p b = (ab) \bmod p = b\cdot_{\Z_p} a$, which completes the proof.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Group order" boxType="definition">
The *order* of a group $G$ is the cardinality of its underlying set, denoted $\operatorname{ord}(G) = |G|$. If $G$ is finite, then $\operatorname{ord}(G)$ is the number of elements in $G$. Meanwhile if $G$ is infinite, then $\operatorname{ord}(G)$ is an infinite cardinal. Explicitly,

$$
  \operatorname{ord}(G) = \begin{cases}
    |G| \in\N,\quad& G \text{ is finite} \\
    \infty,\quad& G \text{ is not finite}
  \end{cases}
$$

A group $G$ is called a $p$-group if there exists a prime number $p$ and an integer $n\geq 0$ such that $\operatorname{ord}(G) = p^n$. Equivalently, a finite group is a $p$-group if its order is a power of the prime $p$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Group exponentiation laws" boxType="definition">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group with identity $e$. For an element $g \in G$, define the power operations

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^n =& \underbrace{g * \cdots * g}_{n \text{ times}} \tag{\label{equation-108}} \\
  g^{-n} =& (g^{-1})^n = \underbrace{g^{-1} * \cdots * g^{-1}}_{n \text{ times}} \tag{\label{equation-109}}
\end{align*}
$$

In particular, $g^0 = e$.

For all $m, n\in\Z$, the element $g$ satisfies the exponentiation laws
1. $g^m g^n =& g^{m+n}$
2. $(g^m)^n =& g^{mn}$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**Inverses of powers:**

We first prove that 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (g^n)^{-1} = g^{-n}
\tag{\label{equation-110}}
\end{equation*}
$$

by induction on $n$. In the base $n = 0$, we find

$$
  (g^0)^{-1} = e^{-1} = e = g^0 = g^{-0}
$$

For the induction step $n+1$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  (g^{n+1})^{-1} =& (g^n g)^{-1} = g^{-1} (g^n)^{-1} \\
  =& g^{-1} g^{-n} = g^{-(n+1)}
\end{align*}
$$

using the identity $(xy)^{-1} = y^{-1} x^{-1}$. Hence, $\eqref{equation-110}$ follows by induction.

**(1):** 

**Case 1:** $m, n \geq 0$

Fix $m\geq 0$. We prove by induction on $n$. In the base case $n = 0$, we have

$$
  a^m a^0 = a^m e = a^m = a^{m+0}
$$

For the induction step $n+1$, we have by associativity

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^m g^{n+1} =& g^m (g^n g) = (g^m g^n) g \\
  =& g^{m+n} g = g^{m+n+1} = g^{m+(n+1)}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2:** $m, n \leq 0$

Write $m = -r$, $n = -s$ with $r,s \geq 0$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^m g^n =& (g^{-1})^r (g^{-1})^s \\
  =& (g^{-1})^{r+s} = g^{-(r+s)} = g^{m+n}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 3:** $m\geq 0$, $n = -r < 0$

If $m \geq r$, write $m = r + t$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^m g^{-r} =& g^{r+t} g^{-r} = g^t \\
  =& g^{m-r} = g^{m+n}
\end{align*}
$$

If $m < r$, write $r = m + t$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  g^m g^{-r} =& g^m g^{-(m+t)} \\
  =& (g^m g^{-m}) g^{-t} = eg^{-t} = g^{-t} \\
  =& g^{m-r} = g^{m+n}
\end{align*}
$$

The remaining mixed-sign case $m < 0$ and $n \geq 0$ is obtained by interchanging $m$ and $n$

Hence $g^m g^n = g^{m+n}$ for all $m,n\in\Z$.

**(2):**

**Case 1:** $n \geq 0$

We prove by induction on $n$. In the base, $n = 0$, we have

$$
  (g^m)^0 = e = g^{m0}
$$

In the inductive step $n+1$, we obtain by associativity and item **(1)**

$$
\begin{align*}
  (g^m)^{n+1} =& (g^m)^n g^m = g^{mn} g^m \\
  =& g^{mn + m} = g^{m(n+1)}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2:** $n = -r < 0$

Using $\eqref{equation-109}$ and $\eqref{equation-110}$, we get

$$
\begin{align*}
  (g^m)^n =& (g^m)^{-r} ((g^m)^r)^{-1} \\
  =& (g^mr)^{-1} = g^{-mr} \\
  =& g^{m(-r)} = g^{mn}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence $(g^m)^n = g^{mn}$ for all $m,n\in\Z$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Group element order" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group. The order of an element $a\in G$, is defined as 

$$
  \operatorname{ord}(A) := \operatorname{ord}(\braket{a}) = \left|\set{a^n}_{n\in\Z} \right|
$$

that is the number of elements in the cyclic subgroup generated by $a$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Finite group element order" boxType="proposition">
Let $G$ be a group. Suppose that the element $a\in G$ has finite order, i.e. $\operatorname{ord}(a) < \infty$. Then

$$
  \operatorname{ord}(a) = \min\set{n\in\N  a^n = e}
$$

and if $m = \operatorname{ord}(a)$, then

$$
  \braket{a} = \set{a^i}_{i=0}^{m-1} = \set{e, a, a^2, \dots, a^{m-1}}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We prove the following two claims:
1. The set $S := \set{n\in\N : a^n = e}$ is nonempty and has a least element $m$
2. The powers $\set{a^l}_{l=0}^{m-1}$ are distinct and exhausts $\braket{a}$

Since $\operatorname{ord}(a) < \infty$, the cyclic subgroup $\braket{a} = \set{a^k}_{k\in\Z}$ is finite and the sequence $\set{a^k}_{k\in\N}$ therefore contains repeated terms. Thus there exist integers $0 \leq i < j$ such that $a^j = a^i$. Multiplying on left by $a^{-i}$ yields $a^{j - i} = e$, showing that $S \neq\emptyset$. By the well-ordering principle, $S$ has a least element denoted $m := \min(S)$.

We first show that the elements $\set{a^l}_{l=0}^{m-1}$ are distinct. Suppose that $a^i = a^j$ for some $0 \leq i < j < m$. Multiplying on the left by $a^{-i}$ gives $a^{j-i} = e$. Since $0 < j - i < m$, this contradicts the minimality of $m$. Thus the elements $a^l$ are pairwise distinct.

Next, let $k\in\Z$. By the division algorithm, there exist unique integers $q, r\in\Z$ such that

$$
  k = qm + r,\; 0 \leq r m
$$

Since $a^m = e$, it follows that

$$
  a^k = a^{qm + r} = (a^m)^q a^r = a^r
$$

Thus every element of $\braket{a}$ is equal to one of $\set{a^l}_{l=0}^{m-1}$, implying $\braket{a} = \set{a^l}_{l=0}^{m-1}$. Since these $m$ elements are distinct, the order of $a$ is

$$
  \operatorname{ord}(a) = |\braket{a}| = m
$$

and therefore

$$
  \operatorname{\ord}(a) = m = \min\set{n\in\N | a^n = e}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $G$ be a group with identity element $e$ and let $a\in G$. Define the set

$$
  I_a := \set{n\in\Z | a^n = e}
$$

Then $I_a$ is an ideal of $(\Z, +)$ and there exists a unique integer $m \geq 0$ such that $I_a = m\Z$. Moreover
1. If $m = 0$, then $I_a = \set{0}$, so $a^n \neq e$ for all $\neq 0$, and $a$ has infinite order
2. If $m > 0$, then $m$ is the least positive integer such that $a^m = e$, and $a$ has finite order $m$. In this case $a^n = e \iff m | n$ and $a^{-1} = a^{m-1}$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**$I_a$ is an ideal of $\Z$**

We first show that $I_a$ is a subgroup of $(\Z, +)$.
- Since $a^0 = e$, we have $0 \in I_a$
- If, $m, n \in I_a$, then

$$
  a^{m-n} = a^m (a^n)^{-1} = e
$$

so $m-n \in I_a$. Thus, $I_a \leq (\Z, +)$.

Next, if $n\in I_a$ and $k\in\Z$ then

$$
  a^{kn} = (a^n)^k = e
$$

so $kn \in I_a$. Thus, $I_a$ is an ideal of $\Z$.

Every ideal of $\Z$ is principal, meaning there exists a unique integer $m \geq 0$, such that $I_a = m\Z$.

**Case 1:** $m = 0$

In this case, $I_a = \set{0}$ so

$$
  a^n = e \iff n = 0
$$

Thus $a$ has infinite order

**Case 2:** $m \geq 0$

In this case, $I_a = m\Z$, so in particular $m \in I_a$ and therefore $a^m = e$. Since $m$ is the positive generator of $I_a$, it is the least positive integer with this property.

If $a^n = e$, then $n\in I_a = m\Z$, so $m|n$. Conversely, if $m | n$, then $n = km$ and

$$
  a^n = a^{km} = (a^m)^k = e
$$

Thus $a^n = e \iff m|n$.

Finally, since $a^m = e$, we have $a^{m-1} a = a^m = e$, so $a^{-1} = a^{m-1}$. Thus, $a$ has order $m$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Left action of a group on set" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group with identity element $e\in G$ and let $M$ be a set. A *left action* of $G$ on $M$ is defined as a map $\Lambda: G\times M \to M$ by $(g,m) \mapsto g.m$ that satisfies for all $h, g \in G$ and $m\in M$

1. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  e.m = m
\tag{\label{equation-38}}
\end{equation*}
$$

2. 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  hg.m = h
\tag{\label{equation-39}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The *stabilizer* of a subset $Q\subset M$ under the left action is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  S_G (Q) := \set{g\in G | g.m = m\;\forall m\in Q}
\tag{\label{equation-40}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Let $G$ be a group which acts by left action on a set $M$. For any subset $Q \subseteq M$ its stabilizer is a subgroup of $G$, i.e. $S_G (Q) \leq G$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By the definining property $\eqref{equation-38}$ of a stabilizer, we have for every $m \in M$ that $e.m = m$, which implies that $e\in S_G (Q)$.

Let $h, g \in S_G (Q)$ and $m\in M$ be arbitrary. Then we have

$$
  (hg).m \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-39}} h.(g.m) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-40}} = h.m \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-40}} = h
$$

such that $hq \in S_G (Q)$, which proves $\eqref{equation-21}$.

Finally, we find

$$
\begin{align*}
  m \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-38}}& e.m \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-15}} = (g^{-1} g).m \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-39}}& g^{-1} .(g.m) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-40}} g^{-1} .m
\end{align*}
$$

and thus $g^{-1} \in S_G (Q)$, which shows $\eqref{equation-22}$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Cancellation property" boxType="proposition" tag="8">
Let $(G, \cdot)$ be a group and $a,b, x, y\in G$. Then
1. **Left cancellation property:** $ax = ay \implies x = y$
2. **Right cancellation property:** $xb = yb \implies x = y$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $e\in G$ be the identity element of $G$.

**(1):** Assume $ax = ay$. Since $a$ is invertible, we can multiply both sides on the left by $a^{-1}$ and apply associativity to get

$$
  a^{-1} (ax) = a^{-1} (ay)
$$

By associativity, this is equivalent with

$$
  \underbrace{(a^{-1} a)}_{=e} x = \underbrace{(a^{-1} a)}_{=e} y
$$

Consequently, $ex = ey$ and therefore $x = y$.

**(2):** Assume $xb = yb$. Since $b$ is invertible, we can multiply both sides of the right by $b^{-1}$ and apply associativity to get

$$
  (xb)b^{-1} = (yb)b^{-1}
$$

By associativity, this is equivalent with

$$
  x \underbrace{(bb^{-1})}_{=e} = y \underbrace{(bb^{-1})}_{=e}
$$

Consequently, $xe = ye$ and therefore $x = y$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Semigroups

<MathBox title="Semigroup" boxType="definition">
A semigroup is a pair $(S, *)$, where $S$ is a non-empty set and $*: S\times S\to S$ is a binary operation on $S$, such that $*$ is associative; that is,

$$
  a\circ (b\circ c) = (a\circ b) \circ b,\; \forall a,b,c \in S
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Relation between groups and semigroups" boxType="proposition">
A pair $(G, \circ)$ is a group if the following conditions hold
1. $(G, \circ)$ is a semigroup
2. There is exists left identity $e\in G$ satisfying $e\circ a = a$ for all $a \in G$
3. Each $a\in G$ is left invertible, i.e. there exists $b\in G$ with $b\circ a = e$. The element $b$ is called a left inverse of $a$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We show that every semigroup satisfying items **(2)** and **(3)** is a group in the usual sense, meaning the identity is two-sided and every element possesses a two-sided inverse (Definition $\ref{definition-2}$).

Let $a\in G$ and choose $b\in G$ with $ba = e$. First we prove that $ab = e$. Since every element has a left inverse, there exists $c\in G$ such that

$$
  c(ab) = e
$$

Using associativity and the fact that $e$ is a left identity

$$
\begin{align*}
  ab =& e(ab) \\
  =& (c(ab))(ab) \\
  =& c((ab)(ab))
\end{align*}
$$

Furtermore, by associatity

$$
\begin{align*}
  (ab)(ab) =& a((ba)b) \\
  =& a(eb) = ab
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, $ab = c(ab) = e$, showing that $b$ is also a right inverse of $a$.

It remains to show that $e$ is a right identity. Since $ab = e$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  ae =& a(ba) \\
  =& (ab)a = ea = a
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, $ea = a$ and $ae = a$ for all $a \in G$ implying that $e$ is a two-sided identity.

In conclusion, every element of $G$ has a two-sided identity, and thus $(G,\circ)$ is a group in the standard sense.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="lemma" tag="4">
Let $(S, \circ)$ be a semigroup. Then $S$ is a group if and only if for all $a,b\in S$ there exist $x,y \in S$ such that $ax = b$ and $ya = b$. Equivalently, for every $a, b \in S$, the equations $ax = b$ and $ya = b$ are solvable in $S$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**($\implies$):** Assume $S$ is a group with identity element $e\in S$ and let $a, b \in S$. Since $a$ has an inverse $a^{-1}$, define

$$
\begin{align*}
  x =& a^{-1} b \\
  y =& b a^{-1}
\end{align*}
$$

Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  ax =& a(a^{1} b) \\
  =& \underbrace{(aa^{-1})}_{=e} b \\
  =& eb = b
\end{align*}
$$

and similarly

$$
\begin{align*}
  ya =& (ba^{-1})a \\
  =& b(a^{-1} a) \\
  =& be = b
\end{align*}
$$

Hence both equations $ax = b$ and $ya = b$ have solutions in $S$.

**($\impliedby$):** Assume that for all $a \in S$, there are $x, y \in S$ with $ax = b$ and $ya = b$. We show that $S$ is a group.

**Existence of a left identity:** Fix $x\in S$. Applying the hypothesis with $b = a$, there exists $e\in S$ such that $ea = a$. We claim that $e$ is a left identity for all of $S$.

Let $b\in S$. By hypothesis, there exists $t\in S$ such that $at = b$. Then, using associativity

$$
\begin{align*}
  eb =& e(at) = (ea)t \\
  =& at = b
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, $eb = b$ for all $b\in S$, showing that $e$ is a left identity of $S$.

**Every element has a left inverse:** Let $a\in S$. Since $e\in S$ and the equation $ya = e$ is solvable, there exists $u\in S$ such that $ua = e$. Thus every element of $S$ has a left inverse.

**Left cancellation:** Suppose $ax = ay$. Let $u$ be a left inverse of $a$, so that $ua = e$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  x =& ex = (ua)x \\
  =& u(ax) = u(ay) \\
  =& (ua)y = ey = y
\end{align*}
$$

Thus $S$ satisfies left cancellation.

**The left identity is a two-sided identity:** Let $a\in S$, and let $u$ be a left inverse of $a$. Since $ua = e$, we have

$$
  u(ae) = (ua)e = ee = e
$$

Alse $ua = e$. By left cancellation with respect to $u$, we have $ae = a$. Therefore $e$ is also a right identity, and hence a two-sided identity.

**Left inverses are two-sided inverses:** Let $u$ be a left inverse of $a$, so $ua = e$. Then

$$
  u(au) = (ua)u = eu = u
$$

and also $ue = u$. By left cancellation with respect to $u$, we have $au = e$. Thus $u$ is both a left and right inverse of $a$.

Since every element of $S$ has a two-sided inverse $e$ is also a two-sided identity, $S$ is a group.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="9">
Let $(S,\circ)$ be a semigroup with finite order $\operatorname{ord}(S) < \infty$. Then $S$ is a group if and only if it satisfies both the left and right cancellation properties (Proposition $\ref{proposition-8}$).

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**($\implies$):** The necessity follows immediately from the fact that every group satisfies both cancellation properties (Proposition $\ref{proposition-8}$).

**($\impliedby$):** Assume that $S$ is finite and satisfies both cancellation properties. For each $a\in S$, define the left and right translation maps

$$
\begin{align*}
  l_a : S\to S,\quad& l_a (x) = ax \\
  r_a : S\to S,\quad& r_a (x) = xa
\end{align*}
$$

The left cancellation property implies that $l_a$ is injective since

$$
\begin{align*}
  & l_a (x) = l_a (y) \\
  \implies& ax = ay \\
  \implies& x = y
\end{align*}
$$

Similarly, the right cancellation property implies that $r_a$ is injective since

$$
\begin{align*}
  & r_a (x) = r_a (y) \\
  \implies& xa = ya \\
  \implies& x = y
\end{align*}
$$

Since $S$ is finite, every injective self-map of $S$ is surjective. Thus $l_a$ and $r_a$ are bijections for every $a\in S$. Thus, for every $a,b\in S$ there exists elements $x, y \in S$ such that $l_a (x) = b$ and $r_a (y) = b$ or equivalently $ax = b$ and $ya = b$. By Lemma $\ref{lemma-4}$ it follows that $S$ is a group.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Composition of real functions" boxType="example">
Let

$$
  \mathcal{F}(\R) = \set{f | f:\R\to\R}
$$

be the set of all real-valued functions on $\R$ and define the operation of function composition $\circ : \mathcal{F}(\R) \times \mathcal{F}(\R) \to \mathcal{F}(\R)$ by

$$
  (f\circ g)(x) := f(g(x)),\; \forall x\in\R
$$

We claim that $(\mathcal{F}(\R), \circ)$ forms a semigroup. To verify associativity, let $f_1, f_2, f_3 \in \mathcal{F}(\R)$. We must show that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  f_1 \circ (f_2 \circ f_3) = (f_1 \circ f_2) \circ f_3
\tag{\label{equation-106}}
\end{equation*}
$$

It suffices to check equality pointwise. For any $x\in\R$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  (f_1 \circ (f_2 \circ f_3))(x) =& f_1 ((f_2 \circ f_3)(x)) \\
  =& f_1 (f_2 (f_3 (x)))
\end{align*}
$$

On the other hand

$$
\begin{align*}
  ((f_1 \circ f_2) \circ f_3)(x) =& (f_1 \circ f_2)(f_3 (x)) \\
  =& f_1 (f_2 (f_3 (x)))
\end{align*}
$$

Since both expression coincide for all $x\in\R$, we conclude that $\eqref{equation-106}$ holds, proving that $(\mathcal{F}(\R), \circ)$ is a semigroup.
</MathBox>

## Subgroups

<MathBox title="Subgroup" boxType="definition">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group. A nonempty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a *subgroup* of $G$, denoted $H \leq G$, if the pair $(H, *)$ forms a group under the binary operation $*$ inherited from $G$. If $H \subsetneq G$, then $H$ is a *proper subgroup* of $G$, written $H < G$. A proper subgroup $H < G$ is *maximal* if there is no subgroup $K < G$ such that $H < K$. Likewise, a nontrivial subgroup $H \leq G$ is *minimal* if there is no subgroup $K \leq G$ such that $\set{e} < K < H$, where $e$ is the identity element of $G$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Subgroup criterion" boxType="proposition" tag="2">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group with identity element $e\in G$. A nonempty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup of $G$ if and only if

$$
\begin{equation*}
  a, b \in H \implies a * b^{-1} \in H
\tag{\label{equation-20}}
\end{equation*}
$$

meaning that $H$ is closed under the operation $a * b^{-1}$. This one-step subgroup test is equivalent to the following two subgroup criteria (two-step subgroup test):
1. **Closure under the group operation:**
$$
\begin{equation*}
  a, b \in H \implies a * b \in H
\tag{\label{equation-21}}
\end{equation*}
$$

2. **Closure under inverses:**
$$
\begin{equation*}
  a\in H \implies a^{-1} \in H
\tag{\label{equation-22}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**One-step subgroup test**

**($\implies$):** Assume $H \leq G$. As a subgroup, $H$ satisfies the group axioms. In particular:
- $a\in H \implies a^{-1} \in H$
- $a, b \in H \implies a * b \in H$

Thus, for all $a, b \in H$, we have $b^{-1} \in H$, and closure of $H$ under the group operation implies that $a * b^{-1} \in H$.

**($\impliedby$):** Assume $a * b^{-1} \in H \neq\emptyset$ for all $a, b\in H$. We show that $H$ satisfies the group axioms.

- **Associativity:** The operation $*$ is associative on $G$, and since $H \subseteq G$, it defines a restriction $*|_H : H\times H \to H$ on $H$, which is inherently associative.

- **Existence of identity element:** Since $H \neq\emptyset$ is nonempty, it must have an element $a\in H$. Consequently, $a * a^{-1} = e \in H$ by assumption.

- **Existence of inverses:** Since $e \in H$, then for any $h\in H$ we have by assumption $eh^{-1} = h^{-1} \in H$.

- **Closure:** Given $a, b \in H$, then $b^{-1} \in H$. It follows by assumption that $a * b = a * (b^{-1})^{-1} \in H$.

**Two-step subgroup test**

**($\implies$):** Assume $H \leq G$. Since $H$ is a subgroup, it is a group under $*$ inherited from $G$. Thus $*$ is closed in $H$, implying

$$
  a, b \in H \implies a * b \in H
$$

Since the identity element of $H$ coincides with the identity element $e\in G$, the inverse of $h \in H$ computed in $H$ is equal, by uniqueness of inverses, to its inverse in $G$. Thus $h\in H \implies h^{-1} \in H$.

**($\impliedby$):** By $\eqref{equation-21}$, the restriction $*|_H : H\times H \to H$ is well-defined. Since associativity of $*$ holds in $G$, it also holds on $H$. This shows that $(H, *)$ is a semigroup. 

Choose $h\in H$. By $\eqref{equation-22}$, we have $h^{-1} \in H$. Applying $\eqref{equation-21}$ yields $h * h^{-1} = e \in H$, showing that $H$ shares the identity element of $G$.

Condition $\eqref{equation-22}$ already guarantees that every element of $H$ has its inverse in $H$. Thus $H$ satisfies the group axioms.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group with $\operatorname{ord}(G) < \infty$ and let $H \subseteq G$ be a non-empty subset. Then $H$ is a subgroup of $G$ if and only if 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  a,b\in H \implies a * b \in H
\tag{\label{equation-107}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In other words, for a nonempty finite subset of a group, closure under the group operation alone guarantees that the subset is a subgroup.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**($\implies$):** If $H\leq G$, then $H$ is a group under the operation $*$ inherited from $G$. In particular, $H$ is closed under $*$ such that $\eqref{equation-107}$ holds.

**($\impliedby$):** Assume $H \neq\emptyset$ and that $\eqref{equation-107}$ holds. Then the restriction of $*$ to $H$ defines a binary operation $*|_H H\times H \to H$. Since associativity holds in $G$, it also holds on $H$. Thus $(H, *)$ is a finite semigroup. Because $H \subseteq G$, the cancellation laws hold in $H$. Consequently, by Proposition $\ref{proposition-9}$ it follows that $(H, *)$ is a group. Since the operation on $H$ is the restriction of the operation on $G$, it follows that $H$ is a subgroup of $G$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Trivial subgroups" boxType="example">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group with identity element $e\in G$. Then the subsets $\set{e}\subseteq G$ and $G \subseteq G$ form are subgroups of $G$ under the operation $*$ inherited from $G$. The subgroup $\set{e}$ is called the trivial subgroup, while $G$ itself is called the improper subgroup of $G$. With this convention, the nontrivial proper subgroups of $G$ are precisely those subgroups $H \leq G$ satisfying

$$
  \set{e} \subsetneq H \subsetneq G
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Subgroups are preserved under intersection" boxType="proposition" tag="15">
Let $G$ be a group and let $\set{H_i \leq G}_{i\in I}$ be a collection of subgroups of $G$, indexed by a set $I\subseteq\N_0$. Then the intersection

$$
  H_\cap := \bigcap_{i\in I} H_i
$$

is also a subgroup of $G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $e_G$ be the identity element of the group $G$. Since each $H_i$ is a subgroup of $G$, it contains the identity element $e_G$. Thus

$$
  e_G \in \bigcap_{i\in I} H_i = H_\cap \neq\emptyset
$$

verifying that $H_\cap$ is nonempty.

To show that $H_\cap$ is a subgroup, we apply the one-step subgroup test. Let $a, b \in H_\cap$. By definition of intersection, it follows that $a, b \in H_i$ for all $i\in I$. Since each $H_i$ is a subgroup of $G$, it follows that $ab^{-1} \in H_i$ for all $i \in I$. Consequently, 

$$
  ab^{-1} \in\bigcap_{i\in I} H_i = H_\cap
$$

proving that $H_\cap \leq G$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Torsion subgroup" boxType="definition">
If $G$ is an Abelian group, the elements of finite order form a subgroup $G_T$ of $G$, called the *torsion subgroup*.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Existence of smallest subgroups' boxType="proposition">
For any subset $H$ of a group $G$, there is a smallest subgroup of $G$ containing $H$. It consists of all finite products of elements of $H$ and their inverses (repetitions allowed).

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The intersection $S$ of all subgroups of $G$ containing $H$ is again a subgroup containing $H$, and is evidently the smallest such group. Clearly, $S$ contains all finite products of elements of $H$ and their inverses. Since $H$ is a subgroup, the set of such products satisfies Proposition $\ref{proposition-2}$, and hence is a subgroup containing $H$. It therefore equals $S$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Integer multiples under addition form subgroups' boxType="example">
For any fixed $n\in\N_+$, the set of integer multiples of $n$ is defined by

$$
  n\Z := \set{nk}_{k\in\Z}
$$

The is a subset of $\Z$ that contains the neutral element $0$ of the Abelian group $(\Z, +)$. It is closed under addition: for any $nk, nl \in n\Z$ we have 

$$
  nk + nl = n(k + l) \in n\Z
$$

Moreover, every element $nk\in n\Z$ has an additive inverse $n(-k) \in n\Z$ because

$$
  nk + n(-k) = n(k - k) = 0
$$

Thus $n\Z$ is a subgroup of $\Z$. In the special case $n = 1$, we recover the entire group, i.e. $1\Z = \Z$. However for $n > 1$, the inclusion is proper, and $n\Z$ forms a proper subgroup of $\Z$, i.e. $n\Z < \Z$.
</MathBox>

### Conjugate subgroups

<MathBox title="Conjugate subgroup" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group. Two subgroups $U, V \subseteq G$ are called conjugate if there exists an element $g\in G$ such that

$$
  V = gUg^{-1} := \set{gug^{-1}}_{u\in U}
$$

Equivalently, $U$ and $V$ are conjuate if $V$ is image of $U$ under an inner automorphism of $G$. For any $g \in G$, the map $\iota_g : G\to G$ defined by $\iota_g (x) = gxg^{-1}$ is an inner automorphism such that $gUg^{-1} = \iota_g [U]$.

</MathBox>

Let $G$ be a group. Conjugacy of subgroups defines an equivalence relation on the collection of subgroups of $G$ 
- **Reflexivity:** $U = eUe^{-1}$
- **Symmetry:** If $V = gUg^{-1}$, then $U = g^{-1} Vg$
- **Transitivity:** If $V = gUg^{-1}$ and $W = hVh^{-1}$, then $W = (hg)U(hg)^{-1}

The equivalence class of a subgroup $U \leq G$ is therefore

$$
   [U] := \set{gUg^{-1}}_{g\in G}
$$

called the conjugacy class of $U$.

If $G$ is Abelian, then every subgroup is fixed by conjugation. In this case, for any $g\in G$ and $u\in U$, we have $gu = ug$ such that

$$
  gug^{-1} = ugg^{-1} = u
$$

Consequently

$$
  gUg^{-1} = \set{gUg^{-1}}_{u\in U} = U
$$

Thus every subgroup of an Abelian group forms a singleton conjugacy class, and conjugacy of subgroups is there trivial in the sense that $U$ and $V$ are conjugate if and only if $U = V$. Generally, a subgroup $U \leq G$ satisfies $gUg^{-1} = U$ for all $g\in G$ if and only if $U$ is a normal subgroup of $G$. Thus, every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.

### Klein four-group

The Klein four-group is the Abelian group $(K_4, *)$ consisting of the four elements $K_4 = \set{e, a, b, c}$, where $e$ is the identity element. The group operation $*$ is defined by the multiplication table

| $*$ | $e$ | $a$ | $b$ | $c$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $e$ | $e$ | $a$ | $b$ | $c$ |
| $a$ | $a$ | $e$ | $c$ | $b$ |
| $b$ | $b$ | $c$ | $e$ | $a$ |
| $c$ | $c$ | $b$ | $a$ | $e$ |

From the table it follows that

$$
  a^2 = b^2 = c^2 = e
$$

so every nonidentity element has order $2$. Moreover, the table is symmetric about its main diagonal, verifying that $K_4$ is Abelian.

Geometrically, $K_4$ can be realized as the symmetry group of a non-square rectangle. Under composition of transformations, the elements of $K_4$ correspond to the four rigid motions that preserve the rectangle:
- $e$: the identity transformation
- $a$: rotation by $180\deg$ about the center of the 
- $b$: reflection across the horizontal axis of symmetry
- $c$: reflection across the vertical axis of symmetry

Since every nonidentity element of $K_4$ has order $2$, the cyclic subgroups generated by each nonidentity element consists of exactly two elements

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{a} =& \set{e, a} \\
  \braket{b} =& \set{e, b} \\
  \braket{c} =& \set{e, c}
\end{align*}
$$

These, together with the trivial subgroup $\set{e}$ and the whole group, exhaust all subgroups of $K_4$. The subgroup lattice of $K_4$ therefore consists of the five subgroups
1. $\set{e}$
2. $\set{e, a}$
3. $\set{e, b}$
4. $\set{e, c}$
5. $K_4$

In summary, $K_4$ has exactly five subgroups, one trivial subgroup, three proper nontrivial subgroups of order $2$, and the improper subgroup $K_4$.

## Cyclic groups

<MathBox title="Generated subgroup" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group and let $K \subseteq G$ be a nonempty subset. Define

$$
  S_K := \set{H \leq G | K \subseteq H}
$$

the collection of all subgroups of $G$ that contain $K$. The intersection

$$
  \braket{K} := \bigcap_{H \in S_K} H
$$

is defined as the subgroup *generated* by $K$. It is the unique smallest subgroup of $G$ containing $K$, in the sense that $K \subseteq \braket{K}$, and whenever $L \leq G$ satisfies $K \subseteq L$, then $\braket{K} \leq L$.

A group $G$ is finitely generated if there exist elements $g_1,\dots,g_n \in G$ such that

$$
  G = \braket{g_1,\dots,g_n} := \braket{\set{g_1,\dots,g_n}}
$$

The finite set $\set{g_1,\dots,g_n}$ is called a generating set for $G$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Cyclic group" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group. The subgroup generated by all integers powers of an element $g \in G$, i.e.

$$
  \braket{g} = \set{g^n | n\in\Z}
$$

is called the *cyclic subgroup* generated by $g$. The order of $g$ is defined as $\operatorname{ord}(g) = |\braket{g}|$, the number of elements in $\braket{g}$. A group $G$ is *cyclic* if there exists an element $g\in G$ such that $G = \braket{g}$, meaning that $G$ is generated by a single element. 

**Finite cyclic groups**
A *finite cyclic group* $G = \braket{g}$ of order $\operatorname{ord}(g) = n < \infty$ is given by the set

$$
  G = \set{e,g,g^2,\dots,g^{n-1}}
$$

where 

$$
  g^i = g^j \iff i \equiv j \pmod n
$$

In particular $g^0 = g^n = e$ and $g^{-1} = g^{n-1}$. Such a group is isomorphic to the additive group $(\Z_n = \Z/n\Z, +)$ under the bijection $\varphi: \Z_n \to G$ given by $\varphi([k]) = g^k$, or equivalently by the inverse map $g^k \mapsto [k]$. Thus, up to isomorphism, there is a unique cyclic group of order $n$, denoted by $C_n$.

**Infinite cyclic groups**

An *infinite cyclic group* $G = \braket{g}$ with $\operatorname{ord}(g) = \infty$ is given by the set

$$
  G = \set{g^n}_{n\in\Z}
$$

Such a group is isomorphic to $(\Z, +)$ under the bijection $\varphi: \Z\to G$ by $\varphi(n) = g^n$. Up to isomorphism, there is therefore a unique inifinite cyclic group, denoted $C_\infty$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Cyclic groups are Abelian" boxType="proposition">
Let $G = \braket{g}$ be a cyclic group generated by an element $g\in G$. Then $G$ is Abelian.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $G$ is cyclic, every element of $G$ can be written as a power of $g$. Let $x = g^m$ and $y = g^n$ for some integers $m, n\in\Z$. Using the laws of exponents in a group, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  xy =& g^m g^n = g^{m+n} \\
  =& g^{n+m} = g^m g^n = yz
\end{align*}
$$

Since $x$ and $y$ were arbitrary elements of $G$, it follows that $xy = yx$ for all $x,y \in G$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Subgroups of cyclic groups are cyclic" boxType="proposition" tag="3">
Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $G = \braket{g}$ be a cyclic group for $g\in G$, and suppose $H \leq G$. If $H = \set{e}$ is the trivial subgroup, then $H = \braket{e}$. Otherwise there is a smallest positive integer $d\in\N_+$ such that $g^d \in H$. We will show that $H = \braket{g^d}$. Let $h\in H$. Then $h = g^m$ for some integer $m\in\Z$. Applying the division algorithm, we find integers $q,r\in\Z$ such that

$$
  m = qd + r,\; 0\leq r < d
$$

Then

$$
  h = g^m = g^{qd + r} = g^{qd} g^r = (g^d)^q g^r
$$

Hence $g^r = (a^d)^{-q} h \in H$. However, $0 \leq r < d$, and since $d$ is the smallest positive integer such that $g^d \in H$, we must have $r = 0$. Hence, $h = g^m = g^{qd} = (g^d)^q \in \braket{g^d} = H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="17">
Every minimal subgroup $H < G$ of a group $G$ is cyclic, that is, it is of the form $H = \braket{g}$ for some $g\in G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Recall that a subgroup $H < G$ is minimal if there is no subgroup $K \leq G$ such that $\braket{e_G} < K < H$.

Let $H$ be minimal and $g \in H\setminus\braket{e_G}$. If $H = \braket{g}$, we are done. Otherwise, take a $k \in H\setminus\braket{g}$. However, then we have

$$
  \braket{e_G} < \braket{g} < \braket{g, k} \leq H
$$

which contradicts our assumption that $H$ is minimal.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $G = \braket{g}$ be a cyclic group of order $n$. For each $k$ dividing $n$, $G$ has a unique subgroup of order $k$, i.e. $\braket{g^{n/k}}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Assuming $t = \frac{n}{k}$, it is easy to see that $\braket{ g^t }$ is a subgroup of order $k$. Let $H$ be any subgroup of $G$ or order $k$. Then by the proof of Proposition $\ref{proposition-3}$, we have $H = \braket{ g^d }$, where $d\in\N_+$ is the smallest positive integer such that $g^d \in H$. Applying the division algorithm, we obtaing integers $q,r \in\Z$ such that

$$
  n = dq + r,\; 0 \leq r < d
$$

Thus, $1 = g^n = g^{dq + r} = (g^d)^q g^r$ and so $g^r = (g^d)^{-q} \in H$. Consequently, $r = 0$ and so $n = dq$. Also $k = |H| = |\braket{ g^d}| = q = \frac{n}{d}$. Thus, $d = \frac{n}{k} = t$, i.e. $H = \braket{ g^d} = \rangle g^t \rangle$. 
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Additive integer groups" boxType="example">
The additive group $(\Z, +)$ is cyclic and can be generated as

$$
  \Z = \braket{1} = \braket{-1}
$$

Explicitly, 

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{1} =& \set{k \cdot 1}_{k\in \Z} \\
  \braket{-1} =& \set{k \cdot (-1)}_{k\in \Z}
\end{align*}
$$

For any $m\in\Z$, the set $m\Z := \set{mk}_{k\in\Z}$ of integer multiples of $m$ is a subgroup of $(\Z, +)$. Moreover, $m\Z$ is cyclic and is generated by $m$ as $m\Z = \braket{m}$. If $m \neq 0$, then $\braket{m} = \braket{-m}$ as well. In the special case $m = 0$, we have $0\Z = \braket{0}$, which is the trivial subgroup of $\Z$.

For an integer $m\geq 1$, the quotient group $\Z/m\Z = \set{[0], \dots, [m-1]}$ is cyclic and is generated by the congruence class $[1]$ as $\Z/m\Z = \braket{[1]}$. For every $k$, the corresponding congruence class is of the form $[k] = k[1]$ in the additive group $\Z/m\Z$, showing that every element is a multiple of $[1]$.
</MathBox>

### Additive congruence classes

Let $n \in\N_+$. Two integers $x, y \in\Z$ are congruent modulo $n$ if their difference is a multiple of $n$, i.e. $n \div (x - y)$. Equivalently, there exists $q\in\Z$ such that $x - y = qm$. This relation is denoted $x \equiv y \pmod{m}$.

The relation $\equiv \pmod{n}$ is an equivalence relation on $\Z$. Its equivalence classes are the sets

$$
  [a]_n = \set{b\in\Z | b \eqiuv a \pmod{n}} = \set{a + qn : q \in\Z}
$$

The set of all congruence classes modulo $n\in\N_+$ is denoted

$$
  \Z_n := \Z / n\Z = \set{[i]_n}_{i=0}^{n-1} \\
$$

Addition on $\Z_n$ is defined by

$$
  [a]_n + [b]_n := [a + b]_n
$$

This operation is well-defined: if $a \equiv a' \pmod{n}$ and $b \equiv b' \pmod{n}$, then

$$
  a + b \equiv a' + b' \pmod{n}
$$

Thus the value of $[a]_n + [b]_n$ does not depend on the chosen representatives.

**The additive group $(\Z_n, +)$**

The structure $(\Z_n, +)$ is a finite group of order $n$. Since $+$ is associative on $\Z$, we have for $[a]_n, [b]_n, [c]_n \in \Z_n$

$$
  [a]_n + ([b]_n + [c]_n) = [a]_n + [b + c]_n = [a + b + c]_n
$$

and

$$
  ([a]_n + [b]_n) + [c]_n = [a + b]_n + [c]_n = [a + b + c]_n
$$

showing that modular addition is associative. 

The identity element is $[0]_n \in \Z_n$ since

$$
  [a]_n + [0]_n = [a + 0]_n = [a]_n
$$

For each $[a]_n \in \Z_n$, the inverse is $-[a]_n = [-a]_n$, because

$$
  [a]_n + [-a]_n = [0]_n
$$

Since $[1]_n$ generates all elements, i.e. $\Z_n = \braket{[1]_n}$, the group $(\Z_n, +)$ is cyclic.

**Roots of unity**

Let

$$
  \mu_n = \set{e^{2\pi ik/n}}_{k=0}^{n-1}
$$

be the set of all $n$-th roots of unity. Under complex multiplication, $\mu_n$ forms a cyclic group of order $n$, generated by $\zeta_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$. The map $\varphi: \Z_n \to \mu_n$ defined by

$$
  \varphi([k]_n) = e^{2\pi ik/n}
$$

is a well-defined group isomorphism, since

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varphi([a]_n + [b]_n) =& e^{2\pi i(a+b)/n} \\
  =& e^{2\pi ia/n} e^{2\pi ib/n} = \varphi([a]_n) \varphi([b]_n)
\end{align*}
$$

Thus $\Z_n \cong \mu_n$ showing that every finite cyclic group of order $n$ is isomorphic both to the additive group $(\Z_n, +)$ and to the multiplicative group of $n$-th roots of unity.

<MathBox title="" boxType="example">
The smallest non-trivial cyclic group is $\Z_2 = \set{[0]_2, [1]_2}$ with the addition table

| + | $[0]_2$ | $[1]_2$ |
|---|---|---|
| $[0]_2$ | $[0]_2$ | $[1]_2$ |
| $[1]_2$ | $[1]_2$ | $[0]_2$ |

Consider the multiplicative group $G = \set{-1, 1} \subset \mathbb{C}$ with the multiplication table

| \cdot | $-1$ | $1$ |
|---|---|---|
| $-1$ | $1$ | $-1$ |
| $1$ | $-1$ | $1$ |

The map $\varphi: \Z_2 \to G$ defined by

$$
  \varphi([0]_2) = 1,\quad \varphi([1]_2) = -1
$$

is a bijection. Moreover

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varphi([a]_2 + [b]_2) =& \varphi([a + b]_2) = (-1)^{a+b} \\
  =& (-1)^a (-1)^b = \varphi([a]_2)\varphi([b]_2)
\end{align*}
$$

so $\varphi$ is a group isomorphism, implying $\Z_2$ is isomorphic to $G$, i.e. $\Z_2 \cong \set{-1,1}$.
</MathBox>

## Independent group

<MathBox title="Independent group" boxType="definition">
A subset $\set{g_1,\dots,g_k} \subseteq G$ of a group $G$ is *independent* if for any $g_j \in\set{g_1,\dots,g_k}$

$$
  g_j \notin \braket{\set{g_1,\dots,g_k}\setminus\set{g_j}}
$$
</MathBox>

Let $P$ denote a permutation of the index set $I = \set{1,\dots,k}$, that is, the map $P: I \to I$ given by $j \mapsto j$ is a bijection.

Independence of a subset $\set{g_1,\dots,g_k} \subseteq G$ of a group means that for every $a_1,\dots,a_k \in\set{0,1}$ and permutation $P$ we must have

$$
  \prod_{j=1}^k g_{P(j)}^{a_j} = e_G \implies a_j = 0,\; \forall j\in\set{1,\dots,k}
$$

This is because otherwise we would have for some $j$ and $P$

$$
  g_{P(j)} = g_{P(j-1)}^{-a_{j-1}} \cdots g_{P(1)}^{-a_1} g_{P(k)}^{-a_k}  g_{P(j+1)}^{-a_{j+1}} \in \braket{\set{g_1,\dots,g_k}\setminus\set{g_{P(j)}}}
$$

and since $P(j) \in\set{1,\dots,k}$ the set $\set{g_1,\dots,g_k}$ would not be independent.

### Normal subgroups

<MathBox title="Centralizer" boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be a group. The *centralizer* $C_G (g)$ of $g\in G$ is the set of elements of $G$ which commute with $g$, i.e.

$$
  C_G (g) := \set{h \in G | hg = gh}
$$

The centralizer of a subset $S \subseteq G$ is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  C_G (S) := \set{h \in G | hg = gh\; \forall g\in S}
\tag{\label{equation-18}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
The centralizer of any subset $S \subset G$ of a group $G$ is a subgroup of $G$, i.e.

$$
  C_G (S) \leq G
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $e_G g = ge_G$ for all $g \in G$, it follows that $e_G \in C_G (S)$.

Let $h_1, h_2 \in C_G (S)$. Then we have for every $g\in S$

$$
  (h_1 h_2) g = h_1 (h_2 g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-18}} h_1 g h_2 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-18}} g h_1 h_2
$$

and thus $h_1 h_2 \in C_G (S)$.

Finally, for any $h\in C_G (S)$ and $g\in S$

$$
\begin{align*}
  hg \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-18}} gh \implies& gh^{-1} = h^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-18}}& h^{-1} \in C_G (S)
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Centre of a group' boxType="definition">
The centre of a group $G$ is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  Z(G) = \Set{g\in G | gx = xg,\;\forall x\in G}
\tag{\label{equation-125}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which is the of group elements which commute with every element in $G$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='The centre is a normal subgroup' boxType="proposition">
The centre of a group $G$ is a normal subgroup, i.e.

$$
  Z(G) \unlhd G
$$

It is a subgroup of $G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The neutral element $e$ of $G$ clearly satisfies $eg = ge$ and so $e\in Z(G)$.

For any $h_1, h_2 \in Z(G)$ we have

$$
  h_1 h_2 g \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-25}} h_1 g h_2 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-25}}
$$

and thus $h_1 h_2 \in Z(G)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-21}$.

Moreover, for any $h \in Z(G)$ and $g \in G$ we have $hg = gh$ which implies $gh^{-1} = h^{-1} g$ and the $h^{-1} \in Z(G)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-22}$

Finally, we find for any $g\in G$ that

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z(G)^G \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}}& \set{ghg^{-1} | h\in Z(G)} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-25}}& \set{hgg^{-1} | h\in Z(G)} = Z(G)
\end{align*}
$$

Since $ge = eg = g$, it follows that $e \in Z(G)$ and $Z(G)\neq\emptyset$. For any $x,y \in Z(G)$, giving $gx = xg$ and $gy = yg$. Applying the group operation yields $(xy)g = x(yg) = x(gy) = (xg)y = (gx)y = g(xy)$, showing that $xy\in Z(G)$. For $x\in Z(G)$, we have $x^{-1} g = x^{-1} (ge) = x^{-1} (gx)x^{-1} = x^{-1} (xg) x^{-1} = (x^{-1}x)gx^{-1} = (eg)x^{-1} = gx^{-1}$, showing that $x^{-1} \in Z(G)$. Hence, $Z(G)$ is a subgroup.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Conjugate of group element" boxType="definition">
Let $S\subseteq G$ be a subset of the group $G$. The *conjugate* $S^g$ of $S$ by $g\in G$ is defined as the set

$$
\begin{equation*}
  S^g := gSg^{-1} = \set{ghg^{-1} | h\in S}
\tag{\label{equation-23}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Let $H \leq G$ be a subset of the group $G$. Then for any $g\in G$ the conjugate of $H$ by $g$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H^g = \set{ghg^{-1} | h \in H}
\tag{\label{equation-19}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is a subgroup of $G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $g \in G$ be any element of $G$. Since $e\in H$, it follows from $\eqref{equation-19}$ that $e\in H^g$.

Now let $h' \in H^g$, that is, there exists $h\in H$ such that $h' = ghg^{-1}$. However, we also have from $\eqref{equation-19}$ that $gh^{-1} g^{-1} \in H^g$. Thus,

$$
  h' (gh^{-1} g^{-1}) = ghg^{-1} gh^{-1} g^{-1} = e
$$

and thus $(h')^{-1} = gh^{-1} g^{-1} \in H^g$, which shows that $\eqref{equation-22}$ is satisfied for $H^g$.

Finally, let $h'_2, h'_2 \in H^g$, which, due to $\eqref{equation-19}$, means that there exist $h_1, h_2 \in H$ such that $h'_j = gh_j g^{-1}$ for $j\in\set{1,2}$. Since $h_1 h_2 \in H$, it follows that

$$
  h'_1 h'_2 = (gh_1 g^{-1})(gh_2 g^{-1}) = gh_1 h_2 g^{-1} \in H^g
$$

which shows that $\eqref{equation-21}$ is also satisfied for $H^g$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Conjugate and normal subgroup" boxType="definition" tag="1">
Let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of the group $G$. For any $g\in G$ the set

$$
  H^g := \set{ghg^{-1} | h\in H}
$$

is called a *conjugate subgroup* to $H$. If for every $g\in G$

$$
  H^g = H
$$

then $H$ is called a *normal* or *invariant subgroup* of $G$. This is written $H \unlhd G$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Normalizer" boxType="definition">
Let $S\subseteq G$ be a subset of the group $G$. The *normalizer* $N_G (S)$ of $S$ in $G$ is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  N_G (S) := \set{g \in G | S^g = g}
\tag{\label{equation-24}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Details</summary>

Note that by definition

$$
\begin{align*}
  g \in N_G (s) \iff& \forall h\in S \; \exists \tilde{h} \in S: ghg^{-1} = \tilde{h} \\
  \iff& \forall h \in S \; \exists \tilde{h} \in S: gh = \tilde{h}g
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
For any subset $S\subseteq G$ of the group $G$, its normalizer is a subgroup of $G$, i.e.

$$
  N_G (S) \leq G
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The neutral element $e \in G$ clearly satisfies $S^e = eSe^{-1} = S$, so that $e\in N_G (S)$.

For any $h_1, h_2 \in N_G (S)$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  S^{h_1 h_2} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}}& h_2 h_2 S(h_1 h_2)^{-1} = h_1 h_2 S h_2^{-1} h_1^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}}& h_1 S^{h_2} h_1^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-24}} h_1 S h_1^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}} S^{h_1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-24}}& S
\end{align*}
$$

such that $h_1 h_2 \in N_G (S)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-21}$.

Finally, for any $h \in N_G (S)$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  & hSh^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}} S^h \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-24}} S \\
  \implies& S = h^{-1} S(h^{-1})^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-23}} S^{h^{-1}}
\end{align*}
$$

such that $h^{-1} \in N_G (S)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-22}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='First normal subgroup criterion' boxType="proposition">
A subgroup $N$ of $G$ is *normal* if and only if $g^{-1}Ng \subseteq N$ for all $g\in G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Suppose $N$ is a subgroup of $G$ satisfying $g^{-1} N g \subseteq N$ for all $g\in G$. Then for all $n\in N$, we have

$$
  gng^{-1} = (g^{-1})^{-1} n (g^{-1}) = n' \in N
$$

for some $n'$, because $(g^{-1}) \in G$. Solving for $n$ we find

$$
  n = g^{-1} n' g \in g^{-1}N g
$$

Hence $N\subseteq g^{-1} N g$ and so $N = g^{-1} N g$. Thus, $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

The converse is obvious.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Second normal subgroup criterion" boxType="proposition">
A subgroup $N$ of $G$ is *normal* if and only if every left coset of $N$ in $G$ is also a right coset, in which case, $gN = Ng$ for all $g\in G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Clearly,

$$
  gNg^{-1} = N \iff gN = Ng
$$

Thus, if $N$ is normal, then every left coset is a right coset. Conversely, if the left coset $gN$ is also a right coset, then it must be the right coset by Proposition $\ref{proposition-4}$. Hence, $gN = Ng$, and so $gNg^{-1}= N$. 
</details>
</MathBox>

The criterions for normal subgroups say that, in order for $N$ to be normal, we must have that for all $g\in G$ and $n\in N$, there exists an $n' \in N$ such that $gn = n'g$ (equivalently, for all $g\in G$ and $n\in N$, there exists an $n'$ such that $ng = gn'$).

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition" tag="13">
If $H$ and $N$ are subgroups of $G$, and $N$ is normal, then $HN$ is a subgroup of $G$. If $H$ is also normal, then $HN$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The set $HN$ is nonempty, and

$$
  (h_1 n_1)(h_2 n_2) = h_1 h_2 n'_1 n_2 \in HN
$$

showing that it is closed under multiplication. Since

$$
  (hn)^{-1} = n^{-1} h^{-1} = h^{-1} n' \in HN
$$

it is also closed under the formation of inverses, and so $HN$ is a subgroup. If both $H$ and $N$ are normal, then

$$
  gHNg^{-1} = gHg^{-1} \cdot gNg^{-1} = HN
$$

for all $g\in G$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Cosets

<MathBox title="Left and right cosets" boxType="definition">
Let $(G, *)$ be a group, and let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of $G$. For any $g\in G$ we define:
- The *left coset* of $H$ by $g$ as 
$$
\begin{equation*}
  gH := \set{g*h | h\in H}
\tag{\label{equation-27}}
\end{equation*}
$$

- The *right coset* of $H$ by $g$ as
$$
\begin{equation*}
  Hg := \set{h*g | h\in H}
\tag{\label{equation-28}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The set of left cosets of $H$ in $G$ is denoted $G:H = \set{gH | g\in G}$. The *index* of $H$ in $G$, denoted $|G:H|$, is the number of left cosets of $H$ in $G$. Similarly, $H:G = \set{Hg | g\in H}$ is the set of right cosets of $H$ in $G$ with index $|H:G|$.

If left and right cosets coincide for all $g\in G$, i.e. $gH = Hg$ for all $g$, then $H$ is a *normal subgroup* of $G$. In this case, we typically refer simply to *cosets* and denote the coset of $g$ by

$$
  [g]_H := gH = Hg
$$
</MathBox>

Clearly, for a subgroup $H \leq G$ of an Abelian group $G$ left and right cosets coincide.

If $H\leq G$ is a subgroup of the group $G$, then we have for any $k\in H$ and any $g\in G$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  kH \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-27}}& \set{kh}_{h\in H} = \set{h'}_{h' \in H} = H \\
  gkH \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-27}}& \set{gkh}_{h\in H} = \set{gh'}_{h' \in H} = gH
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-35}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<MathBox title="Properties of left cosets" boxType="proposition" tag="4">
Let $G$ be a group and $H\leq G$ a subgroup of $G$.
1. An element $a\in G$ lies in a left coset $C$ of $H$ if and only if $C = aH$
2. For any two $g_1, g_2 \in G$ their left cosets $g_1 H$ and $g_2 H$ are either disjoint or identical.
3. $aH = bH$ if and only if $a^{-1} b \in H$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** Clearly, $a\in aH$. Conversely, if $a$ lies in the left coset $bH$, then $a = bh$ for some $h\in H$, and so

$$
  aH = bhH = bH
$$

**(2):** If $g_1 H \cap g_2 H = \emptyset$ they are disjoint, and there is not nothing to prove. Suppose then that there is a $g\in aH \cap bH$, that is, there exists $h_1, h_2 \in H$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g_1 h_1 = g = g_2 h_2
\tag{\label{equation-26}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $h_1, h_2 \in H$ and $H$ is a subgroup, we have $h_1 h_2^{-1} \in H$. Consequently, for any $h\in H$

$$
  g_2 h \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-26}} = g_1 \underbrace{h_1 h_2^{-1} h}_{\in H} \underbrace{\in}_{\eqref{equation-27}} g_1 H
$$

and thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g_2 H \subset g_1 H
\tag{\label{equation-29}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Similarly, we have $h_2 h_1^{-1} \in H$, yielding

$$
  g_1 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-26}} g_2 \underbrace{h_2 h_1^{-1}}_{\in H} \underbrace{\in}_{\eqref{equation-27}} g_2 H
$$

which implies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g_1 H \subset g_2 H
\tag{\label{equation-30}}
\end{equation*}
$$

It follows from $\eqref{equation-29}$ and $\eqref{equation-30}$ that $g_1 H = g_2 H$.

Equivalently, if the cosets $C$ and $C'$ are not disjoint, then they have a common element $a$, and $C = aH$ and $C' = aH$ by **(1)**.

**(3):** If $a^{-1} b\in H$, then $H = a^{-1} bH$, and so $aH = aa^{-1} bH = bH$. Conversely, if $aH = bH$, then $H = a^{-1} bH$, and so $a^{-1} b \in H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

Let $G$ be a group and $H$ a subgroup of a group $G$. Proposition $\ref{proposition-4}.2$ implies that the left cosets of $H$ cover $G$. In other words, the left cosets of $H$ that are disjoint form a partition of $G$. The left cosets of $H$ that are equal form equivalence classes on $G$.

<MathBox title="Left cosets as equivalence classes" boxType="proposition" tag="5">
Let $G$ be a group and $H \leq G$ a subgroup. Define a relation $\sim_\text{L}$ on $G$ by

$$
  a \sim_\text{L} b \iff a^{-1} b \in H
$$

Then $\sim_\text{L}$ is an equivalence relation on $G$, and its equivalence classes are precisely the left cosets of $H$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We show that $sim_\text{L}$ is an equivalence relation on $G$:
- **Reflexivity:** For $a\in G$, then $a^{-1} a = e \in H$ since $H\leq G$, so $a\sim_\text{L} a$.
- **Symmetry:** If $a\sim_\text{L} b$, then $a^{-1} b\in H$. Since $H\leq G$, then $(a^{-1} b)^{-1} = b^{-1} a \in H$ so $b \sim_\text{L} a$.
- **Transitivity:** If $a\sim_\text{L} b$ and $b \sim_\text{L} c$, then $a^{-1} b, b^{-1} c \in H$. Since $H$ is closed under the group operation $(a^{-1} b)(b^{-1} c) = a^{-1} c \in H$. Thus, $a \sim_\text{L} c$.

The equivalence class of $g\in G$ under $\sim_\text{L}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  [g]_{\sim_\text{L}} =& \set{a \in G | g \sim_\text{L} a} \\
  =& \set{a \in G | g^{-1} a = h \in H} \\
  =& \set{gh | h \in H} = gH
\end{align*}
$$

which are exactly the left cosets of $H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Right cosets as equivalence classes" boxType="proposition">
Let $G$ be a group and $H \leq G$ a subgroup. Define a relation $\sim_\text{R}$ on $G$ by
  
$$
  a \sim_\text{R} b \iff ab^{-1} \in H
$$

Then $\sim_\text{R}$ is an equivalence relation on $G$, and its equivalence classes are precisely the right cosets of $H$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We show that $sim_\text{R}$ is an equivalence relation on $G$:
- **Reflexivity:** For $a\in G$, then $aa^{-1} = e \in H$ since $H\leq G$, so $a\sim_\text{R} a$.
- **Symmetry:** If $a\sim_\text{R} b$, then $ab^{-1} \in H$. Since $H\leq G$, then $(ab^{-1})^{-1} = ba^{-1} \in H$ so $b \sim_\text{R} a$.
- **Transitivity:** If $a\sim_\text{R} b$ and $b \sim_\text{R} c$, then $ab^{-1}, bc^{-1} \in H$. Since $H$ is closed under the group operation $(ab^{-1})(bc^{-1}) = ac^{-1} \in H$. Thus, $a \sim_\text{R} c$.

The equivalence class of $g\in G$ under $\sim_\text{R}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  [g]_{\sim_\text{R}} =& \set{a \in G | a \sim_\text{R} g} \\
  =& \set{a \in G | ag^{-1} = h \in H} \\
  =& \set{hg | h \in H} = Hg
\end{align*}
$$

which are exactly the right cosets of $H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Cosets of a subgroup share same order" boxType="proposition" tag="6">
Let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of the group $G$. Then each left and right coset of $H$ has the same order as $H$, i.e.

$$
  |gH| = |H| = |Hg|,\; \forall g\in G
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We prove the statement for left cosets; the case for right cosets is analogous.

Define the map $\alpha: H \to gH$ by $\alpha(h) = g*h$. We show that $\alpha$ is bijective:
- **Injectivity:** Suppose $\alpha(h_1) = \alpha(h_2)$, or $g*h_1 = g*h_2$. Multiplying both sides the left by $g^{-1}$ yields $h_1 = h_2$
-**Surjective:** Let $x \in gH$. Then by definition, $x = gh$ for some $h\in H$. However, since $x = \alpha(h)$, then every element of $gH$ is in the image of $\alpha$.

Since $\alpha$ is both injective and surjective, it is a bijection. Hence, $|gH| = |H|$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Lagrange's theorem" boxType="theorem" tag="5">
Let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of the finite group $G$. Then

$$
  |G| = |G:H|\cdot|H| = |H:G|\cdot|H|
$$

In particular, the order of $H$ divides the order of $G$, and $G$ is the disjoint union of

$$
  J = \frac{|G|}{|H|} \in \N_+
$$

left (or right) cosets of $H$, that is, there are $g_j \in G$ with $j\in\set{1,\dots,J}$ such that

$$
  G = \bigsqcup_{j=1}^J g_j H = \bigsqcup_{j=1}^J H g_j
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let the distinct left cosets of $H$ in $H$ be $g_1 H, \dots, g_m H$, so $m = |G:H|$. By Proposition $\ref{proposition-5}$, the left cosets of $H$ are the equivalence classes for an equivalence relation $\sim$ on $G$. Thus, they form a partition of $G$, i.e.

$$
  |G| = \sum_{i=1}^m |g_i H|
$$

Since $|g_i H| = |H|$ by Proposition $\ref{proposition-6}$, we get $|G| = m|H| = |G:H|\cdot|H|$. The result for right cosets is similar.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Extension of Langrange's theorem" boxType="theorem">
If $H$ is a subgroup of $G$ and $K$ is a subgroup of $H$, then

$$
  |G:K| = |G:H|\cdot|H:K|
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $S$ be a set of coset representatives for $K$ in $H$, so $H = \bigsqcup_{s\in S} s K$, and $|S| = |H:K|$. For any $a \in G$, left multiplication by $a$ is a bijection $G \to G$, so $a H = \bigsqcup_{s\in S} asK$. Thus each left coset of $H$ decomposes into $|H:K|$ left cosets of $K$. Since $G$ decomposes into $|G:H|$ left cosets of $H$, each of which decomposes into $|H:K|$ left cosets of $K$, the total number $|G:K|$ of left cosets of $K$ in $G$ is $|G:H|\cdot|H:K|$. 
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Cosets of integers" boxType="example">
The set of integer multiples of $n\in\N_+$, denoted $n\Z = \Set{nz}_{z\in\Z}$, is a subgroup of the additive Abelian group $(\Z, +)$. The cosets of $n\Z$ in $\Z$ are the sets 

$$
  n\Z + i = \set{nz + i}_{z\in\Z},\; i\in 
$$

each of which corresponds to a congruence class modulo $n$. Together, the cosets partition $\Z$ into the disjoint union

$$
  \Z = \bigsqcup_{i=0}^n n\Z + i = n\Z \sqcup \left(\bigsqcup_{i=1}^n n\Z + i\right)
$$

In other other words, every integer $g\in\Z$ belongs to exactly one coset

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_{n\Z} = \set{g + ni}_{i\in \Z} = [g \bmod n]_{n\Z} \in \Z/n\Z
\tag{\label{equation-37}}
\end{equation*}
$$

since $z \equiv z \bmod n \pmod{n}$ and both lie in the same coset. Thus, every coset $[z]_{n\Z}$ is completely determined by the residue $z \bmod n$.
</MathBox>

## Quotient group

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition" tag="7">
Let $G$ be a group with identity element $e\in G$ and let $H \unlhd G$ be a normal subgroup of $G$. Then the set $\set{[g]_h}_{g\in G}$ of cosets forms a multiplicative group with 
- group operation
$$
\begin{align*}
  \cdot: \set{[g]_h}_{g\in G}\times\set{[g]_h}_{g\in G} \to& \set{[g]_h}_{g\in G} \\
  [g_1]_H \cdot [g_2]_H :=& [g_1 g_2]_H \tag{\label{equation-31}}
\end{align*}
$$

- identitity element:
$$
\begin{equation*}
  [e]_H = H
\tag{\label{equation-32}}
\end{equation*}
$$

- inverses: for each $g\in G$
$$
\begin{equation*}
  ([g]_H)^{-1} := [g^{-1}]_H
\tag{\label{equation-33}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Moreover, for any $g_1, g_2 \in G$ we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g_1]_H = [g_2]_H \iff \exists h\in H: g_1 = g_2 h
\tag{\label{equation-34}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since for any $g_1, g_2 \in G$ we have $g_1 g_2 \in G$, the multiplication deifned in $\eqref{equation-31}$ is a binary map

$$
\begin{align*}
  \cdot: \set{[g]_h}_{g\in G}\times\set{[g]_h}_{g\in G} \to& \set{[g]_h}_{g\in G} \\
  ([g_1]_H, [g_2]_H) \mapsto& [g_1 g_2]_H
\end{align*}
$$

and associativity of $\cdot$ follows from associativity in $G$

$$
\begin{align*}
  ([g_1]_H \cdot [g_2]_H) \cdot [g_3]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}}& [g_1 g_2]_H \cdot [g_3]_H \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}}& [(g_1 g_2) g_3]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-6}} [g_1 g_2 g_3]_H
\end{align*}
$$

To show that the product as defined in $\eqref{equation-31}$ does not depend on the particular $g_1$ and $g_2$ chose to represent the cosets $[g_1]_H$ and $[g_2]_H$, requires the invariance property of $H$. For this let $i \in\set{1,2}$ and $\tilde{g}_i \in G$ be such that $\tilde{g}_i \neq g_i$, but $[\tilde{g}_1]_H = [g_i]_H$. Then there exist $h_i \in H$ for $i\in\set{1,2}$ such that $\tilde{g}_i = g_i h_i$ and thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [\tilde{g}_1 \tilde{g}_2]_H = [g_1 h_1 g_2 h_2]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-35}} [g_1 h_1 g_2]_H
\tag{\label{equation-36}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $H$ is assumed normal, Definition $\ref{definition-1}$ implies that for any $\tilde{h} \in H$ and $g\in G$ there exist $h\in H$ such that $g\tilde{h} g^{-1} = h$ and thus $g\tilde{h} = hg$. Using this for $h = h_1$ and $g = g_2$ in $\eqref{equation-36}$ gives

$$
  [\tilde{g}_1 \tilde{g}_2]_H = [g_1 h_1 g_2]_H = [g_1 g_2 \tilde{h}_1]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-35}} [g_1 g_2]_H
$$

which shows that the product of two cosets $[g_1]_H \cdot [g_2]_H$ as defined in $\eqref{equation-31}$ does not depend on the choice of the $g_i$ to represent the cosets.

For any $g\in G$ we have

$$
  [g]_H \cdot [e]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}} [ge]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-7}} [g]_H
$$

which proves that $[e]_H$ is the identity element.

Finally,

$$
\begin{align*}
  [g]_H \cdot ([g]_H)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-33}}& [g]_H \cdot [g^{-1}]_H \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}}& [e]_H
\end{align*}
$$

which verifies that every $[g]_H$ has an inverse in $\set{[g]_H}_{g\in G}$.

To prove $\eqref{equation-34}$, let $g_1, g_2 \in G$. Then we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  & [g_1]_H = [g_2]_H \\
  \underbrace{\iff}^{\eqref{equation-77}}& \set{g_1 h_1}_{h_1 \in H} = \set{g_2 h_2}_{h_2 \in H} \\
  \iff& \forall h_1 \in H \; \exists h_2 \in H \land \forall h_2 \in H \; \exists h_1 \in H: g_1 h_1 = g_2 h_2 \\
  \iff& \forall h_1 \in H \; \exists h_2 \in H \land \forall h_2 \in H \; \exists h_1 \in H: g_1 = g_2 \underbrace{h_2 h_1^{-1}}_{=h\in H} \\
  \iff& \exists h \in H: g_1 = g_2 h
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Quotient group' boxType="definition">
Let $H \unlhd G$ be a normal subgroup of the group $G$. The group $(\set{[g]_H}_{g\in G}, \cdot)$ given by the cosets of $H$ in $G$ with the multiplication, identity element and inverses as given in Proposition $\ref{proposition-7}$ is called the *quotient group* of $H$ in $G$ and denoted by $G/H$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="example">
Consider the additive Abelian group $(\Z, +)$ and its subgroup $(n\Z, +)$ for some $n > 1$. The addition of two cosets $[g_1]_{n\in\Z}, [g_2]_{n\in\Z} \in\Z/n\Z$, written $+_{\Z/n\Z}$, is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  [g_1]_{n\Z} +_{\Z/n\Z} [g_2]_{n\Z} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-37}}& [g_1 \bmod n]_{n\Z} +_{\Z/n\Z} [g_2 \bmod n]_{n\Z} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}}& [g_1 \bmod n + g_2 \bmod n]_{n\Z} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-37}}& [(g_1 \bmod n + +g_2 \bmod n) \bmod n]_{n\Z} \\
  =& [(g_1 + g_2) \bmod n]_{n\Z} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-16}}& [(g_1 +_{\Z_n} g_2)]_{n\Z} \tag{\label{equation-42}}
\end{align*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
Let $H \unlhd H$ be a normal subgroup of the finite group $G$. Then the order of the quotient group $G/H$ is given by the quotient of the orders of $G$ and $H$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  |G/H| = \frac{|G|}{|H|}
\tag{\label{equation-66}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

From Theorem $\ref{theorem-5}$ we know that there are exactly $|G|/|H|$ distinct cosets in $\set{[g]_H}_{g\in G}$, which is the set of elements of the group $G/H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

# Group homomorphisms

<MathBox title="Group homomorphism" boxType="definition">
Let $(G_1, *_{G_1})$ and $(G_2, *_{G_2})$ be groups. A group homomorphism from $G_1$ to $G_2$ is a map $\varphi: G_1 \to G_2$ satisfies for all $g, h \in G_1$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \phi(g) *_{G_2} \varphi(h) = \phi(g \cdot_{G_1} h)
\tag{\label{equation-41}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In other words, a group homomorphism preserves the group operation.

The set of all isomorphisms from a group $G_1$ to a group $G_2$ is denoted $\operatorname{Hom}(G_1, G_2)$.
</MathBox>

Let $(G, *)$ be a group and let $H \leq G$ be a subgroup. The inclusion map $\iota: H\to G$ by $\varphi(h) = h$ is a group homomorphism. Since every group homomorphism preserves identity elements, it follows that $\varphi(e_H) = e_G$. On the other hand, by definition of the inclusion map, $\iota(e_H) = e_H$. Consequently, $e_H = e_G$.


<MathBox title="Exponential function" boxType="example">
Consider the additive group $(\R, +)$ and the multiplicative group $(\R\setminus\set{0},\cdot)$. Define the map $\varphi: \R\to\R\setminus\set{0}$ by the exponential function $\varphi(x) = e^x$. Then for any $x, y\in\R$

$$
  \varphi(x + y) = e^{x+y} = e^x e^y = \varphi(x)\cdot \varphi(y)
$$

showing that $\varphi$ is a group homomorphism.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Group isomorphism" boxType="definition">
Let $(G_1, *_{G_1})$ and $(G_2, *_{G_2})$ be two groups. A map $\phi: G_1 \to G_2$ is an *isomorphism* if it is a homomorphism and a bijection. The groups $G_1$ and $G_2$ are isomorphic if there exists an isomorphism between them, denoted $G_1 \cong G_2$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Inner group automorphism" boxType="definition">
A inner automorphism of a group $(G, *)$ is a group automorphism $\varphi: G\to G$ for which there exists and element $g\in G$ such that

$$
  \varphi(x) = g * x * g^{-1},\;\forall x\in G
$$

Equivalently, for each $g \in G$, the map $\iota_G : G\to G$ defined by $\iota_g (x) = g * x * g^{-1}$ is an automorphism of $G$, called the inner automorphism induced by $g$.

The set of all inner automorphisms of $G$ forms a subgroup of the automorphism group $\operatorname{Aut}(G)$, denoted $\operatorname{Inn}(G) = \set{\iota_g}_{g\in G}$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Properties of group homomorphisms" boxType="proposition">
Let $G_1$ and $G_2$ be groups. A group homomorphism $\varphi: G_1 \to G_2$ satisfies

1. **Preservation of the identity element:** If $e_{G_1} \in G_1$ is an identity element, then $\varphi(e_{G_1}) = e_{G_2}$
2. **Preservation of inverses:**
$$
\begin{equation*}
  \varphi(g^{-1}) = \phi(g)^{-1},\; \forall g\in G_1
\tag{\label{equation-54}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):**
Since $e_{G_1} \in G_1$ is the identity element we have

$$
  e_{G_1} e_{G_1} = e_{G_1}
$$

Applying $\varphi$ and using the homomorphism property gives

$$
  \varphi(e_{G_1}) = \varphi(e_{G_1} e_{G_1}) = \varphi(e_{G_1}) \varphi(e_{G_1})
$$

Multiplying both sides on the left by $\varphi(e_{G_1})^{-1}$ yields

$$
  \varphi(e_{G_1})^{-1} \varphi(e_{G_1}) = \varphi(e_{G_1})^{-1} (\varphi(e_{G_1} e_{G_1})
$$

Using associativity, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  e_{G_2} =& [\underbrace{\varphi(e_{G_1})^{-1} \varphi(e_{G_1})}_{e_{G_2}}] \varphi(e_{G_1}) \\
  =& \varphi(e_{G_1})
\end{align*}
$$

**(2):**
Let $g\in G_1$. Since $gg^{-1} = e_{G_1}$ we obtain

$$
  \varphi(g) \varphi(g^{-1}) = \varphi(gg^{-1}) = \varphi(e_{G_1}) = e_{G_1}
$$

Similarly

$$
  \varphi(g^{-1}) \varphi(g) = \varphi(g^{-1} g) = \varphi(e_{G_1}) = e_{G_2}
$$

Thus $\varphi(g^{-1})$ is a two-sided inverse of $\varphi(g)$. Uniqueness of inverses in a group implies that $\varphi(g^{-1}) = \varphi(g)^{-1}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="example">
Let $n\in\N_+$. The quotient group $\Z/n\Z$ is isomorphic to the group $\Z$ with isomorphism $\iota: \Z/n\Z \to \Z_n$ given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_{n\Z} \mapsto g\bmod n
\tag{\label{equation-43}}
\end{equation*}
$$

To begin with, we show that $\iota$ is well defined, in other words, that the image $\iota([g]_{n\Z})$ does not depend on the $g\in\Z$ chosen to represent the coset $[g]_{n\Z}$. To see this, let $g_1, g_2 \in\Z$ be such that $[g_1]_{n\Z} = [g_2]_{n\Z}$. Then it follows from $\eqref{equation-37}$ that $[g_1 \bmod n]_{n\Z} = [g_2 \bmod n]_{n\Z}$. Since $g_i \bmod n \in\set{0,\dots,n-1}$ for $i\in\set{1,2}$, this implies $g_1 \bmod n = g_2 \bmod n$, hence $\iota([g_1]_{n\Z}) = \iota([g_2]_{n\Z})$.

Now, suppose $g_1, g_2 \in \Z$ are such that $[g_1]_{n\Z} \neq [g_2]_{n\Z}$. Then it follows from $\eqref{equation-37}$ that $g_1 \bmod n \neq g_2 \bmod n$, since otherwise their cosets would be equal. Consequently, $\iota([g_1]_{n\Z}) \neq \iota([g_2]_{n\Z})$, which means that $\iota$ is injective. It is also surjective, since by $\eqref{equation-37}$, every $m\in\set{0,\dots,n-1}$ uniquely defines a coset $[m]_{n\Z}$, which also satisfies $\iota([m]_{n\Z}) = m$. Thus, $\iota$ is bijective.

It remains to show that $\iota$ is also a homomorphism. For this we apply $\iota$ to both sides $\eqref{equation-42}$ to obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \iota([g_1]_{n\Z} +_{\Z/n\Z} [g_2]_{n\Z}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-42}}& \iota\left([(g_1 + g_2) \bmod n]_{n\Z}\right) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-43}}& (g_1 + g_2) \bmod n \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-16}}& g_1 \bmod n +_{\Z_n} g_2 \bmod n \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-43}}& \iota([g_1]_{n\Z}) +_{\Z_n} \iota([g_2]_{n\Z})
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $\iota$ satisfies $\eqref{equation-41}$. Hence $\Z/n\Z \cong \Z_n$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Subgroups under homomorphisms" boxType="proposition">
Let $(G, *)$ and $(H, \circ)$ be groups and $\varphi: G\to H$ a group homomorphism. If $U\leq G$ and $V\leq H$ are subgroups, then
1. the image $\varphi[U] \subseteq H$ is a subgroup of $H$
2. the preimage $\varphi^{-1} [V] \subseteq G$ is a subgroup of $G$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** We first verify that the image $\varphi[U9$ is nonempty. Since $U\neq\emptyset$, then for any $u$ we have $\varphi(u)\in\varphi[U]$, so $\varphi[U] \neq\emptyset$.

For any $a, b \in \varphi[U] \subseteq H$ there exist $x, y \in U$ such that $\varphi(x) = a$ and $\varphi(y) = b$. Using the homomorphism property and closure of $U$, we get

$$
\begin{align*}
  a * b^{-1} =& \varphi(x) * \varphi(y)^{-1} \\
  =& \varphi(x) \circ \varphi(y^{-1}) \\
  =& \varphi(\underbrace{x \circ y^{-1}}_{\in U}) \in \varphi[U]
\end{align*}
$$

which follows since $y^{-1} \in U$ and $x * y^{-1} \in U$.

Because $\varphi[U]$ is closed under $a \circ b^{-1}$ it follows from the one-step subgroup test (Proposition $\ref{proposition-2}$) that $\varphi(U) \leq H$.

**(2):** We first verify that the preimage $\varphi^{-1} [V]$ is nonempty. Since $e_H \in V$ and $\varphi(e_G) = e_H$, we have $e_G \in \varphi^{-1} (V)$, so $\varphi^{-1} [V]$ is nonempty.

If $x, y \in \varphi^{-1} [V]$, then $\varphi(x), \varphi(y) \in V$. Since $V$ is a subgroup, it is closed under $\circ$ and inverses, so

$$
  \varphi(x) \circ\varphi(y)^{-1} \in V
$$

Using the homomorphism property, we obtain

$$
  \varphi(x\circ y^{-1}) = \varphi(x) * \varphi(y)^{-1}  \in V
$$

implying $x\circ y^{-1} \in \varphi^{-1} [V]$. By Proposition $\ref{proposition-2}$, it follows that $\varphi^{-1} [V] \leq G$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Kernels and quotients

<MathBox title="Kernel of group homomorphisms" boxType="definition">
The *kernel* of a group homomorphism $\phi:G_1 \to G_2$ is the set

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \ker(\phi) = \set{g\in G_1 | \phi(g) = e_2}
\tag{\label{equation-45}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which is the pre-image in $G_1$ of the neutral element $e_2 \in G_2$. It follows immediately from this definition that $\phi$ is injective if and only if $\ker(\phi) = \set{e_2}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $\ker(\phi) = \set{e_2}$, then

$$
\begin{align*}
  &\phi(g) = \phi(g') \implies \phi(g^{-1} g') = e_2
  \implies& g^{-1} g' = e_2 \implies g = g'
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $\phi$ is injective. The converse is obvious.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition" tag="14">
Let $\phi\operatorname{Hom}(G_1, G_2)$ be a homomorphism between the two groups $G_1$ and $G_2$. Then $\ker(\phi)$ is a normal subgroup of $G_1$, i.e.

$$
  \ker(\phi) \unlhd G_1
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We first show that $\ker(\phi)$ is a subgroup of $G_1$. For $i\in\set{1,2}$ let $e_i$ denote the identity element in $G_i$. For any $g\in G_1$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi(e_1) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8},\eqref{equation-7}}& \phi(e_1) \phi(g) (\phi(g))^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(e_1 g) (\phi(g))^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-15}}& \phi(g) (\phi(g))^{-1} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}}& e_2 \tag{\label{equation-44}}
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $e_1 \in\ker(\phi)$.

Next, for any $h\in\ker(\phi)$ it follows that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi(h^{-1}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-15}}& e_2 \phi(h^{-1}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-45}} \phi(h) \phi(h^{-1}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(hh^{-1}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-44}}& e_2
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $h^{-1} \in\ker(\phi)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-22}$.

Finally, for any $h_1, h_2 \ker(\phi)$

$$
  \phi(h_1 h_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \phi(h_1) \phi(h_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-45}} e_2 e_2 = e_2
$$

which shows that $h_1 h_2 \ker(\phi)$, verifying $\eqref{equation-21}$.

It remains to show that $\ker(\phi)$ is normal. For this let $g\in G_1$ be arbitrary and let $h' \in\ker(\phi)^g$, that is, there is $h\in\ker(\phi)$ such that $h' = ghg^{-1}$. Then we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi(h') =& \phi(ghg^{-1}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \phi(g) \phi(h) \phi(g^{-1}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-45}}& \phi(g) e_2 \phi(g^{-1}) = \phi(g)\phi(g^{-1}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(gg^{-1}) = \phi(e_1) \\
  =& e_2
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that for any $g\in G_1$ we have that $h' \in\ker(\phi)^g$ implies $h' \in\ker(\phi)$. Hence, we have shown that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \ker(\phi)^g \subseteq \ker(\phi),\; \forall g\in G_1
\tag{\label{equation-46}}
\end{equation*}
$$

To finally show the reverse inclusion, let $h\in\ker(\phi)$ and $g\in G_1$ be arbitrary. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
  k = g^{-1} hg
\tag{\label{equation-47}}
\end{equation*}
$$

satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi(k) =& \phi(g^{-1} hg) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \phi(g^{-1}) \phi(h) \phi(g) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-45}}& \phi(g^{-1}) e_2 \phi(g) = \phi(g^{-1}) \phi(g) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(g^{-1} g) = \phi(e_1) \\
  =& e_2
\end{align*}
$$

such that $k\in\ker(\phi)$. For the arbitrary $h\in\ker(\phi)$ it then follows that

$$
  h \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-47}} gkg^{-1} \in\ker(\phi)^g
$$

Consequently, we have

$$
  \ker(\phi) \subseteq \ker(\phi)^g,\; \forall g\in G_1
$$

which together with $\eqref{equation-46}$ proves that $\ker(\phi) = \ker(\phi)^g$ for all $g\in G_1$, that is, $\ker(\phi)$ is a normal subgroup of $G_1$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Normal subgroups are kernels of homomorphisms' boxType="proposition">
Every normal subgroup occurs as the kernel of a homomorphism. More precisely, if $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, then there is a unique group structure on the set $G/N$ of cosets of $N$ in $G$ for which the natural map $a\mapsto [a]: G \to G/N$ is a homomorphism.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Write the cosets as left cosets, and define $(aN)(bN) = (ab)N$. We have to check **(1)** that this is well-defined, and **(2)** that it gives a group structure on the set of cosets. It will then be obvious that the map $g\mapsto gN$ is a homomorphism with kernel $N$.
1. Let $aN = a'N$ and $bN = b'N$. We have to show that $abN = a'b'N$. However, by proposition

$$
  abN = a(bN) = a(b'N) = aNb' = a'Nb' = a'b'N
$$
2. The product is certainly associate, the coset $N$ is an identity element, and $a^{-1} N$ is an inverse for $aN$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Universal property of quotient groups' boxType="proposition" tag="16">
The map $a\mapsto aN: G\to G/N$ has the following universal property: for any homomorphism $\alpha:G\to G'$ of groups such that $\alpha(N) = \Set{e}$, there exists a unique homomorphism $G/N \to G'$ making the following diagram commute.

<TikzFigure caption='Universal property for quotient groups'>
{String.raw`\begin{tikzcd}[
  every label/.append style={text=black},
  every arrow/.append style={draw=black},
  every cell/.append style={text=white}
]
  G \arrow[r, "a\mapsto aN"] \arrow[dr, "\alpha" swap] & 
  G/N \arrow[d, dashed] \\
  & G'
\end{tikzcd}`}
</TikzFigure>

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Note that for $n\in N$, we have $\alpha(gn) = \alpha(g)\alpha(n) = \alpha(g)$, and so $\alpha$ is constant on each left coset $gN$ of $N$ in $G$. It therefore defines a map $\bar{\alpha}: G/N \to G'$ by $\bar{\alpha}(gN) = \alpha(g)$, which is a homomorphism because

$$
\begin{align*}
  \bar{\alpha}((gN)\cdot(g'N)) =& \bar{\alpha}(gg'N) = \alpha(gg') \\
  =& \alpha(g)\alpha(g') = \bar{\alpha}(gN)\bar{\alpha}(g'N)
\end{align*}
$$

The uniqueness of $\bar{\alpha}$ follows from the surjectivity of $G\to G/N$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Homomorphism/isomorphism theorems

<MathBox title='First group isomorphism theorem' boxType="theorem">
Let $G_1$ and $G_2$ be groups and let $\phi\in\operatorname{Hom}(G_1, G_2)$. Then we have

$$
  G_1 / \ker(\phi) \cong \phi\set{\phi_1}
$$

where the isomorphism is given by the map $\widehat{\phi}: G_1 /\ker(\phi) \to \phi\set{G_1}$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_{\ker(\phi)} \mapsto \phi(g)
\tag{\label{equation-48}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

From Proposition $\ref{proposition-14}$ we know that $\ker(\phi)$ is a normal subgroup of $G_1$, and we can define the quotient group $G_1 / \ker(\phi)$. To show that $\widehat{\phi}$ is an isomorphism, we first show that it is well defined. For this let $g_a, g_b \in G_1$ and $[g_a]_{\ker(\phi)} = [g_b]_{\ker(\phi)}$. Then we know from $\eqref{equation-34}$ that there exists $h\in\ker(\phi)$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g_a = g_b h
\tag{\label{equation-49}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Consequently, for the identity element $e_2 \in G_2$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \widehat{\phi}([g_a]_{\ker(\phi)}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-48}}& \phi(g_a) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-49}} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(g_b) \phi(h) \underbrace{=}_{h\in\ker(\phi)} \phi(g_b) e_2 \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-8}} \phi(g_b) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-48}} \widehat{\phi}([g_b]_{\ker(\phi)})
\end{align*}
$$

proving that $\widehat{\phi}$ is well defined. Injectivity of $\widehat{\phi}$ is proven by the following chain of implications for $g_c, g_d \in G_1$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \widehat{\phi}([g_c]_{\ker(\phi)}) = \widehat{\phi}([g_d]_{\ker(\phi)}) \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-48}}& \phi(g_c) = \phi(g_d) \\
  \implies& \phi(g_d)^{-1} \phi(g_c) = e_2 \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \phi(g_d^{-1} g_c) = e_2 \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-45}}& g_d^{-1} g_c \in\ker(\phi) \\
  \implies& \exists h \in\ker(\phi): g_c = g_d h \\
  \underbrace{\implies}_{\eqref{equation-27}}& [g_c]_{\ker(\phi)} = [g_d]_{\ker(\phi)}
\end{align*}
$$

To show surjectivity, note that $\phi\set{G_1} = \set{\phi(g)}_{g\in G_1}$. Thus, for any $h\in\phi\set{G_1}$ there exists $g\in G_1$ such that

$$
  h = \phi(g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-48}} = \widehat{\phi}([g]_{\ker(\phi)})
$$

proving that $\widehat{\phi}$ is surjective. It remains to show that $\widehat{\phi}$ is a homomorphism. For this consider

$$
\begin{align*}
  \widehat{\phi}([g_1]_{\ker(\phi)} [g_2]_{\ker(\phi)}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}}& \widehat{\phi}([g_1 g_2]_{\ker(\phi)}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-48}}& \phi(g_1 g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \phi(g_1) \phi(g_2) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-48}}& \widehat{\phi}([g_1]_{\ker(\phi)}) \widehat{\phi}([g_2]_{\ker(\phi)})
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $\widehat{\phi} \in\operatorname{Hom}_{G_1 / \ker(\phi), \phi\set{G_1}}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Homomorphism theorem' boxType="theorem" tag="2">
For any group homomorphism $\alpha:G\to G'$, the kernel $N$ of $\alpha$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, the image $I$ of $\alpha$ is a subgroup of $G'$ and $\alpha$ factors in a natural way into the composite of a surjection, an isomorphism, and an injection

$$
\begin{CD}
  G @>{\alpha}>> G' \\
  @V{g\mapsto gn}V{\text{surjective}}V @AA{\text{injective}}A \\
  G/N @>{gN \mapsto \alpha(g)}>{\text{isomorphism}}> I
\end{CD}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By Proposition $\ref{proposition-14}$, the kernel of $\alpha$ is a normal subgroup of $G$. If $b = \alpha(a)$ and $b' = \alpha(a')$, then $bb' = \alpha(aa')$ and $b^{-1} = \alpha(a^{-1})$, and so $I := \alpha(G)$ is a subgroup of $G'$. The universal property of quotients (Proposition $\ref{proposition-16}$) shows that the map $x\mapsto \alpha(x):G\to I$ defines a homomorphism $\bar{\alpha}:G/N\to I$ with $\bar{\alpha}(gN) = \alpha(g)$. The homomorphism $\bar{\alpha}$ is certainly surjective, and if $\bar{\alpha} (gN) = e$, then $g\in\ker(\alpha) = N$. Thus, $\bar{\alpha}$ has a trivial kernel, making it injective. 
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Isomorphism theorem' boxType="theorem">
Let $G$ be a group, $H$ a subgroup of $G$ and $N$ a normal subgroup of $G$. Then $H\cap N$ is normal in $H$ and $H/(H\cap N) \cong NH/N$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By Proposition $\ref{proposition-13}$, $HN$ is a subgroup. Consider the map $h\mapsto hN: H\to G/N$. This is a homomorphism, and its kernel is $H\cap N$, which is therefore normal in $H$. By Theorem $\ref{theorem-2}$, the map induces an isomorphism $H/(H\cap N) \to I$, where $I$ is its image. However, $I$ is the set of cosets of the form $hN$ with $h\in H$, i.e., $I = HN/N$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Correspondence theorem' boxType="theorem" tag="6">
Let $\alpha: G\to \bar{G}$ be a surjective homomorphism, and let $N = \ker(\alpha)$. Then there is a bijection

$$
  \Set{\text{subgroup of }G\text{ containing }N} \leftrightarrow {\text{subgroup of }G}
$$

under which a subgroup $H$ of $G$ containing $N$ corresponds to $\bar{H} = \alpha(H)$ and a subgroup $\bar{H}$ of $\bar{G}$ corresponds to $H = \alpha^{-1} (\bar{H})$. Moreover, if $H \cong \bar{H}$ and $H' \cong \bar{H}'$, then
1. $\bar{H} \subset \bar{H}' \iff H \subset H'$, in which case $|\bar{H}': \bar{H}| = |H': H|$
2. $\bar{H}$ is normal in $\bar{G}$ if and only if $H$ is normal in $G$, in which case, $\alpha$ induces an isomorphism
$$
  G/H \cong \bar{G}/\bar{H}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $\bar{H}$ is a subgroup of $\bar{G}$, then $\alpha^{-1}(\bar{H})$ is easily seen to be a subgroup of $G$ containing $N$, and if $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $\alpha(H)$ is a subgroup of $\bar{G}$. Clearly, $\alpha^{-1} \alpha(H) = HN$, which equals $H$ if and only if $H \supset N$, and $\alpha\alpha^{-1} (\bar{H}) = \bar{H}$. Thus, the two operations give the required bijection. The remaining statements are easily verified. For example, a decomposition $H' = \bigsqcup_{i\in I} a_i H$ of $H'$ into a disjoint union of left cosets of $H$ gives a similar decomposition $H' = \bigsqcup_{i\in I} \alpha(a_i) \bar{H}$ of $\bar{H}'$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
Let $N$ be a subgroup of $G$. Then there is a bijection between the set of subgroups of $G$ containing $N$ and the set of subgroups of $G/N$, $H\leftrightarrow H/N$. Moreover, $H$ is normal in $G$ if and only if $H/N$ is normal in $G/N$, in which case the homomorphism $g\mapsto gN: G\to G/N$ induces an isomorphism

$$
  G/H \cong (G/N)/(H/N)
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

This is a special case of the correspondence theorem ($\ref{theorem-6}$) with $\alpha:G\to G/N$ given by $g\mapsto gN$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Direct products

<MathBox title='Direct product group' boxType="definition">
Let $(G_1, *_{G_1})$ and $(G_2, *_{G_2})$ be groups. Their *direct product group* $(G_1 \times G_2, *_{\times})$ is defined as the set of pairs $(g_1, g_2) \in G_1 \times G_2$ with component-wise multiplication

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  *_\times : (G_1 \times G_2) \times (G_1 \times G_2) \to& G_1 \times G_2 \\
  \left((g_1, g_2), (g'_1, g'_2) \right) \mapsto& (g_1 *_{G_1} g'_1, g_2 *_{G_2} g'_2)
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-104}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Details</summary>

Associativity of $*_\times$ follows from the associativity of $*_{G_1}$ and $*_{G_2}$. The identity element is given by 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  e_{G_1 \times G_2} := (e_{G_1}, e_{G_2})
\tag{\label{equation-105}}
\end{equation*}
$$

since for any $(g_1, g_2) \in G_1 \times G_2$

$$
  (g_1, g_2) *_\times (e_{G_1}, e_{G_2}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-104}} (g_1 e_{G_1}, g_2 e_{G_2}) = (g_1, g_2)
$$

The inverse of $(g_1, g_2) \in G_1 \times G_2$ is given by

$$
  (g_1, g_2)^{-1} = (g_1^{-1}, g_2^{-1})
$$

since

$$
\begin{align*}
  (g_1, g_2) *_\times (g_1, g_2)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-104}}& (g_1 g_1^{-1}, g_2 g_2^{-1}) \\
  = (e_{G_1}, e_{G_2}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-105}} e_{G_1 \times G_2}
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Order of finite direct product groups' boxType="proposition">
Let $G_1$ and $G_2$ be finite groups. The order of the direct product group $G_1 \times G_2$ is given by

$$
  |G_1 \times G_2| = |G_1| \cdot |G_2|
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $G_1$ and $G_2$ are both finite with respective orders $|G_1|$ and $|G_2|$, then the direct product group $G_1 \times G_2$ is finite as well with order $|G_1 \times G_2| = |G_1| \cdot |G_2|$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Criteria for direct products of groups' boxType="proposition">
A group $G$ is a direct product of subgroups $H_1$ and $H_2$ if and only if
1. $G = H_1 H_2$
2. $H_1 \cap H_2 = \Set{e}$
3. $h_1 h_2 = h_2 h_1$ for all $h_1 \in H_1$ and $h_2 \in H_2$
4. $H_1 \unlhd G$ and $H_2 \unlhd G$ 

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $G$ is the direct product of $H_1$ and $H_2$, then certainly **(1)** and **(3)** hold, and **(2)** holds because, for any $g\in H_1 \cap H_2$, the element $(g, g^{-1})$ maps to $e$ under $(h_1, h_2) \mapsto h_1 h_2$ and so equals $(e,e)$.

Conversely, **(3)** implies that $(h_1, h_2)\mapsto h_1 h_2$ is a homomorphism, and **(2)** implies that it is injective

$$
  h_1 h_2 = e \mapsto h_1 = h_2^{-1} \in H_1 \cap H_2 = \Set{e}
$$

Finally, **(1)** implies that it is injective.

Two elements $h_1, h_2$ of a group commute if and only if their commutator

$$
  [h_1, h_2] := (h_1 h_2)(h_2 h_1)^{-1} = e
$$

However,

$$
  (h_1 h_2)(h_2 h_1)^{-1} = h_1 h_2 h_1^{-1} h_2^{-1} = \begin{cases}
    (h_1 h_2 h_1^{-1})\cdot h_2^{-1} \\
    h_1 \cdot (h_2 h_1^{-1} h_2^{-1})
  \end{cases} 
$$

which is in $H_2$ because $H_2$ is normal, and is in $H_1$ because $H_1$ is normal. Thus, **(2)** implies $[h_1, h_2] = e$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Characters

<MathBox title="Character of Abelian groups" boxType="definition">
A *character* of an Abelian group $G$ is defined as an element $\chi\in\operatorname{Hom}(G, \operatorname{U}(1))$, where

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \operatorname{U}(1) = \set{z\in\mathbb{C} | z\overline{z} = 1} = \set{e^{i\alpha} | \alpha\in\R}
\tag{\label{equation-53}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is the special unitary group in one dimension.

For any character $\chi$ of an Abelian group $G$ we define the *conjugate character* $\overline{\chi}:G\to \operatorname{U}(1)$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g \mapsto \overline{\widehat{\chi}(g)}
\tag{\label{equation-50}}
\end{equation*}
$$

A special character for any Abelian group $G$ is the *trivial character* $1_G : G\to\operatorname{U}(1)$ by 

$$
\begin{align*}
  g\mapsto 1
\tag{\label{equation-58}}
\end{align*}
$$

that maps any group element to $1\in\operatorname{U}(1)$.
</MathBox>

Note that by definition any character $\chi$ of an Abelian group $G$ being a homomorphism from $G$ to $\operatorname{U}(1)$, it satisfies for any $g_1, g_2 \in G$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi(g_1 +_G g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \chi(g_1) \chi(g_2)
\tag{\label{equation-51}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the product on the right side is in $\operatorname{U}(1)$, which is simply a multiplication of two complex numbers of unit modulus.

As a consequence of $\eqref{equation-51}$, any character of an Abelian group has to map the identity element $e\in G$ to $1$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi(e) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-51}} \frac{\chi(g +_G e)}{\chi(g)} = 1
\tag{\label{equation-52}}
\end{equation*}
$$

since $g +_G e = g$. This statement already follows from Proposition $\ref{proposition-14}$ since as a subroup $\ker(\chi) = \set{g\in G | \chi(g) = 1}$ has to contain $e$.

Moreover, since $\chi(g) \in\operatorname{U}(1)$

$$
  1 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-53}} \chi(g) \overline{\chi(g)} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-50}} \chi(g) \overline{\chi(g)}
$$

we have

$$
  \overline{\chi(g)} = \chi(g)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-54}} \chi(g^{-1})
$$

<MathBox title='' boxType="example">
For the group $\Z_n$ we have the characters $\chi_j: \Z_n \to \operatorname{U}(1)$ given by 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_{n\Z} \mapsto e^{i2\pi jg/N},\; j\in\set{0,\dots,n-1}
\tag{\label{equation-55}}
\end{equation*}
$$

To verify $\eqref{equation-51}$, we note that for any $[g_1]_{n\Z}, [g_2]_{n\Z} \in\Z_n$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_j ([g_1]_{n\Z} +_{\Z_n} [g_2]_{n\Z}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-42}}& \chi_j \left([(g_1 + g_2) \bmod n]_{n\Z} \right) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-55}}& \exp\left(i2\pi \frac{j((g_1 + g_2) \bmod n)}{n} \right) = e^{i2\pi j(g_1 + g_2)/n} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-55}}& \chi_j ([g_1]_{n\Z}) \chi_j ([g_2]_{n\Z})
\end{align*}
$$

The kernel of a character $\chi_j$ consists of all cosets $[g]_{n\Z}$ such that $jg/n \in\Z$, that is, for which $jg \bmod n = 0$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="7">
The characters $\widehat{G} = \operatorname{Hom}(G,\operatorname{U}(1))$ of an Abelian group $G$ form an Abelian group with the trivial character $1_G$ as the identity element and with the group operation $*: \widehat{G}\times\widehat{G}\to\widehat{G}$ given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (\chi_1, \chi_2) \mapsto \chi_1 \chi_2
\tag{\label{equation-56}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\chi_1 \chi_2: G\to \operatorname{U}(1)$ is the character

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g \mapsto \chi_1 (g) \chi_2 (g)
\tag{\label{equation-57}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We show that $\widehat{G}$ satisfies the group axioms. 

**Closure:** For $\chi_1, \chi_2 \in\widehat{G}$ and $g_1, g_2 \in G$, we find

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_1 \chi_2 (g_1) \chi_1 \chi_2 (g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}}& \chi_1 (g_1) \chi_2 (g_1) \chi_1 (g_2) \chi_2 (g_2) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \chi_1 (g_1 g_2) \chi_2 (g_1 g_2) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}}& \chi_1 \chi_2 (g_1 g_2)
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $\chi_1 \chi_2 \in\operatorname{Hom}(G, \operatorname{U}(1))$.

**Identity:** For any $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ and $g\in G$ we have

$$
  \chi 1_G (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}} \chi(g) 1_G (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-58}} \chi(g)
$$

showing that $\widehat{1}_G$ is the identity element.

**Inverses:** For any $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ its inverse is given by its conjugate character $\overline{\chi} \in\widehat{G}$ since for any $g\in G$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi\overline{\chi}(g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}} \chi(g) \overline{\chi}(g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-58}} \chi(g) \overline{\chi(g)} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-53}} 1
\tag{\label{equation-68}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Dual group' boxType="definition">
Let $G$ be an Abelian group. The group $\widehat{G} := \operatorname{Hom}(G, \operatorname{U}(1))$ formed by its characters with the group operation in $\eqref{equation-56}$ and $\eqref{equation-57}$ is the *dual* or *character group* of $G$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="3">
Let $H\in G$ be a subgroup of the finite Abelian group $G$. Then any character of $H$ can be extended to a character of $G$, and the number of such extensions is $|G|/|H|$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Recall that subgroups of Abelian groups are always normal, so we can always from quotient groups with them.

If $H = G$ we are done. Otherwise, we have $H < G$ and can pick $g_1 \in G \setminus H$. Let $H_1 = \braket{H, g_1}$ denote the subgroup generated by the set $H\cup\set{g_1}$ such that

$$
  H_1 = \braket{H, g_1} = \set{hg_1^m | h\in H, m\in\Z}
$$

and

$$
  H \leq H_1 \leq G
$$

Furthermore, we set

$$
\begin{equation*}
  k := \min\set{n\in\N_+ | g_1^n \in H}
\tag{\label{equation-59}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which exists since at least $g_1^m = e_G \in H$ for some finite $m\in\N$. Consequently, for any charcter $\chi_H \in \widehat{H}$, we have that $\chi(g_1^k) = e^{i\alpha} \in\operatorname{U}(1)$ for some $\alpha\in\R$. There are $k$ $k$-th roots of $e^{i\alpha}$, which are of the form

$$
  \beta_{k,l} = \frac{\alpha + 2\pi l}{k}\; l \in\set{0,\dots,k-1}
$$

These satisfy

$$
  e^{i\beta_{k,l}} = (\chi_H (g_l^k))^{1/k} =: \mu_l (g_1)
$$

which implies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mu_l (g_1)^k = \chi_H (g_1^k),\; l\in\set{0,\dots,k-1}
\tag{\label{equation-60}}
\end{equation*}
$$

For each such $l$ we want to define a character $\chi_l \in\widehat{H}_l$, which acts on a generic element $hg_1^j \in H_1$ by

$$
  \chi_l (hg_1^j) := \chi_H (h) \mu_l (g_1)^j
$$

For this to be a meaningful definition, we need to make sure that the right side does not depend on the particular $h$ and $g_1^j$ but only their product $hg_1^j$. To show this, suppose that $h, \tilde{h}\in H$ and $j, \tilde{j}\in\Z$ are such that $\tilde{j} \geq j$ and

$$
  hg_1^j = \tilde{h} g_1^{\tilde{j}}
$$

From this it follows that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g_1^{\tilde{j} - j} = h\tilde{h}^{-1} \in H
\tag{\label{equation-61}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and since $\eqref{equation-60}$ implies that $k$ is the smallest natural number such that $g_1^k \in H$, we must have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{j} = j + mk
\tag{\label{equation-62}}
\end{equation*}
$$

for some $m\in\N$. Thus, $\eqref{equation-61}$ implies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  h = \tilde{h} g_1^{mk}
\tag{\label{equation-63}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Consequently

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_H (\tilde{h}) \mu_l (g_1)^{\tilde{j}} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-62}}& \chi_H (\tilde{h}) \mu_l (g_1)^{j + mk} \\
  =& \chi_H (\tilde{h}) (\mu_l (g_1)^k)^m \mu_l (g_1)^j \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-60}}& \chi_H (\tilde{h})(\chi_H (g_1^k))^m \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \chi_H (\tilde{h} g_1^{mk}) \mu_l (g_1)^j \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-63}}& \chi_H (h) \mu_l (g_1)^j
\end{align*}
$$

and

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \chi_l : H_1 \to& \operatorname{U}(1) \\
  hg_1^j \mapsto& \chi_H (h) \mu_l (g_1)^j
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-64}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is well defined. Moreover, for any $h_i g_1^{j_i} \in H_1$, where $i\in\set{1,2}$, we have 

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_l (h_1 g_1^{j_1} h_2 g_1^{j_2}) =& \chi_l (h_1 h_2 g_1^{j_1 + j_2}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-64}}& \chi_H (h_1 h_2) \mu_l (g_1)^{j_1 + j_2} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \chi_H (h_1) \mu_l (g_1)^{j_1} \chi_H (h_2) \mu_l (g_1)^{j_2} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-64}}& \chi_l (h_1 g_1^{j_1}) \chi_l (h_2 g_1^{j_2})
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $\chi_l \in\operatorname{Hom}(H_1, \operatorname{U}(1))$, in other words, $\chi_l$ is a character of $H_1 = \braket{H, g_1}$ that satisfies $\chi_l |_H = \chi_H$.

There are $k$ such extensions $\chi_0,\dots,\chi_{k-1}$ of $\chi_H$ to $H_1$ and since $g_1, g_1^2,\dots, g_1^{k-1} \notin H$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  H_1 = \braket{H, g_1} = \bigcup_{j=0}^{k-1} [g_1^j]_H
\tag{\label{equation-65}}
\end{align*}
$$

such that

$$
  |H_1/H| \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-66}} \frac{|H_1|}{|H|} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-65}} k
$$

We choose any of these extensions and denote it by $\chi_{H_1}$. We thus have a subgroup $H_1$ and a character $\chi_{H_1} \in\widehat{H}_1$. Now we pick any $g_2 \in G\setminus H_1$ and repeat the previous construction with $H_2 = \braket{H_1, g_2}$ leading to a character $\chi_{H_2} \in \widehat{H}_2$ that satisfies $\chi_{H_2} |_{H_1} = \chi_{H_1}$. We continue with this until there is no more $g_{n+1} \in G\setminus H_n$ to be found, which must happend after a finite number of steps since $G$ is assumed finite.

Altogether, we thus have a sequence of subgroups $H_r = \braket{H_{r-1}, g_{r-1}}$, elements $g_r \in G\setminus H_{r-1}$ and characters $\chi_{H_r} \in \widehat{H}_r$ for $r\in\set{0,\dots,n}$ such that

$$
\begin{align*}
  H =& H_0 < \cdots < H_n = G \\
  \chi_{H_r} |_{H_{r-1}} =& \chi_{H_{r-1}} \\
  \chi_G |_H = \chi_{H_n} |_H = (\chi_{H_n} |_{H_{n-1}})|_H = \chi_{H_{n-1}} |_H =\cdots= \chi_{H_0} |_H = \chi_H
\end{align*}
$$

In each step from $r-1$ to $r$ we have $|H_r|/|H_{r-1}|$ possible extensions. The number of ways in which $\chi_H$ can be extended from a character on $H = H_0$ to one on $G = H_n$ is thus

$$
  \prod_{r=1}^n \frac{|H_r|}{|H_{r-1}|} = \frac{|H_n|}{|H_0|} = \frac{|G|}{|H|}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary" tag="2">
The dual group $\widehat{G}$ of a finite Abelian group $G$ satisfies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  |\widehat{G}| = |G|
\tag{\label{equation-81}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $e\in G$ be the identity element of $G$. The subgroup $\braket{e}$ has only one element, i.e. $|\braket{e}| = 1$. Moreover, its only character is the trivial character $1_{\braket{e}}$, which, according to Theorem $\ref{theorem-3}$ can be extended in exactly

$$
  \frac{|G|}{|\braket{e}|} = |G|
$$

ways. Any character $\chi\in\widehat{G}$, when restricted to $\braket{e}$, is equal to $1_{\braket{e}}$. Hence, it must be one of the $|G|$ extensions constructed from $1_{\braket{e}}$ since, otherwise, there would be more than $|G|$ extensions. Hence, the number of characters is $|G|$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary" tag="1">
Let $G$ be a finite Abelian group with identity element $e$ and $g\in G\setminus\braket{e}$. Then there exists a character $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ such that $g\notin\ker(\chi)$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $g\in G\setminus\braket{e}$ be given. Since $G$ is finite, there exists a smallest $n\in\N$ such that $n > 1$ and $g^n = e$. With this $n$ we define the map $\mu_g : \braket{g} \to \operatorname{U}(1)$ by $g^m \mapsto e^{i2\pi m/n}$. Since any element $\tilde{g}\in\braket{g}$ can be written in the form $\tilde{g} = g^m$ for some $m\in\Z$, it follows that $\mu_g$ is defined on all of $\braket{g}$ and for any $g_i = g^{m_i} \in \braket{g}$ with $i\in\set{1,2}$, we have

$$
  \mu_g (g_1 g_2) = \mu_g (g^{m_1 + m_2}) = e^{i2\pi(m_1 + m_2)/n} = \mu_g (g_1) \mu_g (g_2)
$$

Thus, $\mu_g$ is a character of the subgroup $\braket{g} \leq G$, and it satisfies $\mu_g (g) = e^{i2\pi /n} \neq 1$. We then apply Theorem $\ref{theorem-3}$ to the subgroup $\braket{g}$ to extend the character $\mu_g$ to a character $\chi\in\tilde{G}$ such that $\chi |_{\braket{g}} = \mu_g$ and hence $\chi(g) \neq 1$.
</details>

<MathBox title='' boxType="lemma" tag="2">
Let $H \leq G$ be a subgroup of the Abelian group $G$. Then

\begin{equation*}
  H^\perp := \set{\chi\in\widehat{G} | H \subseteq\ker(\chi)}
\tag{\label{equation-67}}
\end{equation*}

is a subgroup of $\widehat{G}$.

<summary>Proof</summary>

  \chi_1 \chi_2 (h) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}} \chi_1 (h) \chi_2 (h) \underbrace{=}_{h\in H\subseteq\ker(\chi_i)} = 1

Thus, $\chi_1 \chi_2 \in H^\perp$, verifying $\eqref{equation-21}$.

For this let $h\in H$ be arbitrary. Then $\chi\in H^\perp$ implies that $\ker(\chi)$ and thus $\chi(h) = 1$ from which it follows that

$$
  1 = \overline{\chi(h)} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-50}} \overline{\chi}(h)
$$

Consequently, $\chi^{-1} = \overline{\chi} \in H^\perp$, verifying $\eqref{equation-22}$

<MathBox title='' boxType="lemma">
Let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of the finite Abelian group $G$ and let $H^\perp$ be as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{h\in H} \chi(h) = \begin{cases}
    |H|,\quad& \chi \in H^\perp \\
    0,\quad& \chi \notin H^\perp
  \end{cases}
\tag{\label{equation-69}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $\chi\in\widehat{G}$, and set

$$
  S_\chi = \sum_{h\in H} \chi(h)
$$

Then for any $\check{h}\in H$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  S_\chi \chi(\check{h}) =& \sum_{h\in H} \chi(h) \chi(\check{h}) \\
  =& \sum_{h\in H} \chi(h\check{h}) = S_\chi
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-70}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the last equation follows since $\chi\in\operatorname{Hom}(G, \operatorname{U}(1))$ and thus satisfies $\eqref{equation-41}$. From $\eqref{equation-70}$ we have that for every $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ and $\check{h}\in H$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  S_\chi (\chi(\check{h} - 1)) = 0
\tag{\label{equation-71}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Now, let $\chi\in H^\perp$. Then $\eqref{equation-67}$ implies that $H \subseteq \ker(\chi)$, and it follows that $\chi(h) = 1$ for all $h\in H$. This implies $S_\chi = |H|$.

On the other hand, if there exists $\check{h}\in H$ such that $\chi(\check{h}) \neq 1$, then this is equivalent to $H \not\subseteq\ker(\chi)$ and this in turn to $\chi \notin H^\perp$. Moreover, $\eqref{equation-71}$ implies that in this case we must have $S_\chi = 0$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="4">
Let $H\leq G$ be a subgroup of an Abelian group $G$ and $H^\perp$ as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H^\perp \cong \widehat{G/H}
\tag{\label{equation-75}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $e_G \in G$ denote the identity element in $G$ and $e_{G/H} \in G/H$ that in the quotient group $G/H$. For any $\varXi\in\widehat{G/H}$, we define the map $\iota_{\varXi}: G\to \operatorname{U}(1)$ by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  g\mapsto\varXi([g]_H)
\tag{\label{equation-76}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Then we have for every $h\in H$ that

$$
\begin{align*}
 \iota_{\varXi}(h) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-76}}& \varXi([h]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-35}} \varXi([eg]_H) \\
 \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-32}}& \varXi(e_{G/H}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-32}}  1
\end{align*}
$$

such that $H\subseteq\ker(\iota_{\varXi})$ and thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \iota_{\varXi} \underbrace{\in}_{\eqref{equation-67}} H^\perp
\tag{\label{equation-77}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Moreover, for $\varXi_1, \varXi_2 \in\widehat{G/H}$ and any $g\in G$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \iota_{\varXi_1 \varXi_2} (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-76}} \varXi_1 \varXi_2 ([g]_H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}}& \varXi_1 ([g]_H) \varXi_2([g]_H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-76}}& \iota_{\varXi_1} (g) \iota_{\varXi_2} (g)
\end{align*}
$$

and from this and $\eqref{equation-77}$ we conclude that the map $\iota: \widehat{G/H} \to H^\perp$ by $\varXi\mapsto\iota_{\varXi}$ satisfies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \iota\in\operatorname{Hom}(\widehat{G/H}, H^\perp)
\tag{\label{equation-78}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Now that we have established that $\iota$ is a homomorphism it remains to show that it is also a bijection. For any $\chi\in H^\perp$ we define the map $j_\chi : G/H \to \operatorname{U}(1)$ by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_H \mapsto \chi(g)
\tag{\label{equation-equation-79}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This is well defined since $[g_1]_H = [g_2]_H$ implies $g_2 = g_1 h$ for some $h\in H$ and thus

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi(g_2) =& \chi(g_1 h) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \chi(g_1) \chi(h) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{h\in H\subseteq\ker(\chi)}& \chi(g_1)
\end{align*}
$$

where in the last equation we used that $\chi\in H^\perp$ implies $\chi(h) = 1$ for all $h\in H$. Moreover, for any $[g_1]_H, [g_2]_H \in G/H$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  j_\chi ([g_1]_H) j_\chi ([g_2]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-79}}& \chi(g_1) \chi(g_2) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \chi(g_1 g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-79}} j_\chi ([g_1 g_2]_H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}}& j_\chi ([g_1]_H [g_2]_H)
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, we have $j_\chi \in\widehat{G/H}$ and the map $j: H^\perp \to\widehat{G/H}$ by $\chi\mapsto j_\chi$ satisfies $j\in\operatorname{Hom}(H^\perp, \widehat{G/H})$. The composition $\iota\circ j: H^\perp \to H^\perp$ satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  \iota\circ j(\chi) : G\to& \operatorname{U}_1 \\
  g\mapsto& \iota_{j_\chi} (g)
\end{align*}
$$

where

$$
  \iota_{j_\chi} (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-76}} j_\chi ([g]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-79}} \chi(g)
$$

such that $\iota\circ j(\chi) = \chi$, that is, every $\chi\in H^\perp$ is an image under $\iota$ of a coset $j(\chi) \in \widehat{G/H}$, which implies that $\iota$ is surjective.

Likewise, the composition $j\circ\iota: \widehat{G/H} \to\widehat{G/H}$ satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  j\circ\iota(\varXi): G/H \to& \operatorname{U}(1) \\
  [g]_H \mapsto& j_{\iota_\varXi} ([g]_H)
\end{align*}
$$

where now

$$
  j_{\iota_\varXi} ([g]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-79}} \iota_\varXi (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-76}} \varXi ([g]_H)
$$

such that $j\circ\iota(\varXi) = \varXi$. From this it follows that for any $\varXi_1, \varXi_2 \in\widehat{G/H}$ satisfying $\varXi_1 \neq \varXi_2$ we must have $j\circ\iota(\varXi_1) \neq j\iota(\varXi_2)$ and thus $\iota(\varXi_1) \neq\iota(\varXi_2)$, proving that $\iota$ is injective as well.

Hence, we have established that $\iota$ is a bijection and because of $\eqref{equation-78}$ thus an isomorphism, implying $H^\perp \cong \widehat{G/H}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="8">
Let $H \leq G$ be a subgroup of the finite Abelian group $G$ and $H^\perp$ be as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then for each $g\in G/H$ there exists $\chi\in H^\perp$ such that $g\notin\ker(\chi)$, and we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H = \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi)
\tag{\label{equation-82}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Moreover, if the $\chi_1,\dots,\chi_L \in H^\perp$ are such that $H^\perp = \braket{\chi_1,\dots,\chi_L}$, then

$$
  H = \bigcup_{l=1}^L \ker(\chi_l)
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

As a subgroup of an Abelian group, $H$ is normal and thus by Proposition $\ref{proposition-7}$ there is a quotient group $G/H$. Let $e_G \in G$ denote the identity element in $G$ and $e_{G/H} \in G/H$ that in the quotient group $G/H$. Applying Corollary $\ref{corollary-1}$ to $G/H$ men that for each coset $[g]_H \neq [e_G]_H = e_{G/H}$ there exists $\chi_{[g]_H} \in \widehat{G/H}$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [g]_H \notin\ker(\chi_{[g]_H})
\tag{\label{equation-83}}
\end{equation*}
$$

For $g\in G$ define the map $\zeta_g : G\to \operatorname{U}_1$ by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{g} \mapsto \chi_{[g]_H} ([\tilde{g}]_H)
\tag{\label{equation-84}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which satisfies

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \zeta_g (g_1) \zeta_g (g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-84}}& \chi_{[g]_H} ([g_1]_H) \chi_{[g]_H} ([g_2]_H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \chi_{[g_1]_H} ([g_1]_H [g_2]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-31}} \chi_{[g]_H} ([g_1 g_2]_H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-84}}& \zeta_g (g_1 g_2)
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-85}}
\end{equation*}
$$

as well as for any $h\in H$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \zeta_g (h) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-84}}& \chi_{[g]_H} ([h]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-35}} \chi_{[g]_H} (H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-32}}& \chi_{[g]_H} (e_{G/H}) = 1
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-86}}
\end{equation*}
$$

whereas

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \zeta_g (g) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-84}} \chi_{[g]_H} ([g]_H) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-83}} \neq 1
\tag{\label{equation-87}}
\end{equation*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-84}$ and $\eqref{equation-85}$ we see that $\zeta_g \in\operatorname{Hom}(G, \operatorname{U}(1)) = \widehat{G}$ and from $\eqref{equation-86}$ that $H \subseteq\ker{\zeta_g}$, which together implies $\zeta_g \in H^\perp$, while $\eqref{equation-87}$ implies $g \notin\ker(\zeta_g)$.

From Lemma $\ref{lemma-1}$ we know that

$$
  H \leq \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi)
$$

However, we have just shown that for any $g\notin\ker{H}$ there exists $\chi\in H^\perp$ such that $g\notin\ker(\chi)$. Consequently, such $g$ cannot be in the intersection of all $\chi\in H^\perp$, and we must have

$$
  H = \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi)
$$

Now let $H^\perp = \braket{\chi_1,\dots,\chi_L}$. This means that for any $\chi\in H^\perp$ there exist $m_1,\dots,m_L \in\Z$ such that

$$
  \chi = \chi_1^{m_1} \cdots \chi_L^{m_L}
$$

Hence, for any $g\in G$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi(g) =& \chi_1^{m_1} \cdots \chi_L^{m_L} (g) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}}& (\chi_1 (g))^{m_1} \cdots (\chi_L (g))^{m_L}
\end{align*}
$$

Consequently, $h\in\bigcap_{l=1}^L \ker(\chi_l)$ implies $h\in\ker(\chi)$ and thus for any $\chi\in H^\perp$

$$
  \bigcap_{l=1}^L \ker(\chi_l) \subseteq \ker(\chi)
$$

from which it follows that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \bigcap_{l=1} \ker(\chi_l) \subeteq \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi)
\tag{\label{equation-88}}
\end{equation*}
$$

On the other hand, since $\chi_1,\dots,\chi_L \in H^\perp$, we also have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi) \subseteq \bigcap_{l=1}^L \ker(\chi_l)
\tag{\label{equation-89}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Together, $\eqref{equation-87}$ and $\eqref{equation-88}$ imply

$$
  \bigcap_{l=1}^L \ker(\chi_l) = \bigcap_{\chi\in H^\perp} \ker(\chi) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-82}} H
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="9">
Let $G$ be a finite Abelian group and $\widehat{G}$ its dual group. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \widehat{\widehat{}} : G\to& \widehat{\widehat{G}} \\
  g\mapsto& \widehat{\widehat{g}}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-90}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\widehat{\widehat{g}} : \widehat{G} \to \operatorname{U}(1)$ is defined as

$$
  \chi \mapsto \widehat{\widehat{g}} (\chi) := \chi(g)
$$

is an isomorphism, and we have $\widehat{\widehat{G}} \cong G$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Applying Theorem $\ref{theorem-7}$ to $\widehat{G}$ implies that $\widehat{\widehat{G}}$ is a finite Abelian group. From Corollary $\ref{corollary-2}$ it follows that $|\widehat{\widehat{G}}| = |\widehat{G}| = |G|$.

For any $g_1, g_2 \in G$ and $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \widehat{\widehat{g_1 g_2}} (\chi) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} \chi(g_1 g_2) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}} \chi(g_1) \chi(g_2) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}}& \widehat{\widehat{g_1}}(\chi) \widehat{\widehat{g_2}}(\chi)
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, $\widehat{\widehat{g_1 g_2}} = \widehat{\widehat{g_1}} \widehat{\widehat{g_2}}$, and it follows that $\widehat{\widehat{}} \in\operatorname{Hom}(G, \widehat{\widehat{G}})$. Moreover, $\widehat{\widehat{g_1}} = \widehat{\widehat{g_2}}$ implies that for any $\chi\in\hat{G}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi(g_1) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} = \widehat{\widehat{g_1}} (\chi) = \widehat{\widehat{g_2}} (\chi) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-41}} = \chi(g_2)
\tag{\label{equation-91}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and thus

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi(g_1 g_2^{-1}) \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-57}}& \chi(g_1) \chi(g_2^{-1}) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-54}} \chi(g_1) \chi(g_2)^{-1} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-91}} 1
\end{align*}
$$

for any $\chi\in\widehat{G}$ from which it follows that $g_1 g_2^{-1} = e_G$ and thus $g_1 = g_2$. Hence, the map $\widehat{\widehat{}}: G\to\widehat{\widehat{G}}$ is also injective, and since $\widehat{\widehat{G}}$ has the same number of elements as $G$, it is also a bijection and altogether an isomorphism.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="lemma" tag="3">
Let $H \leq G$ be a subgroup of a finite Abelian group $G$ and $H^\perp$ as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (H^\perp)^\perp = H
\tag{\label{equation-92}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

For any $h\in H$ and $\chi\in H^\perp$, we have

$$
  1 = \chi(h) = \widehat{\widehat{\chi}}
$$

where $\widehat{\widehat{}}$ denotes the isomorphism defined in $\eqref{equation-90}$. Hence

$$
  h\in (H^\perp)^\perp = \set{\xi\in\widehat{\widehat{G}} | H^\perp \subseteq\ker(\xi)}
$$

and it follows that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H \subseteq (H^\perp)^\perp
\tag{\label{equation-93}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Applying Corollary $\ref{corollary-3}$ to the subgroup $H^\perp \leq\widehat{G}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  |(H^\perp)^\perp| \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-80}}& \frac{|\widehat{G}|}{|H^\perp|} \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-81},\eqref{equation-80}}& \frac{|G|}{|G|/|H|} = |H|
\end{align*}
$$

which together with $\eqref{equation-93}$ implies $(H^\perp)^\perp = H$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="example">
Applying $\eqref{equation-92}$ to the trivial subgroups of Example $\ref{example-1}$ we find

$$
  \braket{e_G} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-92}} (\braket{e_G}^\perp)^\perp \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-72}} \widehat{G}^\perp
$$

whereas for the trivial subgroup $G$ itself

$$
\begin{equation*}
  G \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-92}} (G^\perp)^\perp \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-73}} \braket{e_{\widehat{G}}}^\perp
\tag{\label{equation-94}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem" tag="10">
Let $H_1$ and $H_2$ be subgroups of the finite Abelian group $G$ and let $H_1^\perp$ and $H_2^\perp$ be as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H_1 < H_2 \leq G \iff H_2^\perp < H_1^\perp \leq \widehat{G}
\tag{\label{equation-95}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By Corollary $\ref{corollary-2}$ we know that $\widehat{G}$ is a finite Abelian group, Lemma $\ref{\lemma-2}$ it implies that $H_1^\perp$ and $H_2^\perp$ are subgroups of $\widehat{G}$. Now let $H_1 < H_2$ and $\chi\in H_2^\perp$, which, on account of $\eqref{equation-67}$ implies

$$
  H_1 \subseteq H_2 \subseteq\ker(\chi)
$$

and using once more $\eqref{equation-67}$, then $\chi\in H_1^\perp$. Consequently, $H_2^\perp \leq H_1^\perp$, but from Theorem $\ref{theorem-8}$ we that for each $g\in H_2 / H_1$ there exists $\chi\in H_1^\perp$ such that $g\notin\ker(\chi)$. Hence, $H_2^\perp < H_1^\perp$, and we have shown that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H_1 < H_2 \leq G \implies H_2^\perp < H_1^\perp \leq \widehat{G}
\tag{\label{equation-96}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Applying $\eqref{equation-96}$ again yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H_2^\perp < H_1^\perp  \leq G \implies (H_1^\perp)^\perp < (H_2^\perp)^\perp \leq \widehat{\widehat{G}}
\tag{\label{equation-97}}
\end{equation*}
$$

By Theorem $\ref{theorem-9}$ we have $G \cong\widehat{\widehat{G}}$ and from Lemma $\ref{lemma-3}$ it follows that $(H_i^\perp)^\perp = H_i$ for $i\in\set{1,2}$. Thus, $\eqref{equation-97}$ becomes

$$
  H_2^\perp < H_1^\perp \leq \widehat{G} \implies H_1 < H_2 \leq G
$$

completing the proof.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
Let $G$ be a finite Abelian group, $\widehat{G}$ its dual, $H < G$ a proper subgroup and $H^\perp \leq \widehat{G}$ be as defined in $\eqref{equation-67}$. Then the following hold

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  H \text{ maximal} \iff& H^\perp \text{ minimal} \\
  H \text{ minimal} \iff& H^\perp \text{ maximal}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-98}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $H < G$ be maximal, which means that there is no $K < G$ such that

$$
  H < K < G \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-94}} = \braket{e_{\widehat{G}}}^\perp
$$

Theorem $\ref{theorem-10}$ then implies that there is no subgroup $M < \widehat{G}$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{e_{\hat{G}}} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-92}} (\braket{e_{\widehat{G}}}^\perp)^\perp < M < H^\perp
\tag{\label{equation-99}}
\end{equation*}
$$

because, of there were such an $M$, then it would follow from $\eqref{equation-95}$ that the subgroup $M^\perp$ would satisfy $H < M^\perp < G$, which contradicts the assumption that $H$ is maximal. Hence, there can be no $M$ satisfying $\eqref{equation-99}$ and $H^\perp$ must be minimal.

Now let $H^\perp$ be minimal, which means that there is no $M < G$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  H < M < G
\tag{\label{equation-100}}
\end{equation*}
$$

because, otherwise, then $\eqref{equation-95}$ would imply

$$
  \braket{e_{\widehat{G}}} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-73}} G^\perp < M^\perp < H^\perp
$$

which contradicts $H^\perp$ being minimal. Consequently, there can be no $M$ satisfying $\eqref{equation-100}$ and $H$ must be minimal.

So far we have shown

$$
  H \text{ maximal} \iff H^\perp \text{ minimal}
$$

Applying this statement to the subgroup $H^\perp < \widehat{G}$ yields

$$
  H^\perp \text{ maximal} \iff (H^\perp)^\perp \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-92}} H \text{ minimal}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
Let $\set{g_1,\dots,g_L}$ be a set of elements of the finite Abelian group $G$ that have been selected at random, independently and with replacement from the the uniformly distributed elements of $G$. Then the probability that the whole group is generated by this set satisfies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Pr(G = \braket{g_1,\dots,g_L}) \geq 1 - \frac{|G|}{2^L}
\tag{\label{equation-101}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We show the claim by first deriving a bound on the probability of the complementary event $\set{\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L}} < G$ and then use that

$$
  \Pr(\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} = G) + \Pr(\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} < G) = 1
$$

If $\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} \neq G$ there is a maximal subgroup $H$ of $G$ such that

$$
  \braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} \leq H \leq G
$$

From $\eqref{equation-66}$ we know that $|G|/|H| \in\N$, and since $H < G$, we must have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{|G|}{|H|} \geq 2
\tag{\label{equation-102}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Thus, when a $g_1$ is selected at random from the uniformly distributed $|G|$ elements of $G$, the probability that it is in $H$ satisfies

$$
  \Pr(g_1 \in H) = \frac{|H|}{|G|} \underbrace{\leq}_{\eqref{equation-102}} \frac{1}{2}
$$

When independently selecting $L$ elements $g_1,\dots,g_L$ in this fashion, the probability they all are in $H$ therefore satisfies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Pr(g_1,\dots,g_L \in H) \leq \frac{1}{2^L}
\tag{\label{equation-103}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Thus, the probability that the $g_1,\dots,g_L$ are all in any maximal subgroup $H < G$ satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Pr(g_1,\dots,g_L \in H < G) \leq& \sum_{H < G \text{ maximal}} \Pr(g_1,\dots,g_L \in H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-98}}& \sum_{H^\perp \leq \widehat{G} \text{ minimal}} \Pr(g_1,\dots,g_L \in H) \\
  \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-103}}& \frac{\text{number of minimal subgroups of } \widehat{G}}{2^L}
\end{align*}
$$

From Proposition $\ref{proposition-17}$ we know that any minimal subgroup of $\widehat{G}$ is of the form $\braket{\chi}$ for som $\chi\in\widehat{G}$. Hence

$$
  \text{number of minimal subgroup of } \widehat{G} \leq |\widehat{G}| \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-}} |G|
$$

such that

$$
  \Pr(g_1,\dots,g_L \in H < G) \leq \frac{|G|}{2^L}
$$

from which it follows that

$$
  \Pr(\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} < G) \leq \frac{|G|}{2^L}
$$

Consequently,

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Pr(\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} = G) =& 1 - \Pr(\braket{g_1,\dots,g_L} < G) \\
  \geq& 1 - \frac{|G|}{2^L}
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

# Group representations

<MathBox title="Representation" boxType="definition">
Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$. A representation of a group $G$ on $V$ is a homomorphism $\pi: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$, where $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ is the group of all invertible linear transformations of $V$, called the general linear group. A group representation is commonly denoted by the pair $(\pi, V)$.
</MathBox>

When $V$ is a finite dimensional vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ with $\dim(V) = n$, and a basis of $V$ has been chosen, each linear transformation of $V$ can by uniquely represented by an $n\times n$ invertible matrix with entries in $\mathbb{F}$. Consequently, there is a canonical isomorphism of groups

$$
  \mathrm{GL}(V) \cong \mathrm{GL}_m (\mathbb{F})
$$

<MathBox title='Unitary representation' boxType="definition">
Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ equipped with Hermitian inner product $\braket{\cdot, \cdot}: V \times V \to \mathbb{F}$. A representation $\pi: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ of a group $G$ on $V$ is *unitary* if preserves the inner product, i.e.

$$
  \braket{\pi(g) v_1, \pi(g) v_2} = \braket{v_1, v_2}
$$

for all $g\in G$ and $v_1, v_2 \in V$.
</MathBox>

For a unitary representation, the matrices $\pi(g)$ take values in the unitary group $U(n) \subset \mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{C})$.

<MathBox title='Irreducible representation' boxType="definition">
Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$. A representation $\pi: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ of a group $G$ on $V$ is *irreducible* if it has no nontrivial proper subrepresentations. This means there are no proper subspaces $W \subset V$ such that $\pi|_W$ is a representation of $G$. A representation that does have such a representation is *reducible*.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Direct sum representation' boxType="definition">
Let $(\pi_1, V)$ and $(\pi_2, V)$ be representations of a group $G$ with respective dimensions $n_1 = \dim(V_1)$ and $n_2 = \dim(V_2)$. The direct sum representation $\pi_1 \oplus \pi_2 : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V_1 \oplus V_2)$ is defined on the direct sum of vector spaces $V_1 \oplus V_2$ by

$$
\begin{gather*}
  (\pi_1 \oplus \pi_2)(g)(v_1, v_2) = (\pi_1 (g)v_1, \pi_2 (g)v_2), \\
  \forall g\in G,\; v_1 \in V_1,\; v_2 \in V_2
\end{gather*}
$$

If $V_1$ and $V_2$ are finite dimensional, then $\pi_1 \oplus \pi_2$ can be represented by block-diagonal matrices

$$
  (\pi_1 \oplus \pi_2)(g) = \begin{bmatrix} \pi_1 (g) & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \pi_2 (g) \end{bmatrix}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="theorem">
Any unitary representation $(\pi, V)$, can be decomposed as direct sum of irreducible representations

$$
  \pi \bigoplus_{i=1}^m \pi_i
$$

where each $\pi_i$ are irreducible.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $(\pi, V)$ is not irreducible, there exists a nontrivial invariant subspace $W \subset V$ such that $(\pi|_W, W)$ is a representation. Since $\pi$ is unitary, Hermitian inner product on $V$ is preserved, implying that the ortogonal complement $W^\perp \subset V$ is also invariant under $\pi$. Thus, $(\pi, V)$ decomposes as

$$
  (\pi, V) = (\pi|_W, W) \oplus (\pi|_{W^\perp}, W^\perp)
$$

Then $(\pi|_{W^\perp}, W^\perp)$ is a subrepresentation since, by unitarity, the representation matrices preserve the Hermitian inner product. If either $\pi|_W$ or $\pi|_{W^\perp}$ is reducible, the same argument can be applied recursively to its invariant subspaces. Since $V$ is finite-dimensional, this process terminates after finitely many steps, resulting in a decomposition

$$
  \pi \bigoplus_{i=1}^m \pi_i
$$

where each $\pi_i$ is irreducible.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Schur's lemma" boxType="theorem" tag="1">
If $(\pi, V)$ is a complex irreducible representation of a group $G$, then any linear map $M: V\to V$ that commutes with all representation operators, i.e.

$$
  M\pi(g) = \pi(g) M,\; \forall g\in G
$$

must be a scalar multiple of the identity

$$
  M = \lambda\mathbf{1},\; \lambda\in\mathbb{C}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Assume $M$ commutes with all the $\pi(g)$. Since $V$ is a complex vector space, the characteristic polynomial of $M$ has at least one eigenvalue $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}$:

$$
  \det(M - \lambda\mathbf{1}) = 0
$$

Let

$$
  V_\lambda = \set{v \in V | Mv = \lambda v} = \ker(M - \lambda\mathbf{1})
$$

denote the corresponding eigenspace. By construction $V_\lambda \neq \set{0}$. Because $M$ commutes with $\pi(g)$ commute, for any $v\in V_\lambda$ and $g\in G$ we have

$$
  M(\pi(g)v) = \pi(g)Mv = \pi(g)(\lambda v) = \lambda \pi(g) v
$$

so $\pi(g)v \in V_\lambda$. Thus $V_\lambda$ is a $G$-invariant subspace of $V$. Since $(\pi, V)$ is irreducible, the only $G$-inveriant subspaces are $\set{0}$ and $\set{V}$ itself. Because $V_\lambda = \neq \set{0}$, we must have $V_\lambda = V$. Hence $M = \lambda\mathbf{1}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
If $G$ is an Abelian group, then all of its complex irreducible representations are one-dimensional.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

et $(\pi, V)$ be an irreducible representation of an Abelian group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$. Since $G$ is commutative, then for any $g, h \in G$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \pi(g) \pi(h) =& \pi(gh) = \pi(hg) \\
  =& \pi(h) \pi(g)
$$

so all operators $\pi(h)$ commute with each other. Thus, by Schur's lemma (Theorem $\ref{theorem-1}$), each matrice $\pi(h)$ is of the form $\pi(h) = \lambda_h \mathbf{1}$ for some $\lambda_h \in\mathbb{C}$. Since $\pi(h)$ acts a scalar on $V$, it follows that $V$ must by one-dimensional for irredicibility, i.e. $\pi(h) = \lambda_h$.
</details>
</MathBox>

# Matrix groups

<MathBox title='General linear group' boxType="definition">
The general linear group of degree $n$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$, denoted $\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$, is the set of all invertible $n\times n$-matrices under matrix multiplication, i.e.

$$
  \mathrm{GL}_n (\R) = \Set{\mathbf{A}\in \mathcal{M}_n (\R) : \det(\mathbf{A}) \neq 0}
$$

When $n\geq 2$, the group $\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ is not Abelian.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

To prove that $\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ is a group for $n\in\N_+$ we need to show that it satisfies the group axioms. Recall that a $n\times n$-matrix $\mathbf{A}\in\mathbb{F}^{n\times n}$ is invertible if $\det(\mathbf{A}) \neq 0$.

**Closure:** Let $\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}\in \mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ be invertible matrices. Then

$$
  \det(\mathbf{AB}) = \det(\mathbf{A})\det(\mathbf{B}) \neq 0 \implies \mathbf{AB}\in \mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})
$$

This shows that the matrix product of two invertible matrices is again invertible.

**Associativity:** If $\mathbf{A},\mathbf{B},\mathbf{C}\in\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ then by associativity of matrix multiplication

$$
  (\mathbf{AB})\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{BC}) \in \mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})
$$

**Identity element:** The $n\times n$ identity matrix $\mathbf{I}_n = \mathrm{diag}(1)_{i=1}^n$ is invertible since $det(\mathbf{I}_n) = 1$, implying that $\mathbf{I}_n \in\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$. Furthermore, for any $\mathbf{A}\in\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ we have

**Inverse element:** Any $\mathbf{A}\in\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$ is invertible by definition with a unique inverse $\mathbf{A}^{-1}$. Since $\det(\mathbf{A}^{-1}) = \det(\mathbf{A})^{-1} \neq 0$, it follows that $\mathbf{A}^{-1} \in\mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{F})$. By definition of the matrix inverse we have $\mathbf{AA}^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I}_n$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Special linear group' boxType="definition">
The special linear group of degree $n$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$, denoted $\mathrm{SL}_n (\mathbb{F})$, is the set of all invertible $n\times n$ matrices with unit determinant:

$$
  \mathrm{SL}_n (\R) = \Set{\mathbf{A}\in \mathrm{GL}_n (\R) : \det(\mathbf{A}) = 1}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Unitary group' boxType="definition">
The unitary group of degree $n$, denoted $\mathrm{U}(n)$, is the set of all complex unitary $n\times n$ matrices, i.e.

$$
  \mathrm{U}(n) = \Set{\mathbf{A}\in \mathrm{GL}_n (\mathbb{C}) : \mathbf{AA}^\dagger = \mathbf{A}^\dagger \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I}_n}
$$

<details>
<summary>Details</summary>

For $\mathbf{U}\in U(n)$ we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \det(\mathbf{UU}^\dagger) =& \det(\mathbf{U})\det(\mathbf{U}^\dagger) \\
  =& \det(\mathbf{U})\overline{\det(\mathbf{U}^\top)} \\
  =& \det(\mathbf{U})\overline{\det(\mathbf{U})} \\
  =& \det(\mathbf{I}_n) = 1
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $\det(\mathbf{U}) = e^{i\theta}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Orthogonal group' boxType="definition">
The orthogonal group of degree $n$, denoted $\mathrm{O}_n$, is the set of all orthogonal $n\times n$ matrices:

$$
  \mathrm{O}_n = \Set{\mathbf{A}\in \mathrm{GL}_n (\R) : \mathbf{AA}^\top = \mathbf{A}^\top \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I}_n}
$$
</MathBox>
