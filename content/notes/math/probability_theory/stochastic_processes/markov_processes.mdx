A Markov process is a stochastic process with the property that the probability of a future event depends only on the present state, i.e. the future is independent of the past.

| | Countable (discrete) state space | Continuous or general state space |
|---|---|---|
| **Discrete-time** $T=\N$ | discrete-time Markov chain | Markov process on a measurable space |
| **Continuous-time** $T=[0,\infty)$ | continuous-time Markov chain | Continuous stochastic process with the Markov property |

# Markov property

<MathBox title='Markov process' boxType="definition">
Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a probability space, and let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{S})$ be a measurable state space. A stochastic process $\mathbf{X} = (X_t)_{t\in T}$ adapted to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\in T}$ is a *Markov process* if it satisfies the Markov property

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A|\mathcal{F}_s) \eqas \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A|X_s)
$$

for all $s,t\in T$ and $A\in\mathcal{S}$. In particular, a Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is *time homogeneous* (stationary) if its transition probabilities are invariant under time shifts

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A | X_s = x) = \mathbb{P}(X_t\in A | X_0 = x)
$$
</MathBox>

The Markov property states that the conditional distribution of a future state $X_{s+t}$ given $\mathcal{F}_s$ is the same as the conditional distribution of $X_{s+t}$ just given the current state $X_s$. This means that any additional knowledge of events in the past is irrevelant in terms of predicting the future state $X_{s+t}$.

<MathBox title='Markov property in terms of conditional expectation' boxType="proposition">
A stochastic process $\mathbf{X}$ is a *Markov process* if and only if

$$
  \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{s+t})|\mathcal{F}_s\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{s+t})|X_s\right]
$$

for all $s,t\in T$ and $f\in\mathcal{B}$. If a Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is *time homogeneous* (stationary) then

$$
  \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{s+t})|X_s = x\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{s+t})|X_0 = x\right]
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By letting $f = \mathbf{1}_A$ for $A\in\mathcal{S}$ then $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A(X_{x+t})] = \mathbb{P}(X_{x+s}\in A)$. Thus the conditional expectation formulation is equivalent with the Markov property.

</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Markov property is preserved under coarser filtrations' boxType="proposition">
Suppose the stochastic process $\mathbf{X}$ is adapted to the filtration $\mathscr{F}$, and $\mathscr{F}\subseteq\mathscr{G}$, i.e. $\mathscr{G}$ is finer than $\mathscr{F}$. If $\mathbf{X}$ is a Markov process relative to $\mathscr{G}$, then $\mathbf{X}$ is a Markov process relative to $\mathscr{F}$.

In particular, if $\mathbf{X}$ is a Markov process, then $\mathbf{X}$ satisfies the Markov property relative to the natural filtration $\mathcal{F}^0$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Note $\mathbf{X}$ is adapted to $\mathscr{G}$, since $\mathbf{X}$ is adapted to $\mathscr{F}$ and $\mathscr{F}\subseteq\mathbf{G}$. If $s,t\in T$ and $f\in\mathcal{B}$ then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}[f(X_{t+s})|\mathcal{F}_s] &= \mathbb{E}\left(\mathbb{E}[f(X_{s+t})|\mathcal{G}_s] | \mathcal{F}_s \right) \\
  &= \mathbb{E}\left( \mathbb{E}[f(X_{s+t})|X_s]|\mathcal{F}_s \right) \\
  &= \mathbb{E}[f(X_{s+t})|X_s]
\end{align*}
$$

The first equality is a basic property of conditional expected value. The second uses the fact that $\mathbf{X}$ is Markov relative to $\mathscr{G}$, and the third follows since $X_s$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_s$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Feller process' boxType="definition">
A Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is a *Feller process* if it satisfies
1. Continuity in space: For $t\in T$ and $y\in S$, the distribution of $X_t$ given $X_0 = x$ converges to the distribution of $X_t$ given $X_0 = y$ as $x\to y$. This means that $\mathbb{E}[f(X_t)| X_0 = x] \xrightarrow{x\to y}$ for every $f\in\mathcal{C}$.
2. Continuity in time: Given $X_0 = x$ for $x\in S$, then $X_t \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0} x$ in probability. This means that $\mathbb{P}[X_t \in U | X_0 = x] \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0}$ for every neighbourhood $U$ of $x$.
</MathBox>

Note that if $S$ is discrete, the first Feller property is always satisfied, and if $T$ is discrete, the second Feller property is always satisfied. In partical, every discrete-time Markov chain is a Feller process.

# Strong Markov process (stopping time)

Strong Markov processes arise with stopping times. Recall that random time is a random variable $\tau:\Omega\to T_\infty$ on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ where $T_\infty = T \cup \Set{\infty}$ is an enlarged time space. For a filtration $\mathscr{F} = \Set{\mathcal{F}_t}_{t\in T}$ filtration on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F})$, a random time $\tau$ is called a stopping time relative to $\mathscr{F}$ if $\Set{\tau\leq t}\in\mathcal{F}_t$ for each $t\in T$. For any stopping time $\tau$ on $\Omega$ we can define

$$
  \mathcal{F}_\tau = \Set{ A\in\mathcal{F} | A\cap \Set{\tau \leq t }\in\mathcal{F}_t \; \forall t\in T }
$$

Note that if $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a stochastic process adapted to a filtration $\mathscr{F} = \Set{\mathcal{F}_t}_{t\in T}$, then for a stopping time $\tau$ the random variable $X_\tau$ is not necessarily measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_\tau$. For this we require that $\mathbf{X}$ is progressively measurable relative to $\mathscr{F}$ in the sense that $\mathbf{X}:\Omega\times T_t\to S$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_t \otimes\mathcal{T}_t$ and $\mathcal{S}$. This is always true in the discrete case and more generally if $S$ has an LCCB topology with $\mathcal{S}$ as the Borel $\sigma$-algebra, and $\mathbf{X}$ is right continuous.

<MathBox title='Strong Markov process' boxType="definition">
Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_t)_{t\in T}$ be a stochastic process on the measurable space $(\mathsf{S},\mathcal{S})$, adapted to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\in T}$. The process $\mathbf{X}$ is a strong Markov process if

$$
  \mathbb{E}\left[ f(X_{\tau + t})|\mathcal{F}_\tau \right] \eqas \mathbb{E}[f(X_{\tau + t})|X_\tau]
$$

for every $t\in T$, stopping time $\tau$ and bounded measurable function $f:\mathsf{S}\to\R$. 

If, in addition, $\mathbf{X}$ is *time homogeneous* (stationary) then for all $x\in\mathsf{S}$

$$
  \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau + t})|X_\tau = x\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau+t})|X_0 = x\right]
$$
</MathBox>

For a stationary process, the strong Markov property implies the ordinary Markov process, since a fixed time $t\in T$ is trivially also a stopping time. The converse is also true in discrete time. In continuous time it depends on the continuity of $\mathbf{X}$ and the filtration $\mathscr{F}$

<MathBox title='Stationarity and the strong Markov property' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_n}_{n\in\N}$ is a time homogeneous Markov process in discrete time. Then $\mathbf{X}$ is a strong Markov process.

If $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t }_{t\geq 0}$ is a Feller Markov process, then $\mathbf{X}$ is a strong Markov process relative to the filtration $\mathscr{F}_+^0$, the right-continuous refinement of the natural filtration.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='The strong Markov property is preserved under coarser filtrations' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t}_{t\in T}$ is progressively measurable relative to the filtration $\mathscr{F} = \Set{\mathcal{F}_t}_{t\in T}$ and that the filtration $\mathscr{G} = \Set{\mathcal{G}_t}_{t\in T}$ is finer than $\mathscr{F}$. If $\mathbf{X}$ is a strong Markov process relative to $\mathscr{G}$ then $\mathbf{X}$ is a strong Markov process relative to $\mathscr{F}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $\mathbf{X}$ is adapted to $\mathscr{F}$, it is also adapted to $\mathscr{G}$. Suppose $\tau$ is a stopping time for $\mathscr{F}$ and that $t\in T$ and $f\in\mathcal{B}$. Then $\tau$ is also a stopping time for $\mathscr{G}$ and $\mathcal{F}_\tau \subseteq\mathcal{G}_\tau$. Hence

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau + t})|\mathcal{F}_\tau\right] &= \mathbb{E}\left(\mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau + t})|\mathcal{G}_\tau\right]|\mathcal{F}_\tau \right) \\
  &= \mathbb{E}\left(\mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau + t})|X_\tau\right]|\mathcal{F}_\tau \right) \\
  &= \mathbb{E}\left[f(X_{\tau + t})|X_\tau\right]
\end{align*}
$$

The last equation follows since $\mathbf{X}_\tau$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ given that $\mathbf{X}$ is progressively measurable to $\mathscr{F}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

# Transition kernels of Markov processes

The transition kernels of a time-homogeneous Markov process is the conditional distribution given the initial distribution of the process. This means that the initial distribution and the transition kernels determine the finite dimensional distributions of a Markov process.

<MathBox title='Transition kernel' boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space. A transition kernel is a function $K: \mathsf{X}\times\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}) \to [0,1]$ such that
1. For every $x\in\mathsf{X}$, the mappin $A \mapsto K(x,A)$ is a probability measure on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$
2. For every $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, the mapping $x\mapsto K(x, A)$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$

**Discrete state space**

If $\mathsf{X}$ is finite or countable, the transition kernel reduces to a transition matrix $\mathbf{K} = (P_{xy})_{x,y\in\mathsf{X}}$ with elements

$$
  P_{xy} = P(X_n = y | X_{n-1} = x),\; x,y\in\mathsf{X}
$$

In this case
- $P_{xy} \geq 0$
- $\sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} P_{xy} = 1$ for each $x$

and the kernel acts as

$$
  K(x, A) = \sum_{y\in A} P_{xy}
$$

**Continuous state space**

In the continuous case, the kernel $K(x,\cdot)$ need not admit a density. However, if there exists a reference measure $\mu$ such that $K(x,\cdot)$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu$, there ional density $k(x, x')$ satisfying

$$
  K(x,A) = \int_A k(x,x') \;\d\mu(x')
$$

In probabilistic terms

$$
  P(X_{n+1} \in A| X_n = x) = \int_A k(x,x') \;\d\mu(x')
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Transition kernel of a Markov process' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \set{X_t: \Omega\to \mathsf{S}}_{t\in T}$ is a time-homogeneous Markov process on a measurable space $(\mathsf{S}, \mathcal{S})$. For each $t\in T$ define

$$
  P_t(x,A) := \mathbb{P}(X_t \in A \mid X_0 = x),\; x\in\mathsf{S}, A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

Then $P_t$ is a probability kernel on $(\mathsf{S},\mathcal{S})$, called the transition kernel of $\mathbf{X}$ at time $t$. 

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Fix $t\in T$. We show that $P_t$ satisfies the defining properties of a probability kernel.

**Measure property in $A$**

For each fixed $x\in\mathsf{S}$, 

$$
  A\mapsto P_t (x, A) = \mathbb{P}(X_t\in A \mid X_0 = x)
$$

is a probability measure on $(\mathsf{S}, \mathcal{S})$. This follows because it is the conditional distribution of $X_t$ given $X_0 = x$.

**Measurability in $x$**

For each fixed $A\in\mathcal{S}$, the mapping $x\mapsto\mathbb{P}(X_t \in A \mid X_0 = x)$ is $\mathcal{S}$-measurable. This is a standard property of regular conditional probabilities, which ensures that conditional distributions can be chosen to depend measurably on the conditioning variable.
</details>
</MathBox>

The transition kernel $P_t(x,\cdot)$ of a time-homogeneous Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ at time $t$ is the conditional distribution of $X_t$ given $X_0 = x \in S$. By the time-homogeneous property, $P_t(x, \cdot)$ is also the conditional distribution of $X_{s+t}$ given $X_s = x$ for $s\in T$

$$
  P_t(x, A) = \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A | X_s = x),\; s,t\in T, x\in S, A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

If there is a natural reference measure $\lambda$ on $(S,\mathcal{S})$, the transition kernel $P_t$ admits a transition density with respect to $\lambda$

$$
\begin{align*}
  P_t(x, A) =& \mathbb{P}(X_t \in A \mid X_0 = x) \\
  =& \int_A p_t(x, y) \d\lambda(y),\; x\in S, A\in\mathcal{S}
\end{align*}
$$

Weak Markov property: For every initial distribution $\mu$ and every $(n+1)$ sample $(X_i)_{i=0}^n$, then

$$
  \mathbb{E}_\mu [h(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2},\dots,)|x_0,\dots,x_n] = \mathbb{E}_{x_n} [h(X_1,X_2,\dots,)]
$$

provided that the expectations exist.

<MathBox title='Chapman-Kolmogorov equation' boxType="theorem">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \set{X_t: \Omega\to \mathsf{S}}_{t\in T}$ is a time-homogeneous Markov process on a measurable space $(\mathsf{S}, \mathcal{S})$, with transition kernels $\mathbf{P} = \set{ P_t }_{t\in T}$. For all $s,t\in T$, the kernels satisfy the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation $P_s P_t = P_{s+t}$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_{s+t}(x,A) = \int_S P_s(x, \d y) P_t(y, A),\; x\in S, A\in\mathcal{S}
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Note that $P_x(x,\cdot)$ is the conditional distribution of $X_s$ given $X_0 = x \in S$. Conditioning on $X_s$ for $A\in\mathcal{S}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  P_{s+t}(x,A) &= \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A \mid X_0 = x) \\
  &= \int_S P_s(x, \d y)\mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A \mid X_s = y, X_0 = x)
\end{align*}
$$

By the Markov and time-homogeneous properties, we have

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A \mid X_s = y, X_0 = x) = \mathbb{P}(X_t\in A \mid X_0 = y) = P_t(y,A)
$$

Substituting we get

$$
  P_{s+t}(x,A) = \int_S P_s (x,\d y)P_t(y, A) = (P_s P_t)(x, A)
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

The set of transition kernels $\mathbf{P}$ is a semi-group. Generally, the commutative property does not hold for the product operation on kernels. However, transition kernels a of time-homogeneous Markov process are commutative, i.e. $P_s P_t = P_t P_s = P_{s+t}$ for $s,t\in T$.

In discrete time, $T = \N$, the transition kernels of $\mathbf{X}$ are simply the powers of the one-step transition kernel. That is if $P = P_1$ then $P_n = P^n$ for $n\in\N$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="corollary">
Suppose $\lambda$ is the reference measure on $S$ and that $\mathbf{X}=\Set{X_t: \Omega\to S}_{t\in T}$ is a Markov process with transition densities $\Set{ p_t }_{t\in T}$. If $s,t \in T$, then $p_s p_t = p_{s+t}$

$$
  p_t (x, z) = \int_S p_s(x, x) p_t(y, z) \d\lambda(y),\; x, z\in S
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation, the transition kernels of $\mathbf{X}$ satisfy $P_s P_t = P_{s+t}$. Since $P_s$ has density $p_s$, $P_t$ has density $p_t$ and $P_s P_t$ has density $p_{s + t}$ it follows from the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation that $P_{s+t}$ has density $p_s p_t$.
</details>
</MathBox>

A kernel defines two operations:
- operating on the left with positive measure on $(S,\mathcal{S})$
- operating on the right with measurable, real-valued functions

<MathBox title='Left transition kernel operation' boxType="proposition">
If $\mu_s$ is the distribution of $X_s$, then $X_{s+t}$ has distribution $\mu_{s+t} = \mu_s P_t$ for $s,t\in T$

$$
  \mu_{s+t}(A) = \int_S \mu_s(\d x)P_t(x, A),\; A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Conditioning on $X_s$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A \mid X_s) \right] \\
  &= \int_S \mu_s(\d x)\mathbb{P}(X_{s+t}\in A \mid X_s = x) \\
  &= \int_S \mu_s(\d x)\mathbb{P}_t(x, A) \\
  &= \mu_s P_t (A)
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

If $\mathcal{P}$ denotes the collection of probability measures on $(S,\mathcal{S})$, the left operator $P_t$ maps $\mathcal{P}$ back into $\mathcal{P}$. In particular, if $X_0$ has distribution $\mu_0$ then $X_t$ has distribution $\mu_t = \mu_0 P_t$ for every $t\in T$.

<MathBox title='Invariant positive measures' boxType="definition">
A positive measure $\mu$ on $(S, \mathcal{S})$ is invariant for $\mathbf{X}$ if $\mu P_t = \mu$ for every $t\in T$.
</MathBox>

If $\mu$ is a probability measure that is invariant for $\mathbf{X}$, and $X_0$ has distribution $\mu$, then $X_t$ also has distribution $\mu$ for every $t\in T$ making the process $\mathbf{X}$ identically distributed. In discrete time, note that if $\mu P = \mu$ then $\mu P^n = \mu$ for every $n\in \N$ so $\mu$ is invariant for $\mathbf{X}$

<MathBox title='Right transition kernel operation' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $f:S\to\R$. If $t\in T$ then

$$
\begin{align*}
  P_t f(x) =& \int_S P_t(x, \d y)f(y) \\
  =& \mathbb{E}[f(X_t)\mid X_0 = x],\; x\in S
\end{align*}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

This follows directly from the definition of conditional expectation.
</details>
</MathBox>

In particular, the right operator $P_t$ is defined on $\mathcal{B}$, the vector space of bounded, linear functions $f:S\to\R$, and is in fact a linear operator on $\mathcal{B}$. That is, if $f,g\in\mathcal{B}$ and $c\in\R$ then $P_t (f+g) = P_t f + P_t g$ and $P_t (cf) = cP_t f$. Moreover, $P_t$ is a contraction operator on $\mathcal{B}$, since $\norm{ P_t f}\leq \norm{ f}$ for $f\in\mathcal{B}$. It follows that $P_t$ is a continuous operator on $\mathcal{B}$.

<MathBox title='Harmonic measurable function' boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, with transition semigroup $(P_t)_{t\in T}$. A measurable function $f:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ is harmonic with respect to $(X_n)$ if $f$ is invariant under $(P_t)$, i.e. 

$$
  P_t f(x) = f(x), \; \forall t\in T, x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

where

$$
  P_t f(x) := \int_\mathsf{X} = f(y) P_t (x, \d y)
$$

Equivalently, in probabilistic terms, $f$ is harmonic if the process $(f(X_n))_{n\geq 0}$ satisfies the martingale property

$$
  \mathbb{E}[f(X_{n+t})|\mathcal{F}_n] = f(X_n),\; \forall t \in T
$$

where $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n\geq 0}$ is the natural filtration generated by $(X_n)$

$$
  \mathcal{F}_n := \sigma(X_0,\dots,X_n)
$$
</MathBox>

In discrete time, if $Pf = f$ then $P^n f = f$ for all $n\in\N$, making $f$ harmonic for $\mathbf{X}$.

<MathBox title='Differential form of Markov distributions' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{ X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a Markov process with transition operators $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_t }_{t\in T}$ and that $(t_i)_{i=1}^{n\in\N} \in T^n$ is strictly increasing. If $X_0$ has distribution $\mu_0$, then in differential form the distribution of $(X_i)_{i=0}^{t_n}$ is

$$
  \mu_0(\d x_0)P_{t_1}(x_0, \d x_1)\prod_{i=2}^{n} P_{t_n - t_{n-1}}(x_{n-1}, \d x_n)
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

This follows from induction and repeated use of the Markov property. If $t\in T$ with $t> 0$, then conditioning on $X_0$ gives for $A, B\in\mathcal{S}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(X_0 \in A, X_t\in B) = \int_A \mathbb{P}(X_t\in B \mid X_0 = x)\mu_0 (\d x) \\
  &= \int_A P_t (x, B\mu(\d x) \\
  &= \int_A \int_B P_t(x,\d y)\mu_0 (\d x)
\end{align*}
$$

Hence in differential form, the distribution of $(X_0, X_t)$ is $\mu(\d x)P_t(x,\d y)$. If $s,t\in T$ with $0 < s < t$, then conditioning on $(X_0, X_s=$ and using the previous result gives for $A, B, C \in\mathcal{S}$

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_0\in A, X_s\in B, X_t\in C) = \int_{A\times B} \mathbb{P}(X_t\in C \mid X_0 = x, X_s = y)\mu_0 (\d x) P_s(x, \d y)
$$

By the Markov property

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(X_t\in C \mid X_0 = x, X_s = y) &= \mathbb{P}(X_t \in C | X_s = y) \\
  &= P_{t-s}(y, C) = \int_C P_{t-s}(y,\d z)
\end{align*}
$$

Hence in differential form, the distribution of $(X_0, X_s, X_t)$ is $\mu_0(\d x)P_s(x,\d y)P_{t-s}(y,\d z})$. Continuing in this manner gives the general result.
</details>
</MathBox>

Knowing the transition kernels and the initial distribution determines a finite set of distributions for a Markov process. From the Kolmogorov construction theorem, we know that there exists a stochastic process that has these finite dimensional distributions. This is straightforward in discrete time, however in continuous time two problems arise. First, it is not clear how to construct the transition kernels so that the Chapman-Kolmogorov equations are satisfied. Second, we want the Markov process to exhibit certain properties that go beyond the finite dimensional distributions.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{ X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a Markov process on an LCCB state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$ with transition operators $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_t }_{t\in [0,\infty)}$. Then $\mathbf{X}$ is a Feller process if and only if

1. Continuity in space: If $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ and $t\in[0,\infty)$, then $P_t f\in\mathcal{C}_0$
2. Continuity in time: If $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ and $x\in S$ then $P_t f(x) \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0} f(x)$

A semi-group of probability kernels $\mathbf{P}$ satisfying these properties is called a Feller semi-group.
</MathBox>

This means that a Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is Feller if and only if $\mathbf{P}$ is Feller. The first property means that $P_t$ is an operator on the vector space $\mathcal{C}_0$, in addition to being an operator on the larger space $\mathcal{B}$. The second condition implies a stronger form of continuity in time.

Note that if $S$ is discrete, then the first property is automatically satisfied. If $T$ is discrete, then the second property is automatically satisfied.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_t }_{t\in [0,\infty)}$ is a Feller semi-group of transition operators. Then $t\mapsto P_t f$ is continuous (with respect to the supremum norm) for $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$, i.e.

$$
  \norm{ P_{t+s} f - P_t f} = \sup\Set{|P_{t+s}f(x) - P_t f(x)| : x\in S } \xrightarrow{s\to 0} 0
$$
</MathBox>

## Sampling in time

<MathBox title='Time sampled Markov process gives a time-discrete Markov process' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a Markov process with state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$ and that $(t_i)_{i=0}^{n\in\N}$ is an increasing sequence in $T$. Let $Y_n = X_{t_n}$, then $\mathbb{Y} = \Set{Y_n}_{n\in\N}$ is a Markov process in discrete time.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

For $n\in\N$, let $\mathcal{G}_n = \sigma\Set{ Y_k | k\in\N, k\leq n }$ so that $\Set{\mathcal{G}_n}_{n\in\N}$ is the natural filtration associated with $\mathbf{Y}$. Note that $\mathcal{G}_n \subseteq \mathcal{F}_{t_n}$ and $Y_n = X_{t_n}$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{G}_n$ for $n\in\mathcal{N}$. Let $k, n\in\N$ and let $A\in\mathcal{S}$, then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(Y_{k+n}\in A \mid \mathcal{G}_k) = \mathbb{P}(X_{t_{n+k}}\in A \mid \mathcal{G}_l) \\
  &=  \mathbb{P}(X_{t_{n+k}}\in A \mid X_{t_k}) \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(Y_{n+k}\in A \mid Y_k) 
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a time-homogenous Markov process with state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$ and transition kernels $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_t}_{t\in T}$. Fix $r\in T$ with $r>0$ and define $Y_n = X_{nr}$ for $n\in\N$. Then $\mathbf{Y} = \Set{Y_n}_{n\in\N}$ is a time-homogenous Markov process in discrete time, with one-step transition kernel

$$
  Q(x, A) = P_r (x, A),\; x\in S, A\in\mathcal{A}
$$
</MathBox>

## Enlarging the sample space

A non-homogeneous Markov process can be turned into a time-homogenous Markov process at the expense of enlarging the state space.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_t }_{t\in T}$ is a non-homogenous Markov process with state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$. Suppose $\tau$ is a random variable taking values in $T$, independent of $\mathbf{X}$. Let $\tau_t = \tau + t$ and let $Y_t = (X_{\tau_t}, \tau_t)$ for $t\in T$. Then $\mathbf{Y} = \Set{Y_t}_{t\in T}$ is a homogenous Markov process with state space $(S\times T, \mathcal{S} \otimes \mathcal{T})$. For $t\in T$, the transition kernel $P_t$ is given by

$$
  P_t [(x, r), A\times B] = \mathbb{P}(X_{r+t}\in A\mid X_r = x)\mathbf{1}(r + t\in B), (x,r)\in S\times T, A\times B\in\mathcal{S}\otimes\mathcal{T}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By definition and the substitution rule

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}[Y_{s+t}\in A\times B \mid Y_s = (x,r)] &= \mathbb{P}(X_{\tau_{s+t}}\in A, \tau_{s+t}\in B \mid X_{\tau_{s}} = x, \tau_s = r) \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(X_{tau+s+t}\in A, \tau+s+t\in B \mid X_{\tau+s} = x, \tau+s = r) \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(X_{r+t}\in A, r+t\in B \mid X_r = x, \tau+s = r)
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\tau$ is independent of $\mathbf{X}$ the last term is

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{r+t}\in A, r+t\in B \mid X_r = x, \tau+s = r) = \mathbb{P}(X_{r+t}\in A \mid X_r = x)
$$

which does not depend on $s$, making $\mathbf{Y}$ time-homogenous.
</details>
</MathBox>

Sometimes a stochastic process with weak memory can be made into a Markov process by enlarging the state space appropriately

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_n }_{n\in\N}$ is a random process with state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$ in which the future depends on the last $k$ states, i.e.

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{n+k} \in A | \mathcal{F}_{n+k-1}) = \mathbb{P}(X_{n+2}\in A \mid X_n,\dots,X_{n+k}),\; A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

where $\Set{\mathcal{F}_n}_{n\in\N}$ is the natural filtration associated with the process $\mathbf{X}$. Suppose also that the process is time-homogenous in the sense that

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_{n+k}\in A \mid X_n = x_0, \dots, X_{n+k} = x_k) = Q(x_0,\dots,x_k, A)
$$
 
independently of $n\in\N$. Let $Y_n = (X_n+i)_{i=0}^k-1$, then $\mathbf{N} = \Set{Y_n}_{n\in\N}$ is a time homogenous Markov process with state space $S^k, \bigotimes_{i=1}^k \mathcal{S}$. The one-step transition kernel is

$$
  P[(x_i)_{i=1}^k, \prod_{i=1}^k A_i] = I(x_1, A_1)Q_1(x_1, x_2, A_2)\dots Q_{k-1}(x_1,\dots,x_k, Q_k)\; x_i\in S, A_i\in\mathcal{S}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

For $k=2$ note that $\sigma\Set{Y_k | k\leq n} = \sigma\Set{Y_k | k\leq n } = \mathcal{F}_{n+1}$ for $n\in\N$. Thus, the natural filtration associated with $\mathbf{Y}$ is $\Set{\mathcal{F}_n}_{n\in\N}$. If $C\in\mathcal{S}\mathcal{S}$ then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(Y_{n+1}\in C \mid \mathcal{F}_{n+1}) &= \mathbb{P}[(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2})\in C \mid \mathcal{F}_{n+1}] \\
  &= \mathbb{P}[(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2})\in C \mid X_n, X_{n+1}] = \mathbf{P}(Y_{n+1}\in C \mid Y_n)
\end{align*}
$$

by the given assumption on $\mathbf{X}$. Hence $\mathbf{Y}$ is a Markov process. Next,

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}[Y_{n+1}\in A\times B \mid Y_n = (x,y)] &= \mathbb{P}[(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2})\in A\times B \mid (X_n, X_{n+1}) = (x,y)] \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}\in A, X_{n+2}\in B \mid X_n = x, X_{n+1} = y) \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(y\in A, X_{n+2}\in B \mid X_n = x, X_{n+1} = y) \\
  &= I(y, A)Q(x,y,B)
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

# Potential operators

## Discrete time

<MathBox title='Discrete-time potential kernel' boxType="proposition">

For $\alpha\in (0,1]$, the $\alpha$-potential kernel $R_\alpha$ of a discrete time Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is defined as

$$
  R_\alpha (x, A) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n (x, A),\; x\in S, A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

The special case $R = R_1$ is simply the potential kernel of $\mathbf{X}$ where $R(x, A)$ is the expected number of visits of $\mathbf{X}$ to $A$, starting at $x$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The function $x\mapsto R_\alpha (x, A)$ from $S$ to $[0,\infty)$ is measurable for $A\in\mathcal{S}$ since $x\mapsto P^n(x, A)$ is measurable for each $n\in\N$. The mapping $A\mapsto R_\alpha (x, A)$ is a positive measure on $\mathcal{S}$ for $x\in S$ since $A\mapsto P^n(x, A)$ is a probability measure for each $n\in\N$. The interpretation of $R(x, A)$ comes from interchanging sum and expected value, which is allowed since the terms are nonnegative.

$$
\begin{align*}
  R(x, A) =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty P^n (x, A) \\
  =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}(X_n \in A) | X_0 = x] \\
  =& \mathbb{E}\left( \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}(X_n \in A) \middle| X_0 = x \right) \\
  =& \mathbb{E}[#\Set{n\in\N : X_n \in A}|X_0 = x]
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

For a discrete-time Markov process $\mathbf{X}$, the potential kernel $R_\alpha (x, A)$ for $\alpha\in(0,1)$ can be interpreted as the expected number of times $\mathbf{X}$ visits $A\in\mathcal{S}$ starting at $x\in S$. The parameter $\alpha$ represents a discount rate of this expected value in the sense that a future vists at time $n$ has a present value of $\alpha^n$.

The potential kernel $R_\alpha$ defines two operators, operating on the right on measurable functions, and on the left on positive measures. For measurable functions $f:S\to\R$, the right potential operator is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_\alpha f(x) =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n f(x) \\
  =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n \int_S P^n (x,\d y) f(y) \\
  =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n \mathbb{E}[f(X_n)|X_0 = x]
\end{align*}
$$

The functions $f\in\mathcal{B}$ represents the reward/cost when $\mathbf{X}$ visits $x\in S$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">

If $\alpha\in (0,1)$, then $R_\alpha (x, S) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}$. It follows that the right operator $R_\alpha$ is a bounded, linear operator on $\mathcal{B}$ with $\norm{ R_\alpha} = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}$. It also follows that $(1-\alpha)R_\alpha$ is a probability kernel.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Using geometric series

$$
  R_\alpha (x, S) \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n (x, S) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
If $\alpha\in (0,1)$, then $(1-\alpha)R_\alpha (x, \cdot)$ is the conditional distribution of $X_N$ given $X_0 = x\in S$, where $N$ is independent of $\mathbf{X}$ and has the geometric distribution on $\N$ with parameter $1-\alpha$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Conditioning on $N$ gives

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_N \in A | X_0 = x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbb{P}(N=n)\mathbb{P}(X_N \in A | N = n, X_0 =x)
$$

By the substitution rule and the assumption of independence

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(X_N \in A | N = n, X_0 = x) =& \mathbb{P}(X_n \in A | N=n, X_0 = x) \\
  =& \mathbb{P}(X_n \in A | X_0 = x) = P^n(x,A)
\end{align*}
$$

Since $N$ has the geometric distribution on $\N$ with parameter $1-\alpha$ it follows that $P(N = n) = (1-\alpha)\alpha^n$ for $n\in\N$. Substituting gives

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_N \in A | X_0 = x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (1-\alpha)\alpha^n P^n (x, A) = (1-\alpha) R_\alpha (x, A)
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

The kernel $(1-\alpha)R_\alpha$ is a transition probability kernel corresponding to the random time $N$ rather than the deterministic time $n\in\N$.

For positive measures $\mu$ on $\mathcal{S}$, the left potential operator is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mu R_\alpha (A) =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n \mu P^n (A) \\
  =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n \int_S \mu(\d x)P^n (x, A),\; A\in\mathcal{S} 
\end{align*}
$$

If $X_0$ has distribution $\mu$, then $\mu P^n$ is the distribution of $X_n$ for $n\in\N$. In this case, $(1-\alpha)\mu R_\alpha$ is the distribution of $X_N$, where $N$ is independent of $\mathbf{X}$ and is geometrically distributed on $\N$ with parameter $1 - \alpha$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
The potential kernels $\R = \Set{ R_\alpha }_{\alpha\in (0, 1)}$ completely determine the transition kernels $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_n}_{n\in\N}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

For $x\in S$ and $A\in\mathcal{A}$, the function $\alpha\mapsto R_\alpha(x, A)$ is a power series in $\alpha$ with coefficients $n\mapsto P^n(x,A)$. In combinatorial terms, $\alpha\mapsto R_\alpha(x, A)$ is the ordinary generating function of the sequence $n\mapsto P^n (x, A)$. This power series has radius of convergence at least $1$, extending the domain to $\alpha\in (-1, 1)$. Thus, we can recover the transition kernels by derivating $R_\alpha$ and evaluating at $\alpha = 0$

$$
  P^n (x, A) = \frac{1}{N!}\left. \frac{\d^n}{\d\alpha^n} R_\alpha (x, A) \right|_{\alpha = 0}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

The kernels $\mathbf{R} = \Set{ R_\alpha }_{\alpha\in (0,1)}$, along with the initial distribution, completely determine the finite dimensional distributions of the discrete-time Markov process $\mathbf{X}$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
If $\alpha,\beta\in (0, 1]$ and $k\in\N$, then (as kernels)

1. $P^k R_\alpha = R_\alpha P^k = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^{n+k}$
2. $R_\alpha R_\beta = R_\beta R_\alpha = \sum_{m=0}^\infty\sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^m \beta^n P^{m+n}$
3. $I + \alpha R_\alpha P = I + \alpha P R_\alpha = R_\alpha$

If $\alpha\leq\beta$ then (as kernels)

4. $\beta R_\beta = \alpha R_\alpha + (\beta - \alpha) R_\alpha R_\beta$

If $\alpha\in (0,1)$, then as operator as on $\mathcal{B}$

5. $R_\alpha = (I - \alpha P)^{-1}$
6. $P = \frac{1}{\alpha}(1 - R_\alpha^{-1})$
 
<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Suppose $f\in\mathcal{B}$ is nonnegative. Since the kernels are nonnegative we can interchange sums with kernel operations.

1. Evaluating $R_\alpha P^k$ directly
$$
  R_\alpha P^k f = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n P^k f = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^{n+k} f
$$

The other direction requires an interchange

$$
  P^k R_\alpha f = P^k \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n f = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^k P^n f = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^{n+k}f
$$

2. Only the first direction is shown as the other is similar.
$$
\begin{align*}
  R_\alpha R_\beta &= \sum_{m=0}^\infty \alpha^m P^m R_\beta f \\
  &= \sum_{m=0}^\infty \alpha^m P^m \left( \sum_{n=0}^\infty \beta^n P^n f \right) \\
  &= \sum_{m=0}^\infty \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^m \beta^n P^m P^n f \\
  &= \sum_{m=0}^\infty \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^m \beta^n P^{m+n} f
\end{align*}
$$

3. From the 1st identity
$$
\begin{align*}
  (I + \alpha R_\alpha P)f &= (I + \alpha RP_\alpha)f \\
  &= f + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^{n + 1} P^{n+1} f \\
  &= \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n f = R_\alpha f
\end{align*}
$$

4. Assume $\alpha < \beta$ as the case $\alpha = \beta$ is trivial. From the 2nd identity

$$
  R_\alpha R_\beta f = \sum_{j=0}^\infty \sum_{k=0} \alpha^j \beta^k P^{j+k} f
$$

Changing the sum variables to $n = j + k$ and $j$

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_\alpha R_\beta f = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \sum_{j=0}^n \alpha^j \beta^{n-j} P^n f \\
  &= \sum_{n=0}^\infty \sum_{j=0}^n \left( \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right)^j \beta^n P^n f \\
  &= \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1 - \left( \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right)^{n+1}}{1 - \frac{\alpha}{\beta}} \beta^n P^n f \\
  &= \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}  \beta \left( \sum_{n=0}^\infty \beta^n P^n f \right) - \alpha \left( \sum_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P^n f \right) \\
  &= \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha} (\beta R_\beta f -\alpha R_\alpha f)
\end{align*}
$$

Note that $R_\alpha f$ is finite since $\alpha < 1$.

5. Since the operators are bounded, we can subtract. From the 3rd property

$$
\begin{gather*}
  I + \alpha R_\alpha  P = R_\alpha \iff R_\alpha (I - \alpha P) = I \\
  I + \alpha P R_\alpha = R_\alpha \iff (I - \alpha) R_\alpha = I
\end{gather*}
$$

6. This follows from the 5th property.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Continuous time

<MathBox title='Continuous-time potential kernel' boxType="proposition">
For $\alpha\in [0, \infty)$ the $\alpha$-potential kernel $U_\alpha$ of $\mathbf{X}$ is defined as

$$
  U_\alpha (x, A) := \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t (x, A)\;\d t,\; x\in S, A\in\mathcal{S}
$$

1. The special case $U = U_0$ is simply the potential kernel of $\mathbf{X}$.
2. $U(x, A)$ is the expected amount of time that $\mathbf{X}$ spends in $A$, starting at $x$
3. The family of kernels $\mathbf{U} = \Set{ U_\alpha }_{\alpha\in (0,\infty)}$ is known as the reolvent of $\mathbf{X}$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $\mathbf{P} = \Set{ P_t }_{t\in T}$ is a Feller semigroup of transition operators, the mapping $(t,x)\mapsto P_t (x, A)$ from $[0,\infty)\times S$ to $[0,1]$ is jointly measurable for $A\in\mathcal{S}$. Thus, $U_\alpha (x, A)$ makes sense for $x\in S$ and $A\in\mathcal{S}$, and $x\mapsto U_\alpha (x, A)$ from $S$ to $[0,\infty)$ is measurable for $A\in\mathcal{S}$. That $A\mapsto U_\alpha (x, A)$ is a measure on $\mathcal{S}$ follows from the usual interchange of sum and integral, via Fubini's theorem. Suppose $\Set{ A_j }_{j\in J}$ is a countable collection of disjoint sets in $\mathcal{S}$, and let $S = \bigcup_{j\in J} A_j$, then

$$
\begin{align*}
  U_\alpha (x, A) &= \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t (x, A)\d t = \int_0^\infty \left[ \sum_{j\in J} e^{-\alpha t}P_t (x, A_j) \right]\d t \\
  &= \sum_{j\in J} \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t (x, A_j)\d t = \sum_{j\in J} U_\alpha (x, A_j)
\end{align*}
$$

The interpretation of $U(x, A)$ is another interchange of integrals

$$
\begin{align*}
  U(x, A) &= \int_0^\infty P_t (x, A)\d t = \int_0^\infty \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}(X_t \in A) \mid X_0 = x]\d t \\
  &= \mathbb{E}\left( \int_0^\infty \mathbf{1}(X_t \in A) \middle| X_0 = x \right)
\end{align*}
$$

The integral inside the expectation is the Lebesgue measure of $\Set{ t\in [0,\infty) : X_t\in A }$
</details>
</MathBox>

The potential kernel $U_\alpha$ defines two operators, operating on the right on functions, and left on positive measures. For measurable functions $f:S\to\R$, the right potential operator is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  U_\alpha f(x) =& \int_S U_\alpha (x, \d y)f(y) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t f(x)\d t \\
  =& \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} \int_S P_t (x,\d y) f(y) \\
  =& \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t}\mathbb{E}[f(X_t)\mid X_0]\d t,\; x\in S
\end{align*}
$$

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
If $\alpha > 0$, then $U_\alpha (x, S) = \frac{1}{\alpha}$ for all $x\in S$. It follows that the right potential operator $U_\alpha$ is a bounded, linear operator on $\mathcal{B}$ with $\norm{ U_\alpha} = \frac{1}{\alpha}$. It also follows that $\alpha R_\alpha$ is a probability kernel.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

$$
  U_\alpha (x, S) \int_{n=0}^\infty \alpha^n P_t (x, S)\;\d t = \int_{n=0}^\infty e^{-\alpha t} \d t = \frac{1}{\alpha}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
If $\alpha > 0$, then $\alpha U_\alpha (x, \cdot)$ is the conditional distribution of $X_\tau$ where $\tau$ is independent of $\mathbf{X}$ and has the exponential distribution on $[0,\infty)$ with parameter $\alpha$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $\tau$ is exponentially distributed it has the probability density function $f(t) = \alpha e^{-\alpha t}$ for $t\in[0,\infty)$. Conditioning on $\tau$ gives

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_\tau \in A \mid X_0 = x) = \int_0^\infty \alpha e^{-\alpha t}\mathbb{P}(X_\tau \in A \mid \tau = t, X_0 = x) = P_t (x, A)
$$

By the substitution rule and the assumption of independence

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(X_\tau \in A \mid \tau = t, X_0 = x) &= \mathbb{P}(X_t \in A \mid \tau = t, X_0 = x) \\
  &= \mathbb{P}(X_t \in A \mid X_0 = x) = P_t (x, A)
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting gives

$$
  \mathbb{P}(X_\tau \in A \mid X_0 = x) = \int_0^\infty \alpha e^{-\alpha t} P_t (x, A)\d t = \alpha U_\alpha (x, A)
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

The kernel $\alpha R_\alpha$ is a transition probability kernel corresponding to the random time $N$ rather than the deterministic time $n\in\N$.

For positive measures $\mu$ on $\mathcal{S}$, the left potential operator is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mu U_\alpha =& \int_S \mu(\d x) U_\alpha (x, A) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} \mu P_t f(x)\d t \\
  =& \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} \left[ \int_S \mu(\d x) P_t (x, A)\right] \d t \\
  =& \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t}\left[ \int_S \mu(\d x)\mathbb{P}(X_t \in A) \right]\d t,\; A\in\mathcal{S}
\end{align*}
$$

In particular, suppose $\alpha > 0$ and that $X_0$ has distribution $\mu$. Then $\mu P_t$ is the distribution of $X_t$ for $t\in[0,\infty)$ so that $\alpha \mu U_\alpha$ is the distribution of $X_\tau$, where $\tau$ is independent of $\mathbf{X}$ and is exponentially distributed on $[0,\infty)$ with parameter $\alpha$.

<MathBox title="Resolvent" boxType="definition">
For $\epsilon \in (0, 1)$, a resolvent associated with the kernel $P$ is a kernel of the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  K_\epsilon (x, A) = (1 - \epsilon) \sum_{i=0}^\infty \epsilon^i K^i (x, A)
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Equivalently, viewing $K$ as an operator on bounded measurable functions, $K_\epsilon$ corresponds to the resolvent operator

$$
  K_\epsilon = (1 - \epsilon) (I - \epsilon K)^{-1}
$$

defined via the Neumann series.

A Markov chain whose transition kernel is $K_\epsilon$, is a $K_\epsilon$-chain.
</MathBox>

For a resolvent kernel $K_\epsilon$, we can construct a Markov chain $(X_n^(\epsilon))_{n\geq 0}$ with initial distribution $\mu$ and transition kernel $K$. This chain admits the following probability representation: let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain with initial distribution $\mu$ and transition kernel $K$, and let $(G_n)_{n\geq 1}$ be a sequence of independent and identically distribution $\N_0$-valued random variables, independent of $(X_n)$, such that

$$
  \mathbb{P}(G_n = k) = (1 - \epsilon) \epsilon^k,\; k \geq 0
$$

Define the random times $T_0 = 0$ and $T_{n+1} = T_n + G_{n+1}$. Then the process $X_n^{(\epsilon)} := X_{T_n}$ for $n\geq 0$ is a Markov chain with transition kernel $K_\epsilon$.

In this sense, $(X_n^{(\epsilon)})$ may be viewed as a randomly thinned (or geometrically sampled) subsequence of the original chain $(X_n)$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
The resolvent $\mathbf{U} = \set{U_\alpha}_{\alpha\in (0,\infty)}$ completely determines the family of transition kernels $\mathbf{P} = \set{P_t}_{t\in (0,\infty)}$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Note that the function $\alpha\mapsto U_\alpha (x, A)$ on $(0,\infty)$ is the Laplace transform of the function $t\mapsto P_t (x, A)$ on $[0,\infty)$. The Laplace transform of a function determines the function completely.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\alpha, \beta, t \in [0,\infty)$. Then as kernels

1. $P_t U_\alpha = U_\alpha P_t = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_{s+t}\d s$
2. $U_\alpha U_\beta = U_\beta U_\alpha = \int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} e^{-\beta t} P_{s+t}\d s\;\d t$

If $\alpha\leq\beta$ then as kernels

3. $U_\alpha = U_\beta + (\beta -\alpha) U_\alpha U_\beta$

4. If $\alpha\in (0,\infty)$ and $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ then $U_\alpha f \in\mathcal{U}_0$.
5. If $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ then $\alpha U_\alpha f \xrightarrow{\alpha\to\infty} f$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Suppose $f\in\mathcal{B}$ is nonnegative. Since the integrands are nonnegative we can interchange integrals.

1. Evaluating $U_\alpha P_t$ directly
$$
  U_\alpha P_t f = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s}P_s P_t f \;\d s = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_{s+t}f\;\d s
$$

The 1st identity involves an interchange

$$
  P_t U_\alpha f = P_t \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_s f\;\d s = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s}P_t P_s f\;\d s = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_{s+t} f\;\d s
$$

2. Only the first direction is shown as the other is similar

$$
\begin{align*}
  U_\alpha U_\beta f &= \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_s U_\beta f\;\d s = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_s \int_0^\infty e^{-\beta t} P_t f\;\d t \\
  &= \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} \int_0^\infty e^{-\beta t}P_s P_t f\;\d s\;\d t = \int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} e^{-\beta t} P_{s+t} f\;\d s\;\d t
\end{align*}
$$

3. Assume $\alpha < \beta$ as the case $\alpha = \beta$ is trivial. From the 2nd identity

$$
  U_\alpha U_\beta f = \int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} e^{-\beta t} P_{s+t} f\;\d s\;\d t
$$

The transformation $u = s + t, v = s$ maps $[0,\infty)^2$ one-to-one onto $\Set{(u,v)\in[0,\infty)^2 : u\geq v}$. The inverse transformation is $s = v, t = u - v$ with Jacobian $-1$. This gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  U_\alpha U_\beta f &= int_0^\infty \int_0^u e^{-\alpha v} e^{-\beta(u - v)} P_u f\;\d v\;\d u \\
  &= \int_0^\infty \left(\int_0^u e^{(\beta - \alpha)v}\;\d v \right) e^{-\beta u} P_u f\;\d u \\
  &= \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}\int_0^\infty [e^{(\beta - \alpha)u}] e^{-\beta u} P_u f,\d u \\
  &= \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}\left(\int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha u} P_u f\;\d u -\int_0^\infty e^{-\beta u} P_u f\;\d u \right) \\
  &= \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}(U_\alpha f - U_\beta f)
\end{align*}
$$

Note that $U_\beta f$ is finite since $\beta > 0$.

4. Suppose $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ and that $(x_i)_{i\in\N}$ is a sequence in $S$. Then $P_t f\in \mathcal{C}_0$ for $t\in[0,\infty)$. Hence if $x_n \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} x \in S$ then $e^{-\alpha t}P_t f(x_n) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} e^{-\alpha t}P_t f(x)$ for each $t\in[0,\infty)$. By the dominated convergence theorem

$$
  U_\alpha f (x_n) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t}P_t f(x_n)\;\d t \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t f(x)\;\d t = U_\alpha f(x)
$$

Hence $U_\alpha$ is continuous. Next suppose $x_n \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \infty$. This means that for every compact $C\subseteq S$, there exists $m\in\N_+$ such that $x_n \notin C$ for $n > m$. Then $e^{-\alpha t} P_t f(x_n) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0$ for each $t\in[0,\infty)$. Again by the dominated convergence theorem

$$
  U_\alpha f(x_n) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P_t f(x_n)\;\d t \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0
$$

Hence $U_\alpha f \in \mathcal{C}_0$.

5. Convergence is with respect to the supremum norm on $\mathcal{C}_0$. Suppose $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$. Note first that with change of variables $s = \alpha t$

$$
  \alpha U_\alpha f = \int_0^\infty \alpha e^{-\alpha t} P_t f\;\d t = \int_0^\infty e^{-s} P_{s/\alpha}f\;\d s
$$

and hence

$$
\begin{align*}
  |\alpha U_\alpha f - f| &= \left| \int_0^\infty e^{-s}(P_{s/\alpha} f - f)\;\d s \right| \\
  \leq \int_0^\infty e^{-s} |P_{s/\alpha f - f}\;\d s \\
  \leq \int_0^\infty e^{-s} \norm{ P_{s/\alpha} f - f}\;\d s
\end{align*}
$$

It follows that

$$
  \norm{ \alpha U_\alpha f - f} \leq \int_0^\infty e^{-s} \norm{ P_{s/\alpha} f - f}\d s
$$

However, $\norm{ P_{s\alpha} f - f} \xrightarrow{\alpha\to\infty} 0$ and hence by the dominated convergence theorem

$$
  \int_0^\infty e^{-s}\norm{ P_{s/\alpha} f - f}\;\d s \xrightarrow{\alpha\to\infty} 0
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

## Infinitesimal generator

<MathBox title='Infinitesimal generator' boxType="definition">
The infinitesimal generator of the Markov process $\mathbf{X}$ is the operator $G:\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}_0$ defined by

$$
  Gf := \lim_{t\downarrow 0}\frac{P_t f - f}{t}
$$

on the domain $\mathcal{D}\subseteq\mathcal{C}_0$ for which the limit exists. The limit is with respect to the supremum norm such that $f\in\mathcal{D}$ and $Gf = g$ implies $f,g\in\mathcal{C}_0$ and

$$
\left\| \frac{P_t f - f}{t} g \right\| = \sup\Set{ \Norm{ \frac{P_t f(x) - f(x)}{t} - g(x) } : x\in S } \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0} 0
$$

In particular

$$
\begin{align*}
  Gf(x) =& \lim_{t\downarrow 0} \frac{P_t f(x) - f(x)}{t} \\
  =& \lim_{t\downarrow 0}\frac{\mathbb{E}[f(X_t)\mid X_0 = x] - f(x)}{t},\; s\in S
\end{align*}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
The domain $\mathcal{D}$ is a subspace of $\mathcal{C}_0$ and the generator $G$ is a linear operator on $\mathcal{D}$ satisfying

1. If $f\in\mathcal{D}$ and $c\in\mathcal{R}$ then $cf\in\mathcal{D}$ and $G(cf) = cGf$.
2. If $f,g\in\mathcal{D}$ then $f+g\in\mathcal{D}$ and $G(f+g) = Gf + Gg$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

These properties follows from the linearity of $P_t$ for $t\in[0,\infty)$ and basic results on convergence

1. 
$$
  \frac{P_t(cf) - (cf)}{t} = c\frac{P_t f - f}{t} \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0} cGf
$$

2.
$$
  \frac{P_t (f+g) - (f+g){t} = \frac{P_t f - f}{t} + \frac{P_t g - g}{t} \xrightarrow{t\downarrow 0} Gf + Gg
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

Note that $G$ is the (right) derivative at $0$ of the function $t\mapsto P_t f$. Because of the semigroup property, this differentiability property at $0$ implies differentiability at arbitrary $t\in[0,\infty)$.

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
If $f\in\mathcal{D}$ and $t\in[0,\infty)$, then $P_t f\in\mathcal{D}$ and the following derivative rules hold with respect to the supremum norm

1. Kolmogorov forward equation: $P'_t f = P_t Gf$
2. Kolmogorov backward equation: $P'_t f = GP_t f$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. By assumption

$$
  \lim_{h\downarrow 0} \frac{P_h f - f}{h} = Gf
$$

Since $P_t$ is a bounded, linear operator on $\mathcal{C}_0$, it preserves limits, giving

$$
  \lim_{h\downarrow 0}\frac{P_t P_h f - P_t}{h} = \lim_{h\downarrow 0} \frac{P_{t+h} f - P_t f}{h} = P_t G f
$$

This proves the Kolmogorov forward equation for the derivative from the right. Since $t\mapsto P_t f$ is continuous, the result is also true for the two-sided derivative.

2. From the Kolmogorov forward equation

$$
  \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{P_t P_h f - P_t}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{P_{t+h} f - P_t f}{h} = P_t G f
$$

By definition, this means that $P_t f \in\mathcal{D}$ and $GP_t f = P_t G f = P'_t f$.
</details>
</MathBox>

Infinitesimal generators allows us to construct a Markov process with desired properties, by which the transition operators $\mathbf{P} = \Set{P_t}_{t\geq 0}$ are obtained from the initial value problem

$$
  P'_t = GP_t,\quad P_0 = I
$$

<MathBox title='' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\alpha\in (0,\infty)$

1. If $f\in\mathcal{D}$ then $Gf\in\mathcal{C}_0$ and $f + U_\alpha G f = \alpha U_\alpha f$
2. If $f\in\mathcal{C}_0$ then $U_\alpha f \in\mathcal{D}$ and $f + GU_\alpha f = \alpha U_\alpha f$
3. $U_\alpha = (\alpha I - G)^{-1}:\mathcal{C}_0\to\mathcal{D}$
4. $G = \alpha I - U_\alpha^{-1}:\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}_0$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. By definition, if $f\in\mathcal{D}$ then $Gf\in\mathcal{C}_0$. Hence using the Kolmogorov equations and integrating by parts

$$
\begin{align*}
  f + U_\alpha G f &= f + \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} GP_t f\;\d t = f + \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t} P'_t f\;\d t \\
  &= \left. f - e^{-\alpha t} P_t \right_0^\infty + \alpha\int_0^\infty + \alpha\int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha t}P_t f \;\d t
\end{align*}
$$

Note that $e^{-\alpha t} P_t f \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} 0$ while $P_0 f = f$. The last term is $\alpha U_\alpha f$.

2. From the 1st identity and the substitution $u = s + t$

$$
\begin{align*}
  P_t U_\alpha f &= \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha s} P_{s+t}f\;\d s \\
  &= \int_t^\infty e^{-\alpha (u - t)} P_u f\;\d u \\
  &= e^{-\alpha t}\int_t^\infty e^{-\alpha u} P_u f\;\d u
\end{align*}
$$

Hence

$$
  \frac{P_t U_\alpha f - U_\alpha f}{t} = \frac{1}{t}\left[ e^{-\alpha t} \int_t^\infty e^{-\alpha u} P_u f\;\d u - U_\alpha f \right]
$$

Adding and substracting $e^{\alpha u} U_\alpha f$ and combining integrals gives

$$
  \frac{P_t U_\alpha f - U_\alpha f}{t} = \frac{1}{t}\left[ e^{\alpha t} \int_t^\infty e^{-\alpha u} P_u f\;\d u - e^{\alpha t} \int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha u} P_u f\;\d u \right] + \frac{e^{\alpha t} - 1}{t} U_\alpha f \\
  &= -e^{\alpha t}\frac{1}{t} \int_0^t e^{-\alpha s} P_s f\;\d s + \frac{e^{\alpha t} - 1}{t} U_\alpha f
$$

Since $s\mapsto P_s f$ is continuous, the first term converges to $-f$, and the second term converges to $\alpha U_\alpha f$ as $t\downarrow 0$.

3. From the 1st identity we have $\alpha U_\alpha - U_\alpha G = I \iff U_\alpha (\alpha I - G) = I$, and from the 2nd identity we have $\alpha U_\alpha - GU_\alpha = I \iff (\alpha I - G)U_\alpha = I$. 
4. This follows from the 3rd identity.
</details>
</MathBox>

# Irreducibility

<MathBox title="Irreducibility" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, $K$ a Markov kernel on $\mathsf{X}$, and $\phi$ a $\sigma$-finite measure on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. The Markov chain $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ with transition kernel $K$ is $\phi$-irreducible if, for every measurable set $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\phi(A) > 0$, and for every $x\in\mathsf{X}$, there exists $n\in\N_+$ such that $K^n (x, A) > 0$. The chain is strongly $\phi$-irreducible if $K(x, A) > 0$ for all $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ and $x\in\mathsf{X}$.

In the discrete case, if $\mathsf{X}$ is countable and $\phi$ is a taken as the counting measure, the chain is irreducible if for all $x,y\in\mathsf{X}$, there exists $n \geq 1$ such that $K^n (x,y) > 0$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Characteristics of irreducibility" boxType="proposition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, $K$ a Markov kernel, and $\phi$ a $\sigma$-finite measure. For $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ define the hitting time

$$
  \tau_A := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n \in A}
$$

and the occupation time

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \eta_A := \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A}}
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Then the Markov chain $(X_n)$ is $\phi$-irreducible if and only if for every $x\in\mathsf{X}$ and every $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\phi(A) > 0$, any of the following equivalent properties hold
1. There exists $n\in \N_+$ such that $K^n (x, A) > 0$
2. The hitting probability is strictly positive, i.e. $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A < \infty) > 0$
2. The expected occupation time is strictly positive, i.e. $\mathbb{E}_x [\eta_A] > 0$
3. For some $\epsilon\in (0,1)$ the resolvent kernel satisfies $K_\epsilon (x, A) > 0$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Maximal irreducibility measure" boxType="proposition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, $K$ a Markov kernel, and suppose that the Markov chain $(X_n)$ is $\phi$-irreducible for some $\sigma$-finite measure $\phi$. Then there exists a probability measure $\psi$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, called a maximal irreducibility measure, satisfying
1. **Irreducibility:** The Markov chain $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible
2. **Maximality:** If $\xi$ is any measure for which $(X_n)$ is $\xi$-irreducible, then $\xi$ is dominated by $\psi$, i.e. $\xi\ll\psi$
3. **Absorption property:** For every $A\in\mathcal{B}(A)$ with $\psi(A) = 0$, then $\psi(\set{y\in\mathsf{X} : \mathbb{P}_y (\tau_A < \infty) > 0}) = 0$, where $\tau_A := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in A}$
4. **Resolvent representation:** The measure $\psi$ is equivalent to the measure $\psi_0$ defined by

$$
  \psi_0 (A) := \int_\mathsf{X} K_{1/2} (x, A) \phi (\d x),\; A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$

that is, $\psi\ll\psi_0$ and $\psi_0 \ll\psi$.
</MathBox>

## Atoms and small sets

<MathBox title="Atom" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, $K$ a Markov kernel. A set $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is an atom if there exists a probability measure $\nu$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ such that 

$$
  K(x, A) = \nu(A),\; \forall x\in\alpha,\ \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$

Equivalently, the transition probabilities are identical for all starting points $x\in\alpha$.

If in addition the Markov chain $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible for some $\sigma$-finite measure $\psi$, the atom is accessible if $\psi(\alpha) > 0$.
</MathBox>

For a discrete Markov chain $(X_n)$, every singleton $\set{x}$ is trivially an atom, so the notion is always applicable. In contrast, the existence of an atom is a very strong requirement, since it implies that the transition kernel is constant on a set of positive measure.

A more flexible generalization is given by the minorization condition. A set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ satisfies a minorization condition if there exists a constant $\epsilon > 0$ and a probability measure $\nu$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  K(x, A) \geq \epsilon\nu (A)\; \forall x\in C, \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Equivalently, for all $x\in C$, the transition kernel admits the decomposition

$$
  K(x,\cdot) = \epsilon\nu(\cdot) + (1 - \epsilon) R(x,\cdot)
$$

where $R$ is a Markov kernel. Thus, $\nu$ represents a uniform component of the transition mechanism on the set $C$. This condition relaxes the notion of an atom by requiring only a common lower bound, rather than exact equality of transition probabilities.

Set satisfying the minorization condition $\eqref{equation-1}$ are called small sets, and they play a central role in the study of stability and ergodic properties of general state-space Markov chains.

<MathBox title="Small set" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, $K$ a Markov kernel. A measurable set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is small if there exists $m\in\N_+$ and a probability measure $\nu_m$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ such that

$$
  K^m (x, A) \geq \nu_m (A),\; \forall C, \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain with transition kernel $K$ on the measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Then the following properties hold
1. **Existence of small subsets:** For every measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(A) > 0$, there exist and integer $m\in\N_+$, a measurable set $C\subset A$ with $\psi(C) > 0$, and a nontrivial finite measure $\nu_m$ such that

$$
  K^m (x, B) \geq \nu_m (B),\; \forall x\in C, \forall B\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$

In particular, $C$ is a small set, implying $\nu_m (C) > 0$.

2. **Countable covering by small sets:** The exists a countable collection of small sets $(C_i)_{i\in\N_+}$ suc that $\mathsf{X}$ is decomposed as

$$
  \mathsf{X} = \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty C_i
$$

3. **Explicit construction:** Given a small set $C$, we can construct a countable family of small sets covering the state space via

$$
  C_{n,m} := \set{y\in\mathsf{X} : K^n (y, C) > 1/m},\; n,m\in\N_+
$$

Then each $C_{n,m}$ is a small set, and

$$
  \mathsf{X} = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \bigcup_{m=1}^\infty C_{n,m}
$$
</MathBox>

Suppose the minorizing condition $\eqref{equation-1}$ holds for the Markov chain $(X_n)$. Then there are two ways of constructing a companion Markov chain $(\tilde{X}_n)$, defined on an extended state space, which preserves many of the probabilistic properties of $(X_n)$ and posesses an accessible atom $\alpha$.

1. **Nummelin splitting:** This method augments the state space to $\mathsf{X}\times\set{0,1}$ and constructs a split chain $(\tilde{X}_n)$ by introducing an auxiliary Bernoulli variable that splits transitions from the set $C$. On the event corresponding to the minorizing component, the chain regenerates according to $\mu$, thereby creating an atom of the form $\alpha = C\times\set{1}$. The resulting chain consists of two coupled copies of $(X_n)$, corresponding to the split and residual transitions.
2. **Athreya-Ney construction:** This approach uses a suitable stopping time (typically based on returns to $C$ together with the minorizing condition) to define renewal times. By sampling the chain at these stopping times, we obtain a new Markov chain with an embedded atom. This construction is closely related to renewal theory, as the regeneration times induce an idependent and identically distribution structure between successive visists to the atom.

<MathBox title="Regeneration time" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with natural filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)$, where $\mathcal{F}_n := \sigma(X_0,\dots,X_n)$. A regeneration time (or renewal time) is a stopping time $\tau$ with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ such that the post-$\tau$ process $(X_{\tau + n})_{n\geq 0}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_n$, and has a distribution that does not depend on the past prior to time $\tau$.

Equivalently, $\tau$ is a regeneration time if, conditional on $\tau < \infty$, the process restarts afresh at time $\tau$, in the sense that

$$
  (X_{\tau+n})_{n\geq 0} \overset{d}{=} (X_n)_{n\geq 0}
$$

and is independent of $\mathcal{F}_n$.
</MathBox>

Suppose the minorization condition $\eqref{equation-1}$ holds on a measurable set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, and assume $C$ is recurrent in the sense that $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) = 1$ for all $x\in\mathsf{X}$, where $\tau_C := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in C}$. 

The Athreya-Ney construction modifies the transition kernel when $X_n \in C$ by generating the next state $X_{n+1}$ according to

$$
  X_{n+1} \sim \begin{cases}
    \nu,\quad& \text{with probability } \epsilon \\
    R(X_n, \cdot),\quad& \text{ with probability } 1 - \epsilon
  \end{cases}
$$

where the residual kernel $R$ is defined by

$$
  R(x, A) := \frac{K(X_n, \cdot) - \epsilon\nu(\cdot)}{1-\epsilon},\; x\in C
$$

This construction preserves the original transition law, since for all $x\in C$ and $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \epsilon\nu(A) + (1-\epsilon) R(x, A) =& \epsilon\nu(A) + \frac{K(x_n, A) - \epsilon\nu(A)}{1-\epsilon} \\
  =& K(x_n, A)
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, the marginal distribution of $X_{n+1}$ conditional on $X_n = x$ is unchanged, while the decomposition introduces an additional random mechanism that selects, with probability $\epsilon$, a transition independent of the current state (via $\nu$).

This mechanism induces regeneration events: whenever $X_n \in C$ and the Bernoulli trial selects the $\nu$-component, the future evolution of the chain becomes independent of the past and restarts from $\nu$.

Define, the regeneration times by $\tau_0 := 0$ and for $j > 0$ by

$$
  \tau_j = \inf\set{n \geq t_{j-1} : X_n \in C \land X_{n+1} \sim\nu}
$$

To make this structure explicit, we introduce the split chain $\tilde{X}_n = (X_n, \omega_n)$, where $\omega_n \in\set{0,1}$ indicates whether the $\nu$-component was selected at time $n$ (with $\omega_n = 1$ corresponding to regeneration when $X_n \in C$). In this augmented process, the set $\tilde{\alpha} = C \times\set{1}$ is an atom of the chain $(\tilde{X}_n)$. Whenever, $\tilde{X}_n \in\tilde{\alpha}$, the conditional distribution of $\tilde{X}_{n+1}$ is independent of $\tilde{X}_n$ and depends only on $\nu$. The original process $(X_n)$ is recovered as the first coordinate of the split chain, and it retains its original transition kernel $K$.

## Cycles and aperiodicity

In the discrete case, let $\mathbf{P}$ be the transition matrix. The period of a state $\omega\in\mathsf{X}$ is defined as

$$
  \d(\omega) = \gcd\set{m \geq 1 : K^m (\omega, \omega) > 0}
$$

The value of the period is constant on all states that communicate with $\omega$. In the case of an irreducible chain on a finite state space $\mathsf{X}$, the transition matrix can be put, after a suitable permutation of the states, into a cyclic block matrix

$$
  \mathbf{P} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{D}_1 & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{D}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    \mathbf{D}_d & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0}
  \end{bmatrix}
$$

where each $\mathbf{D}_i$ is stochastic matrices describing transition from one cyclic class to the next.

This representation reflects the decomposition of the state space into $d$ cyclic classes, with transitions occuring deterministically between successive classes and returning to the original class only after multiples of $d$ steps. Consequently, an irreducible Markov chain has a well-defined period $d$, and it is aperiodic if $d=1$, in which case no such cyclic decomposition is present.

<MathBox title="Markov chain period" boxType="definition" tag="1">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain $(X_n)$ with transition kernel $K$ on the measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. The chain has period $d\in\N_+$ if there exists a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, an integer $M$, and a probability distribution $\nu_M$ such that 

$$
  K^M (x, A) \geq \nu_M (A),\; \forall x\in C, \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$

and

$$
  d = \set{m \geq 1: \exists \delta_m > 0: K^m (x, A) \geq \delta_m \nu_M (A), \forall x\in C, \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})}
$$

In other words, $d$ is the greatest common divisor of all integers $m$ for which the $m$-step transition kernel admits a uniform minorization on $C$ by a measure proportional to $\nu_M$.

The chain $(X_n)$ is aperiodic if $d = 1$. The chain is strongly aperiodic if there exists a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, a constant $\epsilon > 0$ and a probability measure $\nu$ such that 

$$
  K(x, A) \geq \epsilon\nu (A),\; \forall x\in C, \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$

and $\nu (C) > 0$. For any $\epsilon\in (0,1)$, the resolvent $K_\epsilon$-chain associated with $K$ is stronly aperiodic, even if the original chain is only aperiodic.
</MathBox>

In the discrete setting, if there exists a state $x\in\mathsf{X}$ such that $K(x,x) > 0$, the chain $(X_n)$ is aperiodic. This condition is sufficient but not necessary.

When the chain is continuous and the transition kernel has a component which is absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure, with density $f(\cdot|x)$, a sufficient condition for aperiodicity is that $f(\cdot|x)$ is positive in a neighbourhood of $x$. The chain can then remain in this neighbourhood for an arbitrary number of instants before visiting any set $A \in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$.

# Transience and recurrence

<MathBox title="Transience and recurrence of finite states" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a finite state space $\mathsf{X}$, and for a state $\omega\in\mathsf{X}$, define the total number of visits to $\omega$ by

$$
  \eta_\omega := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n = \omega}}
$$

The state $\omega$ is transient if $\mathbb{E}_\omega (\eta_\omega) < \infty$ and recurrent if $\mathbb{E}_\omega (\eta_\omega) = \infty$. Equivalently, $\omega$ is recurrent if and only if $\mathbb{P}_\omega (\tau_\omega < \infty) = 1$, where $\tau_\omega := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n = \omega}$, and transient otherwise.
</MathBox>

For an irreducible Markov chain $(X_n)$ on a discrete state space $\mathsf{X}$, the properties of recurrence and transience are class properties, and do not depend on a particular state. In other words, if one state is recurrent (respectively transient), then every state is recurrent (respectively transient). This follows from the Chapman-Kolmogorov equations $\eqref{equation-3}$ together with the communication property. Let

$$
  \eta_y := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n = y}}
$$

denote the total number of visits to a state $y\in\mathsf{X}$. Then, for all $x,y\in\mathsf{X}$, the states are transient if $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_y) < \infty$, and recurrent if $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_y) = \infty$. Accordingly, an irreducible Markov chain is called transient if all states are transient, and recurrent if all states are recurrent. These two cases are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.

<MathBox title="Branching process" boxType="example">
Consider a population evolving in discrete generations, where each individual reproduces independently of the others. Let $X\in\N_0$ denote the number of offspring of a single individual, with probability generating function $\phi(s) := \mathbb{E}(s^X)$ for $s\in [0,1]$. Let $S_t$ denote the size of the $t$th generation, with initial condition $S_0 = 1$. Then

$$
  S_t = \sum_{i=1}^{S_{t-1}} X_{t,i},\; t \geq 1
$$

where $(X_{t,i})$ are independent and identically distributed copies of $X$. The process $(S_t)_{t\geq 0}$ is a Galton-Watson branching process. The generating function of $S_t$ is given recursively by

$$
  g_t (s) := \mathbb{E}(s^{S_t}) = \phi^{\circ t} (s),\; t \geq 1
$$

where $\phi^{\circ t}$ denotes the $t$-fold composition of $\phi$.

Define the extinction probability at time $t$ by

$$
  \rho_t := \mathbb{P}(S_t = 0) = g_t (0)
$$

Then $(\rho_t)$ satisfies the recursion $\rho_t = \phi(\rho_{t-1})$ for $t\geq 1$, with $\rho_0 = 0$. The sequence $(\rho_r)$ is nondecreasing and converges to a limit

$$
  \rho := \lim_{t\to\infty} \rho_t
$$

which is the smallest solution in $[0,1]$ of the fixed-point equation $\rho = \phi(\rho)$. Let $m := \phi' (1) = \mathbb{E}(X)$ denote the mean offspring number. Then
- if $m \leq 1$, the only solution is $\rho \eqas 1$, and extinction with probability $1$
- if $m > 1$, there exists a unique solution $\rho\in (0,1)$, and exctinction occurs with probability $\rho < 1$

Interpreting $(S_t)$ as a Markov chain on $\N_0$, we note the following
- The state $0$ is absorbing
- If $m \leq 1$, the chain hits $0$ almost surely, so $0$ is recurrent, while all other states are transient
- If $m > 1$, the chain has a positive probability $1 - \rho > 0$ of never hitting $0$, so $0$ is transient

Finally, consider a modified chain with regeneration at $0$, i.e. whenever $S_t = 0$, the next generation is restarted according to

$$
  S_{t+1} | \set{S_t = 0} \sim X
$$

In this case, $0$ is no longer absorbing. If $m > 1$, the number of returns to $0$ is geometrically distributed with parameter $1 - \rho$, since after each visit to $0$, the process becomes extinct again with probability $\rho$ and escapes with probability $1 - \rho$. Thus, $0$ is transient. If $m \leq 1$, the chain returns to $0$ infinitely almost surely, and is therefore recurrent.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Transience and recurrence of sets" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on the measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. For measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, define the total number of visits to $A$ by

$$
  \eta_A := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A}}
$$

The set $A$ is 
- recurrent if $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_A) = \infty$ for every $x\in A$.
- uniformly transient if there exists a constant $M < \infty$ such that $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_A) < M$ for every $x\in A$.
- transient if there exists a covering of $\mathsf{X}$ by uniformly transient sets, that is, a countable collection of uniformly transient sets $(B_i)_{i\in\N_+}$ such that

$$
  A \subseteq \bigcup_{i\in\N_+} B_i
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="1">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, and suppose that the chain has an accessible atom $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$. Then the following properties hold:
1. If $\alpha$ is recurrent, then every measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(A) > 0$ is recurrent
2. If $\alpha$ is transient, then $\mathsf{X}$ is transient. In other words, there exists a countable covering of $\mathsf{X}$ by uniformly transient sets.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Transience and recurrence of Markov chains" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Thee chain is recurrent if there exists a $\sigma$-finite measure $\psi$ such that
1. $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible, and
2. for every $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(A) > 0$, then $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_A) = \infty$ for every $x\in A$, where $\eta_A := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{X_n \in A}$

The chain is transient if there exists a $\sigma$-finite measure $\psi$ such that
- $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible, and 
- the state space $\mathsf{X}$ is transient
</MathBox>

The classification of Markov chains as transient or recurrent according to Proposition $\ref{proposition-1}$ can be easily extended to strongly aperiodic chains. If a Markov chain $(X_n)$ satisfies a minorization condition (in particular, if it is strongly aperiodic), we can construct the associated split chain $(\tilde{X}_n)$. The original chain $(X_n)$ and its split version $(\tilde{X}_n)$ are either both recurrent or both transient, which follows from the fact that the splitting procedure preserves occupation times up to a bounded multiplicative factor.

For a general $\psi$-irreducible chain, the classification can be reduced to the strongly aperiodic case by considering the resolvent chain $K_\epsilon$ with kernel $\eqref{equation-4}$, which is strongly aperiodic. The occupation measures of the original and resolvent chains is given by relation

$$
  \sum_{n=0}^\infty K_\epsilon^n = \frac{1-\epsilon}{\epsilon} \sum_{n=0}^\infty K^n
$$

since, for any measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_A) =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty K^n (x, A) \\
  =& \frac{\epsilon}{1-\epsilon} \sum_{n=0}^\infty K_\epsilon^\infty (x, A)
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, finiteness or infiniteness of the expected occupation time is preserved under passage from $K$ to $K_\epsilon$. In particular, $(X_n)$ is recurrent (respectively transient) if and only if the associated $K_\epsilon$-chain is recurrent (respectively transient).

## Criteria for recurrence

<MathBox title="Recurrence criteria" boxType="proposition" tag="2">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. The chain is recurrent if there exists a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(C) > 0$ such that $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) = 1$ for every $x\in C$, where $\tau_C := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in C}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Fix $x\in C$ and define

$$
  u_n := K^n (x, C) = \mathbb{P}_x (X_n \in C),\; n\geq 0
$$ 

and 

$$
  f_n := \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C = n),\; n \geq 1
$$

which is the probability of first visit to $C$ at time $n$. Let

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{U}(s) =& 1 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty u_n s^n \\
  Q(s) =& \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n s^n
\end{align*}
$$

By standard renewal decomposition, we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  u_n = f_n + \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} f_{k} u_{n-k}
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This expresses that a visit to $C$ at time $n$ occurs either at the first return time $n$, or after a first return at time $k < n$ followed by a subsequent visit in $n-k$ steps. Passing to generating functions yields

$$
  \tilde{U}(s) = 1 + Q(s) \tilde{U}(s)
$$

and hence

$$
  \tilde{U}(s) = \frac{1}{1 - Q(s)},\; s\in [0,1)
$$

Letting $s\uparrow 1$, we obtain

$$
  \sum_{n=0}^\infty u_n = \tilde{U}(1) = \frac{1}{1 - Q(1)}
$$

where

$$
  Q(1) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n = \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty)
$$

By assumption, $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) = 1$, hence $Q(1) = 1$ and therefore

$$
  \tilde{U}(1) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty K^n (x, C) = \infty
$$

implying $\mathbb{E}_x (\eta_C) = \infty$. Thus, the set $C$ is recurrent.

Finally, since $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible and $\psi(C) > 0$, it follows from Proposition $\ref{proposition-1}$ that every measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(A) > 0$ is recurrent. Hence, the chain $(X_n)$ is recurrent.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Harris recurrence

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. A measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is Harris recurrent if $\mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = 1$ for all $x\in A$, where

$$
  \eta_A := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A}}
$$

The Markov chain $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent if there exists a $\sigma$-finite measure $\psi$ such that $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible and for every $A \in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(A) > 0$, then $A$ is Harris recurrent.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Suppose that $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A < \infty) = 1$ for every measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ and every $x\in A$, where $\tau_A := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n \in A}$. Then $\mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = 1$ for every $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ and every $x\in A$. In particular, if the chain $(X_n)$ is $\psi$-irreducible, it follows that $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent.
  
<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Fix $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ and $x\in A$. Define recursively the successive return times to $A$ by $\tau_A^{(1)} := \tau_A$ and

$$
  \tau_A^{(k+1)} := \inf\set{n > \tau_A^{(k)} : X_n \in A},\; k \geq 1
$$

By assumption, $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A^{(1)} < \infty) = 1$. Using the strong Markov property at time $\tau_A^{(k)}$, we obtain

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A^{(k+1)} < \infty | \mathcal{F}_{\tau_A^{(k)}}) = \mathbb{P}_{X_{\tau_A^{(k)}}} (\tau_A < \infty)
$$

Since $X_{\tau_A^{(k)}} \in A$ almost surely on $\set{\tau_A^{(k)} < \infty}$, the assumption yields

$$
  \mathbb{P}_{X_{\tau_A^{(k)}}} (\tau_A < \infty) = 1
$$

Thus, $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A^{(k+1)} < \infty) = 1$, and by induction, we obtain $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_A^{(k)} < \infty) = 1$ for all $k\geq 1$. Now observe that

$$
  \set{\eta_A \geq k} = \set{\tau_A^{(k)} < \infty}
$$

which implies that $\mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A \geq k) = 1$ for all $k\geq 1$. Taking the limit $k\to\infty$ yields

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = \lim_{k\to\infty} \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A \geq k) = 1
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

The notion of Harris recurrence is not required when the state space $\mathsf{X}$ is finite or countable. In this case, we have the equivalence 

$$
  \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_x) = \infty \iff \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_x < \infty) = 1,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

which follows from the standard renewal decomposition.

In contrast, for general state spaces, recurrence must be formulated in terms of sets rather than individual states. In this context, if a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent, then for every measurable set $B \in \mathbb{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(B) > 0$

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_B = \infty) = 1,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Suppose there exists a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\psi(C) > 0$ such that $\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) = 1$ for all $x\in\mathsf{X}$, where $\tau_C := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in C}$. Then the chain $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent.
</MathBox>

## Drift condition

<MathBox title="Drift" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain with transition kernel $P$ on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Let $V: \mathsf{X}\to\R$ be a measurable function such that

$$
  \int_\mathsf{X} |V(y)| P(x, \d y) < \infty,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

The drift of $V$ at $x\in\nathsf{X}$ is defined by

$$
  \Delta V(x) := PV(x) - V(x)
$$

where

$$
  PV(x) = \int_\mathsf{X} V(y) P(x, \d y) = \mathbb{E}_x [V(X_1)]
$$

The drift $\Delta V(x)$ represents the expected one-step change in $V(X_n)$ given $X_0 = x$, i.e.

$$
  \Delta V(x) = \mathbb{E}_x [V(X_1) - V(X_0)]
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="lemma" tag="3">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, with transition kernel $P$. Let $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ and define the hitting time $\sigma_C := \inf\set{n \geq 0 : X_n \in C}$. The function $V^* (x) := \mathbb{P}_x (\sigma_C < \infty)$ is the minimal nonnegative measurable function $V:\mathsf{X}\to [0,\infty)$ satisfying:
1. **Superharmonicity outside $C$:** For all $x \notin C$, then $PV(x) \leq V(x)$, equivalently $\Delta V(x) \leq 0$.
2. **Boundary condition on $C$:** For all $x \in C$, then $V(x) \geq 1$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We first verify that $V^*$ satisfies the conditions **(1)** and **(2)**:
- If $x\in C$, then $\sigma_C = 0$, therefore $V^* (x) = 1$.
- If $x\notin C$, then by the Markov property
$$
\begin{align*}
  PV^* (x) =& \mathbb{E}[V^* (X_1)] \\
  =& \mathbb{E}_x [\mathbb{P}_{X_1} (\sigma_C < \infty)] \\
  =& \mathbb{P}_x (\sigma_C < \infty, \sigma_C \geq 1) \\
  \leq& \mathbb{P}_x (\sigma_C < \infty) =& V^* (x)
\end{align*}
$$

so $V^*$ is superharmonic on $C^c$.

To show the minimality, let $V$ satisfy **(1)-(2)**, define the stopped process

$$
  M_n := V(X_{n\land \sigma_C})
$$

For $x\notinc C$, the superharmonicity condition implies

$$
  \mathbb{E}_x [M_{n+1} | \mathcal{F}_n] \leq M_n
$$

so $(M_n)$ is a nonnegative supermatingale.

By optional stopping, $V(x) = \mathbb{E}_x (M_0) \geq \mathbb{E}_x (M_{\sigma_C \land n})$. Letting $n\to\infty$ and using monotone convergence, $V(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_x [V(X_{\sigma_C}) \mathbf{1}_\set{\sigma_C < \infty}]$. Since $V \geq 1$ on $C$, we have

$$
  V(x) \geq \mathbb{P}_x (\sigma_C < \infty) = V^* (x)
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Drift characterization of transience" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ b a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with transition kernel $P$. Then the following are equivalent.
1. The chain $(X_n)$ is transient
2. There exist a bounded measurable function $V:\mathsf{X}\to (0,\infty)$ and a constant $r\in\R$ such that for all $x\in\mathsf{X}$ with $V(x) > r$
$$
  \Delta V(x) = PV(x) - V(x) > 0
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(2) $\implies$ (1)**

Assume there exist a bounded function $V:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ and $r\in\R$ such that $\Delta V(x) > 0$ on $C^c := \set{x \in V(x) > r}$. Define the level set

$$
  C = \set{x : V(x) \leq r}
$$ 

Let $M := \sup_{x\in\mathsf{X}} V(x) < \infty$ be a bound on $V$, and define the normalized function

$$
  \tilde{V}(x) = \begin{cases}
    \frac{M - V(x)}{M - r},\quad& x\in C^c \\
    1,\quad& x\in C
  \end{cases}
$$

Then
- $0 < \tilde{V} (x) \leq 1$ for all $x$
- $\tilde{V}(x) = 1$ on $C$
- and on $C^c$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Delta \tilde{V}(x) =& P\tilde{V}(x) - \tilde{V}(x) \\
  =& -\frac{1}{M-r} (PV(x) - V(x)) < 0
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, $\tilde{V}$ is superhamonic on $C^c$ and satisfies $\tilde{V} \geq 1$ on $C$. By the minimality result for hitting probabilities in Lemma $\ref{lemma-3}$

$$
  \tilde{V} (x) \geq \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) =: V^* (x)
$$

Since $\tilde{V}(x) < 1$ for all $x\in C^c$, it follows that

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) < \infty,\; \forall x\in C^c
$$

Thus, $C$ is transient and by $\psi$-irreducibility, this implies that $(X_n)$ is transient.

**(1) $\implies$ (2)**

Assume $(X_n)$ is transient. Then there exists a measurable set $C$ with $\psi(C) > 0$ such that

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty) < 1,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

Define $V(x) := 1 - \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C < \infty)$. Then
- $0 \leq V(x) \leq 1$, so $V$ is bounded
- $V(x) > 0$ on a set of positive $\psi$-measure
- and on $C^c$
$$
\begin{align*}
  \Delta V(x) =& \mathbb{E}_x [V(X_1)] - V(x) \\
  =& \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C = \infty, \tau_C \geq 1) - \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_C = \infty) > 0
\end{align*}
$$

since conditioning on one step strictly increases the probability of avoiding $C$.

Thus, $V$ satisfies the required drift condition outside the level set $C = \set{x : V(x) = 0}$.
</details>
</MathBox>

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with transition kernel $P$. The chain $(X_n)$ is recurrent undet the following conditions. Suppose there exists a measurable function $V:\mathsf{X}\to[1,\infty)$ such that

## Eaton's admissibility condition

Let $(\Theta, \mathcal{B}(\Theta))$ and $(\mathsf{X}, \mathbb{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be measurable spaces. Let $\pi(\d\theta)$ be a prior measure on $\Theta$, and let $f(x|\theta)$ be a likelihood kernel on $\mathsf{X}$ such that the posterior distribution $\pi(\d\theta|x)$ is well-defined for $\pi$-almost every $\theta$ and $x$. Define the Markov transition kernel $K$ on $\Theta$ by

$$
  K(\theta, A) := \int_\mathsf{X} \pi(A|x) f(x|\theta) \;\d x,\; A\in\mathcal{B}(\Theta)
$$

The kernel $K$ defines Markov chain $(\theta^{(n)})_{n\geq 0}$ via the Gibbs type sampling scheme
1. Given $\theta^{(n)} = \theta$, sample $X\sim f(\cdot|\theta)$
2. Sample $\theta^{(n+1)} \sim\pi(\cdot|X)$

The prior measure $\pi$ is invariant for $K$, i.e.

$$
  \pi(A) = \int_\Theta K(\theta, A) \pi(\d\theta),\; \forall A\in\mathcal{B}(\Theta)
$$

Let $L^2 (\pi)$ denote the Hilbert space of square-integrable functions with respect to $\pi$. For a measurable set $C\in \mathcal{B}(\Theta)$ with $0 < \pi(C) < \infty$, define the class

$$
  \mathcal{V}(C) = \set{h\in L^2 (\pi) : h(\theta) \geq 0 \land \theta\in C \implies h(\theta) \geq 1}
$$

Define the Dirichlet form associated with $K$ by

$$
  \Delta(h) := \frac{1}{2} \iint_{\Theta\times\Theta} [h(\theta) - h(\eta)]^2 K(\theta, \d\eta) \pi(\d\theta),\; h\in L^2 (\pi)
$$

Assume that for every measurable set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\Theta)$ with $0 < \pi(C) < \infty$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \inf_{h\in \mathcal{V}(C)} \Delta (h) = 0
\tag{\label{equation-25}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Then, for every bounded measurable function $g:\Theta\to\R$, the Bayes estimator $\mathbb{E}^\pi [g(\theta)|x]$ is admissible under quadratic loss.

If $\pi$ is a proper prior distribution, then the constant function $1 \in L^2(\pi)$ satisfies $K1 = 1$. Consequently, the associated Dirichlet form satisfies

$$
  \Delta(1) = \frac{1}{2} \iint (1 - 1)^2 K(\theta, \d\eta) \pi(\d\theta) = 0
$$

In this case, $\eqref{equation-25}$ is trivially satisfied in the degenerate case where $h\equiv 1$ is admissible in $\mathcal{V}(C)$.

since $h\equiv 1$ belongs to $L^2 (\pi)$ and $\Delta(1) = 0$ in this case. The extension then consider approximations of $1$ by functions in $V(C)$.

For every measurable set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\Theta)$ with $0 < \pi(C) < \infty$

$$
  \inf_{h\in \mathcal{V}(C)} \Delta(h) = \int_C [1 - \mathbb{P}(\tau_C < \infty | \theta^{(0)} = \eta] \pi(\d\eta)
$$

where $\tau_C := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : \theta^{(n)} \in C}$ is the first hitting time. Thus, the generalized Bayes estimators of bounded function of $\theta$ are admissible if and only if the associated Markov chain $(\theta^{(n)})$ is recurrent.

# Invariant measures

<MathBox title="Invariant measure" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space and let $K(x,\cdot)$ be a Markov transition kernel on $\mathsf{X}$. A $\sigma$-finite measure $\pi$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ is invariant (or stationary) for $K$ if

$$
  \pi(B) = \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, B) \pi(\d x),\; \forall B \in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$
</MathBox>


<MathBox title="Positive Markov chain" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space and let $K(x,\cdot)$ be a Markov transition kernel on $\mathsf{X}$. Suppose the associated Markov chain is $\psi$-irreducible.

- The chain is positive if there exists an invariant probability measure $\pi$
- The chain is null recurrent if it is recurrent, but admits no finite invariant measure
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space and let $K(x,\cdot)$ be a Markov transition kernel on $\mathsf{X}$. Suppose the associated Markov chain is $\psi$-irreducible. If there exists a $\sigma$-finite invariant measure $\pi$ for $K$, then this invariant measure is unique up to a multiplicative constant.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space, and let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain with transition kernel $K$. If the chain is positive, it is recurrent.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

We prove the claim by contradiction. Suppose $(X_n)$ is transient. Then there exists a countable collection of measurable sets $(A_j)_{j\in\N} \subset \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ covering $\mathsf{X}$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathsf{X} = \bigcup_{j=1}^\infty A_j
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Each $A_j$ is uniformly transient, meaning that there exists a constant $M_j < \infty$ such that

$$
  \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_{A_j}) \leq M_j,\; \forall x\in A_j
$$

where $\eta_{A_j}$ is the total number of visits to $A_j$:

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \eta_{A_j} := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A}}
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Let $\pi$ be an invariant probability measure. By invariance, for every $n\geq 0$ and every $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \pi(A_j) =& \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, A_j) \pi(\d x) \\
  =& \int_\mathsf{X} K^n (x, A_j) \pi(\d x)
\end{align*}
$$

Fix $j\in\N$. Summing over $n = 0,\dots,k$ yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  (k+1)\pi (A_j) =& \sum_{n=0}^k \int_\mathsf{X} K^n (x, A_j) \pi(\d x) \\
  =& \int_\mathsf{X} \sum_{n=0}^k K^n (x, A_j) \pi(\d x)
\end{align*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-6}$ it follows that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{n=0}^k K^n (x, A_j) =& \mathbb{E}_x \left[\sum_{n=0}^k \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A_j}} \right] \\
  \leq& \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_{A_j})
\end{align*}
$$

Thus,

$$
\begin{align*}
  (k+1) \pi(A_j) \leq& \int_\mathsf{X} \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_{A_j}) \pi(\d x) \\
  =& \int_{A_j} \mathbb{E}_x (\eta_{A_j}) \pi(\d x) \\
  \leq& M_j \pi(A_j) \leq M_j
\end{align*}
$$

Letting $k\to\infty$ shows that $\pi(A_j) = 0$, for every $j\in\N$. From $\eqref{equation-1}$, countable subadditivity gives

$$
  \pi(X) \leq \sum_{j=1}^\infty \pi(A_j) = 0
$$

which contradicts the assumption $\pi(\mathsf{X}) = 1$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Kac's Theorem" boxType="theorem" tag="1">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, and suppose $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is an atom. The chain is positive if and only if $\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty$, where $\tau_\alpha$ is the return time defined by

$$
  \tau_\alpha := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in \alpha}
$$

In this case, the invariant probability measure $\pi$ satisfies

$$
  \pi(\alpha) = (\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha))^{-1}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

- **Sufficiency: $\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty\implies \text{positive recurrence}$**

If $\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty$, then in particular $\mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha < \infty) = 1$. Thus $(X_n)$ is recurrent by Proposition $\ref{proposition-2}$. Define a measure $\pi$ on $\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \pi(A) :=& \mathbb{E}_\alpha \left(\sum_{n=0}^{\tau_\alpha - 1} \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in A}} \right) \\
  =& \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_n \in A, \tau_\alpha \geq n)
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which is the cycle occupation measure associated with returns to $\alpha$

**Invariance of $\pi$**

Using the Markov property, we find

$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, A) \pi (\d x) =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, A) \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_n \in \d x, \tau_\alpha > n) \\
  =& \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_{n+1} \in A, \tau_\alpha > n)
\end{align*}
$$

Since 

$$
  \set{\tau_\alpha > n} = \set{\tau_\alpha > n + 1} \cup \set{\tau_\alpha = n + 1}
$$

and $X_{n+1} \in\alpha$ on $\set{\tau_\alpha = n + 1}$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_{n+1} \in A, \tau_\alpha > n) =& \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_{n+1} \in A, \tau_\alpha > n + 1) \\
  &+ \mathbf{1}_A (\alpha) \mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha = n + 1)
\end{align*}
$$

Summing over $n$ yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_\mathsf{X}K(x, A) =& \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_n \in A, \tau_\alpha > n) + \mathbf{1}_A (\alpha) \underbrace{\mathbf{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha < \infty)}_{=1} \\
  \overset{\eqref{equation-8}}{=}& \pi(A)
\end{align*}
$$

**Finiteness of $\pi$**

By Tonelli's theorem

$$
\begin{align*}
  \pi(\mathsf{X}) =& \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha \geq n) \\
  =& \sum_{n=1}^\infty \sum_{m=n}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha = m) \\
  =& \sum_{m=1}^\infty m \mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha = m) \\
  =& \mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty
\end{align*}
$$

Thus $\pi$ is a finite invariant measure. Normalizing it yields an invariant probability measure, implying that the chain $(X_n)$ is positive recurrent.

- **Necessity: $\text{positive recurrence} \implies\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty$**

Suppose the chain $(X_n)$ admits an invariant probability measure $\pi$. By $\psi$-irreducibility, $\pi$ is unique. Let $\tilde{\pi}$ be the cycle occupation measure constructed above. By the same argument, $\tilde{\pi}$ is an invariant measure. By uniqueness up to scaling, we have for some constant $c > 0$

$$
  \tilde{\pi} = c\pi
$$

Evaluating at $\mathsf{X}$ with $\pi(\mathsf{X}) = 1$ gives 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  c = \tilde{\pi}(\mathsf{X}) = \mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha)
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $\pi$ is finite, it follows that $\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\alpha_\tau) < \infty$.

- **Identification of $\pi(A)$**

From $\tilde{\pi} = c\pi$ and

$$
  \tilde{\pi}(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}_\alpha \left(\sum_{n=0}^{\tau_\alpha - 1} \mathbf{1}_{\set{X_n \in\alpha}} \right) = 1
$$

we obtain $1 = c\pi(\alpha) \overset{\eqref{equation-9}}{=} \mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) \pi(\alpha)$. Hence,

$$
  \pi(\alpha) = \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha))}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

Kac's theorem shows that positivity is a strictly stronger property  than recurrence. For an atom $\alpha$, recurrence is equivalent to $\mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha < \infty) = 1$, meaning that returns to $\alpha$ occur almost surely. In contrast, positivity requires the stronger integrability condition

$$
  \mathbb{E}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha) < \infty
$$

Thus, almost sure finiteness of the return time is necessary but not sufficient for finiteness of its expectation. The latter distinguishes positive recurrence from null recurrence.

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(X_n)$ be $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. If the chain is recurrent, there exists a $\sigma$-finite invariant measure $\pi$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, which is unique up to a multiplicative factor.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Stationary distribution of a positive discrete Markov chain" boxType="example">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be an irreducible Markov chain on a countable state space $\mathsf{X}$ with transition matrix $\mathbf{P} = (P_{xy})_{x,y\in\mathsf{X}}$. If the chain is positive recurrent, the invariant distribution $\pi$ is given by

$$
  \pi_x = \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}_x (\tau_x)},\; x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

where $\tau_x$ is the return time

$$
  \tau_x := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n = x},\; X_0 = x
$$

More precisely, $\mathbb{E}_x (\tau_x)$ is the expected length of an excursion away from state $x$, i.e. the expected inter-arrival time between successive visits to $x$.

**Spectral interpretation**

The stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (\pi_x)_{x\in\mathsf{X}}$ is the unique probability vector satisfying

$$
  \boldsymbol{\pi}^\top \mathbf{P} = \boldsymbol{\pi}^\top
$$

showing that $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is a left eigenvector of $\mathbf{P}$ with corresponding eigenvalue $1$.
</MathBox>

## Representation of invariant probability measures via regeneration

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Harris recurrent, irreducible and aperiodic Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with stationary distribution $\pi$. Assume that the minorization condition $\eqref{equation-1}$ holds on a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, and let $\tau_C$ denote the regeneration time associate with the split-chain construction.

Under these assumptions $\mathbb{E}_\nu (\tau_C) < \infty$ for a minorizing measure $\nu$, and the chain admits a regenerative representation of its invariant measure: there exists a nonnegative integer-valued random variable $T^*$ and a family of random variables $(N_t)_{t\geq 1}$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \pi(A) = \sum_{t=1}^\infty \mathbb{P} (N_t \in A) \mathbb{P} (T^* = t),\; A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
\tag{\label{equation-12}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This representation is obtained by embedding $(X_n)$ into its split-chain extension, which introduces regeneration events at visits to $C$ with probability $\epsilon$.

Let $\tau_C$ be the first regeneration time. Then $T^*$ is given by the tail probabilities of $\tau_C$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbb{P}(T^* = t) = \frac{\mathbf{P}_\nu (\tau_C \geq 1)}{\mathbb{E}_\nu (\tau_C)}
\tag{\label{equation-11}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The random variable $N_t$ represents the state of the chain at the end of regenerative cycle of length $t$, and is defined as follows:
- If $t=1$, then $N_1 \sim\nu$
- If $t \geq 2$, then $N_t$ is distributed as $X_t$ under $\mathbf{P}_\nu$, conditioned on no regeneration before time $t$, i.e. on $\set{\tau_C \geq t}$

Using the mixture representation $\eqref{equation-12}$, simulating $\pi$ can be done in two steps. First, draw the cycle length sampling $T^* \sim\mathbb{P}(T^* = \cdot)$ according to $\eqref{equation-11}$. Then, conditional on $T^* =t$, generate the terminal state $N_t$, which is constructed from the split-chain dynamics as follows.
The chain is initialized from the minorizing measure $\nu$ and then iterated over $t-1$ steps using the residual transition kernel

$$
  \tilde{K}(x,\cdot) = \frac{K(x,\cdot) - \epsilon \mathbf{1}_C (x) \nu(\cdot)}{1 - \epsilon \mathbf{1}_C (x)}
$$

The resulting state $X_{t-1}$ is then distributed as $N_t$.

If direct simulation from $\tilde{K}$ is computationally difficult, we can instead simulate the split chain until a regeneration occurs, i.e. until $\tau_C \geq t$, and accept the resulting path. This is always valid but may be inefficient depending on the regeneration rate.

When the state space $\mathsf{X}$ is finite and the minorization holds globally, the chain is uniformly ergodic and the residual kernel simplifies to

$$
  \tilde{K}(x,y) = \frac{K(x,y) - \epsilon\nu(y)}{1 - \epsilon}
$$

In this case, the regenerative structure reduces to a geometric mixture representation, and the simulation algorithm becomes
1. Sample $X_0 \sim\nu$ and $\omega\sim\operatorname{Geom}(\epsilon)$
2. Iterate $X_{t+1} \sim\tilde{K}(X_t, \cdot)$ for $t=0,\dots,\omega - 1$
3. Output $X_\omega \sim\pi$

## Reversibility

<MathBox title="Reversible Markov chain" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\in\Z}$ be a stationary Markov chain on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with invariant distribution $\pi$ and transition kernel $K$. Then chain is reversible with respect to $\pi$ if it satisfies the detailed balance equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \pi (\d x) K(x, \d y) = \pi(\d y) K(y, \d x),\; \forall x,y \in\mathsf{X}
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

in the sense of equality of measures on $\mathsf{X}\times\mathsf{X}$. Equivalently, in probabilistic terms, reversibility means that the finite-dimensional distributions are invariant under time reversal

$$
  (X_0, X_1, \dots, X_n) \overset{d}{=} (X_n, X_{n-1}, \dots, X_0)
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="3">
Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with transition kernel $K$. Suppose there exists a measure $\pi$ that satisfies the detailed balance equation $\eqref{equation-10}$. Then:
1. $\pi$ is an invariant measure or $K$
2. $(X_n)$ is reversible with respect to $\pi$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

1. **Invariance of $\pi$**

Let $B\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$. By definition of the kernel and using Fubini-Tonelli's theorem, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, B) \pi(\d x) =& \int_\mathsf{X} \int_B K(x,\d y) \pi(\d x) \\
  =& \int_B \int_\mathsf{X} \pi(\d x) K(x, \dy)
\end{align*}
$$

By the detailed balance equation $\eqref{equation-10}$, we can swap $x$ and $y$ in the joint measure

$$
  \pi(\d x) K(x, \d y) = \pi(\d y) K(y, \d x)
$$

Thus

$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, B) \pi(\d x) =& \int_B \int_\mathsf{X} \pi(\d y) K(y, \d x) \\
  =& \int_B
\end{align*}
$$

showing that

$$
  \int_\mathsf{X} K(x, B) \pi(\d x) = \pi(B)
$$

meaning that $\pi$ is invariant.

2. **Reversbility of $(X_n)$**

Define the joint measure on $\mathsf{X}\times\mathsf{X}$ by

$$
  \mu(\d x,\d y) := \pi(\d x) K(x,\d y)
$$

The detailed balance equation $\eqref{equation-10}$ states precisely that $\mu$ is symmetric under coordinate exchange

$$
  \mu(\d x,\d y) = \mu(\d y,\d x)
$$

This symmetry implies that the two-dimensional distribution of consecutive states satisfy

$$
  (X_n, X_{n+1}) \overset{d}{=} (X_{n+1}, X_n)
$$

By extension, all finite-dimensional distributions are invariant under time reversal, i.e.

$$
  (X_0, X_1,\dots,X_n) \overset{d}{=} (X_n, X_{n-1},\dots,X_0)
$$

which is exactly the definition of reversibility.
</details>
</MathBox>

# Ergodicity and convergence

<MathBox title="Ergodic atom" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with transition kernel $K$ and invariant probability measure $\pi$. An atom $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is ergodic if

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} K^n (\alpha, \alpha) = \pi(\alpha)
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Total variation norm" boxType="definition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ be a measurable space. The total variation distance between two $\sigma$-finite measures $\mu_1, \mu_2$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ is defined by

$$
  \norm{\mu_1 - \mu_2}_\text{TV} =: \sup_{A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})} |\mu_1 (A) - \mu_2 (A)|
$$

For a $\sigma$-finite measure $\mu$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, its total variation measure $|\mu|$ is defined via the Hahn-Jordan decomposition, and the total variation norm is

$$
  \norm{\mu}_\text{TV} := |\mu|(\mathsf{X})
$$

This norm admits the dual representation

$$
  \norm{\mu}_\text{TV} = \sup\Set{\left\lvert \int_\mathsf{X} h\;\d\mu \right\rvert : h \in L^\infty (\mathsf{X}), \norm{h}_\infty \leq 1 }
$$

In terms of this representation, the total variation distance between two $\sigma$-finite measures $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \norm{\mu}_\text{TV} = \frac{1}{2} \sup_{\norm{h}_\infty \leq 1} \left|\int_\mathsf{X} h(x) \mu(\d x) \right|
\tag{\label{equation-20}}
\end{equation*}
$$


If $\mathsf{X}$ is countable, the total variation norm reduces to the sum

$$
  \norm{\mu_1 - \mu_2}_\text{TV} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} |\mu_1 (y) - \mu_2 (y)|
$$

where the supremum is taken over all bounded measurable functions $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ with $\norm{h}_\infty \leq 1$. 

Let $h:\mathsf{X}\to [0,\infty)$ be a measurable function. We can define the weighted variation seminorm

$$
  \norm{h}_h := \sup\Set{\left\lvert \int_\mathsf{X} g\;\d\mu \right\rvert : |g(x)| \leq h(x),\forall x\in\mathsf{X}}
$$

Equivalently

$$
  \norm{\mu}_h = \int_\mathsf{X} h \;\d|\mu|
$$
</MathBox>

Let $(\mu_n)_{n\geq 1}$ and $\mu$ be finite measures on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. If

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{\mu_n - \mu}_\text{TV} = 0
$$

then for every bounded measurable function $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$

$$
  \left| \int h \;\d\mu_n - \int h\;\d\mu \right| \leq \norm{h}_\infty \norm{\mu_n - \mu}_\text{TV} \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0
$$

In particular, if $X_n \sim\mu_n$ and $X \sim\mu$, then

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\mathbb{E}_\mu [h(X_n)] - \mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)]\right|
$$

for every bounded function $h$. Thus, convergence in total variation implies convergence of expectations for all bounded measurable functions.

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="4">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a countable state space $\mathsf{X}$, with transition kernel $K$ and invariant probability measure $\pi$. If there exists an ergodic atom $\alpha\subseteq\mathsf{X}$, then for every $x\in\mathsf{X}$

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{K^n (x, \cdot) - \pi}_\text{TV} = 0
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $\tau_\alpha := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in\alpha}$ denote the first return (or entrance) time to $\alpha$.

Using the strong Markov property at $\tau_\alpha$, we can decompose $K^n$ for any $x,y \in\mathsf{X}$ and $n\geq 1$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  K^n (x,y) =& \mathbb{P}_x (X_n = y, \tau_\alpha \geq n) \\
  &+ \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha = j) K^{n-j} (\alpha, y) \\
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-14}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where, by the atom property, $K^m (z, \cdot) = K^m (\alpha,\cdot)$ for all $z\in\alpha$.

From $\eqref{equation-8}$, we can express $\pi$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \pi(y) = \pi(\alpha) \sum_{j=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_j = y, \tau_\alpha \geq j)
\tag{\label{equation-15}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The total variation distance of $K^n$ and $\pi$ is given by

$$
  \norm{K^n (x,\cdot) - \pi}_\text{TV} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} |K^n (x,y) - \pi(y)|
$$

Substituting $\eqref{equation-14}$ and $\eqref{equation-15}$, and applying the triangle inequality yield

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \mathbb{P}_x (X_n = y, \tau_\alpha \geq n) \leq& \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \mathbb{P}_x (X_n = y, \tau_\alpha > n) \\
  &+ \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \left|\sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha = j) K^{n-j} (\alpha, y) - \pi(\alpha) \sum_{j=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_j = y, \tau_\alpha \geq j) \right|
\end{align*}
$$

Next, we show that each term in this decomposition vanishes as $n\to\infty$. For the first term, note that

$$
  \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \mathbb{P}_x (X_n = y, \tau_\alpha > n) = \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha > n)
$$

Since the chain is Harris reccurent, it follows that 

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha > n)  = 0
$$

Define the tail of the regeneration series

$$
  R_n (y) := \pi(\alpha) \sum_{j=n+1}^\infty \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_j = y, \tau_\alpha \geq j)
$$

Since the series is finite by positive recurrence, it follows that

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_y R_n (y) = 0
$$

It remains to verify the convergence of

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \left|\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha = 1) K^{n-j} (\alpha, y) - \pi(\alpha) \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_j = y, \tau_\alpha \geq j) \right|
\tag{\label{equation-16}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Introduce $a_m := K^m (\alpha, \alpha) - \pi(\alpha)$. By ergodicity of the atom, it follows that $\lim_{m\to\infty} a_m = 0$. Using the atom property and summing over $y$, then $\eqref{equation-16}$ reduces to a convolution-type expression

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha = j) a_{n-j}
\tag{\label{equation-17}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $(\mathbb{P}_x (\tau_\alpha = j))_{j\geq 1}$ is a probability mass function and $a_m$ is bounded, it follows that $\eqref{equation-17}$ converges to $0$ as $n\to\infty$.

We have established that all contributions vanish as $n\to\infty$, therefore

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{K^n (x,\cdot) - \pi}_\text{TV} = 0
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a countable state space $\mathsf{X}$, and suppose $\alpha\subseteq\mathsf{X}$ is an atom. Convergence to stationarity can be proved via a coupling argument based on regeneration at $\alpha$.

Let $(X_n)$ and $(X'_n)$ be two Markov chains sharing the same transition kernel $K$, started from initial distributions $\mu$ and $\nu$, respectively. Define the coupling time

$$
  T := \inf\set{n\geq 0 : X_n \in\alpha \land X'_n \in\alpha}
$$

By the atom property, once both chains enter $\alpha$ at the same time, they can be perfectly coupled from that time onward (i.e. forced to evolve identically), since the transition kernel is the same from every point in $\alpha$. Hence

$$
\begin{align*}
  X_T =& X'_T \in\alpha \\
  \implies (X_{T+k})_{k\geq 0} \deq& (X'_{T+k})_{k\geq 0}
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, if $\mathbb{P}(T < \infty) = 1$, the two chains eventually coalesce almost surely. If $\pi$ is an invariant probability measure on $\mathsf{X}$, this implies convergence in total variation

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{K^n (x, \cdot) - \pi}_\text{TV} = 0
$$

Define the successive return times to $\alpha$ by $\tau_\alpha (0) := 0$ and

$$
  \tau_\alpha (k) := \inf\set{n > \tau_\alpha (k - 1) : X_n \in\alpha},\; k\geq 1
$$

The excursion lengths

$$
  S_k := \tau_\alpha (k + 1) - \tau_\alpha (k),\; k\geq 0
$$

form an independent and identically distributed sequence under $\mathbb{P}_\alpha$, with common distribution $p$, by the strong Markov property and the atom property.

Let
- $p(n) := \mathbb{P}_\alpha (S_1 = n)$ for $n\geq 1$
- $q(n) := \mathbb{P}_\mu (\tau_\alpha (1) = n)$
- $r(n) := \mathbb{P}_\nu (\tau_\alpha (1) = n)$

Then, for $k\geq 1$

$$
  \tau_\alpha (k + 1) = \tau_\alpha (1) + \sum_{j=1}^k
$$

and therefore the distribution of $\tau_\alpha (k + 1)$ is the convolution $q * p^{*k}$.

We introduce the renewal indicator processes

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z_q (n) =& \sum_{k=0}^n \mathbf{1}_{\tau_\alpha (k) = n} \\
  Z_r (n) =& \sum_{k=0}^n \mathbf{1}_{\tau'_\alpha (k) n}
\end{align*}
$$

which correspond to the events that the chains $(X_n)$ and $(X'_n)$ visit $\alpha$ at time $n$. The coupling time is then given by

$$
  T_{q,r} = \min\set{n \geq 0 : Z_q (n) = Z_r (n) = 1}
$$

which satisfies Lemma $\ref{lemma-1}$.

<MathBox title="" boxType="lemma" tag="1">
Let $p = (p(n))_{n\geq 1}$ be a probability distribution on $\N_+$ satisfying
1. **Finite mean:**

$$
  m_p := \sum_{n=1}^\infty n p(n) < \infty
$$

2. **Aperiodicity:** $\gcd\set{n \geq 1 : p(n) > 0} = 1$

Let $q$ be any probability distribution on $\N_0$. Consider two independent renewal processes
1. $(\tau_n)_{n\geq 0}$ with interarrival law $p$ and initial delay $q$
2. $(\tau'_n)_{n\geq 0}$ with interarrival law $p$ and initial delay $r$

\end{align*}

**Recurrence of $V_n^+$**

From $\eqref{equation-18}$ it follows that the chain $(V_n^+)$ almost surely hits $1$ in exactly $j - 1$ steps, starting from any state $j > 2$. Consequently

$$
  \mathbb{P}_j (\tau_1 < \infty) = 1

where $\tau_1 = \inf\set{n\geq 0 : V_n^+ = 1}$.

At state $j = 1$ the chain $(V_n^+)$ regenerates with distribution $p$. Since $p$ is supported on $\N_+$, the state $j = 1$ is recurrent, and therefore the whole chain is recurrent.

**Invariant measure**

Define the probability measure

$$
  \pi(j) := \sum_{n\geq j} p(n)
$$

Then $\pi$ is invariant since

$$
\begin{align*}

  =& \sum_{n\geq j} p(n) = \pi(j)
\end{align*}

Moreover, $\pi$ is finite since

$$
\begin{align*}

  =& \sum_{n\geq 1} np(n) = m_p < \infinity
\end{align*}

showing that $V_n^+$ is positive recurrent.

- then use jumps of size $r$ and $s$ to align both coordinates modulo $1$

## Geometric convergence

<MathBox title="Geometric ergodicity" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, with transition kernel $K$ and invariant probability measure $\pi$. Suppose $h:\mathsf{X}\to [1,\infty)$ be a measurable function. The chain $(X_n)$ is geometrically $h$-ergodic if 

1. $h$ is $\pi$-integrable

$$
  \mathbb{E}^\pi [h] = \int_\mathsf{X} h(x) \pi(\d x) < \infty
$$

2. there exists $r_h > 1$ such that for every $x\in\mathsf{X}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty r_h^n \norm{K^n (x, \cdot) - \pi}_h < \infty
\tag{\label{equation-22}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The case $h = 1$ corresponds to the geometric ergodicity of $(X_n)$.
</MathBox>

Geometric $h$-ergodicity means that $\norm{K^n (x,\cdot) - \pi}_h$ is decreasing at least at a geometric speed, since $\eqref{equation-22}$ implies

$$
  \norm{K^n (x, \cdot) - \pi}_h \leq M r_h^{-n}
$$

with

$$
  M = \sum_{n=1}^\infty r_h^n \norm{K^n (x,\cdot) - \pi}_h
$$

If the chain $(X_n)$ has an atom $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, then $\eqref{equation-22}$ implies that for a real number $r > 1$

$$
  \mathbb{E}_x \left[\sum_{n=1}^{\tau_\alpha} h(X_n) r^n \right] < \infty
$$

and

$$
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty |\mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_\alpha \in\alpha) - \pi(\alpha)| r^n < \infty
$$

where $\tau_\alpha := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n \in\alpha}$ is the first return time to $\alpha$.

The series associated with $|\mathbb{P}_\alpha (X_n \in\alpha) - \pi(\alpha)| r^n$ converges outside of the unit circle of the power series associated with $\mathbb{P}_\alpha (\tau_\alpha = n)$ converges for values of $|r|$ strictly larger than $1$.

### Kendall condition

<MathBox title="Kendall set and Kendall atom" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, and let $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$. Define the first return time to $A$ by $\tau_A := \inf\set{n\geq 1 : X_n \in A}$. The set $A$ is a Kendall set if there exists a constant $\kappa > 1$ such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \sup_{x\in A} \mathbb{E}_x \left[\sum_{k=0}^{\tau_A - 1} \kappa^k \right] < \infty
\tag{\label{equation-24}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Equivalently, using the identity

$$
  \sum_{k=0}^{\tau_A - 1} \kappa^k = \frac{\kappa^{\tau_A} - 1}{\kappa - 1}
$$

the condition $\eqref{equation-24}$ can be written

$$
  \sup_{x\in A} \mathbb{E}_x [\kappa^{\tau_A}] < \infty
$$

Similarly, an atom $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ is a Kendall atom if there exists a constant $\kappa > 1$ such that

$$
  \mathbb{E}_\alpha [\kappa^{\tau_\alpha}] < \infty
$$

where $\tau_A := \inf\set{n \geq 1 : X_n \in\alpha}$.
</MathBox>

  \norm{K^n (x,\cdot) - \pi(\cdot)}_\text{TV} \leq C V(x) \rho^n,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}, n\geq 1
$$
</MathBox>

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with transition kernel $K$. The whole space $\mathsf{X}$ is small if there exists an integer $m\geq 1$, a constant $\epsilon > 0$, and a probability measure $\nu$ on $(\mathsf{X},\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ such that

$$
  K^m (x, A) \geq \epsilon\nu (A),\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}, A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})
$$
</MathBox>

Suppose $\mathsf{X}$ be a finite state space and let $\mathbf{P} = (p_{x,y})_{x,y\in\mathsf{X}}$ be a transition matrix. Define

$$
  \rho_y := \inf_{x\in\mathsf{X}} p_{x,y},\; y\in\mathsf{X}
$$

If $\sum_{y\in\mathsf{X}} \rho_y > 0$, define a probability measure $\nu$ by

$$
  \nu(y) := \frac{\rho_y}{\sum_{z\in\mathsf{X}} \rho_z}
$$

Then for all $x, y\in\mathsf{X}$

$$
  p_{x,y} \geq \rho_y = \left(\sum_{z\in\mathsf{X}} \rho_z \right) \nu(y)
$$

showing that the Doeblin condition holds with $m = 1$ and

$$
  \epsilon := \sum_{z\in\mathsf{X}} \rho_z > 0
$$

## Covariance in Markov chains

Let $(X_n)$ be a stationary ergodic Markov chain with zero mean, $\mathbb{E}(X_0) = 0$ and finite variance, $\mathbb{E}(X_0^2) < \infty$. Define the sample mean

$$
  \bar{X}_n := \frac{1}{n+1} \sum_{i=0}^n X_i
$$

By stationarity, the variance of $\bar{X}_n$ is given by

$$
  \operatorname{var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{(n+1)^2} \sum_{i=0}^n \sum_{j=0}^n \operatorname{cov}(X_i, X_j)
$$

Rewriting in terms of lags gives

$$
  \operatorname{var}\left(\bar{X_n} \right) = \frac{1}{n+1} \operatorname{var}(X_0) + \frac{2}{n+1} \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{n-k+1}{n+1} \operatorname{cov}(X_0, X_k)
$$

If the cumulative covariance is finite

  \sum_{k=\infty} |\operatorname{cov}(X_0, X_k)| < \infty

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition" tag="6">

If every bounded harmonic functions is constant functions, then the chain is Harris recurrent, i.e. for all $A \in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\pi(A) > 0$

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = 1,\; \forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

where $\eta_A = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_A (X_n)$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Fix a measurable set $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $\pi(A) > 0$, and define

$$
  h(x) := \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty)
$$

Since $h\in [0,1]$, it follows that $h$ is bounded. Using the Markov property yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}_x [h(X_1)] =& \mathbb{E}_x [P_{X_1} (\eta_A = \infty)] \\
  =& \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) \\
  =& h(x)
\end{align*}
$$

showing that $h$ is harmonic.

By hypothesis, every bounded harmonic function is constant, hence $h(x) \equiv c$ for some $c\in [0,1]$.

We proceed by showing that $h$ obeys a 0-1 law, i.e. $h(x) \in\set{0,1}$. The event $\set{\eta_A = \infty}$ is a tail event, i.e. it belongs to the tail $\sigma$-algebra

$$
  \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \sigma(X_n, X_{n+1},\dots)
$$

For a $\psi$-irreducible positive Markov chain, the stationary process under $\mathbb{P}_\pi$ is ergodic, and therefore its tail $\sigma$-algebra is trivial. Thus, $\mathbb{P}_\pi (\eta_A = \infty) \in\set{0,1}$. Since $h(x) \equiv c$, we have

$$
  c = \int h(x) \pi(\d x) = \mathbb{P}_\pi (\eta_A = \infty)
$$

so $c\in\set{0,1}$.

Assume for contradiction that $c = 0$, i.e. $\mathbb{P}_x (\eta = \infty) = 0$ for all $x\in\mathsf{X}$. Then $\eta_A < \infty$ almost surely under $\mathbb{P}_\pi$, so

$$
  \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \mathbf{1}_A (X_n) \eqas 0
$$

However, since the chain is positive and $\pi$ is invariant, the ergodic theorem for Markov chain yields

$$
  \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \mathbf{1}_A (X_n) \eqas \pi(A)
$$

Because $\pi(A) > 0$, this is a contradiction. Hence, $c = 1$ such that

$$
  \mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = 1,\;\forall x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

which is presicely Harris recurrence.
</details>
</MathBox>

Proposition $\ref{proposition-6}$ can be interpreted as a continuity property of the transition operator $Kh(x) := \mathbb{E}_x [h(X_1)]$ in the following sense. If $h$ is harmonic, then by iteration of the Markov property, it satisfies $h(x) = \mathbb{E}_x [h(X_n)]$ for all $n\geq 1$. Proposition $\ref{proposition-7}$ implies $h(x)$ is almost surely equal to $\mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)]$, meaning $h$ is constant $\pi$-almost everywhere. For Harris recurrent Markov chains, Proposition $\ref{proposition-6}$ states that this implies $h(x)$ is constant everywhere.

<MathBox title="Ergodic theorem" boxType="theorem">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, with a $\sigma$-finite invariant measure $\pi$. Define the partial sum process $(S_n)$ by

$$
  S_n (h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n h(X_i)
$$

Then the following statements are equivalent:
1. If $f,g \in L^1 (\pi)$ with $\int g(x) \;\d\pi (x) \neq 0$, then under $\mathbb{P}_x$
$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{S_n (f)}{S_n (g)} \eqas \frac{\int f(x) \;\d\pi(x)}{\int g(x)\;\d\pi (x)}
$$

2. The chain $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1) $\implies$ (2)**

Let $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$ with $0 < \pi(A) < \infty$ and take $f = \mathbf{1}_A$ to be the indicator function of $A$ with finite measure, and $g\in L^1 (\pi)$ an arbitrary function with finite and positive integral. The by assumption

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{S_n (\mathbf{1}_A)}{S_n (g)} \eqas \frac{\pi(A)}{\int g \;\d\pi} > 0
$$

Since $S_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s}}{n\to\infty} \infty$, it follows that $S_n (\mathbf{1}_A) \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}{n\to\infty} \infty$. Thus, $\mathbb{P}_x (\eta_A = \infty) = 1$ for every $x\in\mathsf{X}$, where $\eta_A := \sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_A (X_n)$. This establishes Harris recurrence.

**(2) $\implies$ (1)**

Assume $(X_n)$ is Harris recurrent. By Nummelin splitting, there exists an augmented Markov chain possessing an accessible atom $\alpha\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$, such that excursions from $\alpha$ form independent and identically distributed cycles. It suffices to prove the result for this split chain, since the ratio limit transfers back to the original chain.

Let $\tau_\alpha (k)$ be the time of the $(k+1)$th visit to $\alpha$, and define the regeneration blocks

$$
  S_j (f) := \sum_{n=\tau_\alpha (j) + 1}^{\tau_\alpha (j+1)} f(X_n),\; j\geq 0
$$

Then $(S_j (f), S_j (g))_{j\geq 0}$ are independent and identically distributed random vectors with finite expectations

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}_\alpha [S_0 (f)] =& \int_\mathsf{X} f\;\d\pi \\
  \mathbb{E}_\alpha [S_0 (g)] =& \int_\mathsf{X} g\;\d\pi \\
\end{align*}
$$

Let $\ell_n := \max\set{k : \tau_\alpha (k) \leq n}$ be the number of visits to $\alpha$ at time $n$. Then

$$
  S_n (f) = \sum_{j=0}^{\ell_n} = \sum_{j=0}^{\ell_n - 1} S_j (f) + R_n (f)
$$

where $R_n (f)$ is a remaineder term corresponding to the incomlete excursion cycle from $\alpha$. An analogous decomposition holds for $S_n (g)$. It follows that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{R_n (f)}{\ell_n} \eqas& 0 \\
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{R_n (g)}{\ell_n} \eqas& 0
\end{align*}
$$

By the strong law of large numbers for independent and identically distributed sequences

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\ell_n} \sum_{j=0}^{\ell_n - 1} S_j (f) \eqas \mathbb{E}_\alpha [S_0 (f)] = \int_\mathsf{X} f\;\d\pi \\
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\ell_n} \sum_{j=0}^{\ell_n - 1} S_j (g) \eqas \mathbb{E}_\alpha [S_0 (g)] = \int_\mathsf{X} g\;\d\pi
\end{align*}
$$

Hence,

$$
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{S_n (f)}{S_n (g)} \eqas \frac{\int_\mathsf{X} f\;\d\pi}{\int_\mathsf{X} g\;\d\pi}
$$

## Central limit theorems

### Discrete case

<MathBox title="Central limit theorem via regeneration" boxType="theorem">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a countable state space $\mathsf{X}$, with invariant probability measure $\pi$. Assume there exists an accessible atom $\alpha\subseteq\mathsf{X}$, and define the return times to $\alpha$ by $\tau_\alpha (0) = 0$ and

$$
  \tau_\alpha (k + 1) := \inf\set{n > \tau_\alpha (k) : X_n \in\alpha}
$$

such that $\mathbb{E}_\alpha [\tau_\alpha (1)^2] < \infty$. Let $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ satisfy

$$
  \mathbb{E}_\alpha \left[\left(\sum_{n=1}^{\tau_\alpha} |h(X_n)| \right)^2 \right] < \infty
$$

Define the centered function $\bar{h} := h - \pi(h)$ and the asymptotic variance

$$
  \gamma_h^2 = \pi(\alpha) \mathbb{E}_\alpha \left[\left(\sum_{n=1}^{\tau_\alpha} \bar{h}(X_n) \right)^2 \right] > 0
$$

If $\gamma_h^2 > 0$, then

$$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{n=1}^n \bar{h} (X_n) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\gamma_h^2)
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Define the partial sum process $(S_n)$ by

$$
  S_n (h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n h(X_i)
$$

and define the regeneration blocks

$$
  S_k (\bar{h}) := \sum_{n=\tau_\alpha (k) + 1}^{\tau_\alpha (k+1)} \bar{h}(X_n),\; k\geq 0
$$

By the strong Markov property and the atomic structure, the sequence $\set{S_k (\bar{h})}_{k\geq 0}$ is independent and identically distributed under $\mathbb{P}_\alpha$ with $\mathbb{E}_\alpha [S_0 (\bar{h})] = 0$ and $\operatorname{var}_\alpha [S_0 (\bar{h})] < \infty$.

Let $\ell_N := \max\set{k : \tau_\alpha (k) \leq N}$ be the number of completed regeneration cycles up to time $N$. Then

$$
  \sum_{n=1}^N \bar{h} (X_n) = \sum_{k=0}^{\ell_N - 1} S_k (\bar{h}) + R_N
$$

where the remainder satisfies

$$
  R_N = \sum_{n=\tau_\alpha (\ell_N) + 1}^N \bar{h}(X_n)
$$

By the classical central limit theorem

$$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{\ell_N}} \sum_{k=0}^{\ell_N - 1} S_k (\bar{h}) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\operatorname{var}_\alpha [S_0 (\bar{h})])
$$

Applying the strong law of large number to cycle lengths yields

$$
  \frac{\ell_N}{N} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \pi(\alpha) \iff \frac{N}{\ell_N} \xrightarrow{a.s.} \frac{1}{\pi(\alpha)}
$$

Using the bound

$$
  |R_N| \leq \sum_{n=\tau_\alpha (\ell_N) + 1}^{\tau_\alpha (\ell_N + 1)} |\bar{h}(X_n)|
$$

we obtain $|R_N| \leq S_{\ell_N} (|\bar{h}|)$, where the right-hand side is an independent and identically distributed sequence with finite second moment. Thus, by standard moment estimates

$$
  \frac{R_N}{\sqrt{N}} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} 0
$$

Combining the above, we obtain

$$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{n=1}^N \bar{h}(X_n) = \sqrt{\frac{\ell_N}{N}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\ell_N}} \sum_{k=0}^{\ell_N + 1} S_k (\bar{h}) + o(1)
$$

Since $\ell_N / N \to\pi(\alpha)$, Slutsky's theorem yields

$$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{n=1}^N \bar{h}(X_n) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \pi(\alpha) \operatorname{var}_\alpha [S_0 (\bar{h})])
$$

which is precisely $\mathcal{N}(0,\gamma_h^2)$.
</details>
</MathBox>

### Geometric ergodicity and regeneration

<MathBox title="Central limit theorem for geometrically ergodic Markov chains" boxType="theorem">
Let $(X_n)$ be a $\psi$-irreducible, positive Harris recurrent, aperiodic Markov chain with invariant probability measure $\pi$. Suppose $(X_n)$ is geometrically ergodic and let $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ satisfy $\mathbb{E}^\pi [|h(X)|^{2 + \epsilon}] < \infty$ for some $\epsilon > 0$. Define the centered function $\bar{h} := h - \pi(h)$ and the partial sum process $(S_n)$ by

$$
  S_n (h) := \sum_{k=1}^N h(X_k)
$$

Then, under $\mathbb{P}_\pi$

$$
  \sqrt{n} \left(\frac{S_n (h)}{n} - \pi(h)] \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{k=1}^n \bar{h}(X_k) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \gamma_h^2)
$$

where the asymptotic variance $0 \leq \gamma_h^2 < \infty$ is given by

$$
  \gamma_g^2 := \mathbb{E}_\pi [\bar{g}^2 (X_0)] + 2\sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathbb{E}_\pi [\bar{g}(X_0) \bar{g}(X_k)]
$$
</MathBox>

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$. Assume the Markov kernel $P$ satisfies a minorization condition: there exists a measurable function $s:\mathsf{X}\to (0,1]$ and a probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ such that for all $x\in\mathsf{X}$ and all $A\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$

$$
  P(x, A) \geq s(x) \mathbb{Q}(A)
$$

This allows a Nummelin splitting construction, yielding an augmented Markov chain with regeneration structure.

Let $0 = \tau_0 < \tau_1 < \cdots$ be the regeneration times by the split chain. For simplicity, assume the chain is started from the regeneration distribution $X_0 \sim\mathbb{Q}$, so the regeneration blocks are independent and identically distributed. Define the inter-regeneration lengths $N_t := \tau_t - \tau_{t-1}$, and for a measurable function $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ define the regeneration blocks

$$
  \tilde{S}_t := \sum_{j=\tau_{t-1}}^{\tau_t - 1} h(X_j)
$$

Then the sequence $(N_t, \tilde{S}_t)_{t\geq 1}$ is independent and identically distributed under $\mathbb{P}_\mathbb{Q}$. Moreover, for $T\geq 1$

$$
  S_{\tau_T} (h) = \sum_{j=0}^{\tau_T - 1} h(X_j) = \sum_{t=1}^T \tilde{S}_t
$$

Define the block averages

$$
\begin{align*}
  \bar{h}_{\tau_T} :=& \frac{1}{\tau_T} h(X_j) \\
  =& \frac{\sum_{t=1}^T \tilde{S}_t}{\sum_{t=1}^T N_t}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $(N_t, \tilde{S}_t)$ are independent and identically distributed, the strong law of large numbers gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T N_t \eqas \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} [N_1] \\
  \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \tilde{S}_t \eqas \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} [\tilde{S}_1]
\end{align*}
$$

provided the expectations are finite. Thus,

$$
  \lim_{T\to\infty} \bar{h}_{\tau_T} \eqas \frac{\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (\tilde{S}_1)}{\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (N_1)}
$$

Under stationarity, we have

$$
  \frac{\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (\tilde{S}_1)}{\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (N_1)} = \mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)]
$$

Moreover, standard regeneration theory yields the identity

$$
  \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (N_1) = \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}^\pi (s(X))} < \infty
$$

Consequently, $\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (\tilde{S}_1) = \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (N_1) \mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)]$. In particular $\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (|\tilde{S}_1|) < \infty$.

Define the centered block sums $\tilde{S}_t^\circ := \tilde{S}_t - N_t \mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)]$. Then
- $(\tilde{S}_t^\circ, N_t)_{t\geq 1}$ are independent and identically distributed
- $\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q} (\tilde{S}_1^\circ) = 0$

The regenerative decomposition becomes

$$
  S_{\tau_T} (h) - \tau_T \mathbb{E}^\pi [h(X)] = \sum_{t=1}^T \tilde{S}_t^\circ
$$

Thus, the centered regeneration increments form an independent and identically distributed sequence with zero mean.

Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$, satisfying a minorization condition. ### Drift condition

There exist alternative version of the central limit theorem based on drift conditions. Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a $\psi$-irreducible positive Harris recurrent Markov chain on a measurable space $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X}))$ with invariant distribution $\pi$. Assume there exists
- measurable functions $f, V:\mathsf{X}\to [1,\infty)1$
- a small set $C\in\mathcal{B}(\mathsf{X})$
- a constant $b < \infty$

such that the Foster-Lyapunov drift condition holds

$$
  \Delta V (x) \leq -f(x) + b\mathbf{1}_C (x)\; x\in\mathsf{X}
$$

Assume further that $\mathbb{E}^\pi (V^2) < \infty$. Let $g:\mathsf{X} \to\R$ be a measurable function such that $|g(x)| \leq f(x)$. Define the centered function $\bar{g} := g - \pi(g)$ and the partial sums

$$
  S_n (\bar{g}) := \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \bar{g} (X_k)
$$

Then, the asymptotic variance exists with limit

$$
\begin{align*}
  \gamma_g^2 =& \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} \mathbb{E}_\pi [S_n^2 (\bar{g})] \\
  =& \mathbb{E}_\pi [\bar{g}^2 (x_0)] + 2\sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathbb{E}_\pi [\bar{g} (x_0) \bar{g} (x_k)]
\end{align*}
$$

If $\gamma_g^2 > 0$, the central limit theorem holds for $S_n (\bar{g})$, i.e.

$$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} S_n (\bar{g}) \xrightarrow{\text{d}}{n\to\infty} \mathcal{N}(0, \gamma_g^2)
$$

If $\gamma_g = 0$, then $\sqrt{n} S_n (\bar{g})$ almost surely goes to $0$ and $S_n (\bar{g}) = o(\sqrt{n})$ in $L^2$ and in probability.

### Mixing condition

<MathBox title="Mixing process" boxType="definition">
Let $(X_n)_{n\geq 0}$ be a stochastic process on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$. For $k \leq l$, define the $\sigma$-algebra

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{F}_k^l :=& \sigma(X_k, X_{k+1}, \dots, X_l) \\
  \mathcal{F}_k^\infty :=& \sigma(X_k, X_{k+1},\dots)
\end{align*}
$$

The $\alpha$-mixing coefficients are defined by

$$
  \alpha_n =: \sup_{k\geq 0} \sup_{\stackrel{A \in\mathcal{F}_0^k \\ B\in\mathcal{F}_{k+n}^\infty}} |\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) - \mathbb{P}(A) \mathbb{P}(B)|
$$

If $(X_n)$ is strictly stationary, this reduces to

$$
  \alpha_n =: \sup_{\stackrel{A \in\sigma(X_0, X_1,\dots) \\ B\in\sigma(X_n, X_{n+1},\dots)}} |\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) - \mathbb{P}(A) \mathbb{P}(B)|
$$


The process $(X_n)$ is $\alpha$-mixing if $\lim_{n\to\infty} \alpha_n = 0$.
</MathBox>

$$
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty \alpha_n^{\delta/(2+\delta)} < \infty

$$
  \sigma^2 := \lim_{n\to\infty} n \operatorname{var}(\bar{X}_n) < \infty

where $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=1}^n X_k$ is the sample mean. If $\sigma^2 > 0$, then $\sqrt{N} \bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$.

# Examples and applications

## Recurrence relations and differential equations

Markov processes can be viewed as stochastic counterparts of deterministic recurrence relations (discrete time) and differential equations (continuous time)

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_n }_{n\in\N}$ is a stochastic process with state space $(S,\mathcal{S})$ satisfying the recurrence relation

$$
  X_{n+1} = g(X_n),\; n\in\N
$$

where $g:S\to S$ is measurable. Then $\mathbf{X}$ is a homogenous Markov process with one-step transition operator $P$ given by $Pf = g\circ g$ for a measurable function $f:S\to\R$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Clearly $\mathbf{X}$ is determined by the initial state, and in fact $X_n = g^n (X_0)$ for $n\in\N$ where $g^n$ is the $n$-fold composition power of $g$. Therefore, the only possible source of randomness is in the initial state. The Markov and time homogenous properties simply follow from the trivial fact that $g^{m+n}(X_0) = g^n[g^m(X_0)]$ so that $X_{m+n} = g^n(X_m)$. That is, the state at time $m+n$ is completely determined by the state at time $m$ (regardless of the previous states) and the time increment $n$. In particular, $Pf(x) = \mathbb{E}[g(X_1)\mid X_0 = x] = f[g(x)]$ for measurable $f:S\to\R$ and $x\in S$. Note that for $n\in\N$, the $n$-step transition operator is given by $P^n f = f\circ g^n$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Recurrence relation' boxType="proposition">
Suppose $\mathbf{X} = \Set{X_n }_{t\geq 0}$ is a stochastic process with state space $(\R,\mathcal{R})$ satisfying the first-order differential equation

$$
  \frac{\d}{\d t}X_t = g(X_t)
$$

where $g:\R\to\R$ is Lipschitz continuous. Then $\mathbf{X}$ is a Feller Markov process.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Lipschitz continuity means that there exists a constant $k\in(0,\infty)$ such that $|g(y) - g(x)|\leq k|x-y|$ for $x,y\in\R$. This is a standard condition on $g$ that guarantees the existence of a solution to the differential equation on $[0,\infty)$. Therefore, the only source of randomness in the process comes from the initial value $X_0$. Let $t\mapsto X_t(x)$ denote the unique solution with $X_0(x) = x$ for $x\in\R$. The Markov and time homogeneous properties follow from the fact that $X_{t+s}(x) = X_t(X_s(x))$ for $x,t\in[0,\infty)$ and $x\in S$. That is, the state at time $t+s$ depends only on the state at time $s$ and the time increment $t$. The Feller properties follow from the continuity of $t\mapsto X_t(x)$ and the continuity of $x\mapsto X_t(x)$. The latter is the continuous dependence on the initial value, guaranteed by the assumption on $g$. Note that the transition operator is given by $P_t f(x) = f[X_t (x)]$ for a measurable function $f:S\to\R$ and $x\in S$.
</details>
</MathBox>

The deterministic process $\d X_t = g(X_t)\d t$ can be turned into a diffusion processs, with Markov property, by adding a stochastic term related to a Wiener process (Brownian motion).
