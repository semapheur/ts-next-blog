# Test functions (bump functions)

<MathBox title='Partial derivative' boxType='definition'>
Let $\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n) \in\N_0^n$ be a multiindex of order $n$ with length $|\alpha| = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i$. For $\alpha, \beta \in \N_0^n$ and $\mathbf{x}\in\R^n$, we define the following notations

- $\alpha \leq \beta$ if $\alpha_i \leq \beta_i$ for $i=1,\dots,n$
- $\binom{\beta}{\alpha} = \frac{\beta!}{\alpha! (\beta - \alpha)!}$, where $\alpha! = \prod_{i=1}^n \alpha_i!$
- $\mathbf{x}^\alpha = \prod_{i=1}^n x_i^{\alpha_i}$

We denote by $\D^\alpha f$ the partial derivative of order $|\alpha|$ of a function $f:\Omega\subset\R^n \to\Gamma$, defined as

$$
  \D^\alpha f = \frac{\partial^{|\alpha|}}{\partial x_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots\partial x_n^{\alpha_n}} f
$$

where $\D^\alpha = \D_1^{\alpha_1} \cdot D_n^{\alpha_n}$ and $\D_j = \partial/\partial x_j$ for $j = 1,\dots,n$. If the function $f$ has continuous partial derivative up to the order $|\alpha + \beta|$ inclusively, then

$$
  \D^{\alpha + \beta} = \D^\alpha (\D^\beta f) = \D^\beta (\D^\alpha f)
$$

We denote by $C^m (\Omega)$ the of functions $f:\Omega\subset\R^n \to\Gamma$ with continuous derivative of order $m$, that is, $\D^\alpha f$ is continuous on $\Omega$ for every $\alpha$ with $|\alpha| \leq m$. When $m = 0$, we have the set $C^0 (\Omega)$ of continuous functions on $\Omega$. The set of functions on $\Omega$ with continuous derivatives of all orders is denoted

$$
  C^\infty (\Omega) := \bigcap_{k\in\N} C^k (\Omega)
$$

and is called the space of smooth functions. Clearly, we have $C^\infty (\Omega) \subset C^m (\Omega) = C^0 (\Omega)$ for all $m > 0$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Partial derivative' boxType='proposition' tag='proposition-1'>
For $f,g \in C^\p (\Omega)$ with $\Omega\subset\R^n$, then $fg \in C^p (\Omega)$ and we have

$$
\begin{equation}
  \D^\alpha (f\cdot g) = \sum_{\beta+\gamma} \frac{\alpha!}{\beta! \gamma!} \D^\beta f \cdot \D^\gamma g, \; \D^\alpha = \prod_{i=1}^n D_i^{\alpha_i} \tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation}
$$

where $\alpha \in\N_0^n$ with $|\alpha| \leq p$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Support' boxType='definition'>
The support of a function $f:\R^n \to\Gamma$ is the closure of the set of points where $f$ is not zero, i.e.

$$
  \operatorname{supp}(f) := \overline{\set{\mathbf{x}\in\R^n | f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0}}
$$

If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$, then for all $r$-balls $\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0)$ there exists $\mathbf{x}\in\R^n$ such that $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$. In particular, if $\operatorname{supp}(f)$ is bounded, then since $\operatorname{supp}(f)$ is a closed set, it is also compact.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Properties of support' boxType='proposition'>
If $f, g\in\R^n \to\Gamma$ and $\gamma\in\R$ then
1. $\operatorname{supp}(f + g) \subset \operatorname{supp}(f) \cup \operatorname{supp}(f)$
2. $\operatorname{supp}(f\cdot g) \subset \operatorname{supp}(f) \cap \operatorname{supp}(g)$
3. $\operatorname{supp}(\gamma f) = \operatorname{\supp}(f)$ for $\gamma \neq 0$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \operatorname{supp}(f + g)$, then $(f + g)(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0) \subset\R^n$. Consequently, $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ or $g(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ implying that either $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$ or $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(g)$. Hence, $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f) \cup \operatorname{supp}(g)$.

**(2):** If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f\cdot g)$ then $(fg)(x) \neq 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0)$. Consequently, $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ and $g(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ implying that $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$ and $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(g)$. Hence, $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f) \cap \operatorname{supp}(g)$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence' boxType='definition'>
Consider a function $f:A\subset\R^n \to\R$. A $(f_n : A\subset\R^n \to\R)_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly to $f$, denoted $f_n \xrightarrow{\text{u}} f$, if for every $\epsilon > 0$ there is $N(\epsilon) \in\N$ such that 

$$
  |f_n (\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x})| < \epsilon
$$

for all $\mathbf{x}\in A$ and all $n \geq N(\epsilon)$.

In the case of uniform convergence, the index $N(\epsilon)$ depends only on $\epsilon > 0$, and is the same for all $\mathbf{x}\in A$. Meanwhile, for pointwise convergence $N$ depends on $\epsilon$ and $\mathbf{x}\in A$. Thus, uniform convergence implies pointwise converge, but the converse does not necessarily hold.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Space of test functions' boxType='definition'>
Let $\Omega\subset\R^n$ be a compact set and consider the functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$. The set of functions 

$$
  \mathcal{D}^m (\Omega) = \Set{\varphi|\varphi\in C^m (\R^n), \operatorname{\supp}(\varphi) \subset\Omega}
$$

is called the space of test functions. For $m = \infty$, the space of smooth test functions is denoted

$$
  \mathcal{D} (\Omega) = \Set{\varphi|\varphi \in C^\infty (\R^n), \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset\Omega}
$$

If $\varphi\in C^m (\R^m)$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset \Omega$, then $\operatorname{supp}(\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})) \subset \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset \Omega$ for $|\alpha| \leq m$. Consequently, all functions $\varphi\in C^m (\Omega)$ together with all their derivatives up to order $m$ inclusive are null outside the compact $\Omega$.

The space of test functions $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence of test functions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of test functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in \N} \subset\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$, denoted $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)} \varphi$, if the sequence of functions $(\D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})$ in $\Omega$, hence 

$$
  \D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}) \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}),\; 0\leq|\alpha|\leq m,\; \forall \mathbf{x}\in\Omega
$$
</MathBox>

Note that the space $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ for $m < \infty$ becomes a normed vector space if we define the norm by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lVert\varphi\rVert_{\mathcal{D}^m} =& \sup_\substack{|\alpha|\leq m \\ \mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})| \\
  =& \sup_{0\leq|\alpha|\leq m} \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})|, \quad \alpha\in\N^n
\end{align*}
$$

In particular, for $m = 0$, the space $\mathcal{D}^0 (\Omega)$ is denoted by $C_C^0 (\Omega)$. This is the space of continuous functions $C^0 (\R^n)$, whose support are contained in some compact set $\Omega\subset\R^n$. The test function space $C_C^0 (\Omega)$ is a normed space with norm

$$
  \varphi_{C_C^0} = \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\varphi(\mathbf{x})|
$$

The sequence $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset C_C^0 (\Omega)$ converges towards $\varphi\in C_C^0 (\Omega)$ is $\lim_{n\to\infty} \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\varphi_i - \varphi| = 0$, that is, if $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly towards $\varphi$ in $\Omega$.

<MathBox title='Space of test functions with arbitrary support' boxType='definition'>
The space of test functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ with arbitrary support is defined as

$$
  \mathcal{E} = \Set{\varphi | \varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma, \varphi \in C^\infty (\R^n)}
$$

The space $\mathcal{E}$ is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence of test functions with arbitrary support' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of test functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in \N} \subset\mathcal{E}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{E}$, denoted $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{E}} \varphi$ if the sequence of functions $(\D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))_{n\in\N} \subset \mathcal{E}$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \in\mathcal{E}$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$, i.e. 

$$
  \D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}) \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}),\; \forall \alpha\in\N_0^n
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Union of smooth test functions with compact support' boxType='definition'>
Let $\Omega\subset\R^n$ be a compact set and consider the functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$. The set of functions 

$$
  \mathcal{D} = \Set{\varphi|\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n), \operatorname{\supp}(\varphi) = \Omega}
$$

is called the space of functions with compact support in $\Omega$. The space $\mathcal{D}$ is a vector space on $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

Since $\forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, it belongs to a certain $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$, it follows that $\mathcal{D}$ is the union of spaces $\mathcal{D}$ over the compact subsets $\Omega\subset\R^n$. Consequently, we have

$$
  \mathcal{D} = \bigcup_\Omega \mathcal{D}(\Omega),\; \mathcal{D}(\Omega) \subset\mathcal{D} \subset\mathcal{E}
$$

<MathBox title='Convergence of smooth test functions with compact support' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of smooth functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{D}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, written $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$, if the following conditions are satisfied:
1. $\forall n \in\N$, there is a compact set $\Omega \subset\R^n$ such that $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_n), \operatorname{supp}(\varphi)\subset\Omega$
2. $\D^\alpha \varphi_n$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha$ on $\Omega$ for all $\alpha\in\N_0^n$, i.e. $\D^\alpha \varphi_n \xrightarrow{u} \D^\alpha \varphi$ on $\Omega$.

Thus, convergence in the space $\mathcal{D}$ is reduced to convergence in the space $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Multiplier' boxType='definition'>
A function $\psi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ is a multiplier for the space $\mathcal{D}$ if $\forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, the mapping $\varphi\to\psi\varphi$ is continuous from $\mathcal{D}$ in $\mathcal{D}$.
</MathBox>

We can easily verify that any function $\psi \in C^\infty (\R^n)$ is a multiplier for space $\mathcal{D}$. Since $\psi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$ and $\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$, as well as $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$, we apply $\eqref{equation-1}$ and have

$$
  \D^\alpha (\psi\varphi) \in \sum_{\beta+\gamma = \alpha} \frac{\alpha!}{\beta!\gamma!} \D^\beta \psi \D^\gamma
$$

where $\alpha\in\N_0^n$. On the other hand, we have $\operatorname{supp}(\psi\varphi) \subset\operatorname{supp}(\psi) \cap \operatorname{sup}(\psi) \subset \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) = \Omega$.

Next, we show that $\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$ implies $\psi\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \psi\varphi$. From the expression of the derivative $\D^\alpha (\psi\varphi)$, we have

$$
  |\D^\alpha \psi(\varphi_i - \varphi)| \leq \sum_{\lVert\gamma\rVert \leq \lVert\alpha\rVert} A_\gamma |\D^\gamma (\varphi_i - \varphi)|
$$

where $A_\gamma > 0$ are constants. Since $\D^\alpha (\varphi_i - \varphi) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} 0$, we obtain $|\D^\alpha \psi(\varphi_i - \varphi)| \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} 0$, hence $\psi\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \psi\varphi$.

<MathBox title='Partition of unity' boxType='theorem'>
If $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ and $U_i$ for $i = 1,\dots,p$ are open bounded sets, forming a finite covering of $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)$, then there exists functions $e_i \in\mathcal{D}$ for $i=1,\dots,p$ with the properties

1. $e_i (\mathbf{x}) \in [0,1]$ with $\operatorname{supp}(e_i) \subset U_i$
2. $\sum_{i=1}^p e_i (x) = 1$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)$
3. $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^p e_i (\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x})$
</MathBox>

## Schwartz space

<MathBox title='Schwartz space' boxType='definition'>
The Schwartz space on $\R^n$, denoted $\mathcal{S}(\R^n)$, is the set of smooth functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ that are rapidly decreasing. This means that $\varphi$ and all its derivatives decay faster than any inverse power of $\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert$ as $\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert\to\infty$. Formally, for all $k \in\N$ and $\beta\in\N_0^n$, we have

$$
  \lim_{\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert} \lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert^k \D^\beta \varphi = 0
$$

Alternatively, the Schartz space can be defined as the set of functions $\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$ such that for every multi-index $\alpha, \beta \in\N_0^n$, the seminorms

$$
	\lVert \varphi\rVert_{\alpha, \beta} := \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\R^n} \left| \mathbf{x}^\alpha \left(D^\beta \varphi \right)(\mathbf{x}) \right|
$$

are finite. In other words, for $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}(\R^n)$, we require that $\varphi$ and all of its derivatives (with respect to any multi-index $\beta$) grow at most polynomially as $\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert$ grows, while decaying faster than any power of $\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert$ at infinity. Thus, we can write the Schwartz space as

$$
	\mathcal{S}\left(\R^n) := \Set{\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n) | \forall \alpha, \beta \in \N^n \;: \lVert \varphi \rVert_{\alpha, \beta} < \infty }
$$

The Schwartz space is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as its null element.
</MathBox>

We note that $\mathcal{D}\subset\mathcal{S}\subset\mathcal{E}$, where
- $\mathcal{D}$ denotes the space of smooth test functions with compact support
- $\mathcal{S}$ is the Schartz space of rapidly decreasing functions
- $\mathcal{E}$ is the space of smooth test functions with arbitrary support

Let $\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ denote the set of functions $\psi \in C^\infty (\R^n)$ such that $\psi$ and all of its derivatives grow no faster than a polynomial at infinity. Specifically, for $\psi\in\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ we have for all $\alpha\in\N_0^n$

$$
  |\D^\alpha \psi| \leq c_\alpha (1 + \lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert)^{m_\alpha}
$$

where $c_\alpha > 0$ and $m_\alpha \geq 0$ are constants depending on $\alpha$.

It follows that $\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ is the space of multipliers of $\mathcal{S}$, meaning that if $\psi\in\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$, then for every $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$, the product $\psi\varphi \in\mathcal{S}$. Additionally, if a sequence of functions converges in the Schartz space, i.e. $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, then $\psi\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \psi\varphi$. This property implies that that polynomials are multipliers of $\mathcal{S}$. Consequently, if $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$ then $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi \in\mathcal{S}$ for all $\alpha,\beta\in\N_0^n$ is bounded and integrable on $\R^n$.Hence, $\mathcal{S} \subset \mathcal{L}^p$ for $p \geq 1$, where $\mathcal{L}^p$ is the space of $L^p$-integrable functions on $\R^n$.

<MathBox title='Convergence of Schwartz functions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of Schartz functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{S}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$, written $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, if for all $\alpha,\beta\in\N_0^n$

$$
  \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_n \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi, \; \mathbf{x}\in\R^n
$$

Consequently, if $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, then $\forall \alpha, \beta\in\N_0^n$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$ we have $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{u}} \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
The convergence in the space of smooth test functions $\mathcal{D}$ is stronger than the convergence in the Schartz space $\mathcal{S}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$, then there is $\mathcal{D}(\Omega) \subset\mathcal{D}$ so that $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}(\Omega)} \varphi$. Hence $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_n$ converges uniformly to $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi$ for $\alpha, \beta\in\N_0^n$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$, that is $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
The space of smooth test functions $\mathcal{D}$ is dense in the Schartz space $\mathcal{S}$. This means that for all $\varphi \in\mathcal{S}$, there is $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{D}$ such that $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$.
</MathBox>

# Distributions

Let $X, Y$ be two vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$. We call the mapping $\mathrm{T}:U\to Y$ an operator defined on $X$ with values in $Y$. The value of the operator $\mathrm{T}$ at the point $\mathbf{x}\in X$ is denoted $(\mathrm{T},\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{y}\in Y$. A functional is an operator $\mathrm{T}:X\to\mathbb{F}$ is an operator that takes values in the field of $X$.

<MathBox title='Linear operator' boxType='definition'>
Let $X, Y$ be two vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$. The operator $\mathrm{T}:X\to Y$ is linear if

$$
  \mathrm{T}(\alpha_1 \mathbf{x}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{x}_2) = \alpha_1 \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_1) + \alpha_2 \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_2)
$$

for all $\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in\mathbb{F}$ and $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2 \in X$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Distribution' boxType='definition'>
A distribution $f$ is a continuous linear functional defined on a space of test functions $\Phi \in\Set{\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega), \mathcal{D}(\Omega), \mathcal{D}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{S}}$, where
- $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ is the space of test functions with compact support in $\Omega$ up to order $m$
- $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$ is the space of smooth test functions with compact support in $\Omega$
- $\mathcal{E}$ is the space of smooth test functions with arbitrary support
- $\mathcal{S}$ is the Schartz space of rapidly decreasing functions.

Formally, a distribution $f:\Phi\to\mathbb{F}$ satisfies the following properties
1. **Linearity:** $(f, \lambda_1 \varphi_1 + \lambda_2 \varphi_2) = \lambda_1 (f, \varphi_1) + \lambda_2 (f, \varphi_2)$ for all $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{F}$ and $\varphi_1, \varphi_2 \in\Phi$
2. **Continuity:** If $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \in \Phi$, $\varphi\in\Phi$ and $\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\Phi} \varphi$, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} (f, \varphi_n) = (f, \varphi)$

The set of distributions defined on $\Phi$ is denoted $\Phi'$ and forms a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ with the operations
1. **Addition:** $(f + g, \varphi) = (f, \varphi) + (g, \varphi)$ for all $f,g\in\Phi'$ and $\varphi\in\Phi$
2. **Scalar multiplication:** $(\alpha f, \varphi) = \alpha (f, \varphi)$ for all $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Weak convergence of distributions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of distributions $(f_n)_{n\in\N} \subset \Phi'$ converges in weak sense to $f\in\Phi'$ if $\lim_{n\to\infty} (f_n, \varphi) = (f,\varphi)$ for all $\varphi\in\Phi$.
</MathBox>
