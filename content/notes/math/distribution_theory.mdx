# Test functions (bump functions)

<MathBox title='Partial derivative' boxType='definition'>
Let $\alpha = (\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n) \in\N_0^n$ be a multiindex of order $n$ with length $|\alpha| = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i$. For $\alpha, \beta \in \N_0^n$ and $\mathbf{x}\in\R^n$, we define the following notations

- $\alpha \leq \beta$ if $\alpha_i \leq \beta_i$ for $i=1,\dots,n$
- $\binom{\beta}{\alpha} = \frac{\beta!}{\alpha! (\beta - \alpha)!}$, where $\alpha! = \prod_{i=1}^n \alpha_i!$
- $\mathbf{x}^\alpha = \prod_{i=1}^n x_i^{\alpha_i}$

We denote by $\D^\alpha f$ the partial derivative of order $|\alpha|$ of a function $f:\Omega\subset\R^n \to\Gamma$, defined as

$$
  \D^\alpha f = \frac{\partial^{|\alpha|}}{\partial x_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots\partial x_n^{\alpha_n}} f
$$

where $\D^\alpha = \D_1^{\alpha_1} \cdot D_n^{\alpha_n}$ and $\D_j = \partial/\partial x_j$ for $j = 1,\dots,n$. If the function $f$ has continuous partial derivative up to the order $|\alpha + \beta|$ inclusively, then

$$
  \D^{\alpha + \beta} = \D^\alpha (\D^\beta f) = \D^\beta (\D^\alpha f)
$$

We denote by $C^m (\Omega)$ the of functions $f:\Omega\subset\R^n \to\Gamma$ with continuous derivative of order $m$, that is, $\D^\alpha f$ is continuous on $\Omega$ for every $\alpha$ with $|\alpha| \leq m$. When $m = 0$, we have the set $C^0 (\Omega)$ of continuous functions on $\Omega$. The set of functions on $\Omega$ with continuous derivatives of all orders is denoted

$$
  C^\infty (\Omega) := \bigcap_{k\in\N} C^k (\Omega)
$$

and is called the space of smooth functions. Clearly, we have $C^\infty (\Omega) \subset C^m (\Omega) = C^0 (\Omega)$ for all $m > 0$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Partial derivative' boxType='proposition' tag='proposition-1'>
For $f,g \in C^\p (\Omega)$ with $\Omega\subset\R^n$, then $fg \in C^p (\Omega)$ and we have

$$
\begin{equation}
  \D^\alpha (f\cdot g) = \sum_{\beta+\gamma} \frac{\alpha!}{\beta! \gamma!} \D^\beta f \cdot \D^\gamma g, \; \D^\alpha = \prod_{i=1}^n D_i^{\alpha_i} \tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation}
$$

where $\alpha \in\N_0^n$ with $|\alpha| \leq p$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Support' boxType='definition'>
The support of a function $f:\R^n \to\Gamma$ is the closure of the set of points where $f$ is not zero, i.e.

$$
  \operatorname{supp}(f) := \overline{\set{\mathbf{x}\in\R^n | f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0}}
$$

If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$, then for all $r$-balls $\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0)$ there exists $\mathbf{x}\in\R^n$ such that $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$. In particular, if $\operatorname{supp}(f)$ is bounded, then since $\operatorname{supp}(f)$ is a closed set, it is also compact.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Properties of support' boxType='proposition'>
If $f, g\in\R^n \to\Gamma$ and $\gamma\in\R$ then
1. $\operatorname{supp}(f + g) \subset \operatorname{supp}(f) \cup \operatorname{supp}(f)$
2. $\operatorname{supp}(f\cdot g) \subset \operatorname{supp}(f) \cap \operatorname{supp}(g)$
3. $\operatorname{supp}(\gamma f) = \operatorname{\supp}(f)$ for $\gamma \neq 0$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**(1):** If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \operatorname{supp}(f + g)$, then $(f + g)(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0) \subset\R^n$. Consequently, $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ or $g(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ implying that either $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$ or $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(g)$. Hence, $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f) \cup \operatorname{supp}(g)$.

**(2):** If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f\cdot g)$ then $(fg)(x) \neq 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{B}_r (\mathbf{x}_0)$. Consequently, $f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ and $g(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0$ implying that $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f)$ and $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(g)$. Hence, $\mathbf{x}_0 \in\operatorname{supp}(f) \cap \operatorname{supp}(g)$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence' boxType='definition'>
Consider a function $f:A\subset\R^n \to\R$. A $(f_n : A\subset\R^n \to\R)_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly to $f$, denoted $f_n \xrightarrow{\text{u}} f$, if for every $\epsilon > 0$ there is $N(\epsilon) \in\N$ such that 

$$
  |f_n (\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x})| < \epsilon
$$

for all $\mathbf{x}\in A$ and all $n \geq N(\epsilon)$.

In the case of uniform convergence, the index $N(\epsilon)$ depends only on $\epsilon > 0$, and is the same for all $\mathbf{x}\in A$. Meanwhile, for pointwise convergence $N$ depends on $\epsilon$ and $\mathbf{x}\in A$. Thus, uniform convergence implies pointwise converge, but the converse does not necessarily hold.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Space of test functions' boxType='definition'>
Let $\Omega\subset\R^n$ be a compact set and consider the functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$. The set of functions 

$$
  \mathcal{D}^m (\Omega) = \Set{\varphi|\varphi\in C^m (\R^n), \operatorname{\supp}(\varphi) \subset\Omega}
$$

is called the space of test functions. For $m = \infty$, the space of smooth test functions is denoted

$$
  \mathcal{D} (\Omega) = \Set{\varphi|\varphi \in C^\infty (\R^n), \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset\Omega}
$$

If $\varphi\in C^m (\R^m)$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset \Omega$, then $\operatorname{supp}(\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})) \subset \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset \Omega$ for $|\alpha| \leq m$. Consequently, all functions $\varphi\in C^m (\Omega)$ together with all their derivatives up to order $m$ inclusive are null outside the compact $\Omega$.

The space of test functions $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence of test functions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of test functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in \N} \subset\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$, denoted $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)} \varphi$, if the sequence of functions $(\D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})$ in $\Omega$, hence 

$$
  \D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}) \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}),\; 0\leq|\alpha|\leq m,\; \forall \mathbf{x}\in\Omega
$$
</MathBox>

Note that the space $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ for $m < \infty$ becomes a normed vector space if we define the norm by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \norm{\varphi}_{\mathcal{D}^m} =& \sup_\substack{|\alpha|\leq m \\ \mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})| \\
  =& \sup_{0\leq|\alpha|\leq m} \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x})|, \quad \alpha\in\N^n
\end{align*}
$$

In particular, for $m = 0$, the space $\mathcal{D}^0 (\Omega)$ is denoted by $C_C^0 (\Omega)$. This is the space of continuous functions $C^0 (\R^n)$, whose support are contained in some compact set $\Omega\subset\R^n$. The test function space $C_C^0 (\Omega)$ is a normed space with norm

$$
  \varphi_{C_C^0} = \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\varphi(\mathbf{x})|
$$

The sequence $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset C_C^0 (\Omega)$ converges towards $\varphi\in C_C^0 (\Omega)$ is $\lim_{n\to\infty} \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\Omega} |\varphi_i - \varphi| = 0$, that is, if $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N}$ converges uniformly towards $\varphi$ in $\Omega$.

<MathBox title='Space of test functions with arbitrary support' boxType='definition'>
The space of test functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ with arbitrary support is defined as

$$
  \mathcal{E} = \Set{\varphi | \varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma, \varphi \in C^\infty (\R^n)}
$$

The space $\mathcal{E}$ is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Convergence of test functions with arbitrary support' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of test functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in \N} \subset\mathcal{E}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{E}$, denoted $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{E}} \varphi$ if the sequence of functions $(\D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))_{n\in\N} \subset \mathcal{E}$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \in\mathcal{E}$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$, i.e. 

$$
  \D^\alpha \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}) \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \D^\alpha \varphi(\mathbf{x}),\; \forall \alpha\in\N_0^n
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Union of smooth test functions with compact support' boxType='definition'>
Let $\Omega\subset\R^n$ be a compact set and consider the functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$. The set of functions 

$$
  \mathcal{D} = \Set{\varphi|\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n), \operatorname{\supp}(\varphi) = \Omega}
$$

is called the space of functions with compact support in $\Omega$. The space $\mathcal{D}$ is a vector space on $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as the null element.
</MathBox>

Since $\forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, it belongs to a certain $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$, it follows that $\mathcal{D}$ is the union of spaces $\mathcal{D}$ over the compact subsets $\Omega\subset\R^n$. Consequently, we have

$$
  \mathcal{D} = \bigcup_\Omega \mathcal{D}(\Omega),\; \mathcal{D}(\Omega) \subset\mathcal{D} \subset\mathcal{E}
$$

<MathBox title='Convergence of smooth test functions with compact support' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of smooth functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{D}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, written $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$, if the following conditions are satisfied:
1. $\forall n \in\N$, there is a compact set $\Omega \subset\R^n$ such that $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_n), \operatorname{supp}(\varphi)\subset\Omega$
2. $\D^\alpha \varphi_n$ converges uniformly towards $\D^\alpha$ on $\Omega$ for all $\alpha\in\N_0^n$, i.e. $\D^\alpha \varphi_n \xrightarrow{u} \D^\alpha \varphi$ on $\Omega$.

Thus, convergence in the space $\mathcal{D}$ is reduced to convergence in the space $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Multiplier' boxType='definition'>
A function $\psi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ is a multiplier for the space $\mathcal{D}$ if $\forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, the mapping $\varphi\to\psi\varphi$ is continuous from $\mathcal{D}$ in $\mathcal{D}$.
</MathBox>

We can easily verify that any function $\psi \in C^\infty (\R^n)$ is a multiplier for space $\mathcal{D}$. Since $\psi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$ and $\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$, as well as $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$, we apply $\eqref{equation-1}$ and have

$$
  \D^\alpha (\psi\varphi) \in \sum_{\beta+\gamma = \alpha} \frac{\alpha!}{\beta!\gamma!} \D^\beta \psi \D^\gamma
$$

where $\alpha\in\N_0^n$. On the other hand, we have $\operatorname{supp}(\psi\varphi) \subset\operatorname{supp}(\psi) \cap \operatorname{sup}(\psi) \subset \operatorname{supp}(\varphi) = \Omega$.

Next, we show that $\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$ implies $\psi\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \psi\varphi$. From the expression of the derivative $\D^\alpha (\psi\varphi)$, we have

$$
  |\D^\alpha \psi(\varphi_i - \varphi)| \leq \sum_{\norm{\gamma} \leq \norm{\alpha}} A_\gamma |\D^\gamma (\varphi_i - \varphi)|
$$

where $A_\gamma > 0$ are constants. Since $\D^\alpha (\varphi_i - \varphi) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} 0$, we obtain $|\D^\alpha \psi(\varphi_i - \varphi)| \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} 0$, hence $\psi\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \psi\varphi$.

<MathBox title='Partition of unity' boxType='theorem'>
If $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ and $U_i$ for $i = 1,\dots,p$ are open bounded sets, forming a finite covering of $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)$, then there exists functions $e_i \in\mathcal{D}$ for $i=1,\dots,p$ with the properties

1. $e_i (\mathbf{x}) \in [0,1]$ with $\operatorname{supp}(e_i) \subset U_i$
2. $\sum_{i=1}^p e_i (x) = 1$ for $\mathbf{x}\in\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)$
3. $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^p e_i (\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x})$
</MathBox>

## Schwartz space

<MathBox title='Schwartz space' boxType='definition'>
The Schwartz space on $\R^n$, denoted $\mathcal{S}(\R^n)$, is the set of smooth functions $\varphi:\R^n \to\Gamma$ that are rapidly decreasing. This means that $\varphi$ and all its derivatives decay faster than any inverse power of $\norm{\mathbf{x}}$ as $\norm{\mathbf{x}}\to\infty$. Formally, for all $k \in\N$ and $\beta\in\N_0^n$, we have

$$
  \lim_{\norm{\mathbf{x}}} \norm{\mathbf{x}}^k \D^\beta \varphi = 0
$$

Alternatively, the Schartz space can be defined as the set of functions $\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$ such that for every multi-index $\alpha, \beta \in\N_0^n$, the seminorms

$$
	\norm{ \varphi}_{\alpha, \beta} := \sup_{\mathbf{x}\in\R^n} \left| \mathbf{x}^\alpha \left(D^\beta \varphi \right)(\mathbf{x}) \right|
$$

are finite. In other words, for $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}(\R^n)$, we require that $\varphi$ and all of its derivatives (with respect to any multi-index $\beta$) grow at most polynomially as $\norm{\mathbf{x}}$ grows, while decaying faster than any power of $\norm{\mathbf{x}}$ at infinity. Thus, we can write the Schwartz space as

$$
	\mathcal{S}\left(\R^n) := \Set{\varphi\in C^\infty (\R^n) | \forall \alpha, \beta \in \N^n \;: \norm{ \varphi }_{\alpha, \beta} < \infty }
$$

The Schwartz space is a vector space over $\Gamma$, with the zero function $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ as its null element.
</MathBox>

We note that $\mathcal{D}\subset\mathcal{S}\subset\mathcal{E}$, where
- $\mathcal{D}$ denotes the space of smooth test functions with compact support
- $\mathcal{S}$ is the Schartz space of rapidly decreasing functions
- $\mathcal{E}$ is the space of smooth test functions with arbitrary support

Let $\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ denote the set of functions $\psi \in C^\infty (\R^n)$ such that $\psi$ and all of its derivatives grow no faster than a polynomial at infinity. Specifically, for $\psi\in\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ we have for all $\alpha\in\N_0^n$

$$
  |\D^\alpha \psi| \leq c_\alpha (1 + \norm{\mathbf{x}})^{m_\alpha}
$$

where $c_\alpha > 0$ and $m_\alpha \geq 0$ are constants depending on $\alpha$.

It follows that $\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$ is the space of multipliers of $\mathcal{S}$, meaning that if $\psi\in\mathcal{O}_\text{M}$, then for every $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$, the product $\psi\varphi \in\mathcal{S}$. Additionally, if a sequence of functions converges in the Schartz space, i.e. $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, then $\psi\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \psi\varphi$. This property implies that that polynomials are multipliers of $\mathcal{S}$. Consequently, if $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$ then $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi \in\mathcal{S}$ for all $\alpha,\beta\in\N_0^n$ is bounded and integrable on $\R^n$.Hence, $\mathcal{S} \subset \mathcal{L}^p$ for $p \geq 1$, where $\mathcal{L}^p$ is the space of $L^p$-integrable functions on $\R^n$.

<MathBox title='Convergence of Schwartz functions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of Schartz functions $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{S}$ converges to $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$, written $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, if for all $\alpha,\beta\in\N_0^n$

$$
  \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_n \xrightarrow{\text{u}} \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi, \; \mathbf{x}\in\R^n
$$

Consequently, if $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$, then $\forall \alpha, \beta\in\N_0^n$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$ we have $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_i \xrightarrow{\mathcal{u}} \mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
The convergence in the space of smooth test functions $\mathcal{D}$ is stronger than the convergence in the Schartz space $\mathcal{S}$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

If $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$, then there is $\mathcal{D}(\Omega) \subset\mathcal{D}$ so that $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}(\Omega)} \varphi$. Hence $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi_n$ converges uniformly to $\mathbf{x}^\beta \D^\alpha \varphi$ for $\alpha, \beta\in\N_0^n$ on any compact subset of $\R^n$, that is $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
The space of smooth test functions $\mathcal{D}$ is dense in the Schartz space $\mathcal{S}$. This means that for all $\varphi \in\mathcal{S}$, there is $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset\mathcal{D}$ such that $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{S}} \varphi$.
</MathBox>

# Distributions

Let $X, Y$ be two vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$. We call the mapping $\mathrm{T}:U\to Y$ an operator defined on $X$ with values in $Y$. The value of the operator $\mathrm{T}$ at the point $\mathbf{x}\in X$ is denoted $(\mathrm{T},\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{y}\in Y$. A functional is an operator $\mathrm{T}:X\to\mathbb{F}$ is an operator that takes values in the field of $X$.

<MathBox title='Linear operator' boxType='definition'>
Let $X, Y$ be two vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$. The operator $\mathrm{T}:X\to Y$ is linear if

$$
  \mathrm{T}(\alpha_1 \mathbf{x}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{x}_2) = \alpha_1 \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_1) + \alpha_2 \mathrm{T}(\mathbf{x}_2)
$$

for all $\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in\mathbb{F}$ and $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2 \in X$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Distribution' boxType='definition'>
A distribution $f$ is a continuous linear functional defined on a space of test functions $\Phi \in\Set{\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega), \mathcal{D}(\Omega), \mathcal{D}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{S}}$, where
- $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ is the space of test functions with compact support in $\Omega$ up to order $m$
- $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$ is the space of smooth test functions with compact support in $\Omega$
- $\mathcal{E}$ is the space of smooth test functions with arbitrary support
- $\mathcal{S}$ is the Schartz space of rapidly decreasing functions

Formally, a distribution $f:\Phi\to\mathbb{F}$ satisfies the following properties
1. **Linearity:** $(f, \lambda_1 \varphi_1 + \lambda_2 \varphi_2) = \lambda_1 (f, \varphi_1) + \lambda_2 (f, \varphi_2)$ for all $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{F}$ and $\varphi_1, \varphi_2 \in\Phi$
2. **Continuity:** If $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \in \Phi$, $\varphi\in\Phi$ and $\varphi_i \xrightarrow{\Phi} \varphi$, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} (f, \varphi_n) = (f, \varphi)$

The set of distributions defined on $\Phi$ is denoted $\Phi'$ and forms a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ with the operations
1. **Addition:** $(f + g, \varphi) = (f, \varphi) + (g, \varphi)$ for all $f,g\in\Phi'$ and $\varphi\in\Phi$
2. **Scalar multiplication:** $(\alpha f, \varphi) = \alpha (f, \varphi)$ for all $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}$
</MathBox>

Distributions defined on
- $\mathcal{D}^m (\Omega)$ are called distributions of finite order
- $\mathcal{D}^0 (\Omega)$ are called measures
- $\mathcal{S}$ are called Schwartz distributions

<MathBox title='Weak convergence of distributions' boxType='definition'>
A sequence of distributions $(f_n)_{n\in\N} \subset \Phi'$ converges in weak sense to $f\in\Phi'$ if $\lim_{n\to\infty} (f_n, \varphi) = (f,\varphi)$ for all $\varphi\in\Phi$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
Let $f\in\Phi' (\R^n)$ be a distribution and $\varphi_a \in\Phi(\R^n)$ a test function depending on $a\in I\subseteq\R$. If $\partial\varphi_a (\mathbf{x})/\partial a$ exists, $\partial\phi_a (\mathbf{x})/\partial a \in\Phi$, and

$$
  \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\varphi_{a+h} (\mathbf{x}) - \varphi_a (\mathbf{x})}{h} \xrightarrow{\Phi} \frac{\partial\varphi_a (\mathbf{x})}{\partial a}
$$

then the following relation holds

$$
  \frac{\d}{\d a} (f(\mathbf{x}), \varphi_a (\mathbf{x})) = \left(f(\mathbf{x}), \frac{\partial\varphi_a (\mathbf{x})}{\partial a} \right)
$$

If $\varphi \in \mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ and $f \in\mathcal{D}' (\R^n)$, then

$$
  \frac{\d}{\d a} \left(f(x_1,\dots,x_n), \varphi\left(\frac{x_1}{a},\dots,\frac{x_n}{a} \right) \right) = \left(f(\mathbf{x}), -\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{x_i}{a^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} \varphi\left(\frac{\mathbf{x}}{a} \right) \right), \; a > 0
$$
</MathBox>

If $f\in\Phi'$ is a distribution and $\psi\in C^\infty (\R^n)$ a multiplier of the test function space $\Phi$, then the product $\psi f$ is defined by

$$
  (\psi f, \varphi) = (f, \psi\varphi),\; \forall \varphi\in\Phi
$$

It follows that $psi f \in\Phi'$ is also distribution because $\psi$ being the multiplier for $\Phi$ guarantees that $\psi\varphi\in\Phi$ for all $\varphi\in\Phi$.

Since $\mathcal{D}\subset\mathcal{S}$ and convergence in $\mathcal{D}$ is stronger than convergence in $\mathcal{S}$, it follows that the relationship between the corresponding dual spaces is

$$
  \mathcal{S}' \subset \mathcal{D}'
$$

Furthermore, since $\mathcal{S}\subset\mathcal{E}$ and convergence in $\mathcal{S}$ is stronger than convergence in $\mathcal{E}$, we also have

$$
  \mathcal{E}' \subset \mathbb{S}
$$

Thus, inclusion relationships between the spaces of test functions are

$$
  \mathcal{D}\subset\mathcal{S}\subset\mathcal{E}
$$

and the corresponding inclusion relationships between the dual spaces are

$$
  \mathcal{E}' \subset \mathcal{S}' \subset \mathcal{D}'
$$

If $\varphi\in\Phi$ is a complex-valued test functions and $f\in\Phi'$ a complex-valued distribution, the product of a $f$ and a complex function $a:\R^n \to\mathbb{C}$ with complex conjugate $\overline{a}$ is given by

$$
  (af, \varphi) = \overline{a} (f,\varphi) = (f, \overline{a}\varphi)
$$

with the assumption that $\overline{a}\varphi \in\Phi$. A complex-valued distribution $f$ can associated a complex conjugate distrubtion $\overline{f}$ by the relation

$$
  (\overline{f},\varphi) = \overline{(f, \overline{\varphi})}
$$

To each locally integrable complex-valued function $f\in L_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$ corresponds a distribution from $\mathcal{D}'$, $\mathrm{T}_f = f\in\mathcal{D}'$, defined by the formula

$$
  (f, \varphi) = \int_{\R^n} \overline{f(\mathbf{x})} \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x},\; \varphi 
$$

<MathBox title='Dirac delta function' boxType='definition'>
The Dirac delta function $\delta_\mathbf{a}: \R^n \to\R$ is a singular distribution centered at $\mathbf{a}\in\R^n$, and it is commonly expressed as $\delta_\mathbf{a} = \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})$. It can and be defined on any test function space $\Phi$ by the relation

$$
  (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi(\mathbf{x})) = \varphi(\mathbf{a}),\; \forall \varphi\in\Phi
$$

Since $\operatorname{supp}(\delta_\mathbf{a}) = \set{\mathbf{a}}$, it follows that $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is a distribution with compact support. Furthermore, if $\mathbf{a}\notin \operatorname{supp}(\varphi)$ for all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ then $(\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi) = \varphi(\mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{a}$. Thus, $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is zero on the set $\R^n \setminus\set{\mathbf{a}}$. 

The Dirac delta function satisfies the filter property such that for a continuous function $\psi$

$$
  \psi(\mathbf{x}) \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}) = \psi(\mathbf{a}) \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})
$$

If $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is defined on the space $\mathcal{D}^0 (\R^n) = C_C^0 (\R^n)$ of continuous functions with compact support and if $\psi\in C^0 (\R^n)$, then the product $\psi(\mathbf{x}) \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})$ is well defined and for all $\varphi\mathcal{D}^0$ we can write

$$
  (\psi(\mathbf{x})\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})) = (\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})\psi(\mathbf{x})) = \psi(\mathbf{a})\varphi(\mathbf{a})
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

To show that $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is a distribution we need to verify that it is both linear and continuous. To show linearity, we have for $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in\R$ and $\varphi_1, \varphi_2\in\Phi$ that

$$
\begin{align*}
  (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \lambda_1 \varphi_1(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda_2 \varphi_2(\mathbf{x})) =& \lambda \varphi_1 (\mathbf{a}) + \lambda_2 \varphi_2 (\mathbf{a}) \\
  =& \lambda_1 (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi_1 (\mathbf{x})) + \lambda_2 (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi_2 (\mathbf{x}))
\end{align*}
$$

To show continuity, let $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ and consider a sequence $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subseteq\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ with $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$. Since $\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ is complete, tt follows that $\varphi_n (\mathbf{x}) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \varphi (\mathbf{x})$ pointwise. Thus, we have

$$
  (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi_n (\mathbf{x})) = \varphi_n (\mathbf{a}) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \varphi(\mathbf{a}) = (\delta_\mathbf{a}, \varphi(\mathbf{x})) 
$$

showing that $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is continuous.

**Singularity**

We can show that $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is a singular distribution by contradiction. Assume that $\delta_\mathbf{a}$ is a regular distribution so that there is a locally integrable function $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1$ such that for all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$

$$
  \int_{\R^n} f(\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x} = (\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})) = \varphi(\mathbf{a})
$$

Because $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ is arbitrary, we can consider the test function $\norm{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}}^2 \psi(\mathbf{x})$, where $\psi\in\mathcal{D}$ is arbitrary. This gives

$$
  \int_{\R^n} f(\mathbf{x}) \psi(\mathbf{x}) \norm{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}}^2 \;\d\mathbf{x} = 0, \;\forall \psi\in\mathcal{D}
$$

It follows that $\norm{ \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a} }^2 f(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ almost everywhere on $\R^n$, which implies $f(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ almost everywhere, from which we have $f(\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ almost everywhere. However, $f(\mathbf{x})\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ almost everyhere implies $\int_{\R^n} f(\mathbf{x})\varphi(\mathbf{x})\;\d\mathbf{x} = 0$, which contradicts our assumption.

**Filter property**

For all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  (\psi(\mathbf{x})\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})) =& (\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})\psi(\mathbf{x})) = \varphi(\mathbf{a})\psi(\mathbf{a}) \\
  =& \psi(\mathbf{a})(\delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})) \\
  =& (\psi(\mathbf{a}) \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}), \varphi)
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

## Regular distributions

<MathBox title='Local integribility' boxType='definition'>
A function $f:\R^n \to\mathbb{F}$ is locally integrable if for all compact $K\subseteq\R^n$

$$
  \int_K |f(x)| \;\d\mathbf{x} < \infty
$$

In this case we write $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$. 
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
Let $f: \mathcal{D}(\R^n) \to\mathbb{F}$ be a linear function taking values in a field $\mathbb{F}$. Then $f$ is a distribution if and only if for all compact $K\subseteq\R^n$ there are $m\in\N$ and $C > 0$ such that $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subseteq K$ implies $|(f, \varphi)| \leq C \sum_{|\alpha|\leq m} \norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi }$ for all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Let $\varphi_n, \varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ for all $n\in\N$ with $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$. Then there is a compact set $K\subseteq\R^n$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_n) \subseteq K$ and for all $\alpha\in\N$ we have $\norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi_n - \D^\alpha \varphi}_\infty \xrightarrow{k\to\infty} 0$. By linearity, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  |(f, \varphi_n) - (f, \varphi) | =& |(f, \varphi_n - \varphi)| \\
  \leq& C \sum_{|\alpha|\leq m} \norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi_n - \D^\alpha \varphi }_\infty \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0
\end{align*}
$$

The converse can be shown by contraposition. Assume that there is a compact set $K\subseteq\R^n$ such that for all $m\in\N$ nad $C > 0$ there is a test function $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subseteq K$ and $|(f,\varphi)| > C \sum_{|\alpha|\leq m} \norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi }_\infty$. For $C = m = n \in\N$ take $\varphi_n \in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ with $|(f, \varphi_n)| > n \sum_{|\alpha| \leq m} \norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi_n }_\infty$. Define $\psi_n (\mathbf{x}) := \frac{1}{|(f, \varphi(\mathbf{x}))|}\varphi_n (\mathbf{x})$ so that $\psi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} 0$. However, 

$$
  |(f, \psi_n (\mathbf{x}))| = \frac{1}{|(f, \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))|} |(f, \varphi_n (\mathbf{x}))| = 1 \not\xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0
$$

showing that $f$ is not continuous. Hence, by contraposition we must have $|(f, \varphi)| \leq C \sum_{|\alpha|\leq m} \norm{ \D^\alpha \varphi }$.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
The functional $\mathrm{T}_f :\mathcal{D}(\R^n) \to\mathbb{F}$ defined by

$$
  (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi) = \int_{\R^n} f(\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x}, \; \varphi\in\mathcal{D}
$$

is a distribution.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Linearity of $\mathrm{T}_f$ follows from directly from the linearity of the integral. To show continuity, consider a sequence $(\varphi_n)_{n\in\N} \subset \mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ and let $\varphi \in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$. If $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} \varphi$, there is a compact set $\Omega\subseteq\R^n$ such that $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}(\Omega)} \varphi$. This implies that $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_n) \subset \Omega$ and $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset \Omega$. Thus,

$$
\begin{align*}
  |(\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi_i) - (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi)| =& |(\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi_i - \varphi)| \\
  \leq& \int_\Omega |f(\mathbf{x})| |\varphi_n - \varphi| \;\d\mathbf{x} 
\end{align*}
$$

Because $\varphi_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}(\Omega)} \varphi$, the support of $|\varphi_n - \varphi|$ is contained within $\Omega$ and vanishes as $n\to\infty$, i.e. $\Omega \supset \operatorname{supp}(|\varphi_n - \varphi|) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0$. Hence, $(\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi_n) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi)$, showing that $\mathrm{T}_f$ is continuous.
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Regular distribution' boxType='definition'>
A distribution $\mathrm{T}_f \in \mathcal{D}'(\R^n)$ is regular if there is a locally integrable function $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$ such that for all test functions $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$, the action of $\mathrm{T}_f$ on $\varphi$ is given by

$$
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{T}_f (\varphi) = \int_{\R^n} f(\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x}, \; \varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n) \tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation}
$$

Distributions that cannot be represented in this way are called singular distributions.

<details>
<summary>Details</summary>

Note that if $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subseteq K$ for a compact set $K\subseteq\R^n$, then

$$
  |\mathrm{T}_f (\varphi)| \leq \int_{\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)} |f(\mathbf{x})|\cdot\underbrace{|\varphi(\mathbf{x})|}_{\norm{\varphi}_\infty} \;\d\mathbf{x} \leq \int_K |f(x)| \;\d \mathbf{x} \cdot \norm{\varphi}_\infty
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

In general, if $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1$ such that $|f(\mathbf{x})| \leq A\norm{\mathbf{x}}^k$ for $k \in\N_+$ and $\norm{\mathbf{x}} \to\infty$, and if $\varphi\in\mathcal{S}$ is a Schwartz function with $|\varphi(\mathbf{x})| \leq B\norm{\mathbf{x}}^{-(k+2)}$, then the functional $\mathrm{T}_f$ given by $\eqref{equation-2}$ defines a regular distribution on $\mathcal{S}$, because $|f(\mathbf{x})\varphi(\mathbf{x})| \leq AB\norm{\mathbf{x}}^{-2}$ and thus the integral $\eqref{equation-2}$ exists.

<MathBox title='Heaviside function' boxType='definition'>
The Heaviside function $H:\R^n \to\R$ is defined as

$$
  H(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases}
    1, \quad& x_1 \geq 0,\dots, x_n \geq 0 \\
    0, \quad& \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
$$

It is a regular distribution that acts on a test function $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$ according to the rule

$$
  (H(\mathbf{x}), \varphi(\mathbf{x})) =& \int_0^\infty \cdots \int_0^\infty \varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n) \;\d x_1 \cdots \d x_n
$$

This can also be expressed as

$$
  H(x_1,\dots,x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n H(x_i)
$$

where $H(x_i)$ is the one-dimensional Heaviside function given by

$$
  H(x_i) = \begin{cases}
    1, \quad& x_i \geq 0 \\
    0, \quad& x_i < 0
  \end{cases}
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='' boxType='proposition'>
Let $(f_n)_{n\in\N} \subset \mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$ be a sequence of locally integrable functions, uniformly convergent to $f:\R^n \to\mathbb{F}$ on any compact set $\Omega\in\R^n$. Then $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$ and $\mathrm{T}_{f_n} \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}'} \mathrm{T}_f$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Since $f_n$ converges uniformly to $f$ on any compact set $\Omega$, then $f\in\mathcal{L}_\text{loc}^1 (\R^n)$ and $\int_\Omega f_n \;\d \mathbf{x} \to \int_\Omega f \;\d\mathbf{x}$. For all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi)\subseteq\Omega$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  |(\mathrm{T}_{f_n}, \varphi) - (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi)| \leq& \int_\Omega |\varphi| \cdot |f_n - f| \;\d\mathbf{x} \\
  \leq& \operatorname{mes}(\Omega) \operatorname{supp}_\Omega |\varphi(\mathbf{x})| \operatorname{supp}_\Omega |f_n (\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x})|
\end{align*}
$$

where $\operatorname{mes}(\Omega)$ denotes the measure of $\Omega$. Since $\operatorname{mes}(\Omega)\operatorname{supp}_\Omega |\varphi(\mathbf{x})|$ are bounded and $\operatorname{supp}_\Omega |f_n (\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x})| \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} 0$, it follows that $(\mathrm{T}_{f_i}, \varphi) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi)$. This shows that $\mathrm{T}_{f_n}$ converges to $\mathrm{T}_f$ on $\mathcal{D}'$.
</details>
</MathBox>

## Support of distributions

<MathBox title='Equal distribution' boxType='definition'>
A distribution $f\in\mathcal{D}'$ is null on the open set $A\subseteq \R^n$ if for every $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset A$ we have $(f,\varphi) = 0$. We write this as $f = 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\in A$.

Two distributions $f,g \in\mathcal{D}'$ are equal on the open set $A$, denoted $f = g$, if for all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ with $\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset A$ we have $(f - g, \varphi) = 0$. In particular, we write $f = g$ on $\R^n$ if $(f,\varphi) = (g,\varphi)$ for all $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Support of a distribution' boxType='definition'>
The support of a distribution $f\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)$, denoted $\operatorname{supp}(f)$, is defined as the complement of the union of open sets that nullify $f$. If $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \operatorname{supp}(f)$, then the distribution $f$ is not nullified on any open neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}_0$. On the other hand, if $\mathbf{x}_0 \notin\operatorname{supp}(f)$, there exists a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}_0$ where $f = 0$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Distribution generated on a piecewise continuous function' boxType='example'>
Consider a function $f:\R\to\R$ defined by $f(x) = 0$ for $x\in\R\setminus\set{x_1}$. This is a piecewise continuous function with support

$$
  \operatorname{supp}(f) = \overline{\set{x\in\R, f(\mathbf{x}) \neq 0}} = \overline{\set{x_1}} = \set{x_1}
$$

If $\mathrm{T}_f \in\mathcal{D}'(\R)$ is the regular distribution generated by the function $f$, then the support of $\mathrm{T}_f$ is $\emptyset$, i.e. $\operatorname{supp}(\mathrm{T}_f) = \emptyset$. For every test function $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R)$, we have 

$$
  (\mathrm{T}_f, \varphi) = \int_\R f(x) \varphi(x) \;\d x = 0
$$

which implies that $\mathrm{T}_f = 0$ in $\R$.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title='Dirac delta function on a hypersurface' boxType='example'>
Let $\delta_S = \delta(S)$ be a distribution, where $S\subset\R^n$ is a piecewise smooth hypersurface. The functional $\delta_S :\mathcal{D}\to\mathbb{C}$ defined by

$$
\begin{equation}
  (\delta_S, \varphi) = \int_S \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d S \tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation}
$$

represents the Dirac delta distribution concentrated on the hypersurface $S$, where $\d S$ is the differential area element on $S \subset\R^n$.

For any test function $\varphi\in\mathcal{D}$ whose support does not intersect $S$, the distribution $\delta_S$ is null, i.e. $\delta_S = 0$ for $x\notin S$. Thus, $\operatorname{supp}(\delta_S) = S$.

If $S = S_1 \cup S_2$, then from $\eqref{equation-3}$ we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  (\delta_{S_1 \cup S_2}, \varphi) =& \int_{S_1 \cup S_2} \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d S \\
  =& \int_{S_1} \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d S_1 + \int_{S_2} \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d S_2 \\
  =& (\delta_{S_1} + \delta_{S_2}, \varphi),\; \forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $\delta_{S_1 \cup S_2}= \delta_{S_1} + \delta_{S_2}$.

In general, if $f$ is a piecewise continuous function defined on the surface $S$, we have

$$
  (f\delta_S, \varphi) = \int_S f(\mathbf{x}) \varphi(\mathbf{x}) \;\d S, \;\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n)
$$

The distribution $\delta_{S\times\R} \in\mathcal{D}(\R^n \times\R)$ associated to the surface $S\subset\R^n$ and the temporal variable $t\in\R$ is defined by

$$
  (\delta_{S\times\R}, \varphi(\mathbf{x},t)) = \int_\R \d t \int_S \varphi(\mathbf{x}, t) \;\d S, \;\forall\varphi\in\mathcal{D}(\R^n \times\R)
$$

For a continuous function $f(\mathbf{x},t)\in C^0 (\R^n \times\R)$ the distribution $f(\mathbf{x},t) \delta_{S\times\R} \in\mathcal{D}'(\R^n \times\R)$ acts according to the formula

$$
\begin{align*}
  (f(\mathbf{x}, t)\delta_{S\times\R}, \varphi(\mathbf{x}, t)) =& (\delta_{S\times\R}, f(\mathbf{x},t)\varphi(\mathbf{x},t)) \\
  =& \int_\R \d t \int_S f(\mathbf{x}, t) \varphi(\mathbf{x},t) \;\d S
\end{align*}
$$

Note that $\delta_{S\times\R} = 0$ for $\mathbf{x}\notin S$ and $\forall t\in\R$, so that $\operatorname{supp}(\delta_{S\times\R} = S\times\R$.
</MathBox>
