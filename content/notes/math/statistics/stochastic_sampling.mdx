A pseudo-random number generator (PRNG) can be modeled as a finite deterministic dynamical system specified by a tuple $(S, f, \mu, U, g)$, where
- $S$ is a finite set of states
- $f: S\to S$ is the transition function
- $\mu$ is a probability distribution on $S$, representing the law of the initial state $s_0 \in S$, also called the seed 
- $U$ is a measurable output space
- $g: S\to U$ is a measurable output function

Given an inital state (seed) $s_0 \in S$ sampled according to $\mu$, the generator produces sequences $(s_n)_{n\geq 1}$ and $(u_n)_{n\geq 1}$ defined recursively by

$$
  s_{n+1} = f(s_n),\quad u_n = g(s_n),\; n \geq 1
$$

Thus, the evolution of the state is deterministic, and all randomness in the output process $(u_n)$ is induced solely by the random initialization $s_0 \sim\mu$. In other words, the resulting process $(u_n)$ is a deterministic function of the initial state, and its probabilistic properties are inherited by $\mu$.

The generator is designed so that $(u_n)$ approximates a sequence of independent random variables $(U_n)$ with a prescribed target distribution. This approximation is understood in an operational sense through statistical test: For each $n \geq 1$, the sequence $(u_1,\dots,u_n)$ should be statistically indistinguishable from $(U_1,\dots,U_n)$ with respect to a prescribed class $\mathcal{T}$ of statistical tests. More precisely, for every statistic $T \in\mathcal{T}$, the distribution of $T(u_1,\dots,u_n)$ is close, in a specified sense, to that of $T(U_1,\dots,U_n)$.

The definition of a pseudo-random number generator is restricted to testable properties of the output sequence $(u_n)_{n\geq 1}$. Its validity is assessed through a statistical testing framework: for each sample size $n\geq 1$, we evaluate the finite sequence $(u_1,\dots,u_n)$ under the null hypothesis

$$
  \mathrm{H}_0: U_1,\dots,U_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{U}([0,1])
$$

Since $(u_n)$ is deterministic for fixed seed $s_0$, the hypothesis $\mathrm{H}_0$ cannot hold in a literal probabilistic sense. Rather, the generator is deemed satisfactory if, for a prescribed class of statistical tests and significance levels, the observed sequence $(u_1,\dots,u_n)$ does not lead to rejection of $\mathrm{H}_0$.

Because the state space $S$ is finite, the sequence $(s_n)$ is ultimately periodic, meaning there exists integers $t \geq 0$ (the transient length) and $p \geq 1$ (the period) such that

$$
  s_{n+p} = s_n\; \forall n \geq t
$$

The integer $p \in\N_+$ is called teh period length (or cycle length) of the generator for the seed $s_0$. The maximal such $p$, taken over all initial states, is called the period of the generator.

<MathBox title="Random number generator" boxType="algorithm">
1. **Initialize:** Draw the seed $S_0$ from the distribution $\mu$ on $S$. Set $n = 1$
2. **Transition:** Set $S_n = f(S_{n-1})$
3. **Output:** Set $U_n = g(S_n)$
4. **Repeat:** Set $n = n + 1$ and return to step 2.
</MathBox>

# Uniform random number generation

<MathBox title="Uniform pseudo-random generator" boxType="definition">
A uniform pseudo-random number generator is a deterministic algorithm defined by a measurable map $t: S\to S$ on a state space $S$, an initial seed state $s_0 \in S$, and an output function $g: S\to [0,1]$. It generates a sequence $(u_n)_{n\geq 1}$ defined recursively by

$$
  s_{n+1} = f(s_n),\quad u_n = g(s_n) \in [0,1],\; n \geq 1
$$

The sequence $(u_n)$ is intended to approximate a sequence of independent uniform random variables $(U_n)_{n\geq 1}$, where $U_n \sim \mathcal{U}([0,1])$. 
</MathBox>

## Linear recurrence models

### Linear congruential generators

A linear congruential generator (LCG) is a pseudo-random number generator defined by a finite state space $S = \set{0,\dots,m-1}$, where $m\in\N_+$ is called the modulus. Given parameters $a,c \in\Z$, called respectively the multiplier and the increment, the state sequence $(x_t)_{t\geq 0}$ is defined recursively by

$$
  x_t = ax_{t-1} + c \bmod m,\; t \in\N_+
$$

with initial state $x_0 \in S$. Applying the modulo-$m$ operator means that $x_t \in S$ is given by the remainder upon division of $ax_{t-1} + c$ by $m$, i.e.

$$
  x_t = (ax_{t-1} + c) - m\left\lfloor\frac{ax_{t-1} + c}{m} \right\rfloor
$$

Without loss of generality, the parameters $a$ and $c$ can be taken in $\set{0,\dots,m-1}$, since only their residue classes modulo $m$ affect the recursion. When $c = 0$, the generator is called a multiplicative congruential generator.

The associated output sequence $(u_t)_{t\geq 1}$ is obtained via the normalization

$$
  u_t = \frac{x_t}{m} \in [0,1),\; t\geq 1
$$

The statistical quality of a linear congruential model depends on the choice of parameters $(a,c,m)$. In particular, these parameters determine the period length and the lattice structure of the generated sequence. Poor choices can lead to strong correlations and visible regularities, while suitable choices can yield maximal period and improved equidistribution properties.

<MathBox title="Minimal standard LCG" boxType="example">
An often-cited linear congruential model is that of Lewis, Goodman, and Miller, who proposed the parameters
- $a = 7^5 = 16807$
- $c = 0$
- $m = 2^{31} - 1 = 2147483647$

where $m$ is the Mersenne prime $M_{31}$. This choice yields a multiplicative congruential generator with maximal period $m-1$ for all non-zero initial states $x_0 \in\set{1,\dots,m-1}$, since $a$ is a primitive root modulo $m$. The recursion is therefore

$$
  x_t = 16807 x_{t-1} \pmod{2^{31} - 1}
$$

This LCG passes many of the standard statistical test and has been successfully used in many applications. For this reason it is sometimes viewed as the minimal standard LCG, against which other generators are compored.
</MathBox>

### Multiple-recursive generators

A multiple-recursive generator (MRG) of order $k$, is a random number generator with state space $S = \set{0,\dots,m-1}^k$ for some modulus $m\in\N_+$. The state at time $t$ is the vector $(x_{t-1}, \dots, x_{t-k}) \in S$, and the sequence $(x_t)$ is given by the linear recurrence

$$
\begin{equation*}
  x_t = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i x_{t-i}  \pmod m,\; t \geq k
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the multipliers $\set{a_i}_{i=1}^k$ lie in the set $\set{0,\dots,m-1}$. The output sequence $(u_t)$ typically obtained by normalization

$$
  u_t = \frac{x_t}{m} \in [0,1),\; t\geq k
$$

The maximal possible period length for this generator is $m^k - 1$, which is attained if $m$ is a prime number and the characteristic polynomial 
$$
  p(z)) = z^k - \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} a_i z^{k-i}
$$

is a primitive root modulo $m$.

### Matrix congruential generators

A multiple-recursive generator of order $k$ can be equivalently represented as a matrix linear multiplicative congruential generator. In this formulation the state space is 

$$
  S = \set{0,\dots,m-1}^k \cong (\Z/m\Z)^k
$$

where $m\in\N_+$ is the modulus. The state sequence $(\mathbf{x}_t)_{t\geq 0}$ with $\mathbf{x}_t \in S$, evolves according to

$$
  \mathbf{x}_t = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}_{t-1} \bmod m,\; t\geq 1
$$

where $\mathbf{A} \in \mathcal{M}_k (\N_0)$ is a $k\times k$ matrix and the arithmetic is performed componentwise modulo $m$.

In the case of an MRG defined by linear congruence $\eqref{equation-1}$, the matrix $\mathbf{A}$ takes the companion form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix}
    0 & 1 \cdots & 0 \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\
    a_k & a_{k-1} & \cdots & a_1
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
$$

The matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is typically required to by invertible over $\Z/m\Z$, i.e. $\mathbf{A} \in\operatorname{GL}_k (\Z/m\Z)$, in order to avoid degeneracy and ensure maximal period properties. 

The output sequence $(\mathbf{u}_t)$ is often defined component by normalization

$$
  \mathbf{u}_t = \frac{\mathbf{x}_t}{m} \in [0,1)^k
$$

In this matrix formulation, an MRG can be interpreted as linear dynamical system over the finite ring $\Z/m\Z$ and allows its period and structural properties to be analyzed via linear algebra and finite field techniques.

### Binary linear generators

Since binary operations are in general more efficient than floating point operations (which in turn are faster than integer operations), it is natural to consider pseudo-random number generators that are based on linear recurrences over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_2$ (modulo $2$). In $\mathbb{F}_2$, addition is given by bitwise $\operatorname{XOR}$ and multiplication is bitwise $\operatorname{AND}$.

A general linear generator over $\mathbb{F}_2$ is defined by the binary state space $S = \set{0,1}^k \cong \mathbb{F}_2^k$ and two matrices $\mathbf{A}\in\set{0,1}^{k\times k}$ and $\mathbf{B}\in\set{0,1}^{w\times k}$. A state vector $\mathbf{x}_t = (x_{t,1},\dots,x_{t,k}) \in\mathbb{F}_2^k$ is updated in the following steps

1. **Initialize:** Draw the seed $\mathbf{x}_0 \sum\mu$, where $\mu$ is a probability distribution on $\mathbb{F}_2^k$. Set $t = 1$.
2. **Transition:** Set $\mathbf{x}_t = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}_{t-1}$
3. **Output:** Set $\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}_t$ and return the binary expansion
$$
  u_t = \sum_{l=1}^w Y_{t,l} 2^{-l} \in [0,1)
$$

4. **Repeat:** Set $t=t+1$ and return to step 2.

The integer $w$ can be thought of as the word length of the computer, e.g. $32$ or $64$ bit, while the state dimension $k$ is chosen significantly larger than $w$ in order to achieve long periods and good statistical properties.

<MathBox title="Linear feedback shift register generator" boxType="example">
The Tausworthe generator, als known as a linear feedback shift register (LFSR) generator is a special case of a multiple-recursive generator over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_2$, with binary linear recurrence

$$
  x_t = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i x_{t-i} \pmod 2,\; t\geq k
$$

where $a_i \in\set{0,1}$, and all operations are performed in $\mathbb{F}_2$. The output is constructe by grouping of bits from the underlying bit sequence $(x_t)$. For fixed integers $w \leq k$ and $s \geq 1$, define

$$
  u_t = \sum_{l=1}^w x_{ts + l - 1} 2^{-l},\; t \geq 0
$$

so that each $u_t \in [0,1)$ if formed from $w$ successive bits of sequence. The parameter $s$ is a decimation step, and a common choice is $s = w$, which yields non-overlapping blocks.

In vector form, the state at time $t$ is given by $\mathbf{x}_t = (x_{ts},\dots, x_{ts + k-1}) \in\mathbb{F}_2^k$, so that the output corresponds to the first $w$ components of $\mathbf{x}_t$, i.e.

$$
  \mathbf{u}_t = \sum_{l=1}^w (\mathbf{x}_t)_l 2^{-l},\; t\geq 0
$$

In this representation, the state transition is obtained by the linear transformation $\mathbf{x}_t = \mathbf{A}^s \mathbf{x}_{t-1}$, where $\mathbf{A}$ is the companion matrix $\eqref{equation-2}$ associated with the recurrence, and arithmetic is over $\mathbb{F}_2$. The output map is given by the projection $\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}_t$ for the matrix $\mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{I}_w \mathbf{0}_{w\times (k-w)} \end{bmatrix}$, where $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix and $\mathbf{0}$ is the zero matrix.

For computation efficiency, the recurrence is typically sparse, with only a few nonzero coefficients. A common special case is the two-tap recurrence

$$
  x_t = x_{t-r} \overset{2}{\oplus} x_{t-k},\; 1 \leq r < k
$$

where $\overset{2}{\oplus}$ denotes addition modulo $2$ (bitwise $\operatorname{XOR}$). In vector form, this becomes

$$
  \mathbf{x}_t = \mathbf{x}_{t-r} \overset{2}{\oplus} \mathbf{x}_{t-k}
$$

where addition is componentwise in $\mathbb{F}_2$.

The term linear feedback shift register reflects the fact that the generator admits an efficient implementation using shift registers: at each step, the state is shifted and a new bit is coputed as a linear ($\operatorname{XOR}$) combination of selectd previous bits.
</MathBox>

Generalizations of the LFSR generator include generalized feedback shift register generators and their twisted versions, of which the most popular are the Mersenne twisters (MT). A particular instance of the Mersenne twister, MT19937, has become widespread. It has a huge period length of $2^{19937} - 1$, is very fast, has good equidistributional properties, and passes most statistical tests. 

Two drawbacks of the Mersenne twisters are that the initialization procedure is rather complex to avoid correlated sequences. In addition, the algorithms recover slowly the states near zero, i.e. with low Hamming weights. Once the state contains only a few ones, the recurrence may require hundreds of thousands of iterations before returning to a state with a more balanced distribution of bits.

## Combined generators

Combined pseudo-random number generators form a new generator by combining the outputs of several underlying generators, through operations such as shuffling, addition, or output selection. Even if the individual generators are of poor quality, an appropriate combination can yield at generator with significantly superior statistical properties.

<MathBox title="Wichman-Hill generator" boxType="example">
One of the earliest combined generators is the Wichman-Hill generator, which combines three multiplicative congruential generators:

$$
\begin{align*}
  x_t =& 171 x_{t-1} \pmod m_1,\; m_1 = 30269 \\
  y_t =& 172 y_{t-1} \pmod m_1,\; m_1 = 30307 \\
  z_t =& 170 z_{t-1} \pmod m_1,\; m_1 = 30323
\end{align*}
$$

These three integers are then merged into a single uniform variate by

$$
  u_t = \frac{x_t}{m_1} + \frac{y_t}{m_2} + \frac{z_t}{m_3} \pmod 1
$$

The period of the sequence of triples $(x_t, y_t, z_t)$ is given by

$$
  \frac{1}{4}(m_1 - 1)(m_2 - 1)(m_3 - 1) \approx 6.95\times 10^{12}
$$

which is much larger than the periods of the individual components. This combined generator is equivalent to a single multiplicative congruential generator with
- modulus $m = 7817185604309$
- multiplier $a = 16555425264690$
</MathBox>

A well‑studied family of combined generators is the class of combined multiple‑recursive generators (CMRGs), in which a small number of MRGs are combined to form a single generator. A key advantage of this construction is that it can be analyzed theoretically using the same framework as for a single MRG: with appropriate initialization, the output stream of a CMRG is exactly the same as that of a single MRG with a much larger period. Consequently, the quality of the generator can be assessed using the well‑understood theory of MRGs.

This structure also enables a systematic and principled search for good multipliers and moduli, leading to generators with excellent statistical properties. An additional practical benefit is that CMRGs naturally support the construction of high‑quality, easily managed multi‑stream generators.

L’Ecuyer has conducted an extensive numerical search and detailed theoretical analysis to find good combined MRGs. One of the combined MRGs that stood out was MRG32k3a, which employs two MRGs of order $3$ with distinct moduli. The two component sequences $(x_t)$ and $(y_t)$ are defined by

$$
\begin{align*}
  x_t =& (1403580 x_{t-2} - 810728 x_{t-3}) \bmod m_1,\; m_1 = 2^{32} - 209 \\
  y_t =& (527612 y_{t-1} - 1370589 y_{t-3}) \bmod m_2,\; m_2 = 2^{32} - 22853
\end{align*}
$$

The output sequence $(u_t)$ is obtained by combining the two components via

$$
  u_t = \begin{cases}
    \frac{x_t - y_t + m-1}{m_1 + 1},\quad& x_t \leq y_t \\
    \frac{x_t - y_t}{m_1 + 1},\quad& x_t > y_t
  \end{cases}
$$

which ensures that $u_t \in [0,1)$. The resulting generator has period

$$
  \frac{1}{3}(m_1^3 - 1)(m_2^3 - 1) \approx 3\times 10^{57}
$$

Empirically, MRG32k3a exhibits excellent statistical behavior and is known to pass stringent modern test suites (e.g., TestU01). It is therefore widely used in simulation, particularly in contexts requiring reproducibility, long periods, and the ability to construct independent random streams.

## Non-linear geneators

The notion that a deterministic system can imitate a random phenomenon motivates the use of chaotic dynamical systems to create random number generators. Such systems are defined by an iteration of the form $X_{n+1} = D(X_n)$, where $D: S\to S$ is a measurable transformation on a state space $S$. The chaotic behaviour is characterized by sensitivity to initial conditions. In particular, small perturbation of the initial state $X_0$ can lead to trajectories $(X_n)$ that diverge exponentially, making long-term prediction effectively impossible despite the deterministic nature of the system.

<MathBox title="Logistic function" boxType="example">
The logistic map $D_\alpha :[0,1] \to[0,1]$ defined by

$$
  D_\alpha (x) = \alpha x(1 - x)
$$

exhibit chaotic dynamics for a range of parameters $\alpha\in [0,4]$, with fully developed chaos occuring for parameters in a subset of $[3.57, 4]$. In the particular case $\alpha = 4$, the map $D_4$ preserves an absolutely continuous invariant probability measure $\mu$ on $[0,1]$, whose density with respect to the Lebesgue measure is given by

$$
  \frac{\d\mu}{\d x} (x) = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{x(1 - x)}},\; x\in (0,1)
$$

The dynamical system $([0,1], D_4, \mu)$ is ergodic and mixing, and is in factr metrically conjugate to the Bernoulli shift. Consequently, for $\mu$-almost every intial condition $X_0 \in [0,1]$, the sequence $(X_n)$ defined by $X_{n+1} = D_4 (X_n)$ satifies $X_n \sim\mu$ in the sense of time averages. This means that empirical distributions along typical distribution converge to $\mu$. 

Although the sequence $(X_n)$ is deterministic, its statistical behaviour, when viewed through ergodic averages, coincides with that of a stationary stochastic process whose marginal distribution is the arcsine law with density $1/\pi\sqrt{x(1 - x)}$.
</MathBox>

## Test for random number generators

The statistical qualities of pseudo-random number generators can be assessed in two ways
- Theoretical analysis studies intrinsic properties of the generator derived from its mathematical structure, without relying on simulated input. Typical criteria include the period length, lattice structure, and quantitative measures of uniformity and independence. Such analysis is feasible primarily for generators with a sufficiently tractable algebraic form, such as linear congruential generators (LCGs), multiple-recursive generators (MRGs), and their combinations.
- Empirical testing evaluates the generator by applying a battery of statistical tests to its output, with the aim of detecting deviations from the behavior of an ideal independent and identically distributed uniform sequence. These tests probe properties such as marginal uniformity, serial dependence, and higher-order correlations. The most complete contemporary battery for the empirical testing of random number generators is the *TestU01* software library by L'Ecuyer adn Simard.

In practice, both perspectives are complementary: theoretical guarantees provide structural insight, while empirical tests help detect deficiencies that may not be captured analytically.

The ultimate objective is to construct generators whose output is computationally indistinguishable, for all practical purposes, from a sequence of independent $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$ random variables. Accordingly, a candidate generator should successfully pass a wide and stringent collection of statistical tests designed to assess uniformity and independence.

<MathBox title="Two-stage empirical test for randomness" boxType="algorithm">
Suppose that $\mathbf{u} = \set{u_i}$ represents the output stream of the uniform random number generator. Let $\mathrm{H}_0$ be the hypothesis that the $\set{u_i}$ are independently and identically distributed from $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$. Let $Z$ be som deterministic functional of $\mathbf{u}$.
1. **Test construction:** Generate $N$ independent copies $Z_1,\dots,Z_N$ of $Z$ and evaluate a test statistic $T = T(Z_1,\dots,Z_N)$ for testing $\mathrm{H}_0$ versus the alternative that $\mathrm{H}_0$ is not true. Suppose that, under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the test statistic $T$ has distribution or asymptotic distribution (as $N\to\infty$) $\mathcal{D}$.
2. **Meta-test:** Repeat the first stage $K$ times independently to obtain $T_1,\dots,T_K$ of $T$ and perform a goodness of fit test to evaluate the hypothesis that the $\set{T_i}$ are independent and identically distributed from $\mathcal{D}$.
</MathBox>

In the two stage-stage statistical test, the first stage corresponds to an ordinary statistical test, such as a $\chi^2$ goodness of fit test, and the second state aggregrates the results of $K$ such tests by means of a higher-level goodness-of-fit test, such as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov or Anderson-Darling test.

In the following tests, we construct derived sequences and processs from the outpu $\set{u_i}$ of the generator:
- **Discretized sequence:** Define $y_i = \lfloor mu_i \rfloor$, $i \geq 0$, for some integer $m\geq 1$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the sequence $\set{y_i}$ is i.i.d. with the discrete uniform distribution on $\set{0,\dots,m-1}$.
- **Vectorized continuous sequence:** Define $\mathbf{u}_i = (u_{id},\dots,u_{id+d-1})$, $i\geq 0$ for some dimension $d\geq 1$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$ the vectors $\set{\mathbf{u}_i}$ are i.i.d. with the uniform distribution on the $d$-dimensional hypercube $(0,1)^d$.
- **Vectorized discrete sequence:** Define $\mathbf{y}_i = (y_{id},\dots,y_{id+d-1})$, $i\geq 0$ for some dimension $d\geq 1$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$ the vectors $\set{\mathbf{y}_i}$ are i.i.d. with the discrete uniform distribution on $\set{0,1\dots,m-1}^d$.

### Equidistribution tests

Equidistribution (or frequency) test aims to assess whether the sequence $\set{u_i}$ follows a uniform distribution $\mathcal{u}([0,1])$. Two standard approaches are:
1. Apply a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to ascertain whether the emipirical cumulative distribution function of $(u_i)$ matches the theoretical cumulative distribution function of $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
$$
  F(x) = x$ for $0 \leq x \leq 1
$$

2. Apply a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test on the discretized sequence $\set{y_i}$, comparing for each class $k=0,\dots,m-1$ the observed number of occurences in class
$$
  O_k = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} I_{y_i = k}
$$

with the expected number $E_k = n/m$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the $\chi^2$ statistic

$$
  T = \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} \frac{(O_k - E_k)^2}{E_k}
$$

asymptotically follows a $\chi_{m-1}^2$ distribution.

### Serial tests

Serial tests are designed to assess whether successive values of the random number generator are uniformly distributed. Specifically, for a given dimension $d \geq 1$ and size $m\geq 1$ construct the discretized sequence of vectors $\set{\mathbf{y}_i}_{i=0}^{n-1}$. For each $\mathbf{y} \in\set{0,\dots,m-1}^d$, count the number of occurences

$$
  O_\mathbf{y} = \sum_i \mathbf{1}_{\mathbf{y}_i = \mathbf{y}}
$$

and compare these observed counts with the expected value $E_\mathbf{y} = n/m^m$. The comparison can be performed using a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test

$$
  T = \sum_{\mathbf{y}\in\set{0,\dots,m-1}^d} \frac{(O_\mathbf{y} - E_\mathbf{y})^2}{E_\mathbf{y}}
$$

which, under $\mathrm{H}_0$, asymptotically follows a $\chi_{m^d-1}^2$ distribution.

For the validity of the approximation, it is generally recommended that each expected count satisfies $E_\mathbf{y} \geq 5$, which implies $n\geq 5m^d$. In practice, the dimension $d$ is kept small (typically $d=2,3$) to ensure sufficient sample size and statistical power.

Serial test therefore probe joint uniformity and short-range dependence in the sequence.

### Gap test

Let $(T_i)_{i\geq 1}$ denote the times when the output process $(u_i)$ visits a fixed interval $(\alpha,\beta)\subset (0,1)$ and define the gap lengths $(Z_i)$ by

$$
  Z_i = T_i - T_{i-1} - 1,\; i\geq 1
$$

with the convention $T_0 = 0$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the sequence $\set{Z_i}$ is i.i.d. with a geometric distribution $\operatorname{Geom}_0 (p)$ on $\N_0$ with parameter $p = \beta - \alpha$, i.e.

$$
  \mathbb{P}(Z = z) = p(1 - p)^z,\; z\geq 0
$$

The gap test assesses this hypothesis by grouping observed gap lengths into classes and comparing observed and expected frequencies. In particular, a $\chi^2$ test is performed with classes $Z = 0,\dots Z = r-1$, and $Z \geq r$, with corresponding probabilities

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}(Z = z) =& p(1 - p)^z,\; z = 0,\dots, r-1 \\
  \mathbb{P}(Z\geq r) =& (1-p)^r
\end{align*}
$$

To ensure validity of the $\chi^2$ approximation, the integers $n$ (number of gaps) and $r$ should be chosen so that the expected class frequency is $\geq 5$. 

Special cases of gap tests include
- $(\alpha \beta) = (0, 1/2)$: *runs above the mean test*
- $(\alpha, \beta) = (1/2, 1)$: *runs below the mean test*

The gap test is particularly useful for detecting deviations from independence in the spacing between occurrences of specified events.

### Partition (poker) tests

Consider the discretized sequence of $d$-dimensional vectors $(\mathbf{y}_i)_{i=1}^n$, each taking values in $\set{0,\dots,m-1}^d$. For a given vector $\mathbf{y}$, let $Z$ be the number of distinct components. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the components of $\mathbf{y}$ are i.i.d. uniformly distributed on $\set{0,\dots,m-1}$, and the distribution of $Z$ is given by

$$
  \mathbb{P}(Z = z) = \frac{m(m-1) \cdots (m-z+1)  {d \brace z}}{m^d},\; z=1,\dots,\min\set{d,m}
$$

Here ${d \brace z}$ denotes the Stirling number of the second kind, which gives the number of ways a set of size $d$ can be partitioned into $z$ non-empty subsets. An explicit formula Stirling in terms of binomial coefficients is

$$
  {d \brace z} = \frac{1}{z!} \sum_{k=0}^z (-1)^{z-k} \binom{z}{k} k^d
$$

The partition (or poker) test proceeds by computing the observed frequencies of the values $Z_i$ and comparing with the expected probabilties above using a $\chi^2$ test.

This test is sensitive to deviations from independence and uniformity, as it captures the tendency of components within a vector to repeat or cluster, analogous to patterns observed in poker hands.

### Coupon collector's test

Consider the discrete sequence $(y_i)_{i\geq 1}$, where eah $y_i \in\set{0,\dots,m-1}$. Let $T$ denoted the waiting time until all possible $m$ values have appeared at least once. That is, $T$ is the smallest integer such that $\set{y_1,\dots,y_T} = \set{0,\dots,m-1}$. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the probability that $(y_i)_{i=1}^t$ is still incomplete is

$$
  \mathbb{P}(T > t) = 1 - \frac{m!}{m^t} {t \brace m}
$$

and thus

$$
  \mathbb{P}(T = t) = \frac{m!}{m^t} {t-1 \brace m-1},\; t\geq m
$$

where ${t \brace m}$ denotes the Stirling number of the second kind.

The coupon collector's test proceeds by generating successive waiting times $(T_i)_{i=1}^n$ and comparing the observed frequencies with the theoretical distribution using a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test. Specifically, 
we group the waiting times into classes $T=m,\dots,r-1$ and $T > r-1$, with probabilities given above. The parameter $r$ should be chosen so that each expected class frequency is sufficiently large to justify the $\chi^2$ approximation.

This test is sensitive to deviations from uniformity and independence, as it captures how quickly the generator explores the full set of possible values.

### Permutation tests

Condiser the $d$-dimensional random vector $\mathbf{u} = (u_1,\dots,u_d)$. Let $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ denote the permutation of indices $\set{1,\dots,d}$ that order the components of $\mathbf{u}$ from smallest to largest. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the components of $\mathbf{u}$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, and all orderings are equally likely. Hence,

$$
  \mathbb{P}(\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi}) = \frac{1}{d!}
$$

for all permutations $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ of $\set{1,\dots,d}$.

The permutation test assesses this property by computing the observed frequencies of the permutations $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ obtained from successive vectors $\mathbf{u}_i$ and comparing them to the uniform distribution over the $d!$ possible permutations. This comparison is typically carried out with a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test with $d!$ classes, each with expected probability $1/d!$. In practive, the dimension $d$ must remain small, e.g. $d\leq 5$, to ensure that the expected number of observations per class is sufficiently large for the validity of the $\chi^2$ approximation.

### Run tests

Consider the sequence $(u_i)_{i\geq 1}$. Let $Z$ be the run-up length given by $Z = \min\set{k \geq 1 | u_{k+1} < u_k}$, which is the length a maximal increasing run. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, where $u_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{U}([0,1])$, the distribution of $Z$ satisfies

$$
  \mathbb{P}(Z \geq z) = \frac{1}{z!},\; z\geq 1
$$

and hence

$$
  \mathbb{P}(Z = z) = \frac{1}{z!} - \frac{1}{(z + 1)!},\; z\geq 1
$$

The runs test proceeds by generating a sequence of run lengths $(Z_i)_{i=1}^n$ and comparing their empirical distribution with the theoretical one using a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test.

To ensure independence between successive runs, it is important to restart the sampling after each run. This is practically done by throwing away the number immediately after a run. If the $i$th run has length $Z_i$, the second run is started at $u_{Z_i + 2}$ rather than $u_{Z_i + 1}$. Since $u_{Z_i + 1}$ is known to be smaller than $U_{Z_i}$, it is conditionally biased and therefore not $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$-distributed.

This test is sensitive to deviations from independence, particularly in the form of monotonic trends or local ordering structure in the sequence.

### Maximum-of-$d$ tests

Generate the sequence of vectors $(\mathbf{u}_i)_{i=1}^n$ in $[0,1]^d$ for some dimension $d$. For each $\mathbf{u}_i = (u_{i1},\dots,u_{id})$ let $Z_i = \max\set{u_{i1},\dots,u_{id}}$ be the maximum statistic. Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the random variable $Z_i$ has cumulative distribution function

$$
  F(z) = \mathbb{P}(Z_i \leq z) = z^d\; 0\leq z\leq 1
$$

We can apply the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to $(Z_i)$ with distribution function $F(z)$. Another option is to define the transformed variables $W_i = Z_i^d$. Since $W_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{U}([0,1])$ under $\mathrm{H}_0$, we can apply the equidistribution test to $(W_i)_{i=1}^n$.

### Collision tests

Consider a sequence of $d$-dimensional discrete vectors $(\mathbf{y}_i)_{i=1}^n$, each taking values in $\set{0,\dots,m-1}^d$. There are $r = m^d$ possible values for each $\mathbf{y}_i$, and typically, $r \gg b$. This situation can be interpreted as  throwing $b$ balls into $r$ urns. Since there are many more urns than balls, most balls land in empty urns, but sometimes two or more balls land in the same urn, creating a collision. Let $C$ be the number of such collisions. Uner $\mathrm{H}_0$, the probability of observing $c$ collisions is given by

$$
  \mathbb{P}(C = c) = \frac{(r(r-1)\cdots(r-(b-c)+1) {b \brace b-c}}{r^b}\; c=0,\dots,b-1
$$

where ${b \brace b-c}$ is a Stirling number of the second kind.

A $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test can be applied to compare the empirical distribution of $n$ such collision values $(C_i)_{i=1}^n$. In practice, it can be necessary to group various of the classes $C = c$ in order to obtain a sufficient number of observations in each class for the $\chi^2$ approximation to be valid.

### Rank of binary matrix tests

Transform the sequence $(u_i)_{i\geq 1}$ to a binary sequence $(b_i)_{i\geq 1}$ and arrange these bits in a binary array of dimension $r\times c$ (assume $r \leq c$). Under $\mathrm{H}_0$, the distribution of the rank modulo $2$ of this matrix, denoted $Z$, is given by

$$
  \mathbb{P}(Z = z) = 2^{(c-z)(z-r)} \prod_{i=0}^{z-1} \frac{(1-2^{i-c})(1 - 2^{i-r})}{1-2^{i-z}},\; z=0,\dots,r
$$

This can be seen, for example, by defining a Markov chain $(Z_t)_{t\geq 0}$ starting at $Z_0 = 0$ and with transition probabilities 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  p_{i_,i} = 2^{-c+i},\quad p_{i,i+1} = 1 - 2^{-c+i},\; i=0,\dots,r
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Here $Z_t$ represents the rank of a $t\times c$ binary matrix constructed by adding a new random row to a $(t-1) \times c$ matrix. This row is either dependent on the $t-1$ previous previous rows (rank stays the same) or not (rank is increased by $1$). The distribution of $Z_r$ then corresponds to $\eqref{equation-2}$.

These probabilities can be compared with the observed frequencies, via a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test.

### Birthday spacing tests

Consider a discrete sequence $(y_i)_{i=1}^n$ taking values in $\set{0,\dots,m-1}$. Sort the sequences in increasing oder $y_{(1)} \leq\dots\leq y_{(n)}$ and define spacings $s_1 = y_{(2)} - y_{(1)}, \dots s_{n-1} = y_{(n)} y_{(n-1)}$ and $s_n = y_{(1)} + m - y_{(n)}$. Sort the spacings in increasing oder $s_{(1)} \leq\dots\leq s_{(n)}$.

Let $R$ be the number of repeated spacings

$$
  R = \# \set{j | s_{(j)} = s_{(j-1)}, j=1,\dots,n}
$$

The distribution of $R$ depends on $m$ and $n$. The birthday spacing test proceeds by repeating the experiment many times, and comparing the observed values of $R$ with its theoretical distribution via a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit-test. Asymptotically, for large $n$, then $R$ has a Poisson distribution $\mathcal{P}(\lambda)$, with $\lambda = n^3 /(4m)$, where the approximation is valid when $\lambda$ is not too large. 

An alternative is to set $N=1$ (a single trial) and base the decision whether to reject $\mathrm{H}_0$ or not on the approximate $p$-value 

$$
  \mathbb{P}(R\geq r) \approx 1 - \sum_{k=0}^{r-1} e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k /k!
$$

As a rule of thumb, the Poisson approximation is accurate when $m \geq (4N\lambda)^4$, i.e. $Nn^3 \leq m^{5/4}$. This ensures that the expected number of repeated spacings is small relative to the total number of possible values.

## One-dimensional random variables

### Inverse transform

<MathBox title="Generalized inverse function" boxType="definition">
Let $F:\R\to [0,1]$ be a non-decreasing continuous function (in particular, a cumulative distribution function). The generalized inverse (or quantile function) of $F$, denoted $F^{-1}$, is defined by

$$
  F^{-1} (y) = \inf\set{x: F(x) \geq u},\; 0\leq y\leq 1
$$
</MathBox>

In the measure-theoretic formulation of probability, many constructions can be reduced (up to distributional equivalence) to the canonical probability space $([0,1], \mathcal{B}, \lambda)$, where $\mathcal{B}$ are the Borel sets on $[0,1]$ and $\lambda = \mathcal{U}([0,1])$ denotes the Lebesgue uniform probability measure. Specifically, given a random variable $X: (\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}) \to (\mathcal{X}, \mathbb{A})$, there exists a measurable function $\tilde{X}: [0,1] \to \mathcal{X}$ such that $X$ and $\tilde{X}$ have the same law.

In the particular case $\mathcal{X} = \R$, if $F_X$ denotes the cumulative distribution function of $X$, we can take

$$
  \tilde{X}(u) = F_X^{-1} (u),\; u\in[0,1]
$$

where $F_X^{-1}$ is the generalized inverse defined by

$$
  F_X^{-1} (u) = \inf\set{x\in\R | F_X (x) \geq u}
$$

If $U \sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, the cumulative distribution function of the inverse transform $F_X^{-1} (U)$ is given by

$$
  \mathbb{P}(F_X^{-1} (U) \leq x) = \mathbb{P}(U \leq F_X(x)) = F_X(x)
$$

such that the random variable $\tilde{X}(U)$ has the same distribution as $X$. Using the inverse method, a random variable $X$ with cumulative distribution function $F_X$ can be generated in the following steps:
1. Draw $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
2. Set $X = F_X^{-1} (U)$.

<MathBox title="Probability integral transform" boxType="proposition">
If $U \sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, then the random variable $F^{-1} (U)$ has the distribution $F$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

For all $u\in[0,1]$ and for all $x\in F^{-1} ([0,1])$, the generalized inverse satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  F (F^{-1} (u)) \geq& u \\
  F^{-1} (F(x) \leq& x
\end{align*}
$$

Thus

$$
  \set{(u, x) | F^{-1} (u) \leq x} = \set{(u, x) | F(x) \geq u}
$$

and

$$
  P(F^{-1} (U) \leq x) = P(U \leq F(x)) = F(x)
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Exponential variable generation" boxType="example">
If $X\sim \operatorname{Exp}(1)$ such that $F(x) = 1 - e^{-x}$, then solving for $x$ in $u = 1 - e^{-x}$ gives $x = -\ln(1 - u)$. Thus, if $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, the random variable $X = -\ln(U)$ has the exponential distribution, because $U$ and $1 - U$ are both uniform.
</MathBox>

The inverse-transform method also applies to discrete distributions. For a discrete random variable $X$ taking values $x_1 < x_2 < \dots$ with probabilities $p_1,p_2,\dots$, where $\sum_i p_i = 1$, the cumulative distribution function is a step function. In this case the inverse-method has the following steps:
1. Generate $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
2. Find the smallest positive integer $k\in\N_+$ such that $F(x_k) \geq U$, and return $X = x_k$.

### Affine transformation

Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$ be a real random vector, $\boldsymbol{A}\in \R^{m\times n}$ and $m\times n$ matrix, and $\boldsymbol{b}\in\R^m$ a $m$-dimensional vector. The random vector $\mathbf{Z} = \boldsymbol{A}\mathbf{X} + \boldsymbol{b}$ describes an affine transformation of $\mathbf{X}$. If $\mathbf{X}$ has an expectation vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X}$, then the expectation vector of $\mathbf{Z}$ is $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X} = \boldsymbol{A}\boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X} + \boldsymbol{b}$. If $\mathbf{X}$ has a covariance matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}_\mathbf{X}$, then the covariance matrix of $\mathbf{Z}$ is $\mathbf{\Sigma}_\mathbf{Z} = \boldsymbol{A} \mathbf{\Sigma}_\mathbf{X} \boldsymbol{A}^\top$. Finally, if $\boldsymbol{A}\in\operatorname{GL}_n (\mathbf{R})$ is an invertible $n\times n$ matrix and $\mathbf{X}$ has a probability density function $f_\mathbf{X}$, then the probability density function of $\mathbf{Z}$ is given by

$$
  f_\mathbf{Z} = \frac{f_\mathbf{X} (\boldsymbol{A}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{z} - \boldsymbol{b}))}{|\det(\boldsymbol{A})|},\; \boldsymbol{z}\in\R^n
$$

### Location-scale family

A location-scale family is a class of continuous probability distributions with densities $\set{f(x; \mu,\sigma) : \mu\in\R, \sigma > 0}$ of the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  f(x; \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma} \mathring{f} \left(\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \right),\; x\in\R
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\mathring{f}$ is a fixed probability density function, called the base (or standard) density. The parameter $\mu$ is called the location and $\sigma$ is called the scale. Special cases include:
- **Scale families:** $\mu = 0$, so $f(x;0,\sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma} \mathring{f}(x/\sigma)$
- **Location families:** $\sigma = 1$, so $f(x;\mu,1) = \mathring{f}(x-\mu)$

In a location-scale family the graph of $f(\cdot;\mu,\sigma)$ has the same shape as that of $\mathring{f}$, but is shifted by $\mu$ and scaled horizontally by a factor $\sigma$, with a corresponding vertical normalization $1/\sigma$.

Location-scale families of distributions arise from affine transformations. Let $Z = \mu + \sigma X$, where $X\sim \mathrin{f}$ is distributed according to the base density. Then $Z$ has density $\eqref{equation-3}$. In particular, if $X\sim\mathring{f} \equiv f(\cdot;0,1)$ then

$$
  \mu + \sigma X \sim f(\cdot; \mu,\sigma)
$$

Thus, sampling from a location-scale family reduces to the steps:
1. Sample $X$ from the base distribution $\mathring{f}$
2. Apply the affine transformation $Z = \mu + \sigma X$

<MathBox title="Normal distribution and location-scale" boxType="example">
The normal family of distributions $\set{\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)}$ with location parameter $\mu$ and scale parameter $\sigma$ has densities of the form

$$
  f(x;\mu,\sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma} \mathring{f} \left(\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^2} \right)
$$

with base density

$$
  \mathring{f}(x) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-x^2 / 2}
$$
</MathBox>

### Reciprocation

Let $X$ be a univariate random variable and define its reciprocal transformation by

$$
  Z = X^{-1} = \frac{1}{X}
$$

assuming $X \neq 0$ almost surely. If $X$ has probability density function $f_X$, then the density of $Z$ is given by

$$
  f_Z (z) = \frac{f_X (z^{-1})}{z^2},\; z\in\R
$$

Distributions obtained in this way are called inverted or inverse distributions.

<MathBox title="Inverse-gamma distribution" boxType="example">
The inverse-gamma distribution $\operatorname{InvGamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$ has probability density function

$$
  f_Z (z;\alpha,\lambda) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha z^{-\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda z^{-1}}}{\Gamma(\alpha)},\; z > 0
$$
</MathBox>

### Truncation

Let $\mathcal{D}_A$ be a distribution on a set $A$, and let $B\subseteq A$. Let $\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{D}_A$, and suppose $\mathbf{Z}$ is a random variable supported on $B$. If the conditional distribution of $\mathbf{X}$ given $\mathbf{X}\in B$ coincides with the distribution of $\mathbf{Z}$, then $\mathbf{Z}$ is said to follow the truncation of $\mathcal{D}_A$ to $B$. If $f_\mathbf{X}$ is the probability density function of $\mathbf{X}$, then the density of $\mathbf{Z}$ is

$$
  f_\mathbf{Z} (\boldsymbol{z}) = \frac{f_\mathbf{X}(\boldsymbol{z})}{\int_B f_\mathbf{X} (\boldsymbol{x}) \;\d\boldsymbol{x}},\; \mathbf{z}\in B
$$

In the continuous univariate case, truncation of a density $f$ to an interval $[a,b]$ yields

$$
  f_Z (x) = \frac{f(z)}{\int_a^b f(x) \;\d x},\; a\leq z \leq b
$$

In the discrete case, the integral is replaced with a sum over $B$. 

If $F$ is the cumulaive distribution function $X$, then the CDF of the truncated variable is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  F_Z (z) = \frac{F(z) - F(a_-)}{F(b) - F(a_-)},\; a\leq z \leq b
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $F(a_-) = \lim_{x\uparrow a} F(x)$. 

To generate random variables from a truncated distribution on $[a,b]$, we can simply use the acceptance-rejection method:
1. Sample $\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{D}_A$ repeatedly until $\mathbf{X} \in B$
2. Return $\mathbf{Z} = \mathbf{X}$

Inverting the truncated cumulative distribution function $\eqref{equation-4}$ yields the following inverse-tranform method:
1. Generate $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
2. Return $Z = F^{-1} (F(a_-) + U[F(b) - F(a_-)])$

The key difference from standard inverse-transform sampling is that the argument of $F^{-1}$ is uniformly distributed on $(F(a_-), F(b))$ rather than on $(0,1)$.

<MathBox title="Truncated exponential distribution" boxType="example">
The truncation of the exponential distribution $\operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)$ to the interval $[0,a]$ has density

$$
  f_Z (z) = \frac{\lambda e^-{\lambda z}}{1 - e^{-\lambda a}},\; 0\leq z \leq a
$$

The cumulative distribution function of $\operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)$ is $F(z) = 1 - e^{-\lambda z}$, with inverse

$$
  F^{-1} (u) = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \ln(1-u)
$$

Using the inverse transform method for truncation, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z =& F^{-1} (U \cdot (1 - e^{-\lambda a})) \\
  =& -\ln(1 + U(e^{-\lambda a} - 1)) \sim f_Z
\end{alignÆ}
$$

where $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Truncated normal distribution" boxType="example">
Consider the normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ truncated to the interval $[a,b]$ with density

$$
  f_Z (z) = \frac{1}{\sigma C} \phi\left(\frac{z - \mu}{\sigma} \right),\; a\leq z \leq b
$$

where the normalizing constant $C$ is given by

$$
  C = \Phi\left(\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma} \right) - \Phi\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma} \right)
$$

Here $\phi$ and $\Phi$ denoted the probability density function and cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, respectively.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Sampling from the tail of a normal distribution" boxType="example">
Consider the problem of sampling from the truncated normal PDF

$$
  f_Z (z) = \frac{\phi(z) \mathbf{1}_{\set{z \geq a}}}{\Phi(-a)},\; a > 0
$$

where $\phi$ and $\Phi$ denote the probability density function and cumulative distribution function of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, respectively. A direct application of the inverse-transform method gives

$$
  Z = \Phi^{-1} (\Phi(a) + U[1 - \Phi(a)]),\; U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1)
$$

However, when $a$ is large, this expression is numerically unstable, since $\Phi(a)$ is very close to $1$, leading to a loss of precision in the term $1 - \Phi(a)$. 

Using the symmetry of the normal distribution $1 - \Phi(a) = \Phi(-a)$, we obtain the equivalent representation

$$
  Z = -\Phi^{-1} [U\cdot \Phi(-a)],\; U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])
$$

which is significantly more stable because it evaluates $\Phi^{-1}$ in the left tail for small arguments near $0$. In general, numerical approximations of $\Phi^{-1}$ are more accurate and stable in the left tail (arguments near $0$) than in the extreme right tail (arguments near $1$).
</MathBox>

### Composition method

Let $T$ be an index set, and let $\set{H_t}_{t\in T}$ be a collection of CDFs (possibly multivariate). Suppose $G$ is the cumulutive distribution function of a distribution on $T$. Then

$$
  F(\boldsymbol{x}) = \int_T H_t (\boldsymbol{x}) \;\d G(t)
$$

is a again a CDF. The corresponding distribution is called a mixture distribution, with components $\set{H_t}_{t\in T}$. 

It is useful to interpret $G$ as the CDF of a random variable $T$ and $H_t$ as the conditional distribution of a random variable $\mathbf{X}_t$ given $T = t$. Then $F$ is the CDF of the random variable $\mathbf{X}_T$. In other words, if $T\sim G$ and $X_t \sim H_t$, then $X = X_T$ has CDF $F$. This yields the following generator
1. Generate the random variable $T$ according to the CDF $G$
2. Given $T = t$, generate $X$ from the CDF $H_t$

In many applications, $T$ takes values in a finite set $\set{1,\dots,n}$. In this case, $G$ is specified by probabilities $\set{p_t}_{t=1}^n$ with $\sum_{t=1}^n p_t = 1$, and the mixture CDF is of the form 

$$
  F(x) = \sum_{t=1}^n p_t F_t (x)
$$

for some collection of CDFs $\set{F_t}$. If the component distributions have densities $\set{f_t}$, then the finite mixture has density

$$
  f(x) = \sum_{t=1}^n p_t f_t (x)
$$

<MathBox title="Composition method in Bayesian inference" boxType="example">
Consider a Bayesian model for a binomial experiment. Let $\theta\in (0,1)$ denote the probability of success and let $X$ be the number of successes in $n$ independent trials. The joint distribution of $X$ and $\theta$ is defined via the hierarchical model
- **Prior:** $\theta\sim \operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, with $\alpha,\beta > 0$
- **Likelihood:** $(X|\theta) \sim \operatorname{Bin}(n,\theta)$

The (unconditional) distribution of $X$ is obtained by integrating over $\theta$

$$
  f(x) = \int f(x|\theta) f(\theta) \;\d\theta,\; x=0,\dots,n
$$

where $f(\theta)$ is the density of the $\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ distribution and $f(x|\theta)$ is the density of the $\operatorname{Bin}(n,\theta)$ distribution. This defines a continuous mixture distribution. The resulting marginal distribution of $X$ is know as the Beta-Binomial distribution.

The hierchical structure of thiss Bayesian model directly yields a sampling procedure through the composition method: 
1. Sample $\theta \sim\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$
2. Given $\theta$, sample $X \sim\operatorname{Bin}(n,\theta)$.

The resulting random variable $X$ has the marginal distribution $f(x)$.
</MathBox>

### Polar transformation

The polar method is based on the transformation from polar to Cartesian coordinates 

$$
\begin{align*}
  X =& R\cos(\Theta) \\
  Y =& R\sin(\Theta)
\end{align*}
$$

where $\Theta\sim \mathcal{U}([0,2\pi))$ and $R \geq 0$ is an independent random variable with density $f_R$. By the change-of-variables formula, the join density of $(X, Y)$ is

$$
  f_{X,Y} (x,y) = \frac{f_R (r)}{2\pi r}
$$

with $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$.

The marginal density of $X$ is obtained by integrating over $y$

$$
  f_X (x) = \int_0^\infty \frac{f_R (\sqrt{x^2 + y^2)}{\pi\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} \;\d y
$$

In particular, if 

$$
  f_R (r) = re^{-r^2 /2},\; r\geq 0
$$

the joint density of $(X, Y)$ becomes

$$
  f_{X,Y} (x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2}
$$

so that $X, Y \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ are independent standard normal variables.

In this case, $R^2 \sim 2E$ with $E\sim\operatorname{Exp}(1)$, so that $R \overset{d}{=} \sqrt{2E}$, or, equivalently, $R \overset{d}{=} \sqrt{-2\ln(U)}$ with $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$. This leads to the Box-Muller method for generating standard normal random variables

$$
\begin{align*}
  X =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \cos(2\pi U_2) \\
  Y =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \sin(2\pi U_2) \\
\end{align*}
$$

where $U_1, U_2 \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{U}([0,1])$.

A useful extension arises by introducing and additional radial scaling. Specifically, suppose $R\in[0,\infty)$ and $Z_1,Z_2 \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ are independent random variables, and define

$$
  (X_1, X_2) = R(Z_1, Z_2) = (RZ_1, RZ_2)
$$ 

Then $(X_1, X_2)$ is radially symmetric, whose radius satisfies

$$
  \sqrt{X_1^2 + X_2^2} = R\sqrt{Z_1^2 + Z_2^2}
$$

Since $Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 \sim 2E$ with $E\sim\operatorname{Exp}(1)$ independent of $R$, we obtain

$$
  \sqrt{X_1^2 + X_2^2} \overset{d}{=} R\sqrt{2E}
$$

This representation shows that the radial distribution of $(X_1, X_2)$ is determined by the product $R\sqrt{2E}$. For suitable choices of $R$, this product has a tractable distribution, which can be exploitet to construct efficient sampling algorithms for $X_1$ (and $X_2$).

## Table lookup method

One of the fastest general methods for generating discrete random variables is Marsaglia's table lookup method
1. Sample $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$
2. Set $I = \lceil Un \rceil$
3. Return $\mathbf{X} = a_I$

here $(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ is a predetermined table of numbers or, more generally, objects such as vectors, trees, or other structures. Duplication among the $\set{a_i}$ is allowed. If the set of distinct objects is $\set{b_1,\dots,b_k}$, then the algorithm generates random variables $\mathbf{X}$ with probabilities

$$
  \mathbb{P}(\mathbf{X} = b_i) = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{a_j = b_i}}{n} = \frac{\#\set{j: a_j = b_i}}{n},\; i=1,\dots,k
$$

The table lookup method is a resampling technique: given data $\set{a_i}$, each draw selects one of the $a_i$ uniformly and independently. In other words, the method generates samples from the empirical distribution of the data $\set{a_i}$.

## Alias method

The alias method is an alternative to the inverse-transform method for generating discrete random variables, which avoids the need time-consuming cumulative probability lookups. The method is based on the fact that any discrete distribution on $n$ can be represented as an equally weighted mixture of $n$ two-point distributions. Specifically, the algorithm redistributes the original probability mass into $n$ bins of equal weight $1/n$.

Let $\set{p_k}_{k=1}^n$ be a probability distribution on $\set{1,\dots,n}$, i.e. $p_k \geq 0$ and $\sum_{k=1}^n p_k = 1$.
- If $p_1 =\dots = p_n$, then the original distribution is already uniform, and can trivially be expressed an equal mixture of $1$-point (and hence $2$-point) distributions. 
- If not all $\set{p_k}$ are equal, there must exist indices $i$ and $j$ such that $p_i < 1/n$ and $p_j \geq 1/n$. To form the alias structure, we fill bin $i$ by assigning it probability $p_i$ and supplementing it with an amount $1/n - p_i$ taken from $p_j$. After this step, the remaining $n-1$ bins can be treated recursively, using the same procedure with the remaining probabilities.

At the end of this procedure, each bin $k=1,\dots,n$ corresponds to a $2$-point distribution at the points $k$ and another point $a_k$ with probabilities $q_k$ and $1 - q_k$, respectively. The $\set{a_k}$ are called the alias values and the $\set{q_k}$ the cut-off values. These can be determined by the following setup algorithm

Let $\set{p_k}_{k=1}^n$ be a distribution on $\set{1,\dots,n}$.
1. Initialize scaled probabilities $q_k = np_k$ for $k=1,\dots,n$. Let $I = \set{k: q_k < 1}$ and $J = \set{k: q_k \geq 1}$
2. While $I$ and $J$ are non-empty
    - Choose some $i\in I$ and $j\in J$
    - Set $a_i = j$ and $q_j = q_j (1 - q_i)$
    - If $q_j < 1$, remove $j$ from $J$ and add to $I$
    - Remove item $i$ from $I$
    
This algorithm runs in $O(n)$ time, producing arrays $\set{q_k}$ and $\set{a_k}$ that allow for effient sampling

Once the alias and cut-off values have been constructed, a random variable $X$ with distribution $\set{p_k}$ can be generated in tow steps
1. Generate $U \sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$ and set $K = \lceil nU \rceil
2. Sample $V \sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$. If $V \leq q_k$, return $X = K$. Otherwise, return $X = a_K$

This procedure generates $X$ with $\mathbb{P}(X = k) = p_k$, and each sample is produced in constant $O(1)$ time.

## Acceptance-rejection methods

The acceptance-rejection method is a versatile for sampling from general distributions. It applies to discrete, continuous, and even multidimensional distributions, although its efficiency rapidly decreases in the number of dimension.

<MathBox title="Acceptance-rejection" boxType="proposition">
Let $f(\mathbf{x})$ and $g(\mathbf{x})$ be probability density functions on a common space, and suppose there exists a constant $C \geq 1$ such that 

$$
  f(\boldsymbol{x}) \leq Cg(\boldsymbol{x}),\; \forall \boldsymbol{x}
$$

Let $\mathbf{X} \sim g$ and $U\sim \mathcal{U}([0,1])$ be independent. Then, the conditional distribution of $\mathbf{X}$ given 

$$
  U \leq \frac{f(\mathbf{X})}{Cg(\mathbf{X})}
$$

is $f(\boldsymbol{x})$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The joint probaility density function of $(\mathbf{X}, U)$ restricted to the acceptance region is

$$
\begin{align*}
  f_{\mathbf{X}, U} (\boldsymbol{x}, u) =& \frac{g(\boldsymbol{x}) \mathbf{1}_{\Set{u\leq \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}}}{\int\int_0^1 g(\boldsymbol{x}) \mathbf{1}_{\set{u \leq \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}}} \\
  =& \frac{g(\boldsymbol{x}) \mathbf{1}_{\Set{u\leq \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}}}{\int g(\boldsymbol{x}) \left(\int_0^{\frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}} 1 \;\d u \right)} \\
  =& C g(\boldsymbol{x}) \mathbf{1}_{\Set{u} \leq \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}
\end{align*}
$$

The marginal PDF of $\mathbf{X}$ is therefore

$$
\begin{align*}
  f_\mathbf{X} (\boldsymbol{x}) =& \int_0^1 f_{\mathbf{X}, U} (\boldsymbol{x}, u) \;\d u \\
  =& C g(\boldsymbol{x}) \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})} = f(\boldsymbol{x})
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</MathBox>

We call $g(\mathbf{x})$ the proposal distribution and assume that it is easy to sample from. The acceptance-rejection method can be formulates as follows

1. Generate $\mathbf{X} \sim g(\boldsymbol{x})$
2. Generate $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, independently of $\mathbf{X}$
3. If $U \leq f(\mathbf{X})/[Cg(\mathbf{x})]$ accept $\mathbf{X}$; otherwise, reject it and return to step 1

In other words, each candidate $\mathbf{X} \sim g$ is accepcted with probability $f(\mathbf{X})/(Cg(\mathbf{X}))$, and otherwise discarded.

The efficiency of the acceptance-rejection method is defined as the probability of acceptance, i.e.

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{P}\left(C \leq \frac{f(\mathbf{X})}{Cg(\mathbf{X})} \right) =& \int g(\boldsymbol{x}) \int_0^1 \mathbf{1}_{\Set{\frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{Cg(\boldsymbol{x})}}} \;\d u \d\boldsymbol{x} \\
  =& \int \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x})}{C} \;\d \boldsymbol{x} = \frac{1}{C}
\end{align*}
$$

Since the trials are independent, the number of trials required to obtain a successful pair $(\mathbf{X}, U)$ has a $\operatorname{Geom}(1/C)$ distribution, so that the expected number of trials is equal to $C$.

## Generation methods for multivariate random variables

Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$ be a random vector in $\R^n$ with joint density $f(\boldsymbol{x})$. 

### Independent components

If the components $X_i$ are mutually independent, then the joint density factorizes as

$$
  f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \prod_{i=1}^n f_i (x_i)
$$

where $f_i$ is the marginal distribution of $X_i$. In this case, sampling from $f$ reduces to sampling each component independently.
1. Independently generate $X_i \sim f_i$ for $i=1,\dots,n$
2. Return $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$

### Dependent components

For dependent components $X_i$, the joint density admits the chain rule factorization

$$
  f(\boldsymbol{x}) = f_1 (x_1) f_2(x_2|x_1) \cdots f_n (x_n|x_1,\dots,x_{n-1})
$$

where 
- $f_1 (x)$ is the marginal density of $X_1$
- $f_k (x_k|x_1,\dots,x_{k-1})$ is the conditional density of $X_k$ given $X_1=x_1,\dots,X_{k-1} = x_{k-1}$

This factorization yields to the following sequential sampling procedure
1. Generate $X_1 \sim f_1$. Set $t = 1$
2. While $t < n$, given $X_1 = x_1,\dots,X_t = X_t$, generate $X_{t+1} \sim f_{t+1} (x_{t+1}|x_1,\dots,x_t)$ and set $t = t + 1$
3. Return $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$

The applicability of this approach depends on whether the conditional densities $f_k (\cdot|x_1,\dots,x_{k-1})$ are explicitly know and easy to sample from. An especially important class of modesl satisfying this structure is given by Markov models, in which the dependence simplifies according to the Markov property

$$
  f_k (x_k|x_1,\dots,x_{k-1}) = f_k (x_k|x_{k-1})
$$

When the conditional distributions are difficult to compute or sample from, an alternative is to use multidimensional acceptance–rejection methods, although their efficiency typically deteriorates rapidly with increasing dimension.

More generally, for high-dimensional distributions, constructing efficient exact sampling algorithms is often infeasible. In such settings, one typically resorts to approximate methods, such as Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) or variational techniques.

## Generating random vectors uniformly distributed in a unit hyperball and hypersphere

Let

$$
  B_n = \set{\boldsymbol{x} \in\R^n : \norm{\boldsymbol{x}} \leq 1}
$$

denote the $n$-dimensional unit hyperball, and

$$
  S^{n-1} = \set{\boldsymbol{x}\in\R^n : \norm{\boldsymbol{x}} = 1}
$$

the corresponding unit hypersphere.

### Sampling from the unit hyperball via acceptance-rejection

A direct method for sampling uniformly from $B_n$ uses acceptance-rejection from the hypercube $[-1,1]^n$
1. Generate $U_1,\dots,U_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{U}(0,1)$
2. Set $X_i = 1 - 2U_i$ for $i=1,\dots,n$ and compute
$$
  R = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2
$$

3. If $R\leq 1$, accept $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$; otherwise, reject and return to step 1.

The efficiency of this $n$-dimensional acceptance-rejection method equal the ratio of volumes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{1}{C} =& \frac{\operatorname{vol}(B_n)}{\operatorname{vol}([-1,1]^n)} = \frac{\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{(n/2)\Gamma(n/2)}}{2^n} \\
  =& \frac{1}{n 2^{n-1}} \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2)}
\end{align*}
$$

This probability decays rapidly to $0$ as $n\to\infty$, making the method inefficient in high dimensions. 

A more efficient approach using Gaussian vectors relies on two key facts
- If $X_1,\dots,X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then the normalized vector

$$
  \mathbf{Y} = \left(\frac{X_1}{\norm{\mathbf{X}}},\dots,\frac{X_n}{\norm{X}} \right)
$$

is uniformly distributed on the hypersphere $S^{n-1}$.

- If $\boldsymbol{Z}$ is uniformly distributed in $B_n$, then its radius $R = \norm{\mathbf{Z}}$ has cumulative distribution function $F_R (r) = r^n$ for $0\leq r\leq r$, and hence admits the representation $R = U^{1/n}$, where $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$

Combining these facts yields the following algorithm
1. Generate a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$ with i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ components
2. Generate $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$ and set $R = U^{1/n}$
3. Return $\mathbf{Z} = R\mathbf{X}/\norm{X}$

### Sampling from the unit hypersphere

To generate a random vector that is uniformly distributed on the unit hypersphere $S_n$, the procure simplifies to
1. Generate a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$ with i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ components.
2. Return $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}/\norm{\mathbf{X}}$

### Generating random permutations

Suppose we have a collection of $n$ distinct object, labeled $1,\dots,n$ and we wish to generate a uniform random permutation, i.e. a random ordering in which each of the $n!$ possible orderings occurs with equal probability $1/n!$. 

A simple algorithm for generating such uniform permutations is based on ordering independent uniform random variables
1. Generate $U_1,\dots,U_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{U}(0,1)$
2. Let $(X_1,\dots,X_n)$ be the permutation of $\set{1,\dots,n}$ that sort $U_i$ in increasing order $U_{X_1} \leq\cdots\lea U_{X_n}$
3. Return the permutation $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$

Although simple, this algorithm is not computationally efficient as it requires sorting and therefore runs in $O(n \ln(n))$ time.

A more efficient algorithm constructs the permutation sequentially, requiring only $n$ uniform random numbers and no sorting
1. Initialize an array $\boldsymbol{a} = (1,\dots,n)$
2. For $i=1,\dots,n$
    - Generate an index $I$ unformly from $\set{1,\dots,n-i +1}$
    - Set $X_i = a_I$
    - Set $a_I = a_{n-i+1}$ (i.e. remove the selected element by swapping with the last available entry)
5. Return $\mathbf{X} = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$

This method runs in $O(n)$ time, since it avoids sorting and performs only a constant amount of work per iteration. Its correctness follows from the fact that at each step, every remaining element is selected with equal probability, ensuring that all $n!$ permutations are equally likely.

## General transformation methods

<MathBox title="Box-Muller algorithm" boxType="example">
Let $X_1, X_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ be independent standard normal random variables. Then the random vector $(X_1, X_2)$ has a rotationally invariant distribution on $\R^2$, with joint density

$$
  f(x_1, x_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \exp\left(-\frac{x_1^2 + x_2^2}{2} \right)
$$

Transforming to polar coordinates $(R, \Theta)$, defined by

$$
  R^2 = X_1^2 + X_2^2,\quad \Theta = \atan\left(\frac{X_2}{X_1} \right)
$$

it follows that $R^2 \sim\chi_2^2$ and $\Theta\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$, with $R$ and $\Theta$ independent. Since a chi-square distribution with two degrees of freedom coincides with an exponential distribution of rate $1/2$, i.e. $\chi_2^2 \equiv \operatorname{Exp}(1)$, we have $R = \sqrt{-2\ln(U_1)}$, where $U_1 \sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$.

Consequently, if $U_1, U_2$ are independent $\mathcal{U}([0,1])$ random variables, the transformation

$$
\begin{align*}
  X_1 =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \cos(2\pi U_2) \\
  X_2 =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \sin(2\pi U_2)
\end{align*}
$$

produces two indenpendent standard normal random variables $X_1, X_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ with $X_1 \perp X_2$. This construction is known as the Box-Muller transform.

**Box-Muller algorithm**

1. Generate $U_1, U_2 \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{U}([0,1])$
2. Define
$$
\begin{align*}
  X_1 =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \cos(2\pi U_2) \\
  X_2 =& \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \sin(2\pi U_2)
\end{align*}
$$

3. Take $X_1$ and $X_2$ as two independent draws from $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
</MathBox>

# Monte Carlo methods

## Monte Carlo integration

Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}, \pi)$ be a probability space. The goal of Monte Carlo integration is to approximate expectations of the form

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}_\pi [h(X)] = \int_\mathsf{X} h(x) \pi(\d x)
\label{equation-5}
\end{align*}
$$

where $h:\mathsf{X}\to\R$ is a measurable function.

**Measure-theoretic framework**

Assume that $\pi:\mathcal{B}\to[0,1]$ admits a density $f:\mathsf{X}\to[0,\infty)$ with respect to a reference measure $\mu$ on $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B})$ (e.g. Lebesgue measure) via $\pi(\d x) = f(x) \d\mu (x)$. By definition, $f$ satisfies the normalization condition

$$
  \int_\mathsf{X} f(x) \;\d\mu(x) = 1
$$

Assuming $h\in\mathsf{L}_1 (\pi)$ is integrable with respect to $\pi$, the expectation $\eqref{equation-5}$ can be written

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)] = \int_\mathcal{X} h(x) f(x) \;\d\mu (x)
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

**Empirical Monte Carlo estimator**

Let $(X_i)_{i=1}^m \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \pi$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed random variables sampled from $\pi$. We define the empirical Monte Carlo estimator $\bar{h}_m$ as the sample mean

$$
  \bar{h}_m = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i)
$$

Since $h\in\mathsf{L}_1 (\pi)$, the strong law of large numbers guarantees that the estimator $\bar{h}_m$ converges almost surely to the true expectation:

$$
  \bar{h}_m \overset{\text{a.s.}}{m\to\infty} \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)]
$$

To quantify the precision $\hat{h}_m$, we further assume $h \in\mathsf{L}_2 (\pi)$ is square-integrable with respect to $\pi$. The variance of $h$ under $\pi$ is given by

$$
  \operatorname{var}_f [h(X)] = \int_\mathsf{X} (h(x) - \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)])^2 f(x) \;\d\mu(x) < \infty
$$

By independence of the samples $X_i$, the variance of the estimator $\bar{h}_m$ satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  \operatorname{var}(\bar{h}_m) =& \operatorname{var}\left(\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i) \right) \\
  =& \frac{1}{m^2} \sum_{i=1}^m \underbrace{\operatorname{var}[h(X_i)]}_{\operatorname{var}[h(X_i)]} \\
  =& \frac{1}{m} \operatorname{var}_f [h(X)]
\end{align*}
$$

If $\operatorname{var}_f [h(X)]$ is unknown, we can estimate it using the unbiased sample variance

$$
  s_m^2 = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m (h(X_i) - \bar{h}_m)^2
$$

Consequently, an estimator for the variance of $\bar{h}_m$ is

$$
  \widehat{\operatorname{var}}(\bar{h}_m) = \frac{s_m^2}{m}
$$

By the strong law of large numbers, $s_m^2$ is a consistent estimator of $\operatorname{var}_f [h(X)]$, i.e.

$$
  \frac{s_m^2}{m} \xrightarrow{\pi}{m\to\infty} \frac{1}{m} \operatorname{var}_f [h(X)]
$$

The central limit theorem provides the asymptotic distribution of the estimation error. As $m\to\infty$, the normalized distribution of the scaled error $\bar{h}_m - \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)]$ converges to a standard normal distribution:

$$
  \lim_{m\to\infty} \frac{\bar{h}_m - \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)]}{\sqrt{\operatorname{var}_f [h(X)] /m}} \xrightarrow{d}{m\to\infty} \mathcal{N}(0,1)
$$

Equivalently, using the empirical variance $s_m^2$

$$
  \frac{\bar{h}_m - \mathbb{E}_f [h(X)]}{\sqrt{s_m^2 /m}} \xrightarrow{d}{m\to\infty} \mathcal{N}(0,1)
$$

### Importance sampling

Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}, \mu)$ be a measurable space, and let $\pi:\mathcal{B}\to[0,1]$ be a probability measure admitting a density $f:\mathsf{X}\to[0,\infty)$ with respect to the measure $\mu$. Suppose $g:\mathsf{X}\to [0,\infty)$ is another probability density with respect to $\mu$, defining a proposal distribution $\nu$ via the Radon-Nikodym derivative $\nu = g \;\d\mu$. Assume that $\operatorname{supp}(f) \subseteq \operatorname{supp}(g)$, so that $g(x) > 0$ whenever $f(x) > 0$. Then we can refactor the expectation $\eqref{equation-6}$ as

$$
  \mathbb{E}_\pi = ìnt_\mathsf{X} h(x) \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} g(x) \;\d\mu(x) = \mathbb{E}_\nu [h(X) w(X)]
$$

known as the importance sampling identity. The importance weight $w:\mathsf{X}\to[0,\infty)$ is defined by

$$
  w(x) := \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}
$$

Let $(X_i)_{i=1}^m \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \nu$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed random variables sampled from $\nu$. The importance sampling estimator is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{h}_m := \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i) w(X_i) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i) \frac{f(X_i)}{g(X_i)}
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

If $h(X) w(X) \in \mathsf{L}_1 (\nu)$, then by the strong law of large numbes

$$
  \hbar{h}_m \overset{\text{a.s.}}{m\to\infty} \mathbb{E}_\pi [h(X)]
$$

Assuming $h(X) w(X) \in\mathsf{L}_2 (\nu)$, the variance of the estimator is

$$
  \operatorname{var}(\bar{h}_m) = \frac{1}{m} \operatorname{var}_g [h(X) w(X)]
$$

where

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \operatorname{var}_g [h(X) w(X)] = \int_\mathsf{X} h(x)^2 \frac{f(x)^2}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x) - (\mathbb{E}_\pi [h(X)])^2
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The variance is finite if and only if

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbb{E}_g [h^2 (X) w^2 (X)] = \mathbb{E}_f [h^2 (X) w (X)] = \int_\mathsf{X} h^2 (x) \frac{f^2 (x)}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x) < \infty
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This condition imposes requirement on the proposal density $g$. If $g$ has lighter tails than $f$, then the $f/g$ may become unbounded. In such cases, the integrand of $\eqref{equation-10}$ can fail to be integrable, leading to infinite variance. Generally, if $f/g$ is large in regions where $h$ is non-negligible, the weights $w(X_i)$ have high variability, giving too much importance to a few samples $X_j$. This means that the estimator may become unstable, with large fluctuations even for large sample spaces.

Conversely, distributions $g$ with thicker tails than $f$ ensure that the ratio $f/g$ does not cause the divergence of $\eqref{equation-10}$.

The following are proposed sufficient conditions for ensuring finite variance
1. **Uniform boundedness of the likelihood ratio:** $\sup{x\in\mathsf{X} f(x)/g(x) < \infty$ f and $\operatorname{var}_f (h) < \infty$
2. **Compact support with bounded densities:** $\mathsf{X}$ is compact, $\sup_{x\in\mathsf{X}} f(x) < \infty$ and $\inf_{x\in\mathsf{X}} g(x) > 0$

These conditions are quite restrictive. In particular, the boundedness of $f/g < M$ implies that the acceptance-rejection method is feasible with $g$ as the proposal.

An alternative to the empirical mean estimator $\eqref{equation-8}$ is the self-normalized importance sampling estimator

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{h}_m = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i) w(X_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^m w(x_i)}
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where we have replaced $m$ with the sum of the weights. Since $\lim_{m\to\infty} (1/m) \sum_{j=1}^m w(x_j) = 1$, this estimator also converges to $\mathbb{E}_f [h(X)]$ by the strong law of large numbers.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

By the strong law of large numbers

$$
  \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m w(X_i) \xrightarrow{\text{a.s}}{n\to\infty} \mathbb{E}_g [w(X)] = 1
$$

and

$$
  \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m h(X_i) w(X_i) \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}{m\to\infty} \mathbb{E}_\pi [h(X)]
$$

Hence, by Slutsky's theorem

$$
  \tilde{h}_m \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}{m\to\infty} \mathbb{E}_\pi [h(X)]
$$
</details>

Although $\tilde{h}_m$ is biased for finite $m$, it is consisten and often exhibits lower variance than the estimator $\eqref{equation-8}$. In particular, when $h$ is nearly constant, $\tilde{h}_m$ is close to this constant value, while $\eqref{equation-8}$ has a higher variation due to fluctuation is the total weight $\sum_{i=1}^m w(X_i)$.

<MathBox title="" boxType="proposition">
Let $(\mathsf{X}, \mathcal{B}, \mu)$ be a measure space and suppose $g:\mathsf{X}\to [0,\infty)$ is a probability density with respect to $\mu$, defining a proposal distribution $\nu$ via $\nu = g \;\d\mu$. The choice of $g$ that minimizes the variance of the importance sampling estimator $\eqref{equation-8}$ is given by

$$
  g^* (x) = \frac{|h(x)| f(x)}{\int_\mathsf{X} |h(z)| f(z) \;\d\mu(z)}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Writing the variance $\eqref{equation-8}$ as

$$
  \operatorname{var}_g \left(\frac{h(X) f(X)}{g(X)} \right) = \mathbb{E}_g \left(\frac{h^2 (X) f^2 (X)}{g^2 (X)} \right) - \left[\mathbb{E}_g \left(\frac{h(X) f(X)}{g(X)} \right) \right]^2
$$

we note that the second term does not depend on $g$. Thus, we only need to minize the first term

$$
  \mathbb{E}_g \left(\frac{h^2 (X) f^2 (X)}{g^2 (X)} \right) = \int_\mathsf{X} \frac{h^2 (x) f^2 (x)}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x)
$$

Defining $\phi(x) := |h(x)|f(x)$ for $g(x) > 0$, the objective quantity becomes

$$
  \int_\mathsf{X} \frac{\phi(x)^2}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x)
$$


We apply the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality in the form

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left(\int_\mathsf{X} \phi(x) \;\d\mu (x) \right)^2 =& \left(\int_\mathsf{X} \frac{\ph(x)}{\sqrt{g(x)}} \sqrt{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x) \right)^2 \\
  \leq& \left(\int_\mathsf{X} \frac{\phi(x)^2}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x) \right) \underbrace{\left(\int_\mathsf{X} g(x) \;\d\mu(x) \right)}_{=1}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $g$ is a probability density, this inequality yields the lower bound

$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_\mathsf{X} \frac{\phi(x)^2}{g(x)} \;\d\mu(x) \geq& \left(\int_\mathsf{X} \phi(x) \;\d\mu(x) \right)^2 \\
  =& \left(\int_\mathsf{X} |h(x)| f(x) \;\d\mu(x) \right)^2
\end{align*}
$$

which is independent of $g$. Equality holds is and only if

$$
  \frac{\phi(x)}{\sqrt{g(x)}} = c\sqrt{g(x)}
$$

for some constant $c\in\R$, which is equivalent to

$$
  g(x) \propto \phi(x) = |h(x)| g(x)
$$

Normalizing yields the optimal density

$$
  g^* (x) \frac{|h(x)| f(x)}{\int_\mathsf{X} |h(z)| f(z) \;\d\mu(z)}
$$



$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbb{E}_g \left(\frac{h^2 (X) f^2 (X)}{g^2 (X)} \right) \geq& \left[\mathbb{E}_g \left(\frac{|h(X)|f(X)}{g(X)} \right) \right]^2 \\
  =& \left(\int_\mathsf{X} |h(x)| f(x) \;\d x \right)^2
\end{align*}
$$

which provides a lower bound that is independent of the choice of $g$. It is straightforward to verify that this lower bound is attained by choosing $g = g^*$.

## Metropolis-Hastings algorithm

Suppose we wish to generate samples from an arbitrary multidimensional density

$$
  f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{Z}
$$

where $p(\mathbf{x})$ is a known positive function and $Z$ is a known or unknown normalizing constant. Let $q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$ be a proposed density in the sense of a Markov transition describing how to go from state $\mathbf{x}$ to state $\mathbf{y}$. Similar to the acceptance-rejection method, the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is based on the follwoing "trial-and-error" strategy.

To sample from a density $f:\R^n \to\R$ known up to a normalizing constant, initialize with some $\mathbf{X}_0$ for which $f(\mathbf{X}_0) > 0$. Then, for each $t=0,\dots,T-1$ execute the following steps
1. Given the current state $\mathbf{X}_t$, generate $\mathbf{Y} \sim q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X}_t)$
2. Generate $U\sim\mathcal{U}([0,1])$ and return
$$
  \mathbf{X}_{t+1} = \begin{cases}
    \mathbf{Y},\quad& U\leq \alpha(\mathbf{X}_t, \mathbf{Y}) \\
    \mathbf{X}_t,\quad& U > \alpha(\mathbf{X}_t, \mathbf{Y})
  \end{cases}
$$

where

$$
  \alpha(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \min\Set{1, \frac{f(\mathbf{y}) q(\mathbf{x}\lvert\mathbf{y})}{f(\mathbf{x})q(\mathbf{y}\lvert\mathbf{x})}}
$$

is the acceptance probability.

A single Metropolis-Hastings iterarion is equivalent to generating a point from the transition density

$$
  \kappa(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) = \alpha(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) + (1 - \alpha^* (\mathbf{x}) \delta_\mathbf{x} (\mathbf{y})
$$

with $\alpha^* (\mathbf{x}) = \int \alpha(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \;\d\mathbf{y}$ and $\delta_\mathbf{x} (\mathbf{y})$ denoting the Dirac delta function. Since

$$
  f(\mathbf{x}) \alpha(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{y})\alpha(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}) q(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{y})
$$

and

$$
  (1 - \alpha^* (\mathbf{x})) \delta_\mathbf{x} \delta(\mathbf{y}) f(\mathbf{x}) = (1 - \alpha^* (\mathbf{y})) \delta_\mathbf{y} (\mathbf{x}) f(\mathbf{y})
$$

the transition density satisfies the detailed balance equation

$$
  f(\mathbf{x})\kappa(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{y}) \kappa(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{y})
$$

from which it follows that $f$ is the stationary density of the chain. In addition, if the transition density $q$ satisfies the conditions

$$
  \mathbf{P}(\alpha(\mathbf{X}_t, \mathbf{Y}) < 1 |\mathbf{X}_t) > 0
$$

that is, the event $\set{\mathbf{X}_{t+1} = \mathbf{X}_t}$ has positive probability, and $q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$, then $f$ is the limiting density of the Markov chain. As a consequence, to estimate an expectation $\mathbb{E}[H(\mathbf{X})]$, with $\mathbf{X}\sim f$, we can use the following ergodic estimator

$$
  \frac{1}{T+1} \sum_{t=0}^T H(\mathbf{X}_t)
$$
