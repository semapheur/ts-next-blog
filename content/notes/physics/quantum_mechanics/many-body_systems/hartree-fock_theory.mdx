# The Hartree-Fock equations

Let $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denote combined spatial and spin coordinates. With the Hartree-Fock method, we seek a set of orthonormal spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^$ with $\braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \delta_{ij}$, such that the corresponding Slater determinant

$$
  \Psi_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}!} \det[\chi_i (\mathbf{x}_j)]
$$

provides the best approximation to the exact ground-state wavefunction of an $N$-electron system described by the electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$.

According to the variational principle, the optimal spin orbitals minimize the Hartree-Fock energy functional

$$
\begin{align*}
  E[\set{\chi_i}] =& \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

subject to the orthonormality constraints on the spin orbitals. Using Lagrange multipliers, minimization of the energy functional under the orthonormality constraints gives the Hartree-Fock equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{h}(1) \chi_i(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N [\hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1)] \chi_i (1) = \varepsilon_i \chi_i (1)
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\varepsilon_i$ is the Lagrange multiplier, called the orbital energy, associated with spin orbital $\chi_i$.

**Coulomb operator**

The Coulomb operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (\hat{J}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \frac{|\chi_j (2)|^2}{r_{12}} \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \phi(1)
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation*}
$$

for an arbitrary spin orbital $\phi$. Thus, $\hat{J}_j$ is a local multiplicative operator whose value at $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends only on the electron density associated with orbital $\chi_j$. It represents the average electrostatic potential generated by the probability density $|\chi_j|^2$ of electron $2$. Summing over all occupied orbitals therefore gives the mean electrostatic potential experience by an electron occupying orbital $\chi_i$.

The expectation value of $\hat{J}_j$ is given by the Coulomb integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{J}_j|\chi_i} = J_{ij} = [ii|jj]
$$

**Exchange operator**

The exchange operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (\hat{K}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \phi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \chi_j (1)
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Unlike the Coulomb operator, $\hat{K}_j$ is nonlocal, because its action at the point $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends on the values of the function $\phi$ throughout all space. It has no classical analogue, because it arises solely from the antisymmetry of the Slater determinant and therefore reflects the fermionic nature of the electronic wavefunction. Its nonlocal character is a direct consequence of electron exchange. 

The expectation value of $\hat{K}_j$ is given by the exchange integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{K}_j|\chi_i} = K_{ij} = [ij|ji]
$$

**Fock operator**

Since $(\hat{J}_i - \hat{K}_i)\chi_i = 0$, the self-interaction terms cancel identically. The summation in $\eqref{equation-1}$ can therefore be taken over all occupied orbital, allowing the introduction of the Fock operator

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f} := \hat{h} + \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which depends parametrically on the occupied spin orbitals. In terms of the Fock operator, the Hartree-Fock equations assume the compact form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f} \chi_i = \varepsilon_i \chi_i
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The effective one-electron potential

$$
  \hat{v}_\text{HF} = \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
$$

is known as the Hartree-Fock potential, so that $\hat{f} = \hat{h} + \hat{v}_\text{HF}$. The exchange operator can also be expressed using the electron-permutation operator $\hat{p}_{12}$, defined by $\hat{p}_{12} v(1,2) = v(2,1)$. With this notation

$$
  \hat{f}(1) = \hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N \int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1 - \hat{p}_{12}}{r_{12}} \chi_j (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
$$

Although the Hartree-Fock equations resemble a one-electron eigenvalue problem, they do not constitute a linear eigenvalue problem in the usual sense. The Fock operator depends functionally on the occupied orbitals through the Coulomb and exchange operators, i.e. $\hat{f} = \hat{f}[\set{\chi_i}]$, so the orbitals sought determine the operator itself. The Hartree-Fock equations therefore define a nonlinear, self-consisten eigenvalue problem.

Except for atoms and highly symmetric systems, the Hartree-Fock equations cannot be solved analytically. Instead, each spin orbital is expanded in a finite basis set, resulting the Roothan-Hall or Pople-Nesbet matrix equations, which are solved interatively until self-consistency is achieved. As the basis set approaches completeness, the resulting orbitals converge to the Hartree-Fock limint, providing the exact solution within the space of all single Slater determinants.

## Derivation of the Hartree-Fock equations

### Functional variation

Let $\tilde{\Phi}$ be a trial wavefunction in the domain of the electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$. The corresponding energy is define by the Rayleigh quotient

$$
  E[\tilde{\Phi}] =& \frac{\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}}}{\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}}}
$$

Thus, $E[\tilde{\Phi}]$ is a functional: its value depends on the entire function $\tilde{\Phi}$, rather than on the value of an independent variable. If the trial wavefunction is normalized, $\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}}$, the energy functional reduces to

$$
  E[\tilde{\Phi}] = \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}}
$$

Suppose $\tilde{\Phi}$ is subjected to an arbitrary infinitesimal variation

$$
  \tilde{\Phi} \to \tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}
$$

where $\delta\tilde{\Phi}$ is an arbitrary admissible variation. Expanding the energy functional to first order gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  E[\tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}] =& \braket{\tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi} + \delta{\Phi}} + O(\delta\tilde{\Phi}^2) \\
  =& E[\tilde{\Phi}] + \braket{\delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} + \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\delta\tilde{\Phi}} + O(\delta\tilde{\Phi}^2) \\
\end{align*}
$$

The first-order term, $\delta E = \braket{\delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} + \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\delta\tilde{\Phi}}$, is called the first variation of the energy functional.

The variational principle states that the optimal trial wavefunction is obtained by requiring the energy functional to be stationary with respect to all admissible variations, $\delta E = 0$. This condition is neccessary for an extremum of the functional. For the ground state of a Hamiltonian bounded from below, the variational principle guarantees that this stationary point is in fact the global minimum.

Consider the linear variational ansatz

$$
  \ket{\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i=1}^N c_i \ket{\Psi_i}
$$

where the basis functions $\set{\ket{\Psi_i}}$ are linearly independent but not necessarily orthonormal. The normalization condition is

$$
  \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i,j} c_i^* S_{ij} c_j = 1
$$

where $S_{ij} = \braket{\Psi_i|\Psi_j}$ is the overlap matrix. Similarly,

$$
  \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} c_j
$$

where $H_{ij} = \braket{\Psi_i|\hat{H}|\Psi_i}$ are the Hamiltonian matrix elements.

To impose the normalization constraint, we introduce the Lagrangian

$$
\begin{align*}
  L =& \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} - E(\braket{\tilde{\Phi}} - 1) \\
  =& \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} c_j - E\left(\sum_{i,j} c_î^* c_j S_{ij} - 1 \right)
\end{align*}
$$

where the Lagrangian multiplier $E$ is identified with the variational energy

The coefficients $c_i$ and $c_i^*$ are treated as independent variable. Taking the first variation of $L$ with respect to $c_i^*$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{i,j} \delta c_i^* H_{ij} c_j - E \sum_{i,j} \delta c_i^* S_{ij} c_j \\
  &+ \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} \delta c_j - E \sum_{i,j} c_i^* S_{ij} \delta c_i
\end{align*}
$$

Since $E$ is real, after collecting terms and interchanging indices, we get

$$
  \delta L = \sum_i \delta c_i^* \left[\sum_j H_{ij} c_j - E \sum_j S_{ij} c_j \right] + \text{c.c.}
$$

Since the variations $\set{\delta c_i^*}$ are arbitrary, stationarity $\delta L = 0$ requires

$$
  \sum_{j} H_{ij} c_j = E \sum_j S_{ij} c_j
$$

or in matrix form $\mathbf{Hc} = E\mathbf{Sc}$, which is the generalized Hermitian eigenvalue problem arising in the linear variation method. If the basis is orthonormal, then $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{I}$, and the equation reduces to the ordinary eigenvalue problem $\mathbf{Hc} = E\mathbf{c}$. The smallest eigenvalue of the generalized eigenvalue problem provides the best approximation to the ground-state energy within the span of the chosen basis functions, while the corresponding eigenvector determines the optimal linear combination of basis functions.

### Minimization of the energy of a single determinant

Given a single Slater determinant $\ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$, the expectation value $E_0 = \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0}$ defines a functional of the occupied spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}$. The Hartree-Fock equations are obtained by minimizing this functional over all orthonormal sets of spin orbitals satisfying

$$
  \braket{i|j} := \int \chi_i^* (1) \chi_j \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 = \delta_{ij}
$$

This orthonormality conditions produces the constraints $\braket{i|j} - \delta_{ij} = 0$. To enforce these constraints, we introduce the Lagrangian functional

$$
  L[\set{\chi_i}] = E_0 [\set{\chi_i}] - \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} (\braket{i|j} - \delta_{ij})
$$

where the matrix $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = [\varepsilon_{ij}]$ consists of Lagrange multipliers.

where $E_0$ is the expectation value of the single determinant $\ket{\Psi_0}$.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_0 [\set{\chi_i}] = \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [ii|jj] - [ij|ji]
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Because $L$ is real-valued and $\braket{i|j} = \braket{j|i}^*$, the matrix of Lagrange multipliers must be Hermitian, i.e. $\varepsilon_{ji} = \varepsilon_{ij}^*$.

Minimization of $E_0$, subject to the orthonormality constraints, is thus obtained by solving $\delta L = 0$. Treating the spin orbitals and their complex conjugates as independent variables, we consider arbitrary infinitesimal variations

$$
  \chi_i \to& \chi_i + \delta\chi_i \\
  \chi_i^* \to& \chi_i^* + \delta\chi_i^*
$$

Taking the first variation of $\eqref{equation-2}$, we impose

$$
  \delta L = \delta E_0 - \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \delta\braket{i|j} = 0
$$

where have used that $\delta(\delta_{ij}) = 0$. Taking the first variation of the spin orbital overlaps

$$
  \delta\braket{i|j} = \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \braket{\chi_i|\delta\chi_j}
$$

and using the Hermiticity of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} (\braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \braket{\chi_i|\delta\chi_j}) =& \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ij} \braket{\chi_j|\delta\chi_i} \\
  =& \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji}^* \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} \\
  =& 2 \Re \left(\sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} \right)
\end{align*}
$$

The first variation of the energy is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta E_0 =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} + \braket{\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} \\
  &+ \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \delta\chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \chi_i|\delta\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \delta\chi_j] \\
  &- \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \delta\chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \chi_j|\delta\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \delta\chi_i]
\end{align*}
$$

Using the permutation symmetry of the two-electron integrals and relabelling dummy indices, this expression simplifies to

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta E_0 =& \sum_{i=1} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} + \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] - [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] \\
  &+ \text{c.c.}
\end{align*}
$$

Combining these results, the first variation in $L$ becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} \\
  &+ \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] - [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] \\
  &- \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \text{c.c.} = 0
\end{align*}
$$

Using definitions $\eqref{equation-3}$ and $\eqref{equation-4}$ for the Coulomb and exchange operators, respectively, we can write this variation in the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{j=1}^N \int \delta\chi_i^* (1) \left(\hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N [\hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1)] \right) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  &- \sum_{i,j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \int \delta\chi_i^* (1) \chi_j (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 + \text{c.c} = 0
\end{equation*}
$$

Since the variations $\delta\chi_i^* (1)$ are arbitrary, the fundamental lemma of variational calculus implies that

$$
  \left[\hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N \hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1) \right] \chi_i (1) = \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \chi_j (1),\; j=1,\dots,N
$$

Recognizing the Fock operator $\eqref{equation-5}$, this equation takes the compact form

$$
  \hat{f}\ket{\chi_i} = \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \ket{\chi_i}
$$

These equations are not yet in the canonical eigenvalue form of $\eqref{equation-6}$ because the occupied orbitals are determined only up to an arbitrary unitary transformation. Replacing the occupied spin orbitals by any unitary lienar combination leaves the Slater determinant unchanged up to an over phase, and therefore leaves the Hartree-Fock energy invariant.
