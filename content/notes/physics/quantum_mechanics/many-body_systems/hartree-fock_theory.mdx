# Atomic units

Atomic units (a.u.) form a system of natural measurement units that simplify physical equations by normalizing the following fundamental constants to $1$
- the reduced Planck constant $\hbar$
- elementary charge $e$
- electron rest mass $m_e$
- permittivity: $4\pi\epsilon_0$

To see how the atomic unit of length arise naturally let us consider the stationary Schrödinger equation for the hydrogen atom. In SI units we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e} \nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \right) \psi (\mathbf{r}) = E \psi (\mathbf{r})
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

To make this equation dimensionless, we introduce a scaled coordinate $\mathbf{r} \to \lambda\mathbf{r}'$, where $\lambda$ is a characteristic length to be determined. Under this transformation we have

$$
  \nabla^2 = \lambda^{-2} \nabla'^2,\; r = \lambda r'
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-1}$ yields

$$
  \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e \lambda^2} \nabla'^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \lambda r} \right) \psi' (\mathbf{r}') = E \psi'  (\mathbf{r}')
$$

where $\psi' (\mathbf{r}') := \psi(\lambda\mathbf{r}')$. The constants in front of the kinetic and potential energy operators can be factored, provided we choose $\lambda$ such that

$$
  \frac{\hbar^2}{m_e \lambda^2} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \lambda} = E_a
$$

where $E_a$ is the atomic unit of energy called the Hartree. Solving for $lambda$ we find

$$
  \lambda = \frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar^2}{m_e e^2} = a_0
$$

showing that $\lambda$ equals the Bohr radius $a_0$, the atomic unit of length. With this choice of $\lambda$, equation $\eqref{equation-1}$ becomes

$$
  E_a \left(-\frac{1}{2}\nabla'^2 - \frac{1}{r}\right) \psi' = E\psi'
$$

Finally, defining the dimensionless energy $E' = E/E_a$, we obtain

$$
  \left(-\frac{1}{2}\nabla'^2 - \frac{1}{r}\right) \psi' = E' \psi'
$$

which is the Schrödinger equation in atomic units.

# The Born-Oppenheimer approximation

A molecule composed of $N_n$ nuclei and $N_e$ electrons can be described as a coupled electron-nucleus system. For each nucleus $k=1,\dots, N$, with charge $Z_k e$ and mass $m_k$, we denote its position and momentum operators by $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_k^n$ and $\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^n$, respectively, relative to the origin of the frame of reference. Similarly, for each electron $j = 1,\dots, N_e$, with mass $m_e$ and charge $-e$, we write $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_j^e$ and $\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^e$ for its position and momentum operators. In the position representation, the momentum operator is given by

$$
  \hat{\mathbf{p}} = -i\hbar\nabla_\mathbf{r}
$$

The total energy of the system is given by the non-relativistic Hamiltonian 

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{H} =& \hat{T}_n + \hat{V}_n + \hat{H}_e \tag{\label{equation-2}} \\
  \hat{H} =& \hat{T}_e + \hat{V}_{ne} + \hat{V}_e \tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{align*}
$$

where $\hat{H}_e$ is the electronic Hamiltonian. The kinetic energy of the nuclei $\hat{T}_n$ is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{T}_n = \sum_{k = 1}^{N_n} \frac{\left(\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^n \right)^2}{2 m_k} = -\sum_{k = 1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar}{2 m_k} \nabla_k^2
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\nabla_k := \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$. The potential $\hat{V}_n$ represents the repulsion among the nuclei

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{V}_n = \sum_{k < l = 1}^{N_n} \frac{Z_k Z_l e^2}{|\mathbf{r}_k^n - \mathbf{r}_l^n|} 
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which depends only on nuclear positions and acts as a multiplicative potential operator.


The kinetic energy of the electrons $\hat{T}_e$ is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{T}_e = \sum_{j=1}^{N_e} \frac{\left(\hat{\mathbf{p}}_j^e \right)^2}{2m_e} = - \sum_{j=1}^{N_e} \frac{\hbar}{2m_e} \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_j^e}^2
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\nabla_j := \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_j^e}$. The potential $\hat{V}_{ne}$ describes the interaction between electrons and nuclei

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{V}_{ne} = -\sum_{j=1}^{N_e} \sum_{k=1}^{N_n}  \frac{Z_k e^2}{|\mathbf{r}_j^e - \mathbf{r}_k^n|}
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$

while the potential $\hat{V}_e$ represents the repulsion among electrons

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{V}_e = \sum_{i < j = 1}^{N_e} \frac{e^2}{|\mathbf{r}_i^e - \mathbf{r}_j^e|}
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The Born-Oppenheimer approximation separates the nuclear motion from the electronic one, which relies on the different time scales of the two types of motion. Consequently, we try to solve the molecular problem by first considering a "frozen" configuration of the nuclei, which introduces a certain potential on each electron. Changing the nuclear configuration, we assume that the electrons will rearrange themselves almost instantaneously. Then, we shall look for solutions of the Schrödinger equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{H} \Psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}; \set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) = E \Psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}; \set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the electronic states are defined in a parametric way relative to a a given distribution of the nuclei. The solution of the general problem is given by the product

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}; \set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) = \psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e})
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})$ is the nuclear wave function. The electronic wave function $\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e})$ depends parametrically on the parameter set $\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$, and may be taken as a solution of the Schrödinger equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{H}_e \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}) = E \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e})
\tag{\label{equation-13}}
\end{equation*}
$$

for a given nuclear configuration. The left hand side of $\eqref{equation-9}$ can be written

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \hat{H}_e \Psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}; \set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}) =& \left[\hat{T}_n + \hat{V}_n (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) + \hat{H}_e \right] \psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{\mathbf{r}_j^e}) \\
  =& \left[-\sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \nabla_k^2 + \hat{V}_n + \hat{H}_e \right] \psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \\
  =& -\sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \nabla_k^2 \left(\psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \right) + \hat{V}_n \psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} + \underbrace{\hat{H}_e \psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}}_{E_e \psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} } \\
  =& \left(\hat{V}_n + E_e \right) \psi \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \\
  &- \sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \left[\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \nabla_k^2 \psi + \psi \nabla_k^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} + 2\nabla_k \psi \nabla_k \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \right] \\
  =& \psi \left[-\sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \nabla_k^2 + \hat{V}_n + E_e \right] \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}  \\
  &- \sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \left[\psi \nabla_k^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} + 2\nabla_k \psi \nabla_k \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \right]
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-11}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The terms involving derivatives of $\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$ with respect to nuclear coordinates represent nonadiabatic couplings. They couple electronic and nuclear motion and prevent the simple product form $\eqref{equation-10}$.

The term $\nabla_k^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$ can be estimated by recognizing that $\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$ depends on relative coordinates $\mathbf{r}_j^e -\mathbf{r}_k^n$. Hence, differentiation with respect to $\mathbf{r}_k^n$ is equivalently up to a sign to differentiate with respect to $\mathbf{r}_j^e$. Consequently, denoting $\nabla_{\mathbf{r}_j^e}^{\prime 2}$ the Laplacian relative to the electronic coordinates, we have

$$
  \nabla_k^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \sim \nabla_j^{\prime 2} \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}
$$

The corresponding kinetic energy distribution therefore scales as

$$
  \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \nabla_k^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \sim \frac{m_e}{m_k} \frac{\hbar}{2m_e} \nabla_j^2 \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}
$$

which is suppressed by the small ratio $m_e / m_k \approx 1/1840$.

For the same reasons, 

$$
  \nabla_k \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} = -\nabla'_j \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n}
$$

and we also have

$$
  \hbar \nabla_k \psi \sim \hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^n,\quad \hbar \nabla_k \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \sim \hat{\mathbf{p}}_j^e
$$

Consequently, the mixed derivative term scales as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{\hbar^2}{m_k} \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \psi \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} \sim \frac{1}{m_k} \hat{\mathbf{p}}_k^n \hat{\mathbf{p}}_j^e \ll \frac{(\hat{\mathbf{p}}_j^e)^2}{2m_e}
\tag{\label{equation-12}}
\end{equation*}
$$

because the dynamics of the electrons is much faster than that of the nuclei and therefore $p_k^n \ll p_j^e$. The last term of $\eqref{equation-12}$ is of the order of the kinetic energy of one electron, and this finally justifies neglecting those two terms.

With these approximations, we may rewrite $\eqref{equation-9}$ as a Schrödinger equation for the nuclear wave function

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \left[-\sum_{k=1}^{N_n} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_k} \nabla_k^2 + \hat{V}_n (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) + E_e (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) \right] \psi(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) = E \psi (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})
\tag{\label{equation-14}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $E$ is the total energy, and the electron energy eigenvalue $E_e (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})$, may be considered as a correction to the nuclear potential energy $\hat{V}_n (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})$, giving rise to the total potential energy

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{V}(\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) = \hat{V}_n (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}) + E_e (\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n})
\tag{\label{equation-16}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In other word, the electrons with their motion generate a diffuse charge distribution that alters the nuclear Coulomb repulsion term $\hat{V}_n$.

With the Born-Oppenheimer approximation we first solve the electronic Schrödinger equation $\eqref{equation-13}$ for any possible configuration of the nuclei, finding the eigenvalues $E_e$ as a function of the nuclear positions $\set{\mathbf{r}_k^n}$. Then, we use these results in order to solve the Schrödinger equation $\eqref{equation-14}$.

## Diatomic molecule

A diatomic molecule has three types of collective motion:
1. Translation of the center of mass of the molecule, which practically coincides with the center of mass of the nuclei. This motion gives rise to a continuous spectrum characteristic of the motion of a free particle. This motion can be neglected if we work in a reference system in which the center of mass is at rest.
2. Rotation of the molecule about an axis passing through the center of mass. The pure rotational motion is obtained if the two nuclei stay at a fixed distance.
3. Vibration along the axis connecting the two nuclei. If the two nuclei must stay at a fixed distance, the potential energy must have a minimum at $r = r_{ab}$. Under the harmonic approximation, the internuclear distance may oscillate around the equilibrium point $r_{ab}$ giving rise to vibrational energy levels and spectra.

For a diatomic molecule made up of atoms $a$ and $b$, the Schrödinger equation $\eqref{equation-14}$ becomes

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_a} \nabla_a^2 - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_b} \nabla_b^2 + \hat{V}_n (\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n) + E_e (\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n) \right] \psi(\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n) = E \psi (\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n)
\tag{\label{equation-15}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Assuming that the nuclei are almost at rest relative to each other, and taking into account $\eqref{equation-16}$, the potential energy will have the form of a central potential

$$
  \hat{V}(\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n) = \hat{V}(\mathbf{r}_b^n - \mathbf{r}_a^n) = \hat{V}(r_{ab})
$$

Defining the position of the center of mass as

$$
  \mathbf{r}_c = \frac{m_a \mathbf{r}_a^n + m_b \mathbf{r}_b^n}{m_a + m_b}
$$

we apply the change of variables $\set{\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n} \to \set{\mathbf{r}_c, \mathbf{r}_{ab}}$ in order to derive the Hamiltonian

$$
  \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2(m_a + m_b)} \nabla_c^2 - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla_0^2 + \hat{V}(r_{ab})
$$

where $m$ is the reduced mass and $\nabla_0 := \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_{ab}}$. With the transformed Hamiltonian, we have separated the center-of-mass motion from the relative motion of the nuclei. This suggest the ansatz

$$
  \psi(\mathbf{r}_a^n, \mathbf{r}_b^n) = \zeta(\mathbf{r}_c) \eta(\mathbf{r}_{ab})
$$

giving rise to the two Schrödinger equations

$$
\begin{equation*}
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2(m_a + m_b)} \nabla_c^2 \zeta((\mathbf{r}_c)) = E_c \zeta(\mathbf{r}_c)
\tag{\label{equation-17}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla_0^2 + \hat{V}(\mathbf{r}_{ab})\right] \eta(\mathbf{r}_{ab}) = E_0 \eta(\mathbf{r}_{ab})
\tag{\label{equation-18}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $E_c$ is the energy eigenvalue of the center-of-mass degree of freedom and $E_0$ is the energy eigenvalue pertaining to the relative motion. Equation $\eqref{equation-17}$ represents a free-particle Schrödinger equation whose solution is a spherical wave of the form

$$
  \zeta(\mathbf{r}_c) \propto e^{i \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r}_c}
$$

describing the translational motion. The corresponding eigenvalues will be given by

$$
  E_c = \frac{\hbar^2 \mathbf{k}^2}{2(m_a + m_b)}
$$

The solution of $\eqref{equation-19}$ gives the rotational part of the motion. Since $\hat{V}(r_{ab})$ is a central potential, it is convenient to translate this equation into spherical coordinates, where the angular part of the wave function $\eta(\mathbf{r}_{ab})$ will have have a solution of the form

$$
  \eta(\mathbf{r}_{ab}) = R(r_{ab}) Y(\phi, \theta)
$$

where $R(r_{ab})$ is the radial wave function, and the spherical harmonics $Y_j (\phi, \theta)$ have eigenvalues

$$
  E_j = \frac{\hbar^2}{2I} j(j + 1)
$$

where $I = m\bar{r}_{ab}^2$ is the moment of inertia of the two nuclei, with $\bar{r}_{ab}$ being the equilibrium distance between them. The eigenvalues of $\hat{j}^2$ and $\hat{j}_z$ are give by $j(j + 1)$ and $m_j$, respectively.

For the solution of the radial wave function $R(r_{ab})$, we first recall that 

$$
  |r_{ab} - \bar{r}_{ab}| \ll \bar{r}_{ab}
$$

that is, the two nuclei will oscillate around the equilibrium position. This allows a Taylor series expansion of the total potential energy $\hat{V}(r_{ab})$ about $\bar{r}_{ab}$

$$
  \hat{V}(r_{ab}) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \left. \frac{\partial^k \hat{V}(r_{ab})}{r_{ab}^k} \right|_{r_{ab} = \bar{r}_{ab}} (r_{ab} - \bar{r}_{ab})^k
$$

where the first-order term is missing because $\bar{r}_{ab}$ is an equilibrium point, i.e.

$$
  \left. \frac{\partial \hat{V}(r_{ab})}{r_{ab}} \right|_{r_{ab} = \bar{r}_{ab}} = 0
$$

Up to second order, the solutions will be of the harmonic-oscillator type

$$
  \hat{V}(r_{ab}) \approx -D + \frac{\kappa}{2}(r_{ab} - \bar{r}_{ab})^2
$$

where $D = -\hat{V}(\bar{r}_{ab})$ is the dissociation energy, the energy needed to move the two nuclei to an infinite distance from each other. If we neglect the centrifugal term, making use of the rigid-rotator approximation, we can write the radial Schrödinger equation as

$$
  \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r_{ab}^2} + \frac{\kappa}{2} (r_{ab} - \bar{r}_{ab})^2 - D \right] \Phi(r_{ab}) = E^\text{vib} \Phi(r_{ab})
$$

which, apart from the constant $D$, is the harmonic-oscillator equation, whose solutions will be given by the Hermite polynomials with energy levels

$$
  E_n^\text{vib} = -D + \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar\omega_0
$$

where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{\kappa/m}$. Thus, the total energy, relative to the nuclear part, is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  E =& E_c + E_0 = E_c + E_j + E_n^\text{vib} \\
  =& \frac{\hbar^2 \mathbf{k}^2}{2(m_a + m_b)} + \frac{\hbar^2}{2I} j(j + 1) - D + \left(n + \frac{1}{2} \right) \hbar\omega_0
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-19}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Changing the electronic state will change the form of the potential $\hat{V}(r_{ab})$ and thus the harmonic constant $\kappa$. Consequently, there will be several electronic states, the lowest of which is the ground state. Each electronic state will possess many vibrational levels. In turn, every vibrational level will display rotational bands.

Adding the effects of the anharmonicity of the potential $\hat{V}(r_{ab})$ and the centrifugal-barrier term will only introduce two small additional perturbations in $\eqref{equation-19}$

## Spin degrees of freedom

The electronic Hamiltonian $\eqref{equation-3}$ can be extended by specifying the spin orientation of the electrons, in addition to their spatial coordinates. To this end, we introduce two orthonormal spin functions $\alpha(\omega)$ and $\beta(\omega)$, corresponding to spin up and down, respectively. Here $\omega$ is an abstract spin variable. The spin functions satisfy the orthonormality conditions

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\alpha|\alpha} =& \braket{\beta|\beta} = 1 \\
  \braket{\alpha|\beta} =& \braket{\beta|\alpha} = 0
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, the state space for a single electron is the tensor product of spatial and spin spaces, and an electron is specified by both its spatial coordinate $\mathbf{r}$ and its spin coordinate $\omega$. Collectively, we denote this set of variable by $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$. In this formalism, the electronic wavefunction in $\eqref{equation-10}$ depends on the combined variables of electrons $\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\set{x}_j)$.

Because the electronic Hamiltonian $\eqref{equation-3}$ contains no explicit spin-dependent terms, introducing spin coordinates alone does not change its form. However, to obtain a physically meaningful theory, we must impose an additional antisymmetric requirement of the electronic wavefunction

$$
  \phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^{n}} (\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_i, \dots, \mathbf{x}_j, \dots, \mathbf{x}_{N_e}) = -\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^n} (\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_j, \dots, \mathbf{x}_i, \dots, \mathbf{x}_{N_e})
$$

for any pair of electrons $i$ and $j$. This condition states that the electronic wave function $\phi_{\mathbf{r}_k^{n}}$ is antisymmetric under exchange of all coordinates of any two electrons. This antisymmetry requirement os the formal statement of the Pauli exclusion principle: if two electrons occupy the same one-electron state, the wavefunction must vanish. More generally, antisymmetry ensures that electrons cannot simultaneously be in the same quantum state.

# Orbitals

An orbital is a one-particle wave function, describing the quantum state of a single electron. A spatial orbital is a complex-value function $\psi_i: \R^3 \to \mathbb{C}$, of the position vector $\mathbf{r}\in\R^3$. Its squared modulus determines the probability density for the electron's spatial distribution; the probability of finding the electron in an infinitesimal volume element $\d\mathbf{r}$ around $\mathbf{r}$ is $|\psi_i (\mathbf{r})|^2 \;\d\mathbf{r}$.

We typically assume that the spatial molecular orbitals form an orthonormal set in $L^2 (\R^3)$, i.e.

$$
  \int_{\R^3} \psi_i^* (\mathbf{r}) \psi_j (\mathbf{r}) \;\d\mathbf{r} = \delta_{ij}
$$

If the set of spatial orbitals $\set{\psi_i}$ were complete in $L^2 (\R^3)$, then any square-integrable function $f(\mathbf{r}) \in L^2 (\R^3)$ could expanded as a convergent series

$$
\begin{align*}
  f(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty a_i \psi_i (\mathbf{r}) \\
  a_i = \int_{\R^3} f(\mathbf{r}) \psi^* (\mathbf{r}) \;\d\mathbf{r}
\end{align*}
$$

In practice, however, we never work with a truly complete set, but instead with a finite subset $\set{\psi_i}_{i=1}^K$ of $K$ such orbitals. This finite set spans only a $K$-dimensional subspace of $L^2 (\R^3)$ and all approximation are at therefore exact only within that subspace.

To fully describe an electron, we must also specify its spin. The spin degree of freedom is represented by a two dimensional Hilbert space with orthonormal functions $\alpha(\omega)$ and $\beta(\omega)$, corresponding to spin-up and spin-down states, respectively. 

A general spin orbital is a function $\chi: \R^3 \times\Omega \to\mathbb{C}$ of $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$, which describes both the spatial distribution and the spin orientation of a electron. From a spatial orbital, $\psi(\mathbf{r})$, we can form two different spin orbitals, one corresponding to spin up and the other to spin down, by multiplying the spatial orbital by the $\alpha$ and $\beta$ spin function respectively, i.e.

$$
  \chi(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases}
    \psi(\mathbf{r})\alpha(\omega) \\
    \psi(\mathbf{r})\beta(\omega)
  \end{cases}
$$

Given a set of $K$ spatial orbitals $\set{\psi_i}_{i=1}^K$, we can thus form a set of $2K$ spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^{2K}$ as

$$
  \begin{rcases}
    \chi_{2i - 1} (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_i^\alpha (\mathbf{r}) \alpha(\omega) \\
    \chi_{2i} (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_i^\beta (\mathbf{r}) \beta(\omega)
  \end{rcases} i = 1,\dots,k
$$

The spin orbitals inherit orthonormality from the spatial and spin part. Explicitly, with respect to the product measure $\d\mathbf{x} = \d\mathbf{r} \d\omega$

$$
  \int \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}) \chi_j (\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x} = \braket{\chi_i | \chi_j} = \delta_{ij}
$$

## Hartree products

Consider a system of $N$ noninteracting electrons described by Hamiltonian of the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{H} = \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{h}(i)
\tag{\label{equation-20}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\hat{h}(i)$ is a one-electron Hamiltonian acting only on the coordinates of electron $i$. Typically, each $\hat{h}(i)$ can be written

$$
  \hat{h}(i) = \hat{T}(i) + \hat{V}(i)
$$

where $\hat{T}(i)$ is the kinetic energy operator and $\hat{V}(i)$ is an external one-electron potential. 

If electron-electron interactions are neglected entirely, then the full electronic Hamiltonian reduces to the form in $\eqref{equation-20}$. More generally, $\hat{h}(i)$ can denote an effective one-electron Hamiltonian in which the Coloumb interaction with the remaining electrons is replaced by an average mean-field potential.

Let the normalized eigenfunctions of the one-electron Hamiltonians $\hat{h}(i)$ be the spin orbitals $\set{\chi_p}_{p=1}^\infty$, satisfying the eigenvalue equation

$$
  \hat{h}(i) \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_i) = \varepsilon_p \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_i)
$$

where $\mathbf{x}_i = (\mathbf{r}_i, \omega_i)$ denotes the combined spatial and spin coordinates of electron $i$ and $\varepsilon_p$ is the corresponding one-electron orbital energy.

Since each operator $\hat{h}(i)$ acts only on the coordinates $\mathbf{x}_i$, operators associated with different electrons commute

$$
  [\hat{h}(i), \hat{h}(j)] = 0,\; i\neq j
$$

Thus, the operators $\hat{h}(i)$ have a common set of simultaneous eigenfunctions. The tensor-product of one-electron eigenfunctions

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi^\text{HP} (\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \prod_{i=1}^N \chi_{p_i} (\mathbf{x}_i)
\tag{\label{equation-21}}
\end{equation*}
$$

called a Hartree product, is therefore an eigenfunction of the many-electron Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$. Indeed

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{H}\Psi^\text{HP}(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N) =& \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{h}(i) \prod_{j=1}^N \chi_{p_j} (\mathbf{x}_j) \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \left(\prod_{j\neq i} \chi_{p_j} (\mathbf{x}_j) \right) \underbrace{\hat{h}(i) \chi_{p_i} (\mathbf{x}_i)}_{= \varepsilon_{p_i} \chi_{p_i} (\mathbf{x}_i)} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \varepsilon_{p_i} \left(\prod_{j=1}^N \chi_{p_j} (\mathbf{x}_j) \right) \\
  =& \left(\sum_{i=1}^N \right) \Psi^\text{HP}(\mathbf{x}_i,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N)
\end{align*}
$$

which shows that $\Psi$ is an eigenfunction of $\hat{H}$ with eigenvalue

$$
  E = \sum_{i=1}^N \varepsilon_{p_i}
$$

Thus for a system of independent electrons, the many-electron energy is simply the sum of the occupied one-electron energies.

The Hartree product $\eqref{equation-21}$ provides an independent-particle or uncorrelated description of the electronic system. It probability density factorizes as

$$
  |\Psi^\text{HP} (\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N)|^2 = \prod_{i=1}^N |\chi_{p_i} (\mathbf{x}_i)|^2
$$

so that the joint probability of simultaneously finding electron $i$ within the volume element $\d\mathbf{x}_i$ centered at $\mathbf{x}_i$ is

$$
  |\Psi^\text{HP}|^2 \prod_{i=1}^N \d\mathbf{x}_i = \prod_{i=1}^N |\chi_{p_i} (\mathbf{x}_i)|^2 \;\d\mathbf{x}_i
$$

Consequently, the position of one electron is statistically independent of the positions of all other electrons. In particular, the conditional probability of finding one electron at a given point is unaffected by the instantaneous locations of the remaining electrons.

In a real electronic system, however, the electrons interact through the Coloum repulsion

$$
  \sum{i < j} \frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r_{ij}}
$$

which couples their motion. As a reuslt, electrons tend to avoid one another, producing electron correlation. The Hartree product cannot describe this instantaneous correlation because it assumes that the many-electron probability distribution factorizes into independent one-electron probabilities.

Beyond its neglect of electron correlation, the Hartree product suffers from a more fundamental deficiency: it is generally incompatible the indistinguishability of identical fermions. By construction, it assigns electron $i$ to spin orbital $\chi_{p_i}$, thereby artificially labeling electrons that are physically indistinguishable.

According to the antisymmetry principle, the wavefunction of a system of identical fermions must be antisymmetric under every permutation $P$ of the electron labels, i.e.

$$
  \Psi (\mathbf{x}_{P(1)},\dots, \mathbf{x}_{P(N)} = (-1)^P \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N)
$$

where $(-1)^P$ is the parity of the permutation. In particular, exchangig any pair of electrons changes the sign of the wavefunction

$$
  \Psi(\dots,\mathbf{x}_i,\dots,\mathbf{x}_j,\dots) = \Psi(\dots,\mathbf{x}_j,\dots,\mathbf{x}_i,\dots)
$$

A Hartree product does not, in general, satisfy this antisymmetry condition. Although it correctly describes independent particles mathematically, it cannot represent the quantum state of a system of identical electrons.

## Slater determinants

Consider a two-electron system in which the spin orbitals $\chi_{p_1}$ and $\chi_{p_2}$ are occupied. Assigning electron $1$ to $\chi_{p_1}$ and electron $2$ to $\chi_{p_2}$ gives the Hartree product

$$
  \Psi^\text{HP}_{1,2} (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2)
$$

Alternatively, assigning electron $1$ to $\chi_{p_2}$ and electron $2$ to $\chi_{p_1}$ gives

$$
  \Psi^\text{HP}_{2,1} (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_1)
$$

Each Hartree products assigns a definite orbital to a particular electron lavel and therefore treats the electrons as distinguishable. This contradicts the indistinguishability of identical electrons. To construct a valid fermionic wavefunction, we require antisymmetry under exchange of the electron coordinates, i.e. 

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = -\Psi(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1)
$$

The appropriate antisymmetric linear combination is therefore

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left[\chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2) - \chi_{p_2}(\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_1}(\mathbf{x}_2) \right]
\tag{\label{equation-22}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The normalization factor $2^{-1/2}$ follows from the orthonormality of the spin orbitals. 

Under exchange of the electron coordinates

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1) =& \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_1) - \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \right] \\
  =& -\Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)
\end{align*}
$$

so the antisymmetry requirement is satisfied.

If both electrons occupy the same spin orbital, i.e. $\chi_{p_1} = \chi_{p_2}$, then

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left[\chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_2) - \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_2) \right] = 0
$$

Thus, no two electrons can occupy the same spin orbital. This is the Pauli exclusion principle, which follows directly from the antisymmetry of fermionic wavefunctions.

The antisymmetric two-electron wavefunction of $\eqref{equation-22}$ can be expressed as the determinant

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = 2^{-1/2} \begin{vmatrix}
    \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) & \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_1) \\
    \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_2) & \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2)
  \end{vmatrix}
$$

whic is called a Slater determinant. For an $N$-electron system, this generalizes to

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \begin{vmatrix}
    \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) & \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_1) & \cdots & \chi_{p_N} (\mathbf{x}_1) \\
    \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_2) & \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \chi_{p_N} (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_N) & \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_N) & \cdots & \chi_{p_N} (\mathbf{x}_N) \\
  \end{vmatrix}
\tag{\label{equation-40}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The factor $(N!)^{-1/2}$ ensures normalization when the occupied spin orbitals are orthonormal. 

A Slater determinant represents $N$ electrons occupying the set of spin orbitals $\set{\chi_{p_1},\dots,\chi_{p_N}}$ without assigning a particular orbital to a particular electron. Exchanging two electron coordinates corresponds to exchaning two rows of the Slater determinant, which changes its sign. Thus, Slater determinants satisfy the antisymmetry principle. Likewise, if two electrons occupy the same spin orbital, two columns of the determinant become identical, making the determinant vanish.

It is convenient to introduce the compact notation for a Slater determinant

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \ket{\chi_{p_1}(\mathbf{x}_1) \cdots \chi_{p_N} (\mathbf{x}_N)}
$$

where the normalization factor is implicitly included. If we always choose the electron labels to be in the canonical order $\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$, then this can be further shortened to

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \ket{\chi_{p_1} \cdots \chi_{p_N}}
$$

In this notation, the anti-symmetry property of Slater determinants is

$$
  \ket{\cdots \chi_{p_i} \cdots \chi_{p_j}} = -\ket{\cdots \chi_{p_j} \cdots \chi_{p_i} \cdots}
$$

Thus, a Slater determinant is uniquely determined, up to an overall sign, by the set of occupied spin orbitals. Slater determinants constructed from different sets of orthonormal spin orbitals are mutually orthogonal.

### Exchange effects

Antisymmetrization introduces exchange effects, which arise purely from the indistinguishability and fermionic symmetry of electrons. However, exchange is not the same as electron correlation. A single Slater determinant contains exchange exactly, but neglects dynamical correlation arising from the instantaneous Coulomb interaction between electrons.

To examine the origin of exchange effects consider the two-electron Slater determinant

$$
  \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \ket{\chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2)}
$$

Suppose first that the two electrons have opposite spins and occupy different spatial orbitals

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) =& \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1) \alpha(\omega_1) \\
  \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2) =& \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2) \beta(\omega_2)
\end{align*}
$$

Expanding the determinant yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \Psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} [&\psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1) \alpha(\omega_1) \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2) \beta(\omega_2) \\
  &- \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_1) \beta(\omega_1) \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_2) \alpha(\omega_2)]
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-23}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The spatial two-electron probability density is obtained by integrating over the spin coordinates

$$
  \Pr(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = \int |\Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)|^2 \;\d\omega_1 \;\d\omega_2
$$

Because $\braket{\alpha|\beta} = 0$, the exchange cross term vanish, resulting in

$$
  \Pr(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = \frac{1}{2} &\left( |\psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 |\psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2)|^2 \right. \\
  & \left. + |\psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 |\psi(\mathbf{r}_2)|^2 \right)
$$

Thus, opposite-spin electrons described by a single Slater determinant have no spatial correlation induced by exchange. In particular, if the electrons share the same spatial orbital, $\psi_1 = \psi_2 = \psi$, then

$$
  \Pr(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = |\psi(\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 |\psi(\mathbf{r}_2)|^2
$$

which factorizes completely. Consequently, two electrons of opposite spin can occupy the same spatial region with nonzer probability.

Now suppose that the two electrons have the same spin, say $\beta$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_{p_1} (\mathbf{x}_1) =& \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1) \beta(\omega_1) \\
  \chi_{p_2} (\mathbf{x}_2) =& \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2) \beta(\omega_2)
\end{align*}
$$

The resulting spatial probability density becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Pr(\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2) = \frac{1}{2} &\left(|\psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 |\psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2)|^2 + |\psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_2)|^2 |\psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 \right. \\
  &- \psi_1^* (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_2^* (\mathbf{r}_2) \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_2) \\
  & \left. - \psi_2^* (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_1^* (\mathbf{r}_2) \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}_2) \right)
\end{align*}
$$

The final two terms constitute the exchange term. They arise solely from antisymmetry and produce a correlation between the electrons with parallel spins.

For coincident positions, $\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{r}_2 = \mathbf{r}$, the spatial probability vanishes, $\Pr(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}) = 0$. Thus, two electrons with identical spin cannot be found at the same point in space. This exclusion region is called the Fermi hole. 

In summary, a single Slater determinant correctly incorporates exchange effects: electrons with parallel spins exhibit exchange-induced spatial correlation, whereas electrons with opposite spins do not. However, the determinant does not describe the full electron correlation arising from Coloumb repulsion. Recovering this missing dynamical correlation requires a correlated many-electron wavefunction.

## Minimal basis model of $\ce{H2}$

The hydrogen molecule, $\ce{H2}$, provides the simplest example of the linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO) approximation for constructing molecular orbitals. In a minimal basis description, each hydrogen atom contributes a single $1s$ basis function, so the one-electron Hilbert space is approximated by the two-dimensional subsapce spanned by these functions.

Let the nucluei of an $\ce{H2}$ molecule be located at positions $\mathbf{R}_1$ and $\mathbf{R}_2$. We denote the corresponding atomic basis functions by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi_1 (\mathbf{r}) = \phi(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}_1) \\
  \phi_2 (\mathbf{r}) = \phi(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}_2)
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathbf{r}$ is the electronic coordinate.

For an isolated hydrogen atom, the exact normalized $1s$ orbital is

$$
  \phi(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}) = \sqrt{\frac{\zeta^3}{\pi}} e^{-\zeta \norm{\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}}}
$$

where $\zeta$ is the orbital exponent. For hydrogen $\zeta = 1$ in atomic units. This function is an example of a Slater-type orbital (STO).

In practical electronic structure calculations, Gaussian-type orbitals (GTOs) are often used instead because the resulting multicentre integral can be evaluated analytically much more efficiently. The normalized $1s$ Gaussian orbital has the form

$$
  \phi(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}) = \left(\frac{2\alpha}{\pi}\right)^{3/4} e^{-\alpha\norm{\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{R}}^2}
$$

where $\alpha > 0$ is the Gaussian orbital exponent.

The atomic basis functions are individually normalized, i.e. $\braket{\phi_i |\phi_i} = 1$, but they are not orthogonal. Their overlap is

$$
  S_{12} = \braket{\phi_1 |\phi_2} = \int_{\R^3} \phi_1^* (\mathbf{r}) \phi_2 (\mathbf{r}) \;\d\mathbf{r}
$$

The overlap depends continuously on the internuclear separation $R_{12} = \norm{\mathbf{R}_1 - \mathbf{R}_2}$. For coincident centres, $R_{12} = 0$, the two basis functions are identical and $S_{12} = 1$. On the other hand,

$$
  \lim_{R_{12} \to\infty} S_{12} = 0
$$

since the orbitals become spatially disjoint.

### Bonding and antibonding molecular orbitals

Within the two-dimensional space spanned by $\set{\phi_1, \phi_2}$, two orthonormal molecular orbitals are obtained as symmetry-adapted linear combinations. The symmetric (gerade) combination

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \psi_1 = \frac{\phi_1 + \phi_2}{\sqrt{2(1 + S_{12})}}
\tag{\label{equation-24}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is the bonding molecular orbital, while the antisymmetric (ungerade) combination

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \psi_2 = \frac{\phi_1 + \phi_2}{\sqrt{2(1 - S_{12})}}
\tag{\label{equation-25}}
\end{equation*}
$$

is the antibonding molecular orbital.

The normalization constants follow direction from $\braket{\phi_i |\phi_i} = 1$ and $\braket{\phi_1 |\phi_2} = S_{12}$ which give $\braket{\psi_i | \psi_j} = \delta_{ij}$.

The orbital $\psi_1$ is invariant under inversion through the midpoint of the bond and therefore has gerade symmetry, whereas $\psi_2$ changes sign under inversion and therefore has ungerade symmetry.

### LCAO approximation

The above procedure is the simplest example of the general LCAO expansion. Given a finite basis $\set{\phi_\mu}_{\mu=1}^K$, each molecular orbital is approximated as

$$
  \psi_i (\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{\mu=1}^K C_{\mu i} \phi_\mu (\mathbf{r})
$$

where the coefficients $C_{\mu i}$ are determined variationally by solving the Hartree-Fock and Kohn-Sham equations.

In the exact theory, the one-electron Hilbert space is infinite-dimensional, so an exact molecular orbital generally requires an infinite expansion. Restricting the expansion to one basis function per hydrogen atom, $K = 2$, defines the minimal basis approximation for $\ce{H2}$.

Because the hydrogen molecule posesses inversion symmetry, the Hartree-Fock molecular orbitals within this two-dimensional subspace are precisely the normalized symmetric and anitsymmetric combinations given in $\eqref{equation-24}$ and $\eqref{equation-25}$. In a larger basis, additional basis functions would modify these orbitals through further variational optimization.

### Ground state spin orbitals

The spatial molecular orbitals $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ generates each two spin orbitals

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}) \alpha(\omega) \\
  \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_1 (\mathbf{r}) \beta(\omega) \\
  \chi_3 (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}) \alpha(\omega) \\
  \chi_4 (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_2 (\mathbf{r}) \beta(\omega)
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denotes teh combined spatial and spin coordinates.

Since the Hartree-Fock operator is spin independent for a closed-shell system, each spatial orbital gives rise to two degenerate spin orbitals differing only in their spin function. Thus

$$
  \varepsilon_1 = \varepsilon_2 < \varepsilon_3 = \varepsilon_4
$$

The lower-energy pari correspnds to the bonding molecular orbital, whiel the higher-energy pair corresponds to the anitbonding molecular orbital.

### Hartree-Fock ground state

Within the minimal-basis approximation, the Hartree-Fock ground state is obtained by occupying the two lowest-energy spin orbitals

$$
  \ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \chi_2}
$$

Since both electrons occupy the bonding spatial orbital with opposite spins, it is common to suppress the explicit functions and denote
- $\chi_1 \equiv \psi_1$
- $\chi_2 \equiv \bar{\psi}_1$
- $\chi_3 \equiv \psi_2$
- $\chi_4 \equiv \bar{\psi}_2$

where a bar indicates a $\beta$-spin electron and the absence thereof denotes an $\alpha$-spin electron.

In this notation the Hartree-Fock ground state can be written

$$
  \ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\psi_1 \bar{\psi_1}} = \ket{1\bar{1}}
$$

This notation emphasizes that both electrons occupy the bonding molecular orbital while forming a spin-singlet pair. Because the electrons have opposite spin projections, the Pauli exclusion principle is satisfied even though they occupy the same spatial orbital. Within the minimal-basis Hartree-Fock approximation, this single Slater determinant provides the variational ground-state wavefunction of $\ce{H2}$.

## Excited determinants

Let $\set{\chi_p}_{p=1}^{2K}$ be the orthonormal set of spin orbitals obtained from a Hartree-Fock calculation, where $K$ denotes the number of spatial orbitals. The $N$ spin orbitals of lowest energy are occupied and form the Hartree-Fock reference determinant $\ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$, which is the variationally optimal single-determinant approximation to the exact electronic ground state.

The Hartree-Fock determinant is only one of many Slater determinants that can be constructed from the complete set of $2K$ spin orbitals. Since an $N$-electron Slater determinant is uniquely determined (up to an overall sign) by the choice of $N$ occupied spin orbitals, the total number of distinct determinants is

$$
  \binom{2K}{N} = \frac{(2K)!}{N!(2K - N)!}
$$

These determinants form an orthonormal basis for the $N$-electron antisymmetric subspace generated by the Hartree-Fock spin orbitals, and is called the full configuration interaction (FCI) space associated with the chosen one-electron basis.

It is convenient to classify all determinants relative to the Hartree-Fock reference determinant $\ket{\Psi_0}$. To this end, we partition the spin orbitals into
- occupied spin orbitals, $\set{1, \dots, N}$
- virtual spin orbitals, $\set{N+1, \dots, 2K}$

An excited determinant is obtained by replacing one or more occupied spin orbitals in the reference determinant $\ket{\Psi_0}$ by virtual spin orbitals.

A singly excited determinant is obtained by replacing the occupied orbital $\chi_a$ with the virtual orbital $\chi_b$

$$
  \ket{\Psi_a^b} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_{a-1} \chi_a \chi_{a+1} \cdots \chi_N}
$$

where the orbitals are understood to be reordered into the canonical ordering of the determinant, with the corresponding sign determined by the parity of the required permutation.

More generally, an $n$-fold excited determinant is written $\ket{\Psi_{a_1 \cdots a_n}^{b_1 \cdots b_n}}$, where the occupied spin orbitals $\chi_{a_1}, \dots, \chi_{a_n}$ have been replaced by the virtual spin orbitals $\chi_{b_1}, \cdots, \chi_{b_n}$.

Every determinant in the FCI space can therefore be classified uniquely according to its excitation level relative to the Hartree-Fock reference determinant.

Although these excited determinants are generally not eigenfunctions of the electronic Hamiltonian and therefore should not be identified with exact excited states, they provide a convenient orthonormal basis for the $N$-electron Hilbert space. Consequently, both the exact ground state and the exact excited states can be expanded as linear combinations of these determinants. This expansion forms the basis of the configuration interaction (CI) method.

## Configuration interaction

Excited Slater determinants provide a convenient basis for representing correlated many-electron wavefunctions. Their completeness follows from the completeness of the underlying one-electron basis. Let $\set{\chi_p (\mathbf{x})}_{p=1}^\infty$ be a complete orthonormal basis for the one-electron Hilbert space $L^2 (\R^3 \times \set{\alpha, \beta})$, where $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denotes the combined spatial and spin coordinates. Then every square-integrable one-electron wavefunction can be expanded as

$$
  \phi(\mathbf{x}_1) = \sum_{p=1}^\infty a_p \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1)
$$

with expansion coefficients $a_p = \braket{\chi_p |\phi}$.

A two-electron wavefunction $\phi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \in L^2 ((\R^3 \times\set{\alpha, \beta})^2)$ can be expanded in an analogous way by fixing $\mathbf{x}_2$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \phi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \sum_{p=1}^\infty a_p (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1)
\tag{\label{equation-27}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Each coefficient $a_p (\mathbf{x}_2)$ is itself a square-integrable function of one variable that can be expanded as

$$
  a_p (\mathbf{x}_2) = \sum_{q=1}^\infty b_{pq} \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_2)
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-27}$ gives

$$
  \phi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \sum_{p,q} b_{pq} \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_2)
$$

Thus, the products $\chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_2)$ form a complete basis for the two-electron tensor-product space.

For identical electrons, however, the wavefunction must satisfy the fermionic antisymmetry condition $\phi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = -\phi(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1)$, then $b_{pq} = -b_{qp}$ and $b_{pp} = 0$. The expansion therefore becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \phi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) =& \sum_{p < q} b_{pq} [\chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_2) - \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_q(\mathbf{x}_2)] \\
  =& \sum_{p < q} \sqrt{2} b_{pq} \ket{\chi_p \chi_q}
\end{align*}
$$

where

$$
  \ket{\chi_p \chi_q} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{vmatrix}
    \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_1) & \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_1) \\
    \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_2) & \chi_q (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  \end{vmatrix}
$$

is the normalized two-electron Slater determinant.

Thus every antisymmetric two-electron wavefunction can be expandded is a linear combination of Slater determinants constructed from a complete one-electron basis.

This argument extends directly to $N$ electrons. If the one-electron spin orbitals form a complete basis, then the corresponding Slater determinants constitute a complete basis for the antisymmetric $N$-electron Hilbert space. Consequently, every excat electronic eigenstate can be written as a linear combination of Slater determinants.

Using the Hartree-Fock ground state determinant $\ket{\Psi_0}$ as a reference, every determinant can be classified according to its excitation level. The exact wave function for any state of the system can therefore be expanded as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\Phi} =& c_0 \ket{\Psi_0} + \sum_{ab} c_a^b \ket{\Psi_a^b} + \sum_{\substack{a_1 < a_2 \\ b_1 < b_2}} c_{a_1 a_2}^{b_1 b_2} \ket{\Psi_{a_1 a_2}^{b_1 b_2}} \\
  &+ \sum_{\substack{a_1 < a_2 < a_3 \\ b_1 < b_2 < b_3}} c_{a_1 a_2 a_3}^{b_1 b_2 b_3} \ket{\Psi_{a_1 a_2 a_3}^{b_1 b_2 b_3}} +\cdots
\end{align*}
$$

where
- $a_i$ denote occupied Hartree-Fock spin orbitals
- $b_i$ denote virtual spin orbitals

The coefficients are determined by solving the Schrödinger equation in this determinant basis.

The collection of all Slater determinants generated from a complete one-electron basis forms a complete basis for the exact electronic problem. The matrix representation of the electronic Hamiltonian is given by $H_{ij} = \braket{\Psi_i |\hat{H}|\Psi_j}$. The eigenvalues of this Hamiltonian matrix are the exact nonrelativistic electronic energies within the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, while the corresponding eigenvectors determine the CI expansion coefficients.

Because each determinant is specified by an electronic configuration, that is, by the occupied spin orbitals, the method is known as configuration interaction.

### Correlation energy

The Hartree-Fock energy is the minimum energy obtainable from a single Slater determinant

$$
  E_\text{HF} = \min_{\Psi\in\mathcal{D}} \braket{\Psi|\hat{H}|\Psi}
$$

where $\mathcal{D}$ denotes the set of normalized Slater determinants.

The exact ground-state energy satisfies the variational inequality $E_0 \leq E_\text{HF}$ and the difference

$$
  E_\text{corr} = E_0 - E_\text{HF} \leq 0
$$

is called the correlation energy. This energy measures the error introduced by restricting the wavefunction to a single determinant. It includes the dynamical correlation arising from the instantaneous Coulomb interaction between electrons, which is absent in Hartree-Fock theory.

### Full configuration interaction

In practice, we always work with a finite one-electron basis $\ket{\chi_p}_{p=1}^{2K}$. The corresponding determinant space is finite-dimensional, containing $\binom{2K}{N}$ Slater determinants. Although this determinant basis is no longer complete for the exact electronic Hilbert space, it spans the antisymmetric subspace generated by the chosen one-electronic basis.

Diagonalizing the Hamiltonian in this dinite determinant basis yields the exact solution within the finite one-electron basis. This procedure is called full configuration interaction (FCI).

As the one-electron basis is enlarged, the FCI energy converges monotonically from above to the exact Born-Oppenheimer energy.

Since the size of the FCI space grows combinatorially, $\dim(\hat{H}_N) = \binom{2K}{N}$, this procedure is computationally infeasible except for relatively small systems. Practical CI methods therefore truncate the expansion, retining only determinants up to a prescribed excitation level.

# Matrix elements between Slater determinants

Let $\hat{O}$ be a linear operator acting on the antisymmetric $N$-electron Hilbert space $\bigwedge^N \mathcal{H}$, and let $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$ be normalized Slater determinants constructed from an orthonormal set of spin orbitals $\set{\chi_p}_{p=1}^\infty \subseteq \mathcal{H}$. Each spin orbital $\chi_p (\mathbf{x})$ is a function of the combined spatial and spin coordinate $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$. 

Our objective is to evaluate the many-electron matrix element $\braket{K|\hat{O}|L}$. A direct evaluation of this quantity involves integration over the coordinates of all $N$ electrons and is generally impractical. However, when $\hat{O}$ is a one-electron or two-electron operator, the antisymmetric structure of Slater determinants allws these many-electron matrix elements to be expressed entirely in terms of one- and two-electron integrals over the constituent spin orbitals. Exploiting the separability of the spin coordinate, these spin-orbital integrals can in turn be reduced to integrals over the corresponding spatial orbitals.

## Notations for one- and two-electron integrals

For a one-electron operator $\hat{h}$, we define the one-electron integrals

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} := \braket{\chi_i |\hat{h}|\chi_j} = \int \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}) \hat{h} \chi_j (\mathbf{x}) \;\d\mathbf{x}
\tag{\label{equation-37}}
\end{equation*}
$$

If $\hat{h}$ is Hermitian, then

$$
  \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} = \braket{j|\hat{h}|i}^*
$$

For a symmetric two-electron operator $\hat{v}(1,2) = \hat{v}(2,1)$, we define the two-electron integral in physicists' notation by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{ij|kl} =& \braket{\chi_i \chi_j |\hat{v}(1,2)| \chi_k \chi_l} \\
  =& \iint \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_j^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{v}(1,2) \chi_k (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_l (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-38}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In this notation, the bra functions are grouped together on the left and the ket function on the right. The electron coordinates are ordered as $(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)$.

From the symmetry of $\hat{v}(1,2)$, interchanging the dummy integration variables gives

$$
  \braket{ij|kl} = \braket{ji|lk}
$$

Furthermore, Hermiticity of $\hat{v}(1,2)$ implies

$$
  \braket{ij|kl} = \braket{kl|ij}^*
$$

Combining these two identities yields

$$
  \braket{ij|kl} = \braket{lk|ji}^*
$$

For real spin orbitals, complex conjugation can be omitted, giving the four equivalent forms

$$
  \braket{ij|kl} = \braket{ji|lk} = \braket{kl|ij} = \braket{lk|ji}
$$

Since matrix elements between Slater determinants naturally involve the combination of Coulomb and exchange terms, it is convenient to introduce the antisymmetrized two-electron integral

$$
\begin{align*}
  \bra{ij}\ket{kl} =& \braket{ij|kl} - \braket{ij|lk} \\
  =& \iint \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_j^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{v}(i,j) (1 - \hat{P}_{12}) \chi_k (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_l (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

where $\hat{P}_{12}$ denotes the operator that exchanges the coordinates of electron $1$ and $2$, i.e.

$$
  \hat{P}_{12} f(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = f(\mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1)
$$

The antisymmetrized integral inherits the antisymmetry

$$
\begin{align*}
  \bra{ij}\ket{kl} =& -\bra{ji}\ket{kl} \\
  =& -\bra{ij}\ket{lk} \\
  =& \bra{ji}\ket{lk}
\end{align*}
$$

and therefore vanishes whenever either pair of indices is identical

$$
  \bra{ij}\ket{kk} = \bra{ii}\ket{kl} = 0
$$

If the interaction operator $\hat{v}(1,2)$ is Hermitian, then

$$
  \bra{ij}\ket{kl} = \bra{kl}\ket{ij}^*
$$

and for real orbitals, this identity reduces to

$$
  \bra{ij}\ket{kl} = \bra{kl}\ket{ij}
$$

An alternative convention, known as chemists' notation, is defined by

$$
  [ij|kl] = \iint \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_j (\mathbf{x}_1) \hat{v}(1,2) \chi_k^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_l (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
$$

Here, functions depending on the same electron coordinate are grouped together. The two conventions are related by a permutation of the middle indices

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [ij|kl] = \braket{ik|jl}
\tag{\label{equation-62}}
\end{equation*}
$$

By symmetry of $\hat{v}(1,2)$, interchanging the dummy integration varaibles gives the permutation symmetry

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [ij|kl] = [kl|ij]
\tag{\label{equation-60}}
\end{equation*}
$$

By Hermicity of $\hat{v}(1,2)$ we obtain the conjugation identity

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [ij|kl] = [ji|lk]^*
\tag{\label{equation-59}}
\end{equation*}
$$

For real orbitals, these identities combine to the eight-fold permutation symmetry

$$
\begin{align*}
  [ij|kl] =& [ji|lk] = [kl|ij] = [lk|ji] \\
  =& [ji|kl] = [ij|kl] = [lk|ij] = [kl|ji]
\end{align*}
$$

When only spatial orbitals are involved, the corresponding one-electron integral is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  (i|\hat{h}|j) =& h_{ij} := (\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_j) \\
  =& \int \psi_i (\mathbf{r}_1) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_j (\mathbf{r}_1) \;\d\mathbf{r}_1
\end{align*}
$$

while the corresponding two-electron integral is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  (ij|kl) =& (\psi_i \psi_j |\hat{v}(1,2)| \psi_k \psi_l) \\
  =& \iint \psi_i^* (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_j (\mathbf{r}_1) \hat{v}(1,2) \psi_k^* (\mathbf{r}_2) \psi_l (\mathbf{r}_2) \;\d\mathbf{r}_1 \;\d\mathbf{r}_2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-61}}
\end{equation*}
$$

### Coloumb and exchange integral

For the electron-electron Coulomb interaction

$$
  \hat{v}(1,2) = \frac{1}{r_{12}}
$$

two important classes of two-integrals can be identified: the Coulomb and exchange integrals.

The Coulomb integral between the spatial orbitals $\psi_i$ and $\psi_j$ is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  J_{ij} :=& (ii|jj) = \braket{ij|ij} \\
  =& \int |\psi_i (\mathbf{r}_1)|^2 \frac{1}{r_{12}} |\psi_j (\mathbf{r}_2)|^2 \;\d\mathbf{r}_1 \;\d\mathbf{r}_2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-57}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Likewise, the exchange integral is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  K_{ij} :=& (ij|ji) = \braket{ij|ji} \\
  =& \int \psi_i^* (\mathbf{r}_1) \psi_j (\mathbf{r}_1) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \psi_j^* (\mathbf{r}_2) \psi_i (\mathbf{r}_2) \;\d\mathbf{r}_1 \;\d\mathbf{r}_2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-58}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The Coulomb integral $J_{ij}$ is the classical electrostatic interaction energy between the charge distributions $|\psi_i|^2$ and $|\psi_j|^2$, whereas the exchange integral $K_{ij}$ has no classical analogue and arises solely from the antisymmetry of fermionic wavefunctions. It contributes only between electrons of the same spin because of the orthogonality of the spin functions.

The Coulomb and exchange integrals satisfy the following properties
1. $J_{ii} = K_{ii}$
2. $J\in\R$, i.e. $J_{ij}^* = J_{ij}$
3. $J_{ij} = J_{ji}$
4. $K\in\R$, i.e. $K_{ij}^* = K_{ij}$
5. $K_{ij} = K_{ji}$
6. For real spatial orbitals
$$
\begin{align*}
  K_{ij} =& (ij|ij) = (ji|ji) \\
  =& \braket{ii|jj} = \braket{jj|ii}
\end{align*}
$$

## Slater-Condon rules

A general one-electron operator in an $N$-electron Hilbert space takes the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{O}_1 = \sum_{i=1} \hat{h}(i)
\tag{\label{equation-42}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\hat{h}(i)$ acts only on the coordinates of electron $i$. These operators represent dynamic variables that depend only on the position or momentum of the electron in question, independent of the position or momentum of other electrons.

A general two-electron operator in an $N$-electron Hilbert space is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{O}_2 = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j>i}^N \hat{v}(i,j) = \sum_{i < j} \hat{v}(i,j)
\tag{\label{equation-43}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\hat{v}(i,j)$ acts on the coordinates of electrons $i$ and $j$ simultaneously.

Suppose $\hat{O}_n$ is an $n$-electron operator that acts simultaneously on at most $n$ electron coordinates simultaneously. If the Slater determinants $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$ differ in more than $n$ occupied spin orbitals, then $\braket{K|\hat{O}_n|L} = 0$. This follows because an $n$-electron operator can alter the occupations of at most $n$ electrons. Any additional mismatched spin orbitals remain orthogonal between the two determinants, forcing the overlap to vanish. Consequently,
- a one-electron operator connects only identical or singly excited determinants
- a two-electron operator connects only identical, singly excited, or doubly excited determinants

Thus, if $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$ differ by three or more spin orbitals, then

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = 0
$$

This leaves us with three cases for the Slater-Condon rules for evaluating matrix elements $\braket{K|\hat{O}|L}$. 

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

Suppose $\ket{K} = \ket{L}$ and that the occupied spin orbitals are $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^N$.

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|K} = \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i}
$$

- Two-electron operators

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|K} =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N (ii|jj) - (ij|ji)
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2: Determinants differing by one spin orbital**

Assume $\ket{L}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing the occupied orbital $\chi_i$ with $\chi_p$.

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = \braket{i|\hat{h}|p}
$$

- Two-electron operator

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} =& \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{pj} \\
  =& \sum_{n=1}^N (ip|jj) - (ij|jp)
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 3: Determinants differing by two spin orbitals**

Assume $\ket{L}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing $\chi_i, \chi_j$ with $\chi_p, \chi_q$, respectively.

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = 0
$$

- Two-electron-operator

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} =& \bra{ij}\ket{pq} \\
  =& (ip|jq) - (iq|jp)
\end{align*}
$$

### Maximum coincidence

Before applying the Slater-Condon rules, the two determinants must first be brought into maximum coincidence. This is achieved by permuting columns so that all common occupied spin orbitals appear in corresponding positions. Each column interchange changes the sign of a slater determinant such that

$$
  \ket{L} = (-1)^\sigma \ket{L'}
$$

where $\ket{L'}$ is in maximum coincidence with $\ket{K}$, and $\sigma$ is the parity of the permutation. The Slater-Condon rules are then applied to $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L'}$, with the overall phase factor $(-1)^\sigma$ included in the final result.

Consider, for example, a matrix element between the Slater determinants $\ket{\Psi_1} = \ket{abcd}$ and $\ket{\Psi_2} = \ket{crds}$. At first glance, it might appear that the two determinants differ in all four columns. However, permuting the spin orbitals of $\ket{\Psi_2}$ yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\Psi_2} =& \ket{crd} \\
  =& -\ket{crsd} = \ket{srcd}
\end{align*}
$$

The determinants are now in maximum coincidence and differ only by the substitutions $a\to s$ and $b\to r$, giving $\ket{\Psi_1|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_2} = 0$ and $\braket{\Psi_1|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_2} = \bra{ab}\ket{sr}$.

### Energy of a single Slater determinant

For an electronic Hamiltonian of the form $\hat{H} = \hat{O}_1 + \hat{O}_2$, the energy expectation value over a Slater determinant $\ket{K}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{H}|K} =& \braket{K|\hat{O}_1 + \hat{O}_2|K} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

where

$$
  \hat{h}(i) = \frac{-1}{2} \nabla_i^2 - \sum_A \frac{Z_A}{r_{iA}}
$$

Since

$$
  \bra{ii}\ket{ii} = \bra{jj}\ket{jj} = 0
$$

and $\bra{ij}\ket{ij} = \bra{ji}\ket{ji}$, the double sum can be restricted to unique pairs, giving

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{K|\hat{H}|K} =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j>i}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j>i}^N [ii|jj] - [ji|ij]
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-63}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This shows that the energy of a single Slater determinant decomposes into the sum of the one‑electron contributions from each occupied spin orbital, together with the antisymmetrized electron–electron interaction terms associated with each unique pair of occupied spin orbitals.

### Hartree-Fock grund state

Consider the Hartree-Fock ground state $\ket{\Psi_0}$, where
- occupied spin orbitals are indexed by $a,b,\dots$
- virtual spin orbital are indexed by $r,s,\dots$

The Slater-Condon rules in terms of the Hartree-Fock ground state become as follows.

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_1|\Psi_0} = \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i}
$$

- Two-electron operators

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_0} =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [ii|jj] - [ij|ji]
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2: Determinants differing by one spin orbital**

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_1|\Psi_i^p} = \braket{i|\hat{h}|p}
$$

- Two-electron operator

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_i^p} =& \sum_{i=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{pj} \\
  =& \sum_{b=1}^N [ip|jj] - [ij|jp]
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 3: Determinants differing by two spin orbitals**

- One-electron operator

$$
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_1|\Psi_{ij}^{pq}} = 0
$$

- Two-electron-operator

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_{ij}^{pq}} =& \bra{ij}\ket{pq} \\
  =& [ip|jq] - [iq|jp]
\end{align*}
$$

---

Using the Slater-Condon rules, the energy of the Hartree-Fock ground state is

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  E_0 =& \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{b=1}^N [ii|jj] - [ij|ji]
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-53}}
\end{equation*}
$$

#### Ionization energy

Denote the Hartree-Fock ground state for an $N$-electron system by $\ket{{}^N \Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$, with energy

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  {}^N E_0 =& \braket{{}^N \Psi_0|\hat{H}|{}^N \Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-39}}
\end{equation*}
$$

If we remove the electron occupying spin orbital $\chi_a$, the ionized $(N-1)$-electron determinant is

$$
  \ket{{}^{N-1} \Psi_a} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_{a-1} \chi_{a+1} \cdots \chi_N}
$$

with energy 

$$
\begin{align*}
  {}^{N-1} E_a =& \braket{{}^{N-1} \Psi_a |\hat{H}|{}^{N-1} \Psi_a} \\
  =& \sum_{\substack{i=1 \\ i \neq a}}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\substack{i,j=1 \\ i,j\neq a}}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

To compare this energies, we separate all terms involving $a$ in the double sum of $\eqref{equation-39}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij} =& \sum_{\substack{i\neq a \\ j\neq a}} \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \\
  &+ \sum_{j\neq a} \bra{aj}\ket{aj} + \sum_{i\neq a} \bra{ia}\ket{ia} \\
  &+ \bra{aa}\ket{aa}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\bra{aa}\ket{aa} = 0$ and $\bra{aj}\ket{aj} = \bra{ja}\ket{ja}$, it follows that the two-electron energy contributions in $\eqref{equation-39}$ can be decomposed as

$$
  \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \bra{ij}\ket{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j\neq a} \bra{ij}\ket{ij} + \sum_{j\neq a} \bra{aj}\ket{aj}
$$

The energy required for this ionization process is therefore

$$
\begin{align*}
  {}^N E_0 - {}^{N-1} E_a =& \left(\sum_i \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} - \sum_{i\neq a} \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} \right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(\sum_{i,j\neq a} \bra{ij}\ket{ij} + \sum_{j\neq a} \bra{aj}\ket{aj} - \sum_{i,j\neq a} \bra{ij}\ket{ij} \right) \\
  =& \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \sum_{j\neq a}^N \bra{aj}\ket{aj}
\end{align*}
$$

### Derivation of Slater-Condon rules

From $\eqref{equation-40}$, an $N$-electron Slater determinant constructed from orthonormal spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^N$ can be written as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{P\in S_N} (-1)^P \hat{P} [\chi_1 (1) \cdots \chi_N (N)]
\tag{\label{equation-41}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where 
- $S_N$ denotes the symmetric group of all permutations of the electron labels $1,\dots,N$
- $\hat{P}$ is the permutation operator corresponding to the permutation $P$
- $(-1)^P$ is the parity of the permutation $P$

Here we use the shorthand $\chi_p (i) = \chi_p (\mathbf{x}_i)$.

Let $\ket{K} = \ket{\chi_{p_1} \cdots \chi_{p_N}}$ and $\ket{L} = \ket{\chi_{q_1} \cdots \chi_{q_N}}$ be two Slater determinants with maximal coincidence. To derive the Slater-Condon rules, we first consider the overlap $\braket{K|L}$ corresponding to the identity operator. Substituting $\eqref{equation-41}$ for both determinants gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|L} = \frac{1}{N!} \sum_{P,Q \in S_N} (-1)^P (-1)^Q \int& \hat{P} [\chi_{p_1}^* (1) \cdots \chi_{p_N}^* (N)] \\
  &\times\hat{Q} [\chi_{q_1} (1) \cdots \chi_{q_N} (N)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots \d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{align*}
$$

If the determinants do not contain exactly the same set of occupied orbitals, then at least one factor in every product is an overlap between distinct orbitals. By orthonormality of the spin orbitals, that overlap vanishes, implying $\ket{K|L} = =$, whenever the set of occupied spin orbitals differ. Consequently, two Slater determinants constructed from different sets of spin orbitals are orthogonal.

Now suppose that $K$ and $L$ are identical determinants. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|K} = \frac{1}{N!} \sum_{P,Q \in S_N} (-1)^P (-1)^Q \int & \hat{P} [\chi_1^* (1) \cdots \chi_N^* (N)] \\
  &\times \hat{Q} [\chi_1 (1) \cdots \chi_N (N)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots \d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{align*}
$$

Each term is a product of one-electron overlaps. Such a product is nonzero only if every electron occupies the same spin orbital in both permuted products. Since the spin orbitals are orthonormal, this occurs only when the permutations are identical, i.e. $P = Q$. All cross terms therefore vanish, leaving

$$
  \braket{K|K} = \frac{1}{N!} \sum_{P\in S_N} \int \hat{P} [\chi_1^* (1) \cdots \chi_N^* (N)] \hat{P} [\chi_1 (1) \cdots \chi_N (N)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots \d\mathbf{x}_N
$$

Because a permutation only relabels the dummy integration variables, each integral is equal to

$$
  \prod_{i=1}^N \braket{\chi_i |\chi_i} = 1
$$

so that

$$
  \braket{K|K} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{P\in S_N} 1 = \frac{N!}{N!} = 1
$$

Thus every Slater determinant constructed from an orthonormal set of spin orbitals is normalized.

So far, we have established the orthonormality relations for Slater determinants
- $\braket{K|K} = 1$ (case 1)
- $\braket{K|L} = 0$ for $K\neq L$ (case 2)

#### One-electron operators

Consider the matrix elements of the one-electron operator $\eqref{equation-42}$ between two Slater determinants

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{K|\hat{h}(i)|L}
$$

Because the determinants are totally antisymmetric under exchange of electron labels and the operator $\hat{h}(i)$ acts identically on every electron coordinate, each term in the sum is identical after a relabeling of the dummy integration variables. This implies

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = N \braket{K|\hat{h}(1)|L}
\tag{\label{equation-44}}
\end{equation*}
$$

meaning it is sufficient to evaluate the matrix element of $\hat{h}(1)$.

Substituting the Slater determinant expansion $\eqref{equation-41}$ for $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$ into $\eqref{equation-44}$ yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} =& \frac{N}{N!} \sum_{P,Q \in S_N} (-1)^P (-1)^Q \\
  &\times \int \hat{P}[{\chi_1^{(K)}}^* (1) \cdots {\chi_N^{(K)}}^* (N)] \hat{h}(1) \hat{Q}[\chi_1^{(L)} (1) \cdots \chi_N^{(L)} (N)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-46}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $\hat{h}(1)$ acts only on $\mathbf{x}_1$, the integrand factorizes into a one-electron part and an $(N-1)$-electron part

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \int {\chi_{P(1)}^{(K)}}^* (1) \hat{h}(1) \chi_{Q(1)}^{(L)} (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \prod_{i=2}^N \int {\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}}^* (i) \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)} \;\d\mathbf{x}_i
\tag{\label{equation-45}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The integrations over $\mathbf{x}_2,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$ factors into a product of one-electron overlaps

$$
  \int \prod_{i=2}^N {\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}}^* (i) \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)} (i) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N = \prod_{i=2}^N \Braket{\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}|\chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)}}
$$

By orthonormality of the spin orbitals, $\braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \delta_{ij}$, every term in $\eqref{equation-46}$ vanishes unless 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi_{P(i)}^{(K)} = \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)},\, \forall i \in\set{2,\dots,N}
\tag{\label{equation-47}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This means only those permutation pairs for which the bra and ket assign the same spin orbital to every electron coordinate $\mathbf{x}_2,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$ contribute to the matrix element $\eqref{equation-46}$. The only spin orbitals that may differ between the two determinants are therefore those associated with coordinate $\mathbf{x}_1$, which are coupled by $\hat{h}(1)$.

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

Let $\ket{K} = \ket{L}$. In this case, condition $\eqref{equation-47}$ becomes

$$
  \chi_{P(i)} = \chi_{Q(i)},\, \forall i \in\set{2,\dots,N}
$$

which implies $P(i) = Q(i)$ for all $i=2,\dots,N$. Because $P$ and $Q$ are permutations of $\set{1,\dots,N}$, agreement on $\set{2,\dots,N}$ forces $P(1) = Q(1)$ and therefore $P = Q$. Thus, every term with $P \neq Q$ vanishes, leaving only the diagonal terms

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|K} = \frac{N}{N!} \sum_{P\in S_N} \int chi_{P(1)}^* (1) \hat{h}(1) \chi_{P(1)} (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1
$$

For each occupied orbital $i\in\set{1,\dots,N}$, exactly $(N-1)!$ satisfy $P(1) = i$. Using $N! = N(N-1)!$ we get

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|K} =& \frac{N(N-1)!}{N!} \sum_{i=1}^N \int \chi_i^* (1) \hat{h}(1) \chi_i (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2: Determinants differing by one spin orbital**

Suppose $\ket{L} = \ket{K_a^p}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing the occupied spin orbital $\chi_a$ with $\chi_p$. Choosing a maximally coincident representation

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{K} =& \ket{\chi_a (1) \chi_2 (2) \cdots \chi_N (N)} \\
  \ket{L} =& \ket{\chi_p (1) \chi_2 (2) \cdots \chi_N (N)}
\end{align*}
$$

In this case, the two determinants share $N-1$ occupied spin orbitals. Condition $\eqref{equation-47}$ requires these $N-1$ common spin orbitals to be paired identically in every overlap factor associated with coordinates $\mathbf{x}_2,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$. Consequently, the only spin orbitals that can appear in the coordinate $\mathbf{x}_1$ are the unmatched orbitals $\chi_a$ and $\chi_p$. Every nonvanishing term therefore satisfies $P(1) = a$ and $Q(1) = p$. The remaining $N-1$ common spin orbitals can be assigned arbitrarily to the remaining coordinates, yielding exactly $(N-1)!$ admissible permutation pairs. Since each permutation contribute the same one-electron integral in the sum over $S_N$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} =& \frac{N(N-1)!}{N!} \int \chi_a^* (1) \hat{h}(1) \chi_p (1) \\
  =& \braket{a|\hat{h}|p}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 3: Determinants differing by two spin orbitals**

Suppose $\ket{L} = \ket{K_{ab}^{pq}}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing $\chi_a, \chi_b$ with $\chi_p, \chi_q$, respectively. In a maximally coincident representation

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{K} =& \ket{\chi_a (1) \chi_b (2) \chi_3 (3) \cdots \chi_N (N)} \\
  \ket{L} =& \ket{\chi_p (1) \chi_q (2) \chi_3 (3) \cdots \chi_N (N)}
\end{align*}
$$

In this case, the determinants share $N-2$ occupied spin orbitals. Since $\hat{h}(1)$ acts on a single electron coordinate, at most one pair of differing spin orbitals can appear in the coordinates $\mathbf{x}_1$. Consequently, at least one of the unmatched spin orbitals must appear among the overlap factors associated with coordinates $\mathbf{x}_2,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$. That is, for every pair of permutations $(P,Q)$, at least one index $i\in\set{2,\dots,N}$ satisfies $\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)} \neq \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)} = 0$ so that $\Braket{\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}|\chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)}} = 0$.

Thus every product of over integrals contains a vanishing factor, implying that every term in in the permutation expansion in $\eqref{equation-46}$ is zero, resulting in

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_1|L} = 0
$$

#### Two-electron operators

Consider the matrix elements of the two-electron operator $\eqref{equation-43}$ between two Slater determinants

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = \sum_{i<j}^N \braket{K|\hat{v}(i,j)|L}
$$

Since the two-electron interaction $\hat{v}(i,j)$ has the same functional form for every electron pair, and the Slater determinant are totally antisymmetric under permutations of electron coordinates, every term in the sum is identical after up to a relabeling of dummy integration variables. As there are

$$
  \binom{N}{2} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2}
$$

distinct electron pairs, it follows that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2} \braket{K|\hat{v}(1,2)|L}
\tag{\label{equation-48}}
\end{equation*}
$$

meaning it is sufficient to evaluate the matrix element of $\hat{v}(1,2)$.

Let $\Phi_K$ and $\Phi_L$ denote the Hartree product associated with $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$, respectively. Substituting the Slater determinant expansion $\eqref{equation-41}$ for $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$ into $\eqref{equation-48}$ yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} =& \frac{N(N-1)}{2 N!} \sum_{P,Q \in S_N} (-1)^P (-1)^Q \\
  &\times \int \hat{P}\Phi_K^* \hat{h}(1) \hat{Q}\Phi_L \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-49}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $\hat{v}(1,2)$ acts only on $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$, the integrand factorizes into a two-electron part and an $(N-2)$-electron part

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \int {\chi_{P(1)}^{(K)}}^* (1) {\chi_{P(2)}^{(K)}}^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) \chi_{Q(1)}^{(L)} (1) \chi_{Q(2)} (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \cdot \prod_{i=3}^N \int {\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}}^* (i) \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)} \;\d\mathbf{x}_i
\tag{\label{equation-50}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The integrations over $\mathbf{x}_3,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$ factors into a product of one-electron overlaps

$$
  \int \prod_{i=3}^N {\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}}^* (i) \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)} (i) \;\d\mathbf{x}_3 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N = \prod_{i=3}^N \Braket{\chi_{P(i)}^{(K)}|\chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)}}
$$

By orthonormality of the spin orbitals, $\braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \delta_{ij}$, every term in $\eqref{equation-48}$ vanishes unless 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi_{P(i)}^{(K)} = \chi_{Q(i)}^{(L)},\, \forall i \in\set{3,\dots,N}
\tag{\label{equation-51}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This means only those permutation pairs for which the bra and ket assign identical spin orbital to the coordinates $\mathbf{x}_3,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$ contribute to the matrix element $\eqref{equation-49}$. Consequently, the two determinants can differ only in the spin orbitals associated with the coordinates $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$, which are coupled by $\hat{v}(1,2)$.

Since $P$ and $Q$ are permutations of $\set{1,\dots,N}$, their remaining images must satisfy $\set{P(1), P(2)} = \set{Q(1), Q(2)}$. Thus, for each fixed permutation $P\in S_N$, there are exactly two admissible permutations $Q$
- $Q = P$, or
- $Q = (12) \circ P$

where $(12)$ is the transposition exchanging $1$ and $2$. Using antisymmetry, $(-1)^{(12)\circ P} = -(-1)^P$, the contributions of the two admissible $Q$-terms can be combined into an antisymmetrized two-particle factor of the form $(1 - \hat{p}_{12})$, where the operator $\hat{p}_{12}$ exchanges coordinates $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$. Then $\eqref{equation-49}$ can be expressed as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = \frac{1}{2(N-2)!} \sum_{P\in S_N} \int \hat{P} (\Phi_K^*) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12})\hat{P} (\Phi_L) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N
\tag{\label{equation-52}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Every nonvanishing term in the sum over $S_N$ is determined by the two occupied spin orbitals assigned to the coordinates $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$.

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

Let $\ket{K} = \ket{L}$, so that the two determinants contain the same set of occupied spin orbital. Fix an ordered pair of distinct occupied orbitals $(\chi_i, \chi_j)$, where $i,j\in\set{1,\dots,N}$ and $i\neq j$. The conditions $P(1) = i$ and $P(2) = j$ uniquely determine the images of $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$ under the permutation $P$. The remaining $N-2$ orbital labesl can be assigned arbitrarily to the remaining coordinates $\mathbf{x}_3,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$, giving exactly $(N - 2)!$ such permutations. In this case, $\eqref{equation-52}$ evaluates to

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|K} =& \frac{(N-2)!}{2(N-2)!} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j\neq i}^N \int \chi_i^* (1) \chi_j^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) [\chi_i (1) \chi_j (2) - \chi_i (2) \chi_j (1)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \cdots\d\mathbf{x}_N \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j\neq i}^N \braket{ij|ij} - \braket{ij|ji} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 2: Determinants differing by one spin orbital**

Suppose $\ket{L} = \ket{K_a^p}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing the occupied spin orbital $\chi_a$ with $\chi_p$. Choosing a maximally coincident representation

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{K} =& \ket{\chi_a (1) \chi_2 (2) \cdots \chi_N (N)} \\
  \ket{L} =& \ket{\chi_p (1) \chi_2 (2) \cdots \chi_N (N)}
\end{align*}
$$

In this case, the two determinants share $N-1$ occupied spin orbitals. For nonzero contributions in $\eqref{equation-52}$, the differing orbitals $\chi_a$ in $\ket{K}$ and $\chi_p$ in $\ket{K}$ must occupy one of the two active coordinates $\set{\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2}$ acted under $\hat{v}(1,2)$. The second active coordinate must then by occupied by one of the $N-1$ spin orbitals common to both $\ket{K}$ and $\ket{L}$.

For each common occupied orbital $\chi_b$ with $b \neq a$, there are two possible assignment to the active coordinates, $(\chi_a, \chi_b)$ or $(\chi_b, \chi_a)$, and similarly for $\chi_p$. The remaining $N-2$ orbitals can be permuted arbitrarily among the inactive coordinates $\mathbf{x}_3,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$, giving $(N-2)!$ identical contributions. Substituting these contributions into $\eqref{equation-52}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = \frac{(N-2)!}{2(N-2)!} \sum_{a \neq b}^N \int& \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1-\hat{p}_{12})\chi_p (1) \chi_b (n) \\
  &+ \chi_b^* (1) \chi_a^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_b (1) \chi_p (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

Interchanging the dummy integration variables $\mathbf{x}_1 \leftrightarrow \mathbf{x}_2$ in the second integral and using the symmetries $\hat{v}(1,2) = \hat{v}(2,1)$ and $\hat{p}_{12} = \hat{p}_{21}$, yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  & \int \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1-\hat{p}_{12}) \chi_b (1) \chi_p (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \int \chi_b^* (2) \chi_a^* (1) \hat{v}(2,1) (1 - \hat{p}_{21}) \chi_b (2) \chi_p (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \int \chi_a^* (2) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_p (1) \chi_b (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, the factor $1/2$ is cancelled by the two identical contribution, resulting in

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} =& \sum_{a\neq b}^N \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_p (1) \chi_b (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \sum_{a\neq b}^N \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) [\chi_p (1) \chi_b (2) - \chi_b (1) \chi_p (2)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \sum_{a\neq b}^N \braket{ab|pb} - \braket{ab|bp} = \sum_{b=1}^N \bra{ab}\ket{pb}
\end{align*}
$$

**Case 3: Determinants differing by two spin orbitals**

Suppose $\ket{L} = \ket{K_{ab}^{pq}}$ is obtained from $\ket{K}$ by replacing $\chi_a, \chi_b$ with $\chi_p, \chi_q$, respectively. In a maximally coincident representation

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{K} =& \ket{\chi_a (1) \chi_b (2) \chi_3 (3) \cdots \chi_N (N)} \\
  \ket{L} =& \ket{\chi_p (1) \chi_q (2) \chi_3 (3) \cdots \chi_N (N)}
\end{align*}
$$

In this case, the determinants share $N-2$ occupied spin orbitals. For nonzero contributions on $\eqref{equation-52}$, the differing orbitals $\set{\chi_a, \chi_b}$ in $\ket{K}$ and $\set{\chi_p, \chi_q}$ in $\ket{L}$ must occupy the two active coordinates $\set{\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2}$ acted under $\hat{v}(1,2)$. The remaining $N-2$ orbitals can be permuted arbitrarily among the inactive coordinates $\mathbf{x}_3,\dots,\mathbf{x}_N$, giving $(N-2)!$ identical contributions. Substituting these contributions into $\eqref{equation-52}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} = \frac{1}{2} \int& \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_p (1) \chi_q (2) \\
  &+ \chi_b^* (1) \chi_a^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_q (1) \chi_p (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

Interchanging the dummy integration variables $\mathbf{x}_1 \leftrightarrow \mathbf{x}_2$ in the second integral and using the symmetries $\hat{v}(1,2) = \hat{v}(2,1)$ and $\hat{p}_{12} = \hat{p}_{21}$, yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  & \int \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1-\hat{p}_{12}) \chi_q (1) \chi_p (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \int \chi_b^* (2) \chi_a^* (1) \hat{v}(2,1) (1 - \hat{p}_{21}) \chi_q (2) \chi_p (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \int \chi_a^* (2) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1 - \hat{p}_{12}) \chi_p (1) \chi_q (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_N
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, the factor $1/2$ is cancelled by the two identical contribution, resulting in

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|L} =& \int \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) (1-\hat{p}_{12}) \chi_p (1) \chi_q (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \int \chi_a^* (1) \chi_b^* (2) \hat{v}(1,2) [\chi_p (1) \chi_q (2) - \chi_q (1) \chi_p (2)] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \braket{ab|pq} - \braket{ab|qp} = \bra{ab}\ket{pq}
\end{align*}
$$

## Transition from spin orbitals to spatial orbitals

The Hartree-Fock equations are naturally formulated in terms of spin orbitals, since the electronic wavefunction depends on both the spatial and spin coordinates of each electron. For most computational purposes, however, it is convenient to eliminated the explicit spin dependence so that the resulting equations involve only spatial orbitals and spatial integrals.

Consider a closed-shell system containing an even number $N$ of electrons. In the restricted Hartree-Fock (RHF) approximation, each spatial orbital is doubly occupied by one electron with spin $\alpha$ and one electron with spin $\beta$. The occupied spin orbitals therefore occur in pairs

$$
\begin{align*}
  \chi_i (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_i (\mathbf{r}) \alpha(\omega) \\
  \bar{\chi}_i (\mathbf{x}) =& \psi_i (\mathbf{r}) \beta(\omega) \\
\end{align*}
$$

where $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denotes the combined spatial and spin coordinate. The RHF ground state determinant is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\Psi_0} =& \ket{\chi_1 \chi_2 \chi_3 \chi_4 \cdots \chi_{N-1} \chi_N} \\
  =& \ket{\psi_1 \bar{\psi}_1 \psi_2 \bar{\psi}_2 \cdots \psi_{N/2} \bar{\psi}_{N/2}}
\end{align*}
$$

Since the $\alpha$- and $\beta$-spin electron occupy identical spatial orbitals, the Hartree-Fock energy can be rewritten entirely in terms of the $N/2$ occupied spatial orbitals.

The Hartree-Fock energy expressed in spin orbitals is given by $\eqref{equation-53}$.

**Restricted Hartree-Fock summation rules**

In the closed-shell wavefunction $\eqref{equation-54}$ every occupied spatial orbital appears once with $\alpha$ and once with spin $\beta$. Every sum over occupied spin orbitals can therefore be decomposed as

$$
  \sum_{i=1}^N \chi_i = \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \psi_i + \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \bar{\psi}_i
$$

For double sums over spin occupied spin orbitals, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \chi_i \chi_j =& \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} (\psi_i + \bar{\psi}_i) \sum_{j=1}^{N/2} (\psi_b + \bar{\psi}_b) \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \sum_{j=1}^{N/2} \psi_i \psi_j + \psi_i \bar{\psi}_j + \bar{\psi}_i \psi_j + \bar{\psi}_i \bar{\psi}_j
\end{align*}
$$

**One-electron contribution**

The one-electron operator $\hat{h}$ acts only on the spatial coordinates and is therefore independent of spin. Consequently, the one-electron integral in $\eqref{equation-53}$ can be decomposed as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} =& \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \braket{\bar{i}|\hat{h}|\bar{i}} \\
  =& 2 \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} (\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i) \label{equation-55}
\end{align*}
$$

where $(\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i) = (i|\hat{h}|i)$ denotes the spatial one-electron integral.

**Two-electron contribution**

Using chemists' notation, the two-electron integral in $\eqref{equation-53}$ can be expanded as

$$
\begin{align*}
  &\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [ii|jj] - [ij|ji] \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \left(\sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} [ii|jj] - [ij|ji] + \sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} [ii|\bar{j}\bar{j}] - [i\bar{j}|\bar{j}i] \right. \\
  +& \left. \sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} [\bar{i}\bar{i}|jj] - [\bar{i}j|j\bar{i}] + \sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} [\bar{i}\bar{i}|\bar{j}\bar{j}] - [\bar{i}\bar{j}|\bar{j}\bar{i}] \right) \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \sum_{j=1}^{N/2} 2(\psi_i \psi_i|\psi_j \psi_j) - (\psi_i \psi_j|\psi_j \psi_i) \tag{\label{equation-56}}
\end{align*}
$$

Thus each pair of occupied spatial orbitals contributes two Coloumb interactions, but only one exchange interaction.

**Closed-shell Hartree-Fock energy**

Combining $\eqref{equation-55}$ and $\eqref{equation-56}$, the Hartree-Fock energy of a closed-shell ground state is

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_0 =& 2 \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} (\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i) \\
  &+ \sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} 2(\psi_i \psi_i|\psi_j \psi_j) - (\psi_i \psi_j|\psi_j \psi_i)
\end{align*}
$$

Equivalently using physicists' notation

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_0 =& 2 \sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \braket{\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i} \\
  &+ \sum_{i,j=1}^{N/2} 2\braket{\psi_i \psi_j|\psi_i \psi_j} - \braket{\psi_i \psi_j|\psi_j \psi_i}
\end{align*}
$$

In terms of the Coulomb and and exchange integrals, $\eqref{equation-57}$ and $\eqref{equation-58}$, the energy can be rewritten as

$$
  E_0 = 2 \sum_i h_{ii} + \sum_{i,j} 2J_{ij} - K_{ij}
$$

### Pseudo-classical interpretation of Hartree-Fock energies

The Hartree-Fock energy $\eqref{equation-63}$ can be decomposed into one-electron and pairwise contributions. Each occupied spin orbitals $\chi_i$ contributes the one-electron energy $\braket{i|\hat{h}|i} = h_{ii}$. If $\chi_i = \psi_i \alpha$ then

$$
  \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} = \braket{\psi_i \alpha|\hat{h}|\psi_i \alpha} = (\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i) = h_{ii}
$$

Similarly, if $\chi_i = \psi_i \beta$, then 

$$
  \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} = \braket{\psi_i \beta|\hat{h}|\psi_i \beta} = (\psi_i|\hat{h}|\psi_i) = h_{ii}
$$

Thus, an electron in spatial orbital $\psi_i$ contributes the one-electron energy $h_{ii}$, regardless of its spin.

Each unique pair of electrons in spin orbitals $\chi_i$ and $\chi_j$ contributes the antisymmetrized two-electron integral $\bra{ij}\ket{ij}$. If the two electrons have opposite spins, say $\chi_i = \psi_i \alpha$ and $\chi_j = \psi_j \beta$, then

$$
  \bra{ij}\ket{ij} = [\psi_i \psi_i|\bar{\psi}_j \bar{\psi}_j] - [\psi_i \bar{\psi}_j|\bar{\psi}_j \psi_i] = J_{ij}
$$

On the other hand, if the electrons have parallel spins, say $\chi_i = \psi_i \beta$ and $\chi_j = \psi_j \beta$, then

$$
  \bra{ij}\ket{ij} = [\bar{\psi}_i \bar{\psi}_i|\bar{\psi}_j \bar{\psi}_j] - [\bar{\psi}_i \bar{\psi}_j|\bar{\psi}_j \bar{\psi}_i] = J_{ij} - K_{ij}
$$

Thus, every pair of electrons contributes the Coulomb interaction energy $J_{ij}$, while each pair of electrons with parallel spins contributes the additional exchange stabilization $-K_{ij}$.

Accordingly, the total energy of an $N$-electron Slater determinant can be interpreted as the sum of
- the one-electron energies $h_{ii}$ associated with each occupied spatial orbital
- the Coulomb interaction energies $J_{ij}$ associated with every distinct pair of electrons
- the exchange contributions $-K_{ij}$ associated with every distinct pair of parallel-spin electrons

This interpretation should be regarded as a convenient decomposiiton of the expectation value of the Hamiltonian rather than as a description of separate physical interactions. The Hamiltonian contains the Coulomb interaction $r_{ij}^{-1}$, which is independent of the electron spins. The exchange contribution does not represent an additional force between electrons. Instead, it arises solely from the anitsymmetry of the fermionic wavefunction and the consequent modification of the spatial probability distributin imposed by the Pauli exclusion principle.

### Exchange correlation

For two electrons occupying spin orbitals with parallel spin, for example $\ket{\bar{\psi}_1 \bar{\psi}_2}$, antisymmetry of the Slater determinant requires the spatial part of the wavefunction to vanish whenever the two electron coordinates coincide, i.e. $\Psi(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}) = 0$. Consequently, the pair probability density satisfies $|\Psi(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r})|^2 = 0$ so the electrons cannot be found at the same time in space.

By contrast, for electrons with opposite spin, for example $\ket{\psi_1 \bar{\psi}_2}$, the exchange term vanishes because the spin functions are orthogonal, and the spatial wavefunction is not required to vanish electron coalescence. The corresponding pair density at $\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{r}_2$ is therefore generally nonzero.

Since the Coulomb interaction $r_{12}^{-1}$ is largest when the electrons are closer together, we expect the state with parallel spins to have a energy than the corresponding state with opposte spins.

Using $\eqref{equation-63}$, the energy of $\ket{\psi_1 \bar{\psi}_2}$ denoted $E_{\uparrow\downarrow}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_{\uparrow\downarrow} =& [\psi_1|\hat{h}|\psi_1] + [\bar{\psi}_2|\hat{h}|\bar{\psi}_2] + [\psi_1 \psi_1|\bar{\psi}_2 \bar{\psi}_2] - [\psi_1 \bar{\psi}_2|\bar{\psi}_2 \psi_1] \\
  =& (1|\hat{h}|1) + (2|\hat{h}|2) + (11|22) \\
  =& h_{11} + h_{22} + J_{12}
\end{align*}
$$

and the energy of $\ket{\bar{\psi}_1 \bar{\psi}_2}$ denoted $E_{\downarrow\downarrow}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_{\downarrow\downarrow} =& [\bar{\psi}_1|\hat{h}|\bar{\psi}_1] + [\bar{\psi}_2|\hat{h}|\bar{\psi}_2] + [\bar{\psi}_1 \bar{\psi}_1|\bar{\psi}_2 \bar{\psi}_2] - [\bar{\psi}_1 \bar{\psi}_2|\bar{\psi}_2 \bar{\psi}_1] \\
  =& (1|\hat{h}|1) + (2|\hat{h}|2) + (11|22) - (12|21) \\
  =& h_{11} + h_{22} + J_{12} - K_{12}
\end{align*}
$$

Because $K_{12} \geq 0$ is positive, $E_{\downarrow\downarrow} = \leq E_{\uparrow\downarrow} - K_{12} \leq E_{\uparrow\downarrow}$. Thus, within the single-determinant approximation, a pair of electrons occupying parallel-spin orbitals is energetically stabilized relative to the corresponding opposite-spin configuration by the exchange contribution $-K_{12}$. This stabilization is a direct consequence of the antisymmetry of the Slater determinant. 

Antisymmetry requires the many-electron wavefunction to vanish whenever two electrons with parallel spin occupy the same spatial point, thereby reducing the pair probability density for like-spin electrons close together. This implies that the expectation value of the Coulomb repulsion is reduced. This phenomenon is known as Fermi correlation (or exchange correlation) and is incorporated within a single Slater determinant.

# Second quantization

Let $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^\infty$ be an orthonormal basis of spin orbitals for the one-electron Hilbert space. For every ordered set of distinct indices $p_1 < \cdots < p_N}$ let $\ket{p_1 \cdots p_N}$ denote the normalized Slater determinant constructed from the occupied spin orbitals $\set{\chi_{p_1}, \dots, \chi_{p_N}}$. By requiring the orbital indices to be written in ascending canonical order, every $N$-electron Slater determinant has a unique representation.

## Creation and annihilation operator

The creation operator $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ is the linear operator increases the particle number by one by occupying the spin orbital $\chi_i$. Its action on the Slater determinant basis is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \braket{p_1 \cdots p_N} = \begin{cases}
    (-1)^m \ket{p_i \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_N},\quad& i\notin\set{p_k}_{k=1}^N \\
    0,\quad& i\in\set{p_k}_{k=1}^N
  \end{cases}
\tag{\label{equation-66}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $m$ is the number of occupied orbitals preceding $i$ in the canonical ordering. The phase factor $(-1)^m$ is the signature of the permutation required to restore the determinant to canonical ordering after inserting the orbital $\chi_i$. The second case in $\eqref{equation-66}$ expresses the Pauli exclusion principle, which states that no spin orbital can be occupied by more than one fermion.

The annihilation operator $\hat{a}_i$ is defined as the Hermitian adjoint of the creation operator

$$
  \hat{a}_i = (\hat{a}_i^\dagger)^\dagger
$$

and decreases the particle number by one by removing an electron from the spin orbital $\chi_i$. Its action on the Slater determinant basis is

$$
  \hat{a}_i \ket{p_1 \cdots p_N} = \begin{cases}
    (-1)^m \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+2} \cdots p_N},\quad& i = p_{m+1} \\
    0,\quad& i\notin\set{p_k}_{k=1}^N
  \end{cases}
$$

where $i = p_{m+1}$ indicates that the orbital $\chi_i$ occupies the $(m+1)$ position in the canonical ordering.

The adjoint relationship between the creation and annihilation operators is expressed by defining the identity

$$
  \braket{\Phi|\hat{a}_i^\dagger|\Psi} = \braket{\hat{a}_i \Phi|\Psi}
$$

which holds for all states $\ket{\Phi}$ and $\ket{\Psi}$ in Fock space. To illustrate this relationship, consider the Slater determinant $\ket{K} = \ket{\chi_i \chi_j}$. Since

$$
  \ket{K} = \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{\chi_j}
$$

taking the adjoint gives

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \bra{K} = \bra{\chi_j} (\hat{a}_i^\dagger)^\dagger = \bra{\chi_j}\hat{a}_i
\tag{\label{equation-64}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Multiplying on the right by $\ket{K}$, we obtain

$$
  \braket{K|K} = \braket{\chi_j|\hat{a}_i|K}
$$

Since $\ket{K}$ is normalized, $\braket{K|K} = 1 = \braket{\chi_j|\chi_j}$, it follows that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{a}_i \ket{K} = \hat{a}_i \ket{\chi_i \chi_j} = \ket{\chi_j}
\tag{\label{equation-65}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Equation $\eqref{equation-64}$ shows that $\hat{a}_i$ acts like a creation operator if it acts on bras to the left. Likewise, taking the adjoint of $\eqref{equation-65}$

$$
  \bra{K}\hat{a}_i^\dagger = \bra{\chi_j}
$$

shows that $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ acts like an annihilation operator if it acts on kets to the left.

## Anticommutation relations

The fermionic creation and annihilation operators satisfy the canonical anticommutation relations

$$
\begin{align*}
  [\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_j]_+ =& 0 \\
  [\hat{a}_i^\dagger, \hat{a}_j^\dagger]_+ =& 0 \\
  [\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_j^\dagger]_+ =& \delta_{ij} \hat{I} \\
\end{align*}
$$

where $[\hat{p}, \hat{q}]_+ = \hat{p}\hat{q} + \hat{q}\hat{p}$ is the anticommutator.

### Creation operators

Let $\ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots p_N}$ with $p_1 < \cdots < p_N$ be a normalized Slater determinant in canonical order, and suppose that the orbitals $\chi_i$ and $\chi_j$ are unoccupied with $i < j$. Define

$$
\begin{align*}
  m =& \#\set{p_k | p_k < i} \\
  n =& \#\set{p_k | p_k < j}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $i < j$ and neither orbital is occupied, it follows that $m \leq n$. Applying creation operators in the order $\hat{a}_j^\dagger$ followed by $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \ket{P} =& (-1)^n \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{p_1 \cdots p_n j p_{n+1} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{n+m} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_n j p_{n+1} \cdots p_N} 
\end{align*}
$$

Inserting $\chi_j$ does not alter the number of occupied orbitals preceding $\chi_i$, so applying $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ produces the sign factor $(-1)^m$.

On the other hand,

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P} =& (-1)^m \hat{a}_j^\dagger \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{m+(n+1)} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_n j p_{n+1} \cdots p_N} 
\end{align*}
$$

Inserting $\chi_i$ increases the number of occupied orbitals preceding $\chi_j$ from $n$ to $n+1$. Thus, applying $\hat{}_j^\dagger$ produces the sign factor $(-1)^{n+1}$.

Since $(-1)^{m+(n+1)} = -(-1)^{m+n}$, it follows that

$$
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \ket{P} = -\hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P}
$$

Because this identity holds for every Slater determinant, we must have

$$
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger = -\hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i^\dagger
$$

leading to the anticommutation relation

$$
  [\hat{a}_i^\dagger, \hat{a}_j^\dagger]_+ = \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j + \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i = 0
$$

In particular, for $i = j$ we get $(\hat{a}_i^\dagger)^2 = 0$, which is the algebraic expression of the Pauli exclusion principle.

The same result can also be understood geometrically from the determinant representation. The states produced by $\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger$ and $\hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i^\dagger$ differ only by the interchange of the newly inserted spin orbitals. Exchanging two columns of a Slater determinant multiplies the determinant by $-1$ such that the resulting many-electron states differ by a minus sign.

can also be explained from the fact that interchanging the order of the two creation operators changes only the order in which the two new columns are inserted into the determinant. Since exchanging two columns of a determinant changes its sign.

### Annihilation operators

Assume that both spin orbitals $\chi_i$ and $\chi_j$ in the Slater determinant $\ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots P_N}$ with $p_1 < \cdots p_N$. Suppose $i = p_{m+1}$ and $j = p_{n+1}$ with $m < n$ and therefore $i < j$.

Applying annihilation operators in the order $\hat{a}_j$ followed by $\hat{a}_i$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j \ket{P} =& (-1)^n \hat{a}_i \ket{p_1 \cdots p_n p_{n+2} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{n+m} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+2} \cdots p_n p_{n+2} \cdots p_N}
\end{align*}
$$

Removing $\chi_j$ does not alter the position of $\chi_i$, so applying $\hat{a}_i$ produces the sign factor $(-1)^m$.

Conversely,

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i \ket{P} =& (-1)^m \hat{a}_j \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+2} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{m + (n-1)} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+2} \cdots p_n p_{n+2} \cdots p_N}
\end{align*}
$$

Removing $\chi_i$ decreases the number of occupied orbitals preceding $\chi_j$ from $n$ to $n$. Thus, applying $\hat{a}_j$ produces the sign factor $(-1)^{n-1}$.

Since $(-1)^{m+n-1} = -(-1)^{m+n}$, it follows that $\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j \ket{P} = -\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i \ket{P}$. Because this identity holds for all Slater determinants, we get $\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j = -\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i$, leading to the commutation relation

$$
  [\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_j]_+ = \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j + \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i = 0
$$

In particular, this implies that $(\hat{a}_i)^2 = 0$ expressing the fact that an electron cannot be removed twice from the same orbital.

### Mixed anticommutation relation

Assume that $\chi_j$ is occupied while $\chi_i$ is unoccupied in the Slater determinant $\ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots p_n j p_{n+2} \cdots p_N}$, with $i < j$. Let

$$
\begin{align*}
  m =& \#\set{p_k | p_k < i} \\
  n =& \#\set{p_k | p_k < j}
\end{align*}
$$

so that $j = p_{n+1}$.

**Case $i\neq j$**

Applying the annihilation operator $\hat{a}_j$ followed by the creation operator $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j \ket{P} =& (-1)^n \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{p_1 \cdots p_n p_{n+2} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{n+m} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_n p_{n+2} \cdots p_N}
\end{align*}
$$

Removing $\chi_j$ does not alter the number of occupied orbitals preceeding $i$, so applying $\hat{a}_i^\dagger$ gives the sign factor $(-1)^m$.

Conversely,

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{p_1 \cdots p_n j p_{n+2} \cdots p_N} =& (-1)^n \hat{a}_j \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_n j p_{n+2} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& (-1)^{m + (n+1)} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_n p_{n+2}\cdots p_N}
\end{align*}
$$

Inserting $\chi_i$ increases the number of occupied orbitals preceding $j$ from $n$ to $n+1$, so applying $\hat{a}_j$ contributes the sign factor $(-1)^{n+1}$.

Since $(-1)^{m+n+1} = -(-1)^{m+n}$ it follows that $\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P} = -\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j \ket{P}$. Because this identity holds for all Slater determinants, it follows that $\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger = -\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j$. Taking the Hermitian adjoint gives the equivalent identity $\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j^\dagger = -\hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i$. Thus, for $i \neq j$, we get the anticommutation relations

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [\hat{a}_i^\dagger, \hat{a}_j]_+ = [\hat{a}_j, \hat{a}_i^\dagger]_+ = 0,\; i \neq j
\tag{\label{equation-67}}
\end{equation*}
$$

**Case $i = j$**

For the diagonal case $i = j$, consider an arbitrary Slater determinant $\ket{P}$. 

Suppose first that $\chi_i$ is occupied in $\ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m j p_{m+2} \cdots p_N}$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_i \ket{P} =& (-1)^m \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+1} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& \underbrace{(-1)^{2m}}_{=1} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_N} = \ket{P}
\end{align*}
$$

whereas $\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P} = 0$ because creation on an already occupied orbital is forbidden by the Pauli principle. Thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \underbrace{(\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_i + \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_i^\dagger)}_{=[\hat{a}_i^\dagger, \hat{a}_i]_+}\ket{P} = \ket{P}
\tag{\label{equation-69}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Now suppose that $\chi_i$ is unoccupied in $\ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+1} \cdots p_1}$. Then

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P} =& (-1)^m \hat{a}_i \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m i p_{m+1} \cdots p_N} \\
  =& \underbrace{(-1)^{2m}}_{=1} \ket{p_1 \cdots p_m p_{m+1} \cdots p_N} = \ket{P}
\end{align*}
$$

while $\hat{a}_i \hat{a}^i^\dagger \ket{P} = 0$, since annihilation of unoccpied orbital is zero. Thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \underbrace{(\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_i^\dagger + \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_i)}_{=[\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_i^\dagger]}\ket{P} = \ket{P}
\tag{\label{equation-70}}
\end{equation*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-69}$ and $\eqref{equation-70}$, we obtain the anticommutation relations

$$
\begin{equation*}
  [\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_i^\dagger]_+ = [\hat{a}_i^\dagger, \hat{a}_i]_+ = \hat{I}
\tag{\label{equation-68}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Combining $\eqref{equation-67}$ and $\eqref{equation-68}$ yields the canonical anticommutation relations

$$
  [\hat{a}_i, \hat{a}_j^\dagger]_+ = [\hat{a}_j^\dagger, \hat{a}_i]_+ = \delta_{ij}\hat{I}
$$

## Number operator

The operator product $\hat{n}_i := \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_i$ is called the number operator associated with the spin orbital $\chi_i$. Acting on a Slater determinant $\ket{P}$, it satisfies

$$
  \hat{n}_i \ket{P} = \begin{cases}
    \ket{P_i},\quad& \chi_i \text{ occupied in } \ket{P} \\
    0,\quad& \chi_i \text{unoccupied in } \ket{P}
  \end{cases}
$$

Thus $hat{n}_i$ counts the occupation of $\chi_i$. Equivalently, if $n_i (P) \in\set{0,1}$ denotes the occupation number of the spin orbital $\chi_i$ in $\ket{P}$, then

$$
  \hat{n}_i \ket{P} = n_i (P) \ket{P}
$$

where

$$
  n_i (P) = \begin{cases}
    1,\quad& \chi_i \text{ occupied} \\
    0,\quad& \chi_i \text{ unoccupied}
  \end{cases}
$$

Consequently, every Slater determinant is an eigenstate of the number operator, and, in accordance with the Pauli principle, the only possible eigenvalues are $0$ and $1$.

## Representation of Slater determinants

To formulate Slater determinants in the formalism of second quantization, we introduce a vacuum state $\ket{0}$,  representing the state containing no particles. It is normalized according to $\braket{0|0} = 1$ and is characterized by the property that every annihilation operator annihilates the vacuum, i.e.

$$
  \hat{a}_i \ket{0} = 0,\,\forall i
$$

Taking the Hermitian adjoint gives the equivalent relation

$$
  \bra{0}\hat{a}_i^\dagger = 0,\,\forall i
$$

An $N$-electron Slater determinant is obtained by successively creating electrons in the occupied spin orbitals

$$
   \ket{P} = \ket{p_1 \cdots p_N} = \hat{a}_{p_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{p_N}^\dagger \ket{0}
$$

where the orbital indices are canonically ordered $p_1 < \cdots < p_N$.

Consider the matrix element $\braket{P|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|P}$. Using the anticommutation relation

$$
  \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j = \delta_{ij}\hat{I} - \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger
$$

we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{P|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|P} =& \braket{P|\delta_{ij}\hat{I} - \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger|P} \\
  =& \delta_{ij} \underbrace{\braket{P|P}}_{=1} - \braket{\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger|P} \\
  =& \delta_{ij} - \braket{P|\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger|P}
\end{align*}
$$

We distinguish two cases related to the occupation of the spin orbital $\chi_i$
- If $\chi_i$ is occupied in $\ket{P}$, then $\hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{P} = 0$ by the Pauli exclusion principle. Thus $\braket{P|\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_i^\dagger|P} = \delta_{ij}$ yielding

$$
  \braket{P|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|P} = \delta_{ij}
$$

- If $\chi_i$ is unoccupied in $\ket{P}$, then $\delta_{ij} = 0$ whenever $\hat{a}_j \ket{P} \neq 0$, because $j$ must be an occupied orbital. Moreover, if $j = i$, then $\hat{a}_i \ket{P} = 0$. Thus $\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j \ket{P} = 0$ for every $j$, and consequently

$$
  \braket{P|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|P} = 0
$$

Combining these two cases yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{P|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|P} = \delta_{ij} n_i (P)
\tag{\label{equation-73}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $n_i (P)$ is the occupation number of the spin orbital $\chi_i$ in $\ket{P}$,

$$
  n_i (P) = \begin{cases}
    1,\quad& i \in\operatorname{occ}(P) \\
    0,\quad& i \notin\operatorname{occ}(P)
  \end{cases}
$$

### Inner products of Slater determinants

Consider the two $N$-electron Slater determinants

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{I} =& \ket{i_1 \cdots i_N} = \hat{a}_{i_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{i_N}^\dagger \ket{0} \\
  \ket{J} =& \ket{j_1 \cdots j_N} = \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger \ket{0} \\
\end{align*}
$$

where the indices within each determinant are in increasing order. Their overlap is

$$
  \braket{I|J} = \braket{0|\hat{a}_{i_N} \cdots \hat{a}_{i_1} \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger|0}
$$

To evaluate this expression, we commute the annihilation operators $\hat{a}_{i_1},\dots,\hat{a}_{i_N}$ successively to the right through the string of creation operators using the canonical anticommutation relation

$$
  \hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j^\dagger = \delta_{ij} \hat{I} - \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i
$$

Starting with the rightmost annihilation operator $\hat{a}_{i_1}$, we consider two cases.

**Case 1: $i_1 \notin\set{j_1,\dots,j_N}$**

Since $i_1$ does not coincide with any occupied orbital of $\ket{J}$, it follows that  $\delta_{i_1 j_p} = 0$ for every $q\in\set{1,\dots,N}$. Consequently for every $q$,

$$
  \hat{a}_{i_1} \hat{a}_{j_p}^\dagger = -\hat{a}_{j_p}^\dagger \hat{a}_{i_1}
$$

After movingg $\hat{a}_{i_1}$ through all $N$ creation operators, we obtain

$$
  \braket{I|J} = (-1)^N \braket{0|\hat{a}_{i_N} \cdots \hat{a}_{i_2} \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger \hat{a}_{i_1}|0}
$$

Since $\hat{a}_{i_1} \ket{0} = 0$, it follows immediately that $\braket{I|J} = 0$. Thus, if any occupied orbital of $\ket{I}$ is absent from $\ket{J}$, the two Slater determinants are orthogonal.

**Case 2: $i_1 = j_p$ for some $p$**

Suppose $i_1$ coincides with the $p$th occupied orbital of $\ket{J}$. Passing $\hat{a}_{i_1}$, through the first $p-1$ creation operators gives the factor $(-1)^{p-1}$. Using $\hat{a}_{i_1} \hat{a}_{j_p}^\dagger = \hat{I} - \hat{a}_{j_p}^\dagger \hat{a}_{i_1}$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{I|J} =& (-1)^{p-1} \braket{0|\hat{a}_{i_N} \cdots \hat{a}_{i_2} \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_{p-1}}^\dagger \hat{a}_{j_{p+1}}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger|0} \\
  &- (-1)^{p-1} \braket{0|\hat{a}_{i_N} \cdots \hat{a}_{i_2} \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger \hat{a}_{i_1}|0}
\end{align*}
$$

The second term vanishes because $\hat{a}_{i_1} \ket{0} = 0$. Thus,

$$
  \braket{I|J} = (-1)^{p-1} \braket{0|\hat{a}_{i_N} \cdots \hat{a}_{i_2} \hat{a}_{j_1}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_{p-1}}^\dagger \hat{a}_{j_{p+1}}^\dagger \cdots \hat{a}_{j_N}^\dagger|0}
$$

which is an overlap involving one fewer annihilation and one fewer creation operator.

---

Repeating this procedure successively for the remaining annihilation operators $\hat{a}_{i_2},\dots,\hat{a}_{i_N}$ leads to one of two possibilities:
1. If, at any stage, an annihilation operator has no matching creation operator, the overlap vanishes.
2. Otherwise, every annihilation operator is paired with a unique creation operator, implying that the sets of occupied orbitals are identical, i.e. $\set{i_1,\dots,i_N} = \set{j_1,\dots,j_N}$. The accumulated sign is $(-1)^\tau$, where $\tau$ is the parity of the permutation that transforms the ordering $(j_1,\dots,j_N)$ into $(i_1,\dots,i_N)$. 

Consequently,

$$
  \braket{I|J} = \begin{cases}
    (-1)^\tau,\quad& \set{i_1,\dots,i_N} = \set{j_1,\dots,j_N} \\
    0,\quad& \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
$$

In the standard convention, each Slater determinant is written with its occupied orbitals in canonical increasing order. Then $\tau = 0$, and the overlap simplifies to the orthonormality relation

$$
  \braket{I|J} = \delta_{IJ}
$$

This establishes that Slater determinants form an orthonormal basis of the $N$-electron Hilbert space.

---

As a special case, consider the one-particle states 

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{i} =& \hat{a}_i^\dagger \ket{0}
  \ket{j} =& \hat{a}_j^\dagger \ket{0}
$$ 

Their inner product is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{i|j} =& \braket{0|\hat{a}_i \hat{a}_j|0} \\
  =& \braket{0|(\delta_{ij} - \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_i)|0} \\
  =& \delta_{ij} \underbrace{\braket{0|0}}_{=1} - \bra{0} \hat{a}_j^\dagger \underbrace{\hat{a}_i \ket{0}}_{=0} \\
  =& \delta_{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

## Operator representation

A one-electron operator $\eqref{equation-42}$ has the second-quantized form

$$
  \hat{O}_1 = \sum_{i,j} \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j
$$

while a two-electron operator $\eqref{equation-43}$ has the second-quantized form

$$
  \hat{O}_2 = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j,k,l}\bra{ij|kl} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k
$$

where the sums run over the set spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}$.

### Electronic Hamiltonian

The electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H} = \hat{H}_1 + \hat{H}_2$ can be decomposed into the the one- and two-electron operators

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{H}_1 =& \sum_i \hat{h}_i
  \hat{H}_2 =& \sum_{i < j} \hat{v}_{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

where

$$
  \hat{h}_i = -\frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 - \sum_A \frac{Z_A}{r_{iA}}
$$

and $\hat{v}_{ij} = 1/r_{ij}$. The second-quantized form of the Hamiltonian is then

$$
  \hat{H} = \sum_{i,j} \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j,k,l} \brake{ij|\hat{v}|kl} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k
$$

Since $\bra{ij}\ket{kl} = -\bra{ij}\ket{kl}$ and $\hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k = -\hat{a}_k \hat{a}_l$, we can also write the Hamiltonian in the form

$$
  \hat{H} = \sum_{i,j} \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j + \frac{1}{4} \sum_{i,j,k,l} \bra{ij}\ket{kl} \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k
$$

The factor $1/4$ arises because both the operator product and the antisymmetrized integrals are antisymmetric under the exchanges $i \leftrightarrow j$ and $k \leftrightarrow l$, so each unordered pair of indices is counted four times in the unrestricted summation.

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

\end{equation*}

Using the canonical anticommutation relation

$$
  \hat{a}_p \hat{a}_i^\dagger = \delta_{pi} \hat{I} - \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_p
$$

gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{K|\hat{a}_a^\dagger \hat{a}_p \hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j|K} =& \delta_{pi} \braket{K|\underbrace{\hat{a}_a^\dagger \hat{a}_j}_{=\delta_{aj} - \hat{a}_j \hat{a}_a^\dagger}|K} - \braket{K|\hat{a}_a^\dagger \hat{a}_i^\dagger \underbrace{\hat{a}_p \hat{a}_j}_{=-\hat{a}_j \hat{a}_p}|K} \\

\end{align*}
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-71}$ yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{L|\hat{O}_1|K} =& \sum_{i,j} \braket{i|\hat{h}|j} \delta_{pi} \delta_{aj} \\
  =& \braket{p|\hat{h}|a}
\end{align*}
$$

### Two-electron operators

**Case 1: Identical determinants**

Suppose $\ket{L} = \ket{K}$. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{K|\hat{O}_2|K} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j,k,l} \braket{ij|kl} \braket{K|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k|K}
\tag{\label{equation-72}}
\end{equation*}

The matrix element $\braket{K|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k|K}$ is nonzero only if both $k$ and $l$ label occupied spin orbitals of $\ket{K}$. Assume therefore $k, l \in\operatorname{occ}(K)$. After removing the electrons occupying $k$ and $l$, the creation operators must recreate the same determinant. This is possible only if the following two cases
1. $i = k$, $j = l$ (direct contraction) resulting in $\braket{K|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k|K} = 1$
2. $i = l$, $j = k$ (exchange contraction) leading to $\braket{K|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k|K} = -1$, where the minus sign follows from exchange the order of two creation (or annihilation) operators.

$$
  \braket{K|\hat{a}_i^\dagger \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_l \hat{a}_k|K} = n_k (K) n_l (K) (\delta_{ik} \delta_{jl} - \delta_{il} \delta_{jk})
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-70}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}

  =& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j \in\operatorname{occ}(K)} \braket{ij|ij} - \braket{ij|ji} \\

\end{align*}

# Spin-adapted configurations

# Minimal basis $\ce{H2}$ model

In the minimal basis model of $ce{H2}$ with $N=2$ electrons, there are four $(2K = 4)$ spin orbitals $\chi_i$ for $i=1,2,3,4$. We can therefore form $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ Slater determinants. The Hartree-Fock ground state determinant is

$$
  \ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \chi_2} = \ket{\psi_1 \bar{\psi}_1} = \ket{1\bar{1}}
$$

There are four singly excited determinants
- $\ket{\Psi_1^2} = \ket{2\bar{1}}$
- $\ket{\Psi_1^{\bar{2}}} = \ket{\bar{2}\bar{1}}$
- $\ket{\Psi_{\bar{1}}^2} = \ket{12}$
- $\ket{\Psi_{\bar{1}}^{\bar{2}}} = \ket{1\bar{2}}$

There is only one doubly excited determinant

$$
  \ket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} = \ket{2\bar{2}} = \ket{\chi_3 \chi_4} = \ket{\Psi_{12}^{34}}
$$

These six Slater determinants span the complete FCI space associated with the minimal basis.

The Hartree-Fock ground state has gerade ($g$) symmetry because both electrons occupy the gerade bonding molecular orbital. Likewise, the doubly excited determinant has both electrons occupying the ungerade ($u$) antibonding orbital, and sice $u\times u = g$, it also transforms as a gerade state.

In contrast, every singly excited determinant contains one electron in a gerade orbital and one in an ungerade orbital, i.e. $g \times u = u$ and therefore has ungerade symmetry.

Because the electronic Hamiltonian commutes with the inversion operator, determinants belonging to different irreducible representations cannot mix. Consequently, ground state CI expansion contains only determinants of gerade symmetry

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \ket{\Phi_0} =& c_0 \ket{\Psi_0} + c_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}} \ket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} \\
  =& c_0 \ket{\Psi_0} + c_{12}^{34} \ket{\Psi_{12}^{34}}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-29}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The exact value of the coefficients, which describe the wavefunction $\ket{\Phi_0}$ and the value of the exact energy $\braket{\Phi_0|\hat{H}|\Phi_0}$, can be found by diagonalizing the full CI matrix, i.e. the $2\times 2$ Hamiltonian matrix in the basis $\ket{\Psi_0}$ and $\ket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{H}|\Psi_0} & \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{H}|\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} \\
    \braket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} & \braket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}|\hat{H}|\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}}
  \end{bmatrix}
\tag{\label{equation-28}}
\end{equation*}
$$

## Matrix elements

Within the minimal-basis full configuration interaction (CI) description of $\ce{H2}$, the exact ground state wavefunction is expanded in the two-dimensional gerade subspace $\eqref{equation-29}$. The CI Hamiltonian $\eqref{equation-28}$ therefore consist of four matrix elements. Since the electronic Hamiltonian is Hermitian, we have

$$
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{H}|\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} = \braket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}|\hat{H}|\Psi_0}^* 
$$

so only three independent matrix elements must be evaluated.

The Hamiltonian for any two-electron molecule is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{H} =& \left(-\frac{1}{2} \nabla_1^2 - \sum_A \frac{Z_A}{r_{1A}} \right) + \left(-\frac{1}{2} \nabla_2^2 - \sum_A \frac{Z_A}{r_{2A}} \right) + \frac{1}{r_{12}} \\
  =& \hat{h}(1) + \hat{h}(2) + \frac{1}{r_{12}}
\end{align*}
$$

where 

$$
  \hat{h}(i) = -\frac{1}{2} \nabla_i^2 - \sum_A \frac{Z_A}{r_{iA}}
$$

is the one-electron Hamiltonian. The Hamiltonian can be separated into its one- and two-electron components

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{O}_1 =& \hat{h}(1) + \hat{h}(2) \\
  \hat{O}_2 =& \frac{1}{r_{12}}
\end{align*}
$$

so that $\eqref{equation-30}$ can be decomposed as $\hat{H} = \hat{O}_1 + \hat{O}_2$.

Using the Slater-Condon rules, we find that the elements of the full CI matrix $\eqref{equation-28}$ is 

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{H}|\Psi_0} =& \braket{1|\hat{h}|1} + \braket{2|\hat{h}|2} + \bra{12}\ket{12} \\
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{H}|\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} =& \bra{12}\ket{34} \\
  \braket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} =& \bra{34}\ket{12} \\
  \braket{\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}|\hat{H}|\Psi_{1\bar{1}}^{2\bar{2}}} =& \braket{3|\hat{h}|3} + \braket{4|\hat{h}|4} + \bra{34}\ket{34}
\end{align*}
$$

Using only spatial orbitals, the full CI matrix $\eqref{equation-28}$ becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{H} =& \begin{bmatrix}
    2(1|\hat{h}|1) + (11|11) & (12|12) \\
    (21|21) & 2(2|\hat{h}|2) + (22|22)
  \end{bmatrix} \\
  =& \begin{bmatrix}
    2h_{11} + J_{11} & K_{12} \\
    K_{12} & 2h_{22} + J_{22}
  \end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}
$$

## Hartree-Fock energy

### One-electron contribution

Consider first the matrix element 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{O}_1 |\Psi_0} = \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{h}(1)|\Psi_0} + \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{h}(2)|\Psi_0}
\tag{\label{equation-31}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Using the normalized Slater determinant

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Psi_0 (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[\chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) - \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2)]
\tag{\label{equation-33}}
\end{equation*}
$$

the first expectation value of $\eqref{equation-31}$ becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{h}(1)|\Psi_0} =& \iint \Psi_0^* (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \hat{h}(1) \Psi_0 (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \iint \left[\chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) \right. \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} + \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} - \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} - \left. \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) \right] \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\hat{h}(1)$ only acts on $\mathbf{x}_1$, integration over $\mathbf{x}_2$ yields

$$
  \int \chi_i^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_j (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 = \braket{\chi_i (\mathbf{x}_2)|\chi_j (\mathbf{x}_j)} = \delta_{ij}
$$

by orthonormality of the spin orbitals. Consequently, the exchange terms vanish, leaving

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{h}(1)|\Psi_0} =& \frac{1}{2}\int \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  &+ \frac{1}{2} \int \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1
\end{align*}
$$

By symmetry it follows that $\braket{\Psi_0|\hat{h}(2)|\Psi_0} = \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{h}(1)|\Psi_0}$ and thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{O}_1|\Psi_0} =& \int \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  &+ \int \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \hat{h}(\mathbf{r}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-32}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Using the one-electron integral notation $\eqref{equation-37}$ we can express $\eqref{equation-32}$ compactly as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{\Psi_0 |\hat{O}_1|\Psi_0} = \braket{1|\hat{h}|1} + \braket{2|\hat{h}|2}
\tag{\label{equation-35}}
\end{equation*}
$$

### Two-electron contribution

Next consider the matrix element $\braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_0}$. Using $\eqref{equation-33}$, the expectation value becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_0} =& \iint \Psi_0^* (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \Psi_0 (\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \iint \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} + \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} - \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \\
  & \hphantom{\frac{1}{2}\iint} - \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

Since $r_{12} = r_{21}$, interchanging the dummy variables $\mathbf{x}_1$ as $\mathbf{x}_2$ shows that first two terms are identical, as well as the last two. Thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_0} =& \int \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  &- \int \chi_1^* (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_2^* (\mathbf{x}_2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_2 (\mathbf{x}_1) \chi_1 (\mathbf{x}_2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-34}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Using the two-electron integral notation in $\eqref{equation-38}$, we can rewrite $\eqref{equation-34}$ compactly as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_2|\Psi_0} = \braket{12|12} - \braket{12|21}
\tag{\label{equation-36}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\braket{12|12}$ is the Coulomb integral and $\braket{12|21}$ is the exchange integral.

### Hartree-Fock energy

Combining the one- and two-electron contributions $\eqref{equation-35}$ and $\eqref{equation-36}$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} =& \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{O}_1 + \hat{O}_2|\Psi_0} \\
  =& \braket{1|\hat{h}|1} + \braket{2|\hat{h}|2} + \braket{12|12} - \braket{12|21}
\end{align*}
$$

which is the Hartree-Fock energy for a two-electron Slater determinant. The first two terms represent the one-electron kinetic and nuclear-attraction energies, the Coloumb integral $\braket{12|12}$ accounts for the classical electron-electron repulsion, and the exchange integral $\braket{12|21}$ arises solely from the antisymmetry of the wavefunction.
