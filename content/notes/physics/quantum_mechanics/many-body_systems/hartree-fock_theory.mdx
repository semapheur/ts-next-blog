# The Hartree-Fock equations

Let $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denote combined spatial and spin coordinates. With the Hartree-Fock method, we seek a set of orthonormal spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^$ with $\braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \delta_{ij}$, such that the corresponding Slater determinant

$$
  \Psi_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det[\chi_i (\mathbf{x}_j)]
$$

provides the best approximation to the exact ground-state wavefunction of an $N$-electron system described by the electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$.

According to the variational principle, the optimal spin orbitals minimize the Hartree-Fock energy functional

$$
\begin{align*}
  E[\set{\chi_i}] =& \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

subject to the orthonormality constraints on the spin orbitals. Using Lagrange multipliers, minimization of the energy functional under the orthonormality constraints gives the Hartree-Fock equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{h}(1) \chi_i(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N [\hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1)] \chi_i (1) = \varepsilon_i \chi_i (1)
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\varepsilon_i$ is the Lagrange multiplier, called the orbital energy, associated with spin orbital $\chi_i$.

**Coulomb operator**

The Coulomb operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (\hat{J}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \frac{|\chi_j (2)|^2}{r_{12}} \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \phi(1)
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation*}
$$

for an arbitrary spin orbital $\phi$. Thus, $\hat{J}_j$ is a local multiplicative operator whose value at $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends only on the electron density associated with orbital $\chi_j$. It represents the average electrostatic potential generated by the probability density $|\chi_j|^2$ of electron $2$. Summing over all occupied orbitals therefore gives the mean electrostatic potential experience by an electron occupying orbital $\chi_i$.

The expectation value of $\hat{J}_j$ is given by the Coulomb integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{J}_j|\chi_i} = J_{ij} = [ii|jj]
$$

**Exchange operator**

The exchange operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  (\hat{K}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \phi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \chi_j (1)
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Unlike the Coulomb operator, $\hat{K}_j$ is nonlocal, because its action at the point $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends on the values of the function $\phi$ throughout all space. It has no classical analogue, because it arises solely from the antisymmetry of the Slater determinant and therefore reflects the fermionic nature of the electronic wavefunction. Its nonlocal character is a direct consequence of electron exchange. 

The expectation value of $\hat{K}_j$ is given by the exchange integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{K}_j|\chi_i} = K_{ij} = [ij|ji]
$$

**Fock operator**

Since $(\hat{J}_i - \hat{K}_i)\chi_i = 0$, the self-interaction terms cancel identically. The summation in $\eqref{equation-1}$ can therefore be taken over all occupied orbital, allowing the introduction of the Fock operator

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f} := \hat{h} + \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which depends parametrically on the occupied spin orbitals. In terms of the Fock operator, the Hartree-Fock equations assume the compact form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f} \chi_i = \varepsilon_i \chi_i
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The effective one-electron potential

$$
  \hat{v}_\text{HF} = \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
$$

is known as the Hartree-Fock potential, so that $\hat{f} = \hat{h} + \hat{v}_\text{HF}$. The exchange operator can also be expressed using the electron-permutation operator $\hat{p}_{12}$, defined by $\hat{p}_{12} v(1,2) = v(2,1)$. With this notation

$$
  \hat{f}(1) = \hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N \int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1 - \hat{p}_{12}}{r_{12}} \chi_j (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
$$

Although the Hartree-Fock equations resemble a one-electron eigenvalue problem, they do not constitute a linear eigenvalue problem in the usual sense. The Fock operator depends functionally on the occupied orbitals through the Coulomb and exchange operators, i.e. $\hat{f} = \hat{f}[\set{\chi_i}]$, so the orbitals sought determine the operator itself. The Hartree-Fock equations therefore define a nonlinear, self-consisten eigenvalue problem.

Except for atoms and highly symmetric systems, the Hartree-Fock equations cannot be solved analytically. Instead, each spin orbital is expanded in a finite basis set, resulting the Roothan-Hall or Pople-Nesbet matrix equations, which are solved interatively until self-consistency is achieved. As the basis set approaches completeness, the resulting orbitals converge to the Hartree-Fock limint, providing the exact solution within the space of all single Slater determinants.

## Derivation of the Hartree-Fock equations

### Functional variation

Let $\tilde{\Phi}$ be a trial wavefunction in the domain of the electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$. The corresponding energy is define by the Rayleigh quotient

$$
  E[\tilde{\Phi}] =& \frac{\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}}}{\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}}}
$$

Thus, $E[\tilde{\Phi}]$ is a functional: its value depends on the entire function $\tilde{\Phi}$, rather than on the value of an independent variable. If the trial wavefunction is normalized, $\braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}}$, the energy functional reduces to

$$
  E[\tilde{\Phi}] = \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}}
$$

Suppose $\tilde{\Phi}$ is subjected to an arbitrary infinitesimal variation

$$
  \tilde{\Phi} \to \tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}
$$

where $\delta\tilde{\Phi}$ is an arbitrary admissible variation. Expanding the energy functional to first order gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  E[\tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}] =& \braket{\tilde{\Phi} + \delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi} + \delta{\Phi}} + O(\delta\tilde{\Phi}^2) \\
  =& E[\tilde{\Phi}] + \braket{\delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} + \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\delta\tilde{\Phi}} + O(\delta\tilde{\Phi}^2) \\
\end{align*}
$$

The first-order term, $\delta E = \braket{\delta\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} + \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\delta\tilde{\Phi}}$, is called the first variation of the energy functional.

The variational principle states that the optimal trial wavefunction is obtained by requiring the energy functional to be stationary with respect to all admissible variations, $\delta E = 0$. This condition is neccessary for an extremum of the functional. For the ground state of a Hamiltonian bounded from below, the variational principle guarantees that this stationary point is in fact the global minimum.

Consider the linear variational ansatz

$$
  \ket{\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i=1}^N c_i \ket{\Psi_i}
$$

where the basis functions $\set{\ket{\Psi_i}}$ are linearly independent but not necessarily orthonormal. The normalization condition is

$$
  \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i,j} c_i^* S_{ij} c_j = 1
$$

where $S_{ij} = \braket{\Psi_i|\Psi_j}$ is the overlap matrix. Similarly,

$$
  \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} = \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} c_j
$$

where $H_{ij} = \braket{\Psi_i|\hat{H}|\Psi_i}$ are the Hamiltonian matrix elements.

To impose the normalization constraint, we introduce the Lagrangian

$$
\begin{align*}
  L =& \braket{\tilde{\Phi}|\hat{H}|\tilde{\Phi}} - E(\braket{\tilde{\Phi}} - 1) \\
  =& \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} c_j - E\left(\sum_{i,j} c_î^* c_j S_{ij} - 1 \right)
\end{align*}
$$

where the Lagrangian multiplier $E$ is identified with the variational energy

The coefficients $c_i$ and $c_i^*$ are treated as independent variable. Taking the first variation of $L$ with respect to $c_i^*$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{i,j} \delta c_i^* H_{ij} c_j - E \sum_{i,j} \delta c_i^* S_{ij} c_j \\
  &+ \sum_{i,j} c_i^* H_{ij} \delta c_j - E \sum_{i,j} c_i^* S_{ij} \delta c_i
\end{align*}
$$

Since $E$ is real, after collecting terms and interchanging indices, we get

$$
  \delta L = \sum_i \delta c_i^* \left[\sum_j H_{ij} c_j - E \sum_j S_{ij} c_j \right] + \text{c.c.}
$$

Since the variations $\set{\delta c_i^*}$ are arbitrary, stationarity $\delta L = 0$ requires

$$
  \sum_{j} H_{ij} c_j = E \sum_j S_{ij} c_j
$$

or in matrix form $\mathbf{Hc} = E\mathbf{Sc}$, which is the generalized Hermitian eigenvalue problem arising in the linear variation method. If the basis is orthonormal, then $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{I}$, and the equation reduces to the ordinary eigenvalue problem $\mathbf{Hc} = E\mathbf{c}$. The smallest eigenvalue of the generalized eigenvalue problem provides the best approximation to the ground-state energy within the span of the chosen basis functions, while the corresponding eigenvector determines the optimal linear combination of basis functions.

### Minimization of the energy of a single determinant

Given a single Slater determinant $\ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$, the expectation value $E_0 = \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0}$ defines a functional of the occupied spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}$. The Hartree-Fock equations are obtained by minimizing this functional over all orthonormal sets of spin orbitals satisfying

$$
  \braket{i|j} := \int \chi_i^* (1) \chi_j \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 = \delta_{ij}
$$

This orthonormality conditions produces the constraints $\braket{i|j} - \delta_{ij} = 0$. To enforce these constraints, we introduce the Lagrangian functional

$$
  L[\set{\chi_i}] = E_0 [\set{\chi_i}] - \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} (\braket{i|j} - \delta_{ij})
$$

where the matrix $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = [\varepsilon_{ij}]$ consists of Lagrange multipliers.

where $E_0$ is the expectation value of the single determinant $\ket{\Psi_0}$.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_0 [\set{\chi_i}] = \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [ii|jj] - [ij|ji]
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Because $L$ is real-valued and $\braket{i|j} = \braket{j|i}^*$, the matrix of Lagrange multipliers must be Hermitian, i.e. $\varepsilon_{ji} = \varepsilon_{ij}^*$.

Minimization of $E_0$, subject to the orthonormality constraints, is thus obtained by solving $\delta L = 0$. Treating the spin orbitals and their complex conjugates as independent variables, we consider arbitrary infinitesimal variations

$$
  \chi_i \to& \chi_i + \delta\chi_i \\
  \chi_i^* \to& \chi_i^* + \delta\chi_i^*
$$

Taking the first variation of $\eqref{equation-2}$, we impose

$$
  \delta L = \delta E_0 - \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \delta\braket{i|j} = 0
$$

where have used that $\delta(\delta_{ij}) = 0$. Taking the first variation of the spin orbital overlaps

$$
  \delta\braket{i|j} = \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \braket{\chi_i|\delta\chi_j}
$$

and using the Hermiticity of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} (\braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \braket{\chi_i|\delta\chi_j}) =& \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ij} \braket{\chi_j|\delta\chi_i} \\
  =& \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji}^* \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} \\
  =& 2 \Re \left(\sum_{i,j} \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} \right)
\end{align*}
$$

The first variation of the energy is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta E_0 =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} + \braket{\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} \\
  &+ \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \delta\chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \chi_i|\delta\chi_j \chi_j] + [\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \delta\chi_j] \\
  &- \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \delta\chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \chi_j|\delta\chi_j \chi_i] + [\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \delta\chi_i]
\end{align*}
$$

Using the permutation symmetry of the two-electron integrals and relabelling dummy indices, this expression simplifies to

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta E_0 =& \sum_{i=1} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} + \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] - [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] \\
  &+ \text{c.c.}
\end{align*}
$$

Combining these results, the first variation in $L$ becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{\delta\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} \\
  &+ \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N [\delta\chi_i \chi_i|\chi_j \chi_j] - [\delta\chi_i \chi_j|\chi_j \chi_i] \\
  &- \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\delta\chi_i|\chi_j} + \text{c.c.} = 0
\end{align*}
$$

Using definitions $\eqref{equation-3}$ and $\eqref{equation-4}$ for the Coulomb and exchange operators, respectively, we can write this variation in the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \delta L =& \sum_{j=1}^N \int \delta\chi_i^* (1) \left(\hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N [\hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1)] \right) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  &- \sum_{i,j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \int \delta\chi_i^* (1) \chi_j (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 + \text{c.c} = 0
\end{equation*}
$$

Since the variations $\delta\chi_i^* (1)$ are arbitrary, the fundamental lemma of variational calculus implies that

$$
  \left[\hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N \hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1) \right] \chi_i (1) = \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \chi_j (1),\; j=1,\dots,N
$$

Recognizing the Fock operator $\eqref{equation-5}$, this equation takes the compact form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f}\ket{\chi_i} = \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \ket{\chi_i}
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$

These equations are not yet in the canonical eigenvalue form of $\eqref{equation-6}$ because the occupied orbitals are determined only up to an arbitrary unitary transformation. Replacing the occupied spin orbitals by any unitary lienar combination leaves the Slater determinant unchanged up to an overall phase, and therefore leaves the Hartree-Fock energy invariant. Exploiting this unitary freedom, the Hermitian matrix $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ can be diagonalized, resulting in $\eqref{equation-6}$.

### Canonical Hartree-Fock equations

Let $\mathbf{U} = [U_{ij}]$ be a unitary transformation satisfying 

$$
  \mathbf{U}^\dagger \mathbf{U} = \mathbf{UU}^\dagger = \mathbf{I}
$$

Consider a new set of occupied spin orbitals $\set{\chi'_i}_{i=1}^N$ transformed by $\mathbf{U}$ from the original set $\set{\chi_i}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \chi'_i (\mathbf{x}_j) = \sum_{k=1} \chi_k (\mathbf{x}_j) U_{ki}
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Defining the matrix $\mathbf{A} = [\chi_k (j)]$, this can be written as $\mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{AU}$.

The normalized Slater determinant constructed from the transformed orbitals is

$$
  \Psi' = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det[\chi'_i (\mathbf{x}_j)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det(\mathbf{AU})
$$

Using the multiplicative property of determinants

$$
  \det(\mathbf{AU}) = \det(\mathbf{A})\det(\mathbf{U})
$$

we obtain

$$
  \Psi' = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det(\mathbf{A})}_{=\Psi}\det(\mathbf{U}) = \det(\mathbf{U}) \Psi
$$

By unitarity of $\mathbf{U}$, it follows that $|\det{\mathbf{U}}| = 1$ such that $\det(\mathbf{U}) = e^{i\phi}$ for some real phase $\phi\in\R$. Consequently, $\ket{\Psi'} = e^{i\phi} \ket{\Psi}$ showing that the two Slater determinants constructed from occupied spin orbitals related by a unitary transformation differ only by an overall phase. 

Since all expectation values depend on the projector $\ket{\Psi}\bra{\Psi}$, they are invariant under unitary transformations. That is $\braket{\Psi'|\hat{A}|\Psi'} = \braket{\Psi|\hat{A}|\Psi}$ for every observable $\hat{A}$. Consequently, the occupied orbitals that minimize the Hartree-Fock energy are not uniquely determined; only the occupied subspace has physical significance.

---

To obtain the canonical Hartree-Fock equations, we examine how the Fock operator and the matrix of Lagrange multipliers transform under orbital rotations.

The transformed total Coulomb operator under $\mathbf{U}$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_i \hat{J}'_i (1) =& \sum_i \int {\chi'_i}^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi'_i (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \sum_{j,k} \left(\sum_i U_{ji}^* U_{ki} \right) \int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_k (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
\end{align*}
$$

Since

$$
  \sum_i U_{ji}^* U_{ki} = (\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\dagger)_{kj} = \delta_{kj}
$$

it follows that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_i \hat{J}'_i (1) =& \sum_j \int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_j (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \\
  =& \sum_j \hat{J}_j (1)
\end{*}
$$

showing that the total Coulomb operator is invariant under unitary transformations of the occupied spin orbitals. Similarly, for an arbitrary spin orbital $\varphi$, the transformed total exchange operator under $\mathbf{U}$ satisfies

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left(\sum_i \hat{K}'_i \right)\varphi =& \sum_i \left(\int {\chi'_i}^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \varphi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right)\chi'_i (1) \\
  =& \sum_{j,k} \underbrace{\left(\sum_i U_{ji}^* U_{ki} \right)}_{=\delta_{jk}} \left(\int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \varphi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right) \chi_k (1) \\
  =& \sum_j \left(\int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \varphi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right) \chi_j (1) \\
  =& \left(\sum_j \hat{K}_j \right) \varphi
\end{align*}
$$

Since this holds for every spin orbital $\varphi$, the total exchange operator is invariant under unitary transformations. 

These results implies that the Fock operator is invariant under arbitrary unitary transformations of the spin orbitals, i.e. $f' = f$.

It remains to determine the effect of the unitary transformation on the Lagrange multipliers. Taking the inner product of $\eqref{equation-7}$ with $\ket{\chi_k}$ yields the expectation value

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{\chi_k|\hat{f}|\chi_i} =& \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_{ji} \braket{\chi_k|\chi_j} \\
  =& \varepsilon_{ki}
\end{align*} 
$$

so that the matrix of Lagrange multipliers is simply the representation of the Fock operator in the occupied orbital basis. The transformed Lagrange multipliers under $\mathbf{U}$ become

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varepsilon'_{ij} =& \int {\chi'_i}^*(1) \hat{f}(1) \chi'_j (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  =& \sum_{k,l} U_{ki}^* U_{kj} \int \chi_k^* (1) \hat{f}(1) \chi_k (1) \;\d\mathbf{x}_1 \\
  =& \sum_{k,l} U_{ki}^* \varepsilon_{kl} U_{lj}
\end{align*}
$$

or in matrix form 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}' = \mathbf{U}^\dagger \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \mathbf{U}
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is a Hermitian matrix, the spectral theorem guarantees the existence of a unitary matrix $\mathbf{U}$ for which

$$
  \mathbf{U}^\dagger \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \mathbf{U} = \operatorname{diag}(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_N)
$$

Choosing the occupied orbitals to be the corresponding transformed orbitals under $\mathbf{U}$ therefore diagonalizes the matrix of Lagrange multiplies, reducing the Hartree-Fock equations to the canonical form

$$
  \hat{f}\ket{\chi'_i} = \varepsilon'_i \ket{\chi'_i}
$$

The resulting orbitals from these equations are called the canonical Hartree-Fock orbitals. For non-degenerate orbital energies they are unique up to individual phase factors, whereas degenerate canonical orbitals may still be mixed by arbitrary unitary transformations within each degenerate subspace. More generally, any unitary transformation among the occupied orbitals leaves the Hartree-Fock wavefunction and energy unchanged. We can therefore replace the canonical orbitals by alternative sets satisfying additional criteria, such as maximal spatial localization, without altering any physical observable computed from the Hartree-Fock determinant.

## Koopman's theorem

For an $N$-electron system, minimization of the Hartree-Fock energy with respect to all normalized Slater determinants $\ket{\Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$ leads to the Hartree-Fock equations $\hat{f}\ket{\chi_i} = \varepsilon_i \ket{\chi_i}$ for the $N$ occupied spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1^N}$. The Fock operator $\hat{f}$ has a functional dependence on these occupied spin orbitals through the Coulomb and exchange operators. Once the self-consistent solution has been obtained, however, $\hat{f}$ becomes a well-defined Hermitian one-electron operator. Consequently, it possesses a complete orthonormal set of eigenstates satisfying

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{f} \ket{\chi_i} = \varepsilon_i \ket{\chi_i},\; i\in\N
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the spectrum contains both the $N$ occupied orbitals and an infinite set of unoccupied (virtual) orbitals.

Each eigenstate $\ket{\chi_i}$ is associated with an orbital energy $\varepsilon_i$. By construction, the occupied spin orbitals are those corresponding to the $N$ lowest eigenvalues of the Fock operator, while all remaining eigenstates aare virtual orbitals. In the following we derive explicit expressions for both occupied and virtual orbital energies.

Since the eigenstates of a Hermitian operator form an orthonormal basis, we have

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{f}|\chi_j} = \varepsilon_j \braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \varepsilon_j \delta_{ij}
$$

so that the matrix representation of the Fock operator in its eigenbasis is diagonal.

Using definition $\eqref{equation-5}$ for the Fock operator, the orbital energies can be expressed as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varepsilon_i =& \braket{\chi_i|\hat{f}|\chi_i} \\
  =& \Braket{\chi_i|\hat{h} + \sum_j (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)|\chi_i} \\
  =& \braket{\chi_i|\hat{h}|\chi_i} + \sum_j \braket{\chi_i|\hat{J}_j|\chi_i} - \braket{\chi_i|\hat{K}_j|\chi_i} \\
  =& \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_j \braket{ij|ij} - \braket{ij|ji} \\
  =& \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_j \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

**Occupied orbital energies**

The orbital energy for an occupied spin orbital $\ket{\chi_a}$ is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \varepsilon_a = \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{aj}\ket{aj}
\tag{\label{equation-11}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since $\bra{aa}\ket{aa} = 0$, we can rewrite this as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varepsilon_a =& \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \sum_{\substack{j=1 \\ j\neq a}}^N \braket{aj|aj} - \braket{aj|ja} \\
  =& h_{aa} + \sum_{\substack{j=1 \\ j\neq a}} J_{aj} - K_{aj}
\end{align*}
$$

In addition to the one-electron contribution $h_{aa}$, the occupied orbital energy $\varepsilon_a$ consists of the Coulomb ($J_{aj}$) and exchange ($K_{aj}$) interactions between the electron occupying $\ke{\chi_a}$ and every other occupied spin orbital.

The exchange integrals $K_{ja}$ vanishes whenever the spin functions of $\ket{\chi_a}$ and $\ket{\chi_j}$ are orthogonal. In this general spin formulation, the exchange term is retained explicitly in the orbital energy expression.

**Virtual orbital energies**

The orbital energy for a virtual spin orbital $\ket{\chi_p}$ is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \varepsilon_p =& \braket{p|\hat{h}|p} + \sum_{j=1}^N \bra{pj}\ket{pj} \\
  =& \braket{p|\hat{h}|p} + \sum_j \braket{pj|pj} - \braket{pj|jp}
\end{align*}
$$

Although $\ket{\chi_p}$ is unoccupied in the Hartree-Fock ground state $\ket{\Psi_0}$, its orbital energy $\varepsilon_p$ include Coulomb and exchange interactions will all $N$ occupied electrons. Thus $\varepsilon_p$ can be interpreted as the energy of an additional electron present in the mean field generated by $\ket{\Psi_0}$.

**Relation between orbital energies and the total Hartree-Fock energy**

Summing the occupied orbital energies $\varepsilon_a$ in $\eqref{equation-11}$ for all $N$ electrons in the ground state $\ket{\Psi_0}$ yields

$$
  \sum_a^N \varepsilon_a = \sum_{a=1}^N \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \sum_{a, j=1}^N \bra{aj}\ket{aj}
$$

In contrast the Hartree-Fock expectation value $E_0 = \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}\Psi_0}$ is given by

$$
  E_0 = \sum_{a=1}^N \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{a,j=1}^N \bra{aj}\ket{aj}
$$

Thus, the total occupied orbital energy does not coincide with the ground state energy

$$
  E_0 \neq \sum_{a=1}^N \varepsilon_a
$$

The discrepancy arises because each pairwise electron-electron interaction appears twice in the sum of orbital energies: once in $\varepsilon_a$ through its interaction with orbital $j$, and again in $\varepsilon_j$ through its interaction with orbital $a$. The factor $1/2$ in the Hartree-Fock energy removes this double counting.

**Physical interpretation of orbital energy**

The physical significance of the orbital energies becomes apparent when we consider the removal or addition of an electron while keeping all remaining spin orbitals fixed.

Consider the $N$-electron Slater determinant $\ket{{}^N \Psi_0} = \ket{\chi_1 \cdots \chi_N}$ with Hartree-Fock energy 

$$
\begin{align*}
  {}^N E_0 =& \braket{{}^N \Psi_0|\hat{H}|{}^N \Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{a=1}^N \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{a,b=1}^N \bra{ab}\ket{ab}
\end{align*}
$$

Removing an electron from the occupied spin orbital $\chi_c$ produces the $(N-1)$-electron state

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \ket{{}^{N-1} \Psi_c} = \hat{a}_c \ket{{}^N \Psi_0}
\tag{\label{equation-13}}
\end{equation*}
$$

with Hartree-Fock expectation value ${}^{N-1} E_c = \braket{{}^{N-1} \Psi_c|\hat{H}|{}^{N-1} \Psi_c}$. Assuming that the remaining orbitals are unchanged this becomes

$$
  {}^{N-1} E_c = \sum_{a\neq c} \braket{a|\hat{h}|a} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{a,b\neq c} \bra{ab}\ket{ab}
$$

The corresponding ionization potential energy for this process is

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_\text{IP} =& {}^{N-1} E_c - {}^N E_0 \\
  =& -\braket{c|\hat{h}|c} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{a,(b\equiv c)} \bra{ab}\ket{ab} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_{b,(a\equiv c)} \bra{ab}\ket{ab} \\
  =& -\braket{c|\hat{h}|c} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_a \bra{ac}\ket{ac} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_b \bra{cb}\ket{cb} \\
  =& -\braket{c|\hat{h}|c} - \sum_b \bra{cb}\ket{cb} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-11}} -\varepsilon_c
\end{align*}
$$

Consequently, the ionization potential for removing an electron from the occupied spin orbital $\chi_c$ is just the negative of the orbital energy $\varepsilon_c$.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_\text{IP} = {}^{N-1} E_c - {}^N E_0 = -\varepsilon_c
\tag{\label{equation-12}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Thus, within the single-determinant Hartree--Fock approximation, the occupied orbital energies provide estimates of the energies required to remove electrons from their respective spin orbitals, differing only by a minus sign. Since occupied Hartree--Fock orbital energies are typically negative, the corresponding ionization potentials are generally positive.

The validity of $\eqref{equation-12}$ relies on two key approximations
1. **Frozen-orbital approximation:** The orbitals of the remaining $N-1$ electrons are assumed to remain unchanged following ionization, so that the ionized state is represented by the Slater determinant obtained simply by removing the occupied spin orbital $\chi_c$.
2. Electron correlation beyond the Hartree-Fock approximation is neglected. 

In reality, the optimal spin orbitals of $(N-1)$-electron system generally differ from those of the neutral $N$-electron system, and the determinant $\eqref{equation-13}$ need not correspond to the Hartree-Fock ground state of the ionized species. Depending on the choice of the removed spin orbital $\chi_c$, this determinant may represent either the ground state or an excited state of the cation. 

---

Now let us consider the process of adding an electron to a virtual spin orbital $\chi_p$, producing the $(N+1)$-electron Slater determinant 

$$
  \ket{{}^{N+1} \Psi^p} = \hat{a}_p^\dagger \ket{{}^N \Psi_0}
$$

with Hartree-Fock expectation value ${}^{N+1} E_p = \braket{{}^{N+1} \Psi^p|\hat{H}|{}^{N+1} \Psi^p}$. Assuming that the remaining orbitals are unchanged we get

$$
  {}^{N+1} E_p = \braket{p|\hat{h}|p} + \sum_{a=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \sum_{a=1}^N \bra{pa}\ket{pa} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
$$

The electron affinity of $\ket{{}^N \Psi_0}$ for this process is

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_\text{EA} =& {}^N E_0 - {}^{N+1} E_p \\
  =& -\braket{p|\hat{h}|p} - \sum_b \bra{pb}\ket{pb} \underbrace{=}_{\eqref{equation-11}} -\varepsilon_p
\end{align*}
$$

This shows that the electron affinity for adding an electron to the virtual spin orbital $\chi_p$ is just the negative of the orbital energy of that virtual spin orbital

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_\text{EA} = {}^N E_0 - {}^{N+1} E_p = -\varepsilon_p
\tag{\label{equation-14}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This result is consistent with the interpretation of $\varepsilon_p$ as the energy of an electron occupying the virtual orbital $\chi_p$, while moving in the mean field generated by the $N$-electron Hartree Fock ground state $\ket{{}^N \Psi_0}$. If $\varepsilon_p < 0$, then the $(N+1)$-electron determinan is lower in energy than the neutral determinan, and the corresponding electron affinity is positive.

As in the ionization process, the exact $(N+1)$-electron Hartree-Fock state generally posesses a different set of self-consisten spin orbitals. Consequently, $\eqref{equation-14}$ is not exact, but follows from the frozen-orbital approximation.
