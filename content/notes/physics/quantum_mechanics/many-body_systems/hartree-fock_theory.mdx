# The Hartree-Fock equations

Let $\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r}, \omega)$ denote combined spatial and spin coordinates. With the Hartree-Fock method, we seek a set of orthonormal spin orbitals $\set{\chi_i}_{i=1}^$ with $\braket{\chi_i|\chi_j} = \delta_{ij}$, such that the corresponding Slater determinant

$$
  \Psi_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}!} \det[\chi_i (\mathbf{x}_j)]
$$

provides the best approximation to the exact ground-state wavefunction of an $N$-electron system described by the electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$.

According to the variational principle, the optimal spin orbitals minimize the Hartree-Fock energy functional

$$
\begin{align*}
  E[\set{\chi_i}] =& \braket{\Psi_0|\hat{H}|\Psi_0} \\
  =& \sum_{i=1}^N \braket{i|\hat{h}|i} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^N \bra{ij}\ket{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

subject to the orthonormality constraints on the spin orbitals. Using Lagrange multipliers, minimization of the energy functional under the orthonormality constraints gives the Hartree-Fock equation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{h}(1) \chi_i(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N [\hat{J}_j (1) - \hat{K}_j (1)] \chi_i (1) = \varepsilon_i \chi_i (1)
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\varepsilon_i$ is the Lagrange multiplier, called the orbital energy, associated with spin orbital $\chi_i$.

**Coulomb operator**

The Coulomb operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
  (\hat{J}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \frac{|\chi_j (2)|^2}{r_{12}} \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \phi(1)
$$

for an arbitrary spin orbital $\phi$. Thus, $\hat{J}_j$ is a local multiplicative operator whose value at $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends only on the electron density associated with orbital $\chi_j$. It represents the average electrostatic potential generated by the probability density $|\chi_j|^2$ of electron $2$. Summing over all occupied orbitals therefore gives the mean electrostatic potential experience by an electron occupying orbital $\chi_i$.

The expectation value of $\hat{J}_j$ is given by the Coulomb integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{J}_j|\chi_i} = J_{ij} = [ii|jj]
$$

**Exchange operator**

The exchange operator associated with the occupied spin orbital $\chi_j$ is defined by

$$
  (\hat{K}_j \phi)(1) = \left[\int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \phi(2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2 \right] \chi_j (1)
$$

Unlike the Coulomb operator, $\hat{K}_j$ is nonlocal, because its action at the point $\mathbf{x}_1$ depends on the values of the function $\phi$ throughout all space. It has no classical analogue, because it arises solely from the antisymmetry of the Slater determinant and therefore reflects the fermionic nature of the electronic wavefunction. Its nonlocal character is a direct consequence of electron exchange. 

The expectation value of $\hat{K}_j$ is given by the exchange integral

$$
  \braket{\chi_i|\hat{K}_j|\chi_i} = K_{ij} = [ij|ji]
$$

**Fock operator**

Since $(\hat{J}_i - \hat{K}_i)\chi_i = 0$, the self-interaction terms cancel identically. The summation in $\eqref{equation-1}$ can therefore be taken over all occupied orbital, allowing the introduction of the Fock operator

$$
  \hat{f} := \hat{h} + \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
$$

which depends parametrically on the occupied spin orbitals. In terms of the Fock operator, the Hartree-Fock equations assume the compact form

$$
  \hat{f} \chi_i = \varepsilon_i \chi_i
$$

The effective one-electron potential

$$
  \hat{v}_\text{HF} = \sum_{j=1}^N (\hat{J}_j - \hat{K}_j)
$$

is known as the Hartree-Fock potential, so that $\hat{f} = \hat{h} + \hat{v}_\text{HF}$. The exchange operator can also be expressed using the electron-permutation operator $\hat{p}_{12}$, defined by $\hat{p}_{12} v(1,2) = v(2,1)$. With this notation

$$
  \hat{f}(1) = \hat{h}(1) + \sum_{j=1}^N \int \chi_j^* (2) \frac{1 - \hat{p}_{12}}{r_{12}} \chi_j (2) \;\d\mathbf{x}_2
$$

Although the Hartree-Fock equations resemble a one-electron eigenvalue problem, they do not constitute a linear eigenvalue problem in the usual sense. The Fock operator depends functionally on the occupied orbitals through the Coulomb and exchange operators, i.e. $\hat{f} = \hat{f}[\set{\chi_i}]$, so the orbitals sought determine the operator itself. The Hartree-Fock equations therefore define a nonlinear, self-consisten eigenvalue problem.

Except for atoms and highly symmetric systems, the Hartree-Fock equations cannot be solved analytically. Instead, each spin orbital is expanded in a finite basis set, resulting the Roothan-Hall or Pople-Nesbet matrix equations, which are solved interatively until self-consistency is achieved. As the basis set approaches completeness, the resulting orbitals converge to the Hartree-Fock limint, providing the exact solution within the space of all single Slater determinants.
