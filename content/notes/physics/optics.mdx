# Polarization

The electric field of an electromagnetic wave propagating in non-conductive medium with speed $v = \frac{c}{n}$ is given

$$
  \left(\nabla^2 - \smash[b]{\underbrace{\frac{1}{v^2}}_{\mathclap{k^2/\omega^2}}}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\right) \mathbf{E} = 0 \vphantom{\underbrace{\frac{1}{v^2}}_{\mathclap{k^2/\omega^2}}}
$$

where $k = \frac{\omega}{v}$ is the wave number. 


The solution for a plane monochromatic propagating along a wave vector $\mathbf{k} = k\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ takes the form

$$
  \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)}
$$

In isotropic medium, the wave vector $\mathbf{k}$ and the complex amplitude $\mathbf{E}_0$ are perpendicular by Gauss' law. Thus, after specifying the direction of $\mathbf{k}$ we are free to have $\mathbf{E_0}$ point anywhere in the dimension perpendicular to $\mathbf{k}$. If we apply a Cartesian coordinate system and orient it with $\mathbf{k}$ in the $z$-direction, i.e. $\mathbf{k} = k \hat{\mathbf{z}}$, the electric field takes the form

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{E}(z, t) =& (E_x \hat{\mathbf{x}} + E_y \hat{\mathbf{y}}) e^{i(kz - \omega t)} + 0\hat{\mathbf{z}} \\
  =& (|E_x|e^{i\phi_x} \hat{\mathbf{x}} + |E_y|e^{i\phi_y} \hat{\mathbf{y}}) e^{i(kz - \omega t)}
\end{align*}
$$

The relationship between the complex amplitudes $E_x$ and $E_y$ describes the polarization of the electromangetic wave:
- linear polarization occurs when 
    - $|E_x| = 0$ or $|E_y| = 0$ is zero, or
    - $|\phi_x - \phi_y| = m\pi$ for $m\in\N$
- circular polarization occurs when $|E_x| = |E_y|$ and $|\phi_x - \phi_y| = m\frac{\pi}{2}$
- elliptical polarization occurs when 
    - $|E_x| = |E_y|$ and $0 < |\phi_x - \phi_y| \neq m\frac{\pi}{2}$
    - $|E_x| \neq |E_y|$ and $|\phi_x - \phi_y| = m\frac{\pi}{2}$

## Jones vector

The polarization state of an electromagnetic wave propagating in the $z$-direction is characterized by the amplitude and phase of the electric field in the $x$ and $y$ direction. This information can be conveniently represented as two-dimensional complex vector

$$
\mathbf{j} = \begin{bmatrix} |E_x|e^{i\phi_x} \\ |E_y|e^{i\phi_y} \end{bmatrix}
$$

known as a Jones vectors. The Jones vector can be normalized by refactoring

$$
  \mathbf{E}(z, t) = E (A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}})e^{i(kz - \omega t)}
$$

where

$$
\begin{align*}
  E = \lVert E \rVert =& \sqrt{|E_x|^2 + |E_y|^2 e^{i\phi_x}} \\
  A =& \frac{|E_x|}{\sqrt{|E_x|^2 + |E_y|^2}} \\
  B =& \frac{|E_y|}{\sqrt{|E_x|^2 + |E_y|^2}} \\
  \delta =& \phi_y - \phi_x
\end{align*}
$$

Note that if $E_x = 0$, then the phase $e^{i\phi_x}$ is indeterminant. In this case we let $E = |E_y| e^{i\phi_y}$, $B = 1$ and $\delta = 0$.  This refactoring ensures that the Jones vector $\mathbf{j} = A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is a normalized vector since

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lVert A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}} \rVert =& (A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \cdot (A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}})^* \\
  =& (A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \cdot (A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + Be^{-i\delta}\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \\
  =& A^2 + B^2
\end{align*}
$$

Because $A^2 + B^2 = 1$, we can set $A = \cos\theta$ and $B = \sin\theta$ for an angle $\theta$ with respect to the $x$-axis. Written as a column vector the Jonas vector takes the form

$$
  \mathbf{j} = \begin{bmatrix} A \\ Be^{i\delta} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta \\ e^{i\delta}\sin\theta \end{bmatrix}
$$

| Polarization | Jones vector | Ket notation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Horizontal (linearly polarized along $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$) | $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{H}$ |
| Vertical (linearly polarized along $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$) | $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{V}$ |
| Diagonal (linearly polarized along $\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \hat{\mathbf{y}}$)  | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{D} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{H} + \ket{V})$ |
| Anti-diagonal (linearly polarized along $\hat{\mathbf{x}} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}$)  | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{A} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{H} - \ket{V})$ |
| Right-hand circular (RHCP) | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -i \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{R} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{H} - i\ket{V})$ |
| Left-hand circular (LHCP) | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix}$ | $\ket{R} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{H} + i\ket{V})$ |

## Elliptical polarization

In its general form, the Jones vector represents elliptical polarization. In this sense, linear and circular polarization can be considered special cases of elliptical polarization. Elliptically polarizared waves travelling in the $z$-directions make a spiral motion and trace out an ellipse in the $xy$-plane with a minor or major axis at angle

$$
  \alpha = \frac{1}{2}\atan\left( \frac{2AB\cos(\delta)}{A^2 - B^2} \right)
$$

with respect to the $x$-axis. To determine whether this axis corresponds to the minor or major axis, we can compare the strength of the electric field for $\alpha$ and $\alpha \pm \frac{\pi}{2}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_\alpha =& E \sqrt{A^2 \cos^2\alpha + B^2 \sin^2\alpha + AB\cos\delta \sin 2\alpha}\\
  E_{\alpha\pm\pi/2} =& E \sqrt{A^2 \sin^2\alpha + B^2 \cos^2\alpha - AB\cos\delta \sin 2\alpha}&
\end{align*}
$$

The ellipticity is given by

$$
  e = \frac{\max\Set{E_\alpha, E_{\alpha\pm\pi/2}}}{\min\Set{E_\alpha, E_{\alpha\pm\pi/2}}}
$$

Linear polarization occurs when $e = 0$, while circular polarization occurs when $e = 1$. The helicity, or handedness of polarized light is as follows
- left-handed: $0 < \delta < \pi$
- right-handed: $\pi < \delta < 2\pi$

## Linear polarizers and wave plates

A polarizer acts on a plane wave by absorbing its electric field along certain axis, while allowing transmission along another axis. The electric field of an monochromatic plane wave propagating in the $z$-direction is given by

$$
  \mathbf{E}(z, t) = (E_x \hat{\mathbf{x}} + E_y \hat{\mathbf{y}})e^{i(kz -\omega t)}
$$

Suppose a polarizer has a transmission axis along $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1$ and absorption axis along $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_2$ in the $xy$-plane. If $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1$ and $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_2$ are orthogonal, then they are related to $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ through a rotation by an angle $\theta$ with respect to the $x$-axis. This gives the basis transform

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{\mathbf{x}} =& \cos(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 - \sin(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 \\
  \hat{\mathbf{y}} =& \sin(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 + \cos(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{e}}_2
\end{align*}
$$

with inverse

$$
\begin{align*}
  \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 =& \cos(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{y}} \\
  \hat{\mathbf{e}}_2 =& -\sin(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos(\theta) \hat{\mathbf{y}}
\end{align*}
$$

In terms of the $(\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1, \hat{\mathbf{e}}_2)$-basis the electric field becomes

$$
  \mathbf{E}(z, t) = (E_1 \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 + E_2 \hat{\mathbf{e}}_2)e^{i(kz -\omega t)}
$$

where

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_1 =& E_x \cos\theta + E_y \sin\theta \\
  E_2 =& -E_x \sin\theta + E_y \cos\theta
\end{align*}
$$

The polarizer allows transmission along $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1$ and causes absorption along $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_2$. Thus, after traversing the polarizer $E_1$ gets unaffected while $E_2$ is changed by a factor $0 \leq \xi \leq 1$. If $\xi = 0$, then $E_2$ gets extinguished, which is the case of a linear polarizer. The polarized wave takes the form

$$
  \mathbf{E}'(z, t) = (E_1 \hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 + \xi E_2 \hat{\mathbf{e}}_2)e^{i(kz -\omega t)}
$$

In terms of the $(\hat{\mathbf{x}}, \hat{\mathbf{y}})$-basis the electric field takes the form

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{E}'(z, t) =& [(E_x \cos\theta + E_y \sin\theta)(\cos\theta\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\theta\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \\
  &+ \xi(-E_x \sin\theta + E_y \cos\theta)(-\sin\theta \hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos\theta\hat{\mathbf{y}})]e^{i(kz - \omega t)} \\
  =& [E_x (\cos^2\theta + \xi\sin^2 \theta) + E_y (1 - \xi) \sin\theta\cos\theta]\hat{\mathbf{x}}e^{i(kz - \omega t)} \\
  &+ [E_x (1 - \xi)\sin\theta\cos\theta + E_y(\sin^2 \theta + \xi\cos^2 \theta)]\hat{\mathbf{y}}e^{i(kz - \omega t)} \\
\end{align*}
$$

or in matrix form

$$
  \mathbf{E}'(z, t) = \begin{bmatrix} 
    \cos^2 \theta + \xi\sin^2 \theta & (1 - \xi)\sin\theta\cos\theta \\ 
    (1 - \xi)\sin\theta\cos\theta & \sin^2 \theta + \xi\cos^2\theta
  \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} E_x \hat{\mathbf{x}} \\ E_y \hat{\mathbf{y}} \end{bmatrix} e^{i(kz - \omega t)}
$$

The Jones matrix describing the polarization thus takes the form

$$
  \begin{bmatrix} 
    \cos^2 \theta + \xi\sin^2 \theta & (1 - \xi)\sin\theta\cos\theta \\ 
    (1 - \xi)\sin\theta\cos\theta & \sin^2 \theta + \xi\cos^2\theta
  \end{bmatrix}
$$

For a linear polarizer, $\xi = 0$, and the Jones matrix reduces to

$$
  \mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix} 
    \cos^2 \theta & \sin\theta\cos\theta  \\ 
    \sin\theta\cos\theta & \sin^2 \theta 
  \end{bmatrix}
$$

## Wave plates (phase retarder)

Wave plates are made out of birefringent (birefractive) crystals for which the index of refraction depends on the on the orientation of the electric field polarization. The crystal is cut such that the wave plate has a *fast* and *slow* axis, which are orthogonal. Along the fast axis, the elecromagnetic wave experiences index $n_{\text{fast}}$, while the orthogonal axis experiences higher index $n_{\text{slow}}$.

When a plane wave passes through a wave plate, the component of the electric field oriented along the fast axis travels faster than its orthogonal counterpart, which introduces a relative phase between the two polarization components. As light passes through a wave plate of thickness $d$, the phase difference that accumulates between the fast and the slow polarization component is

$$
  k_{\text{slow}}d - k_{\text{slow}}d = \frac{2\pi d}{\lambda_c} (n_{\text{slow}} - n_{\text{fast}})
$$

A desired phase shift can obtained by adjusting the thickness of the wave plate. The most common types of wave plates are the quarter-wave and half-wave plates. The quarter-wave plate induces a phase shift $\frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi m$ for $m\in \N$ and has a form factor $\xi = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}} = i$. The half-wave plate induces a phase shift $\pi + 2\pi m$ and has form factor $\xi = e^{i\pi} = -1$.

# Diffraction

## Huygens-Fresnel principle

The Huygens-Fresnel principle states that every point on a wavefront is itself the source of spherical wavelets, and the secondary wavelets emanating from different points mutually interfere. The propagation of each wavelet can be described by a complex amplitude $U: \R^3 \times \R \to \mathbb{C}$ given by

$$
  U(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{U_0}{r}e^{i\left(\omega t - \mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} \right)}
$$

According to the Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diffraction theory, the Huygens-Fresnel principle can be formulated as the superposition of spherical wavelets emanating from within an aperture region $\Sigma$

$$
  U(P) = \frac{1}{i\lambda} \int_\Sigma U(\sigma) \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \cos\theta \d s
$$

Applying Cartesian coordinates such that the aperture plane $\xi \eta$ and observation plane $xy$ are orthogonal to the $z$ axis, the obliquity angle $\theta$ is given by

$$
  \cos\theta = \frac{z}{r}
$$

and the distance $r$ is

$$
  r = \sqrt{z^2 + \left(x - \xi \right)^2 + (y - \eta)^2}
$$

## Kirchhoff's integral theorem

Kirchhoff's integral theorem provides a formulation for the diffraction problem in terms of a surface integral.

The field propagation monochromatic spherical scalar wave is of the form

$$
  A(\mathbf{r}, t) = U(\mathbf{r})e^{-i\omega t}
$$

Inserting into the wave equation $\nabla^2 A - \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial t^2} = 0$ yields the Helmholtz equation

$$
\begin{gather*}
  \nabla^2 \left[ U e^{-i\omega t} \right] = \frac{\left(-i\omega \right)^2}{v^2} U e^{-i\omega t} \\
  \nabla^2 U(\mathbf{r}) = -\frac{\omega^2}{v^2} U(\mathbf{r}) = -k^2 U(\mathbf{r})
\end{gather*}
$$

Considering a monochromatic scalar wave function $U$ and an impulse response function $G$ with continuous first and second order derivatives. Applying Green's second indentity gives

$$
  \iiint_\mathcal{V} \left( U \nabla^2 G - G \nabla^2 U  \right) \d V = -\oint_\mathcal{S} \left( U \frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} - G \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \right) \d S
$$

Since $\nabla^2 U = -k^2$ and $\nabla^2 G = -k^2$, the volume integral vanishes, leaving 

$$
  \oint_\mathcal{S} \left( U \frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} - G \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \right) \d S = \oint_\mathcal{S} \left( U \nabla_n G - G \nabla_n G \right) \d\mathbf{S} = 0
$$

The impulse response function $G$ can be choosen as the Green's function $G(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{e^{iks}}{s}$ where $s$ is the distance from an observation point $P$ in the closed volume $\mathcal{V}$. Since there is a singularity for $G$ at $P$, the integral surface must not include $P$. A suggested integral surface is an inner sphere $\mathcal{S}_\epsilon$ centered at $P$ with the radius of $\epsilon$ and an outer arbitrary closed surface $\mathcal{S}$. The surface integral becomes

$$
  \oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} \left( U \nabla_n \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} - \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} \nabla_n U \right) \d S + \oint_\mathcal{S} \left( U \nabla_n \frac{e^{iks}}{s} - \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \nabla_n U \right) \d S = 0 \tag{1}
$$

For the integral on the inner sphere $\mathcal{S}_\epsilon$

$$
  \frac{\partial}{\partial\hat{\mathbf{n}}}\frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} = \nabla \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = \left( \frac{ik}{\epsilon} - \frac{1}{\epsilon} \right)e^{ik\epsilon}
$$

and by introducing the solid angle $\d\Omega$ given by $\d S = \epsilon^2 \d\Omega$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} \left( U \nabla_n \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} - \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} \nabla_n U \right) \d S &= \oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} \left[ u \left( \frac{ik}{\epsilon} - \frac{1}{\epsilon} \right)e^{ik\epsilon} - \frac{e^{ik\epsilon}}{\epsilon} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}U \right] \epsilon^2 \d\Omega \\
  &= \oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} \left(ik\epsilon U - U - \epsilon\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}U \right)e^{ik\epsilon} \d\Omega
\end{align*}
$$

Letting $\epsilon \to 0$ gives

$$
  \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} \left(ik\epsilon U - U - \epsilon\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}U \right)e^{ik\epsilon} \d\Omega = -\oint_\mathcal{S_\epsilon} U \d\Omega = -4\pi U(\mathbf{r})
$$

Inserting back into eq. 1 gives Kirchoff's integral theorem

$$
\begin{align*}
  U(\mathbf{r}) &= \frac{1}{4\pi} \oint_\mathcal{S}\left( U \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \frac{e^{iks}}{s} - \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} U \right) \d S \\
  &= \frac{1}{4\pi} \oint_\mathcal{S}\left[ U\nabla\left( \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \right) - \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\nabla U \right]\cdot \, \d\mathbf{S}
\end{align*}
$$

## Kirchhoff's diffraction formula

The field distribution of a monochromatic spherical wave emitted from a point source $P_0$ is given by

$$
  U(r) = \frac{ae^{ikr}}{r}
$$

Since the intensity of the wave falls of as the inverse square of the distance, the amplitude falls off as the inverse of the distance. 

The wave amplitude at an observation point $P$ can be found by applying Kirchhoff's integral theorem to the closed surface formed by the intersection of a sphere with the screen.

$$
  U(P) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \left[\int_\Sigma + \int_{S_1} + \int_{S_2} \right] \left( U \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \frac{e^{iks}}{s} - \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\frac{\partial U}{\partial n} \right)\d S
$$

To solve the equation, Kirchhoff's boundary conditions are applied, assuming
1. The values of $U$ and $\frac{\partial U_\Sigma}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}$ in the aperture area $\Sigma$ are the same as when the screen is not present
2. The values of $U$ and $\frac{\partial U_\Sigma}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}$ are zero on the screen $S_1$. This implies that $U$ is discontinuous at the edge of the aperture. A solution of the wave equation that vanishes on any finite surface element, must also vanish in all space. This boundary condition therefore implies that the fields are zero everywhere behind the aperture, which is contradicts reality.

The first assumption implies

$$
\begin{gather*}
  U_\Sigma = \frac{ae^{ikr}}{r} \\
  \frac{\partial U_\Sigma}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} = \nabla U_\Sigma \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{ae^{ikr}}{r}\left(ik - \frac{1}{r}\right) \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r})
\end{gather*}
$$

At $Q$, we also have

$$
  \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \left( \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \right) = \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\left(ik - \frac{1}{s}\right) \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{s})
$$

It is assumed that the distance between observation point $P$ and the integral area, and the dimension of the aperture are much greater than the wavelength $\lambda$. Thus the inverse terms $\frac{1}{r}$ and $\frac{1}{s}$ are negligable compared with the wavenumber $k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$.

In the limit $R \to \infty$, it can be shown that the integral over the hemisphere $S_2$ vanishes. In this limit

$$
  \lim_{R \to \infty} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \left( \frac{e^{ikR}}{R} \right) = \lim_{R \to \infty} \frac{e^{ikR}}{R}\left(ik - \frac{1}{R}\right) = \frac{ik}{R}e^{ikR}
$$

and the integral becomes

$$
  \iint_{S_2} \left(\frac{e^{ikR}}{R} \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} - U ik \frac{e^{ikR}}{R} \right) \d S_2 = \iint_{S_2} e^{ikr} \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} - ikU \right) R \d\Omega = 0
$$

where $\d\Omega$ is the solid angle given by $\d S_2 = R^2 \d\Omega$. The quantity $|RG|$ is uniformly bounded on $S_2$ and the integral will vanish as $R$ becomes arbitrarily large, provided the amplitude has the property

$$
  \lim_{R\to\infty} R \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} - ikU \right) = 0
$$

which is known as the Sommerfeld radiation condition.

Given that the Sommerfeld radiation condition is satisfied, the amplitude at $P$ becomes

$$
  U(P) = -\frac{ia}{2\lambda}\iint_{\Sigma} \frac{e^{ik(r+s)}}{rs} \left[ \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}) - \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{s}) \right] \d\sigma
$$

which is known as Kirchhoff's diffraction formula.

## Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diffraction formula

Kirchhoff's diffraction theory can be modified with alternative Green's functions that vanish in the aperture area, thus removing the necessity of the inconsistent Kirchhoff boundary conditions.

This can be accomplished by introducing a mirrored source point $P'_0$ on the opposite side of the screen. Suppose the two sources $P_0$ and $P'_0$ are emitting with the same wavelength, but oscillation out phase (phase difference of $\pi$). The Green's function in this case is

$$
  G_{-} (\mathbf{r}) = \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} - \frac{e^{ikr'}}{r'} 
$$

Clearly the function vanishes on the aperture $\Sigma$, leaving the following expression for the field distribution

$$
  U_{-}(P) = -\frac{1}{4\pi}\iint_{\Sigma} U \frac{\partial G_{-}}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma
$$

which is called the first Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solution. On the aperture $r = r'$ and $\cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}) - \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}')$, so that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left.\frac{\partial G_{-}}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}\right|_\Sigma &= \left(ik - \frac{1}{r} \right)\frac{e^{ikr}}{r}\cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}) - \left(ik - \frac{1}{r'} \right)\frac{e^{ikr'}}{r'}\cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}') \\
  &= 2 \left(ik - \frac{1}{r} \right)\frac{e^{ikr}}{r}\cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}) \\
  &= 2 \left.\frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \right|_\Sigma
\end{align*}
$$

where $G$ is the Green's function used in Kirchhoff's diffraction theory. The first Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solution can thus be rewritten as

$$
  U_{-}(P) = -\frac{1}{2\pi}\iint_\Sigma U\frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma
$$

Applying Kirchhoff's integral theorem with the Green's function $G_-$ it follows that (assuming $r \gg \lambda$)

$$
  U_-(P) = \frac{1}{i\lambda} \iint_\Sigma U(\sigma) \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}) \;\d\sigma
$$

For a monochromatic spherical wave

$$
\begin{gather*}
  U(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{a}{r}e^{ikr} \\
  \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} = \left[ik - \frac{1}{r} \right]\frac{e^{ikr}}{r}\cos(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r})
\end{gather*}
$$

we obtain

$$
  U_-(P) = \frac{a}{i\lambda}\iint_\Sigma \frac{e^{ik(r + s)}}{rs}\cos\left(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{s} \right)
$$

which is known as the Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diffraction formula. The formula can be interpreted as the superposition of spherical wavelets emanating from secondary sources within the aperture. This is consistent with the Huygens-Fresnel principle.

### Rayleigh-Sommerfeld's second solution

An alternative and equally valid Green's function is found by allowing the two point sources to oscillate in phase

$$
  G_+ (\mathbf{r}) = \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} + \frac{e^{ikr'}}{r'}
$$

The normal derivative of this function vanishes across the screen and aperture, leading to the second Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solution

$$
  U_{+}(P) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\iint_{\Sigma} G_+ \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma
$$

It can similarly be rewritten in terms of the Green's function used by Kirchhoff

$$
  U_{+}(P) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\iint_\Sigma G\frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma
$$

Applying the Kirchhoff's integral theorem

$$
  U_-(P) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \iint_\Sigma \frac{U(\sigma)}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \;\d\sigma
$$

### Relation to Kirchhoff's diffraction theory

Kirchhoff's solution is the arithmetic average of the two Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solutions

$$
\begin{align*}
  U(P) &= \frac{1}{4\pi} \iint_\Sigma \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}}G - U\frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \right) \d\sigma \\
  &= \frac{1}{2}\left[ \frac{1}{2\pi}\iint_\Sigma G \frac{\partial U}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma - \frac{1}{2\pi}\iint_\Sigma U\frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{n}}} \d\sigma \right] \\
  &= \frac{1}{2}\left[U_+(P) + U_-(P) \right]
\end{align*}
$$

The Rayleigh-Sommerfeld diffraction formula differs from Kirchhoff's through what is known as the obliquity factor $\psi$, which is the angular dependence introduced by the cosine terms. In the most general case

$$
  U(P) = \frac{a}{i\lambda}\iint_\Sigma \frac{e^{ik(r + s)}}{rs}\psi \;\d\sigma
$$

where

$$
  \psi = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}\left[ \cos\left(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r}\right) - \cos\left(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{s}\right) \right] &\quad \textrm{Kirchhoff theory} \\ \cos\left(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{s} \right) &\quad \textrm{First Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solution} \\
  -\cos\left(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{r} \right) &\quad \textrm{Second Rayleigh-Sommerfeld solution}
  \end{cases}
$$

## Fresnel diffraction (near field)

The Fresnel approximation to the Huygens-Fresnel principle is derived by applying the binomial expansion of the aperture distance

$$
\begin{align*}
  r &= \sqrt{z^2 + \left(x - \xi \right)^2 + \left( y - \eta \right)^2} = \sqrt{z + \rho^2} \\
  &= z\sqrt{1 + \frac{\rho^2}{z^2} ^2} \\
  &= z\left[ 1 + \frac{\rho^2}{2z^2} - \frac{1}{8}\left(\frac{\rho^2}{z^2} \right)^2 + \dots \right]
\end{align*}
$$

The distance can be approximated to first order if the higher order terms have negligable contributions to the complex exponential factor $e^{ikr}$. In other words, the higher order terms have to be much smaller than the period of the complex exponential

$$
\begin{gather*}
  k\frac{\rho^4}{8z^3} \ll 2\pi \\
  \frac{\rho^4}{z^3 \lambda} \ll 8 \\
  \frac{\rho^4}{\lambda^4} = \frac{1}{\lambda^4} \left[ \left(x - \xi \right)^2 + \left( y - \eta \right)^2 \right]^2 \ll 8\frac{z^3}{\lambda^3}
\end{gather*}
$$

For applications involving optical wavelengths

$$
\begin{align*}
  \lambda &\ll z \\
  \lambda &\ll \rho
\end{align*}
$$

The Fresnel approximation will thus generally hold if $\rho \ll z$. The resulting expression for the field at the observation plane becomes

$$
    U(x, y) = \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} \iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} U\left(\xi, \eta \right) e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left[ \left(x - \xi \right)^2 + \left( y - \eta \right)^2  \right]} \d\xi \;\d\eta
$$

The integral can be expressed as a convolution

$$
  U(x, y) = \iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} U\left(\xi, \eta \right) h\left(x - \xi, y - \eta \right) \d\xi \;\d\eta
$$

where the convolution kernel is

$$
  h(x, y) = \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} e^{i\frac{k}{2z}\left(x^2 + y^2 \right)}
$$

The integral can also be expressed as a Fourier transform

$$
\begin{align*}
  U(x, y) &= \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left(x^2 + y^2 \right)} \iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} \left[ U\left(\xi, \eta \right) e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left(\xi^2 + \eta^2 \right)} \right]e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{\lambda z}\left(x\xi + y\eta \right)} \d\xi \;\d\eta \\
  &= \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left(x^2 + y^2 \right)} \mathcal{F}\left\{ U\left(\xi, \eta \right) e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{\lambda z}\left(x\xi + y\eta \right)} \right\}
\end{align*}
$$

## Fraunhofer diffraction (far field)

The Fraunhofer approximation is more stringent than the Fresnel approximation in that it also requires

$$
  z \gg \frac{k}{2}\max{\left(\xi^2 + \eta^2 \right)} = \frac{\pi}{\lambda} \max{\left(\xi^2 + \eta^2 \right)}
$$

If this is satisfied then the quadratic phase factor in the Fourier formulation of Fresnel diffraction is approximately unity over the entire aperture

$$
  U(x, y) = \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left(x^2 + y^2 \right)} \iint_{-\infty}^\infty U(\xi, \eta) e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{\lambda z}\left(x\xi + y\eta \right)} \d\xi \;\d\eta
$$

The integral can be expressed as Fourier transform of the aperture distribution evaluated at frequencies

$$
\begin{align*}
  f_x = \frac{x}{\lambda z} &= \frac{l}{\lambda} \\
  f_y = \frac{y}{\lambda z} &= \frac{m}{\lambda} \\
\end{align*}
$$

The integral can be rewritten as

$$
  U(x, y) = \frac{e^{ikz}}{i\lambda z} e^{i\frac{k}{2z} \left(x^2 + y^2 \right)} \mathcal{F}\left\{ U(\xi, \eta) \right\}_{f_x f_y}
$$
