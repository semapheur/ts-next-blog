# Electromagnetic radiation

## Time-harmonic Maxwell equations

The fundamental electromagnetic equations in differential form are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\mathbf{E} =& -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \tag{\label{equation-12}} \\
  \nabla\times\mathbf{H} =& \frac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{J}_\text{t} \tag{\label{equation-13}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} =& \rho_\text{t} \tag{\label{equation-14}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} =& 0 \tag{\label{equation-15}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_\text{t} =& -\frac{\partial\rho_\text{t}}{\partial t} \tag{\label{equation-16}}
\end{align*}
$$

where the first four are Maxwell's equations and the last is the continuity equation.

If the impressed sources $\rho_\text{t} (\mathbf{r}, t)$ and $\mathbf{J}_\text{t} (\mathbf{r}, t)$ vary sinusoidally at radian frequency $\omega > 0$, then, by linearity of Maxwell's equation, all fields will respond sinusoidally at the same frequency. The fundamental electromagnetic equations are considerably simplified by introducing phasor fields of the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) =& \Re(\tilde{\mathbf{E}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{j\omega t}) \\
  \mathbf{H}(\mathbf{r}, t) =& \Re(\tilde{\mathbf{H}}(\mathbf{r}) e^{j\omega t})
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-11}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the phasor quantities $\tilde{\mathbf{E}}$, $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}$, $\tilde{\mathbf{D}}$, $\tilde{\mathbf{B}}$, are complex vector fields and $\tilde{\rho}_\text{T}$ is a complex scalar field, all depending only on spatial coordinates. Substituting $\eqref{equation-11}$ into $\eqref{equation-12}$-$\eqref{equation-16}$ and eliminating the phase factors $e^{j\omega t}$ yields the time-harmonic Maxwell system

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{E}} =& -j\omega\tilde{\mathbf{B}} \tag{\label{equation-17}} \\
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& j\omega\mathbf{D} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}}_\text{t} \tag{\label{equation-18}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{D}} =& \rho_\text{t} \tag{\label{equation-19}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{B}} =& 0 \tag{\label{equation-20}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{J}}_\text{t} =& -j\omega\tilde{\rho}_\text{t} \tag{\label{equation-21}}
\end{align*}
$$

If multiple frequency is present, the full time-domain solution can be recovered by inverse Fourier transform of phasor solutions.

The total current density $\tilde{\mathbf{J}}$ decomposes into a conduction current and an impressed current

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{J}}_\text{t} = \sigma\tilde{\mathbf{E}} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}}
\tag{\label{equation-22}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\sigma \geq 0$ is the conductity. The current density $\sigma\tilde{\mathbf{E}}$ is induced on a nearby conductor due to the fields created by the source $\mathbf{J}_s$. In addition to conductivity $\sigma$, a material is further characterized by permittivity $\varepsilon$ and permeability $\mu$. For isotropic, homogenous media we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{D}} =& \varepsilon\tilde{\mathbf{E}} \tag{\label{equation-23}} \\
  \tilde{\mathbf{B}} =& \mu\tilde{\mathbf{B}} \tag{\label{equation-24}}
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting $\eqref{equation-22}$ and $\eqref{equation-23}$ into $\eqref{equation-18}$ gives

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& j\omega\left(\varepsilon + \frac{\sigma}{j\omega} \right) \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}} \\
  =& j\omega\varepsilon' \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-25}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where we have introduce the complex permittivity $\varepsilon' = \varepsilon - j(\sigma/\omega)$. Let $\rho$ be the source charge corresponding to the source current density $\tilde{\mathbf{J}}$. Then using $\eqref{equation-22}$ to $\eqref{equation-25}$ in $\eqref{equation-17}$ and $\eqref{equation-19}$ to $\eqref{equation-11}$, and repeating $\eqref{equation-25}$ permits Maxwell's equations to be expressed in terms of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{E}} =& -j\omega\mu\tilde{\mathbf{H}} \tag{\label{equation-26}} \\
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& -j\omega\varepsilon' \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}} \tag{\label{equation-27}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{E}} =& \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon'} \tag{\label{equation-28}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& 0 \tag{\label{equation-29}} \\
  \nabla\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{J}} =& -j\omega\rho \tag{\label{equation-30}}
\end{align*}
$$

Introducing a fictitious magnetic current density $\mathbf{M}$, we can rewrite $\eqref{equation-26}$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{E}} = -j\omega\mu\tilde{\mathbf{H}} - \mathbf{M}
\tag{\label{equation-31}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Magnetic currents are useful as equivalent sources that replace complicated electric fields. 

Across an interface between two homogenous media with constitutive parameters $\varepsilon_k$, $\mu_k$, $\sigma_k$ for $k = 1,2$, the time-harmonic boundary conditions are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \unitvec{n}\times(\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_2 - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_1) =& \mathbf{J}_s \\
  (\tilde{\mathbf{E}}_2 - \tilde{\mathbf{E}}_1) \times\unitvec{n} =& \mathbf{M}_s
\end{align*}
$$

where $\unitvec{n}$ is the unit normal directed from medium $1$ into medium $2$, and $\mathbf{J}_s$ and $\mathbf{M}_s$ denote electric and magnetic surface currents. The magnetic suface current $\mathbf{M}_s$ is zero unless an equivalent magnetic current sheet is used. The cross product with the unit normal form the tangential components to the boundary

$$
\begin{align*}
  H_{\text{tan},2} =& H_{\text{tan},1} + J_s \\
  E_{\text{tan},2} =& E_{\text{tan},1} + M_s
\end{align*}
$$

These boundary conditions are derived from the integral form of $\eqref{equation-27}$ and $\eqref{equation-31}$. If one side is a perfect electrical conductor, the boundary conditions become

$$
\begin{align*}
  H_\text{tan} =& J_s \\
  E_\text{tan} =& 0
\end{align*}
$$

## Poynting's theorem

Consider a volume $\nu \subseteq\R^3$ bounded by a closed surface $s = \partial\nu$. By Poynting's theorem the complex power balance is

$$
  P_s = P_f + \braket{P_d} + j2\omega(\braket{W_m} - \braket{W_e})
$$

where
- $P_s$ is the complex power delivered by impressed sources
- $P_f$ is the outward complex power flux
- $\braket{P_d}$ is the time-averaged dissipated power 
- $\braket{W_m}$, $\braket{W_e}$ are time-averaged stored magnetic and electric energies

The complex power flowing out of through the closed surface $s$ is given by

$$
  P_f = \frac{1}{2} \oiint_s \tilde{\mathbf{E}} \times \tilde{\mathbf{H}}^* \cdot \d\mathbf{s}
$$

where $\d\mathbf{s} = \d s \unitvec{n}$ and $\unitvec{n}$ is the unit normal to the surface directed out from the surface. The integrand is defined as the Poynting vector

$$
  \mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\mathbf{E}} \times\tilde{\mathbf{H}}^*
$$

which is a power density with units $\text{W}/\text{m}^2$.

The dissipated power and stored energies are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \braket{P_d} =& \frac{1}{2} \iiint_\nu \sigma |\tilde{E}|^2 \;\d\nu \\
  \braket{W_m} =& \frac{1}{2} \iiint_\nu \frac{1}{2} \mu |\tilde{H}|^2 \;\d\nu \\
  \braket{W_e} =& \frac{1}{2} \iiint_\nu \frac{1}{2} \varepsilon' |\tilde{E}|^2 \;\d\nu
\end{align*}
$$

If the source distribution is known, the complex source power can be computed directly from

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_s = -\frac{1}{2} \iiint_\nu \tilde{\mathbf{E}} \cdot\tilde{\mathbf{J}}^* \;\d\nu
\tag{\label{equation-71}}
\end{equation*}
$$

with an additional contribution $\frac{1}{2} \iiint_\nu \tilde{\mathbf{H}}^* \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{M}} \;\d\nu$ if magnetic current density is present.

The real power flowing through an arbitray surface $s$ is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \braket{P} = \Re\left(\iint_s \mathbf{S} \cdot \d\mathbf{s} \right)
\tag{\label{equation-84}}
\end{equation*}
$$

## Radiation fields

The antenna problem consist of solving for the electromagnetic fields that are created by an impressed current distribution $\tilde{\mathbf{J}}$. The fields $\tilde{\mathbf{E}}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}$ are determined by the curl equations $\eqref{equation-26}$ and $\eqref{equation-27}$, which are two coupled linear first-order differential equation. In order to simplify the solution for $\tilde{\mathbf{E}}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}$ with a given $\tilde{\mathbf{J}}$, we introduce the scalar potential $\Phi$ and the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$.

By $\eqref{equation-29}$, the magnetic field is divergenceless allowing us to define $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}$ as the curl of a magnetic vector potential $\mathbf{A}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} = \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times\mathbf{A}
\tag{\label{equation-32}}
\end{equation*}
$$

since $\nabla\cdot\nabla\times\mathbf{A} \equiv 0$ for any $\mathbf{A}$. The scalar potential is derived by substituting $\eqref{equation-32}$ into $\eqref{equation-26}$ resulting in

$$
  \nabla\times(\tilde{\mathbf{E}} + j\omega\mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{0}
$$

Since the expression in the parentheses is curl free, it is a conservative field and behaves as a static electric field. The electric scalar potential $\Phi$ is defined from

$$
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + j\omega\mathbf{A} = -\nabla\Phi
$$
 
which satisfies $\nabla \times (-\nabla\Phi) \equiv \mathbf{0}$. The electric field can be written

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} = -j\omega\mathbf{A} -\nabla\Phi
\tag{\label{equation-33}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Substituting $\eqref{equation-32}$ into $\eqref{equation-27}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times\nabla\times\mathbf{A} \\
  =& j\omega\varepsilon' \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + \tilde{\mathbf{J}}
\end{align*}
$$

Using the vector identity $\eqref{equation-63}$ and inserting $\eqref{equation-33}$ we obtain

$$
  \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = j\omega\mu\varepsilon' \Phi + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} + \mu\tilde{\mathbf{J}}
$$

or

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} + \omega^2 \mu\varepsilon' \mathbf{A} - \nabla(j\omega\mu\varepsilon' \Phi + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}) = -\mu\tilde{\mathbf{J}}
\tag{\label{equation-34}}
\end{equation*}
$$

To eliminate the gradient term of $\eqref{equation-34}$ we can use the Lorentz condition

$$
  \nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}= -j\omega\mu\varepsilon' \Phi
$$

so that $\eqref{equation-34}$ simplifies to

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} + \omega^2 \mu\varepsilon' \mathbf{A} = -\mu\tilde{\mathbf{J}}
\tag{\label{equation-35}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which is known as the vector wave equation. Solving for $\mathbf{A}$ we can determine the electric field from

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} = -j\omega\mathbf{A} - j\frac{\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A})}{\omega\mu\varepsilon'}
\tag{\label{equation-64}}
\end{equation*}
$$

A more cumbersome approach would be to solve the scalar wave equation

$$
  \nabla^2 \Phi + \omega^2 \mu\varepsilon' \Phi = -\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon'}
$$

in addition to the vector wave equation.

From $\eqref{equation-35}$ we recognize the coefficient $\beta = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon'}$ as the phase constant for a propagating wave. By definition a wave shift $2\pi$ radians of phase for one full cycle, corresponding to one wavelength $\lambda$, so that

$$
  \beta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}
$$

since $\lambda = c/f$ and $\omega = 2\pi f$, we have

$$
  \beta = \frac{2\pi f}{c} = \frac{\omega}{c}
$$

implying that $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon'}}$.

For a point source, the wave equation takes the form

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \nabla^2 \psi + \beta^2 = -\delta(x) \delta(y) \delta(z)
\tag{\label{equation-36}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\psi$ is the response to a point source at the origin, and $\delta$ is the Dirac delta function. Despite the infinitesimal extent of a point source, its associated current has a direction. If the point source current is taken as $z$-directed, then

$$
  \psi = A_z
$$

Because the point source is zero except at the origin $\eqref{equation-36}$ becomes

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \nabla^2 \psi + \beta^2 \psi = 0
\tag{\label{equation-37}}
\end{equation*}
$$

away from the origin. This is the complex scalar wave equation, also known as the Helmholtz equation. Due to spherical symmetry, the Laplacian is written in spherical coordinates and $\psi$ has only radial dependence. The two solutions to $\eqref{equation-37}$ are $e^{\pm j\beta r} / r$, which correspond to waves propagating radially outward and inward, respectively. The physically meaningful solution is the one for waves traveling away from the point source, resulting in

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \psi = \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r}
\tag{\label{equation-38}}
\end{equation*}
$$

With respect to an observation point $P$ at an arbitrary position $\mathbf{r}'$, the position of the point source is $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r}' - \mathbf{r}$, so that $\eqref{equation-38}$ is given in terms of $R = |\mathbf{r}' - \mathbf{r}|$, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \psi = \frac{e^{-j\beta R}}{4\pi R}
\tag{\label{equation-39}}
\end{equation*}
$$

If we consider the source to be a collection of point sources weighted by the distribution $\tilde{\mathbf{J}}$, the response $\mathbf{A}$ is a sum of the point source responses of $\eqref{equation-39}$. This is expressed by the integal over the source volume $\nu'$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{A} = \iiint_{\nu'} \mu \tilde{\mathbf{J}} \frac{e^{-j\beta R}}{4\pi R} \;\d\nu'
\tag{\label{equation-40}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Having determined $\mathbf{A}$, we can compute $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}$ from $\eqref{equation-32}$. The electric field is then given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{1}{j\omega\varepsilon'} (\nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} - \tilde{\mathbf{J}})
\tag{\label{equation-42}}
\end{equation*}
$$

## The ideal dipole

An ideal (Hertzian) dipole of length $l$ is a radiating element that is electrically small compared to the wavelength, i.e. $l \ll \lambda$. It is ideal in the sense that the current is uniform in both magnitude and phase along its length, with no end effects. Any practical antenna can be decomposed into infinitesimal current elements, which can be modeled as dipoles. The fields from the antenna are given as a suporposition of fields from the the ideal dipoles. For instance, a short center-fed wire antenna has a sinusoidal current tapering to zero at the ends, but has the same radiation pattern as an ideal dipole.

Consider a current element of length $\Delta z$ along the $z$-axis, centered at the origin, carrying a uniform current $I$. The current density is

$$
  \tilde{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}') = I \delta(x') \delta(y') \unitvec(z), \; z' \in\left(-\frac{\Delta z}{2}, \frac{\Delta z}{2}\right)
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-40}$ simplifies the volume integral for the vector potential to the one-dimensional integral

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{A} =& \unitvec{z} \mu I \int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(x') \;\d x' \int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(y') \;\d y' \int_{-\Delta z/2}^{\Delta z/2} \frac{e^{-j\beta R}}{4\pi R} \;\d z' \\
  =& \unitvec{z} \mu I \int_{-\Delta z/2}^{\Delta z/2} \frac{e^{-j\Beta R}}{4\pi R} \;\d z' \tag{\label{equation-41}}
\end{align*}
$$

For small $\Delta z \ll \lambda$, we approximate $R = |\mathbf{r}' - \mathbf{r}| \approx |\mathbf{r}| = r$. With this approximation, the integral $\eqref{equation-41}$ evaluates to

$$
  \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu I e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \Delta z \unitvec{z} = A_z \unitvec{z}
$$

This is exact of a point current element and an adequate approximation for a small but finite current element.

The magnetic field of the ideal dipole is given by

$$
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} = \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times\mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times (A_z \unitvec{z})
$$

Applying the vector identity $\eqref{equation-59}$, we get

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& \frac{1}{\mu} (\nabla A_z) \times\unitvec{z} + \frac{1}{\mu} A_z \underbrace{(\nabla\times\unitvec{z})}_{=\mathbf{0}} \\
  =& \frac{1}{\mu} (\nabla A_z) \times\unitvec{z} = \nabla\left(\frac{I\Delta z e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r}\right) \times\unitvec{z}
\end{align*}
$$

Taking the gradient of $A_z$, which only depends on $r$, in spherical coordinates using $\eqref{equation-60}$, we get

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \right) \unitvec{r}\times\unitvec{z} \\
  =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \left(\frac{-j\beta e^{-j\beta r}}{r} - \frac{e^{j\beta r}}{r^2} \right) \unitvec{r}\times\unitvec{z} \tag{\label{equation-53}}
\end{align*}
$$

From the unit vector representation $\eqref{equation-62}$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  \unitvec{r}\times\unitsym{z} =& \unitvec{r} \times (\unitvec{r} \cos(\theta) - \unitsym{\theta} \sin(\theta)) \\
  =& -\unitsym{\phi} \sin(\theta)
\end{align*}
$$

Applying this identity to $\eqref{equation-53}$, we get

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} = \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \left(\frac{j\beta}{r} + \frac{1}{r^2}\right) e^{-j\beta r} \sin(\phi) \unitsym{\phi}
\tag{\label{equation-43}}
\end{equation*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-42}$, we find the electric field with $\tilde{\mathbf{J}} = \mathbf{0}$ as

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \left(\frac{j\omega\mu}{r} + \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon'}} \frac{1}{r^2} + \frac{1}{j\omega\varepsilon' r^3} \right) e^{-j\beta r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta} \\
  &+ \frac{I\Delta z}{2\pi} \left(\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon'}} \frac{1}{r^2} + \frac{1}{j\omega\varepsilon'} \frac{1}{r^3} \right) e^{-j\beta r} \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-44}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Factoring out $\beta = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon'}$ and $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon'}$, the fields $\eqref{equation-43}$ and $\eqref{equation-44}$ can be refactored as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}} = \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} j\beta \left(1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r}\right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\phi) \unitsym{\phi} \tag{\label{equation-45}}
\end{equation*}
$$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} j\omega\mu \left(1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2} \right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta} \\
  &+ \frac{I\Delta z}{2\pi} \eta \left(\frac{1}{r} - j\frac{1}{\beta r^2} \right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-46}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In the far-field region $r \gg \lambda$ of the dipole, corresponding to $\beta r \gg 1$, all terms with inverse powers of $\beta r$ are negligible in $\eqref{equation-45}$ and $\eqref{equation-46}$, and the fields converge asymptotically to

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}}^\text{ff} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} j\beta \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\theta) \unitvec{\phi} \tag{\label{equation-47}} \\
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}}^\text{ff} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} j\omega\mu \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\theta) \unitvec{\theta} \tag{\label{equation-48}}
\end{align*}
$$

These are called radiation fields of the ideal dipole. The ratio of the electric and magnetic far-field components is

$$
  \frac{E_\theta^\text{ff}}{H_\phi^\text{ff}} = \frac{\omega\mu}{\beta} = \frac{\omega\mu}{\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon'}} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon'}} = \eta
$$

where $\eta$ is the intrinsic impedance of the medium. The complex power density of the radiation fields $\eqref{equation-47}$ and $\eqref{equation-48}$ is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \mathbf{S} =& \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\mathbf{E}} \times\tilde{\mathbf{H}}^* \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi}\right)^2 \omega\mu\beta \frac{\sin^2 (\theta)}{r^2} \left[j e^{-j\beta r} \unitsym{\theta} \times (-j e^{j\beta r}) \unitsym{\phi}\right] \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi}\right)^2 \omega\mu\beta \frac{\sin^2 (\theta)}{r^2} \unitvec{r}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-49}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The radiated power flux through a sphere of radius $r$ surrounding the ideal dipole is

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  P_f =& \iint \mathbf{S} \cdot \d\mathbf{s} \\
  =& \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi}\right)^2 \omega\mu\beta \int_0^{2\pi} \d\phi \int_0^\pi \sin^3 (\theta) \;\d\theta \\
  =& \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi}\right)^2 \omega\mu\beta 2\pi \frac{4}{3} \\
  =& \frac{\omega\mu\beta}{12\pi} (I\Delta z)^2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-50}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In the near-field region $r \ll\lambda$ of the dipole, corresponding to $\beta r \ll 1$, the terms with the largest inverse powers of $\beta r$ dominate in $\eqref{equation-45}$ and $\eqref{equation-46}$, giving

$$
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\mathbf{H}}^\text{nf} =& \frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r^2} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\phi} \tag{\label{equation-51}} \\
  \tilde{\mathbf{E}}^\text{nf} =& -j\eta \frac{I\Delta z}{2\pi\beta} \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r^3} \left(\frac{1}{2} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta} + \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r}\right) \tag{\label{equation-52}}
\end{align*}
$$

The magnetic near-field in $\eqref{equation-51}$ decays as $r^{-2}$, which is analogous to an induction field generated from a steady or slowly oscillating current. The electric near-field decays as $r^{-3}$, which is analogous to the field of an electrostatic dipole field formed by charges $q$ and $-q$ spaced $\Delta z$ apart. In the near-field, the electric field components $E_\theta^\text{nf}$ and $E_r^\text{nf}$ are in-phase, but are in phase-quadrature ($\pm \pi/4$ out of phase) with the magnetic field component $H_\phi^\text{nf}$. This phase relationship becomes explicit by evaluating the near-field Poynting vector

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \mathbf{S}^\text{nf} =& \frac{1}{2} [E_\theta^\text{nf} H_\phi^{\text{nf}*} \unitvec{r} - E_r^\text{nf} H_\phi^{\text{nf}*} \unitsym{\theta}] \\
  =& -\frac{j\eta}{2\beta} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \right)^2 \frac{1}{r^5} (\sin^2 (\theta) \unitvec{r} - \sin(2\theta) \unitsym{\theta})
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-52}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Because $\mathbf{S}^\text{nf}$ is purely imaginary, the near-field region exhibits no net time-averaged radial power flow. The radiation fields, in contrast, are in-phase giving a real-valued Poynting vector that is radially directed. The imaginary power density corresponds to standing waves, rather than traveling waves associated with radiation, and indicate stored energy as in a reactive device. 

The quadrature phase relationship between between $\tilde{\mathbf{E}}^\text{nf}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}^\text{nf}$ and indicates that energy is periodically interchanged between the electric and magnetic near-fields. At instants when dipole charge distribution is maximal the electric field dominate; a quarter-period, when the current is maximal at the dipole center, the magnetic field magnetic field dominates. This oscillatory exchange reflects stored energy.

The imaginary power density in the near field is manifested by a reactive component of the antenna input impedance. The real part of the input impedance represents radiation if ohmic losses on the antenna structure can be neglected, while the imaginary part corresponds to stored energy.

The radiated power density $\eqref{equation-49}$ and the reactive power density $\eqref{equation-52}$ are both maximum for $\theta = \pi/4$. The characteristic distance at which the magnitudes of the radiated and reactive powers are equal is 

$$
  r = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

From $\eqref{equation-49}$ and $\eqref{equation-52}$ we find that the ratio of radial power densities magnitues $|S_r / S_r^\text{nf}|$ is given by

$$
  \frac{S_r}{|S_r^\text{nf}|} = \frac{\omega\mu\beta^2 r^3}{\eta}
$$

Since $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\epsilon}$ and $\beta = \omega\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}$ we find that

$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{S_r}{S_r^\text{nf}} =& \omega\mu\beta^2 r^3 \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon}{\mu}} \\
  =& \underbrace{\omega\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}}_{\beta} \beta^2 r^3 \\
  =& (\beta r)^3
\end{align*}
$$

The radial power density magnitues are equal when $|S_r / S_r^\text{nf}| = (\beta r)^3 = 1$ or $r = 1/\beta$. Since $\beta = 2\pi/\lambda$, it follows that

$$
  r = \frac{1}{\beta} = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi}
$$
</details>

The region inside this radius, reactive power dominates, is known as the radiansphere. At large distances $r \gg \lambda/2\pi$, called the far-field region, radiated power dominates. In the far-field region, the angular field distribution (radiation pattern) is independent of distance from the dipole.

From the fields $\eqref{equation-45}$ and $\eqref{equation-46}$, the wave impedance $Z(r) = E_\theta / E_\phi$ for the ideal dipole is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z(r) =& \frac{\omega\mu}{\beta} \frac{1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2}}{1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r}} \\
  =& \eta \left(1 - \frac{\frac{1}{(\beta r)^2}}{1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r}} \right) \\
  =& \eta \left(1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r(j\beta r + 1)}\right)
\end{align*}
$$

In the far-field region $\beta r \gg 1$ the fraction term in the parenthesis converges to

$$
  \lim_{x\to\infty} \left|\frac{1}{jx(jx + 1)}\right| \leq \frac{1}{(x)^2} = 0,\; x = \beta r
$$

so that $Z(r) \to \eta$ when $\beta r \gg 1$.

The magnitude error of $E_\theta$ relative to the far-field value $E_\theta^\text{ff}$ is given by

$$
  \epsilon_{E_\theta} = \frac{|E_\theta| - |E_\theta^\text{ff}|}{|E_\theta^\text{ff}|}
$$

Noting that

$$
  |E_\theta| = |E_\theta^\text{ff}| \cdot \left|1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2} \right|
$$

we find

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \epsilon_{E_\theta} =& \left|1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2} \right| - 1 \\
  =& \sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2} + \frac{1}{(\beta r)^4}}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-70}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Computing the magnitude with $x = \beta r$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left|1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2} \right| =& \left|1 - \frac{1}{(\beta r)^2 -  \frac{j}{\beta r}} \right| \\
  =& \sqrt{\left(1 - \frac{1}{x^2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^2} \\
  =& \sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{1}{x^4}}
\end{align*}
$$

## Radiation patterns

A radiation pattern is a graphical representation of the far-field radiation characteristics of an antenna. A general property for all antennas is that their radiated fields decay with distance as spherical waves. The angular variation of these fields, however, depends on the antenna's geometry, size and construction.

Radiation patterns are determined either by calculation or direct measurement of the fields radiated by the antenna. A pattern taken in a plane containing the electric field vector is called an *E-plane pattern*, while a pattern taken in the plane orthogonal to the E-plane, which contains the magnetic field, is called an *H-plane pattern*. For linearly polarized antennas, these two are collectively referred to as principal plane patterns. Patterns that are uniform in one plane, e.g. independent of the azimuthal angle, are called *omnidirectional patterns*.

### Radiation from line currents

Consider a filament of current along the $z$-axis and located near the origin, known as a line source. The current distribution is given by

$$
  \tilde{\mathbf{J}} (\mathbf{r}') = I(z') \delta(x') \delta(y') \unitvec{z}
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-40}$ results in a one-dimensional integral for the vector potential

$$
\begin{equation*}
  A_z = \mu \int I(z') \frac{e^{-j\beta R}}{4\pi R} \;\d z'
\tag{\label{equation-54}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Due to rotational symmetry of the source about the $z$-axis, the radiation fields are independent of $\phi$. We therefore restrict attention to the $yz$-plan corresponding to $\phi = \pi/4$ for clarity. In spherical coordinates, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
  r^2 =& y^2 + z^2 \tag{\label{equation-55}} \\
  z =& r\cos(\theta) \tag{\label{equation-56}} \\
  y =& r\sin(\theta) \tag{\label{equation-57}}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\mathbf{r} = y\unitvec{y} + z\unitvec{z}$ and $\mathbf{r}' = z' \unitvec{z}$, we have $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}' = y\unitvec{y} + (z - z')\unitvec{z}$, and then

$$
\begin{align*}
  R =& \sqrt{y^2 + (z - z')^2} \\
  =& \sqrt{y^2 + z^2 - 2zz' + (z')^2}
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting $\eqref{equation-55}$ and $\eqref{equation-56}$, by switching to spherical coordinates, we get

$$
  R = \sqrt{r^2 + [-2r \cos(\theta) z' + (z')^2]}
$$

Applying binomial expansion, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  R =& \sum_{k=0}^\infty \binom{1/2}{k} (r^2)^{1/2 - k} [-2r \cos(\theta) z' + (z')^2]^k \\
  =& r - z' \cos(\theta) + \frac{(z')^2 \sin^2 (\theta)}{2r} + \frac{(z')^3 \sin^2 (\theta) \cos(\theta)}{2r^2} + \dots \tag{\label{equation-58}}
\end{align*}
$$

In the far-field region, $r \gg |z'|$, we make the following approximation in $\eqref{equation-54}$
- **Amplitude factor:** Since variations of $R$ relative to $r$ are small small we approximate $R^{-1} \approx r^{-1}$.
- **Phase factor:** Since $e^{-j\beta R}$ is oscillatory, small path length variations $\Delta R \sim z' \cos(\theta)$ can produce significant phase shifts of order $\beta z' \cos(\theta)$. Thus, the first-order variation in $R$ must be retained, i.e $R \approx r - z' \cos(\theta)$. 

With these far field approximations, $\eqref{equation-54}$ reduces to

$$
\begin{align*}
  A_z =& \mu \in I(z') \frac{e^{-j\beta(r - z' \cos\theta)}}{4\pi r} \;\d z' \\
  =& \mu \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \underbrace{\int I(z') e^{j\beta z' \cos \theta} \;\d z'}_{= F(\theta)} \tag{\label{equation-91}}
\end{align*}
$$

The integral $F(\theta)$ describes a Fourier transform of the current distribution $I(z')$, and is called the radiation integral or diffraction integral. From $\eqref{equation-32}$ the magnetic field is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{H} =& \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times\mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times (A_z \unitvec{z}) \\
  =& \frac{1}{\mu} \nabla\times (-A_z \sin(\theta) \unitsym(\theta) + A_z \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r})
\end{align*}
$$

where $\eqref{equation-62}$ was used. Taking the curl of $A_z$, depending on $r$ and $\theta$, in spherical coordinates using $\eqref{equation-61}$, leads to

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{H} = \unitsym{\phi} \frac{1}{\mu r} \left[\frac{\partial}{\partial r} (-rA_z \sin(\theta)) - \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} (A_z \cos(\theta)) \right]
\tag{\label{equation-68}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The radial derivative evaluates to

$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial}{\partial r} (-rA_z \sin(\theta)) =& \mu \frac{-\sin(\theta)}{4\pi} F(\theta) \frac{\partial}{\partial r} e^{-j\beta r} \\
  =& j\beta r \sin(\theta) \mu \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} F(\theta) \\
  =& j\beta r \sin(\theta) A_z
\end{align*}
$$

and the angular derivative can be refactored as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} (A_z \cos(\theta)) =& \cos(\theta) \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial\theta} - A_z \sin(\theta) \\
  =& \mu\frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \cos(\theta) \frac{\d F(\theta)}{\d \theta} - A_z \sin(\theta)
\end{align*}
$$

Inserting the partial derivatives into $\eqref{equation-68}$ leads to

$$
  \mathbf{H} = \unitsym{\phi} \frac{1}{\mu} \left[j\beta \sin(\theta) A_z + \frac{\sin(\theta)}{r} A_z - \frac{\cos(\theta)}{\mu} \frac{\mu \cos(\theta) e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r^2} \frac{\d F(\theta)}{\d\theta} \right]
$$

In the far-field region, i.e. $\beta r \gg 1$, the $r^{-1}$ and $r^{-2}$ terms become negligible, so that in leading-order the magnetic field is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{H} = \frac{j\beta}{\mu} \sin(\theta) A_z \unitsym{\phi}
\tag{\label{equation-65}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The electric field is obtained from $\eqref{equation-64}$. Using $\eqref{equation-54}$ in $\eqref{equation-55}$ and retaining only the $r^{-1}$ term under the assumption $\beta r \gg 1$, yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{E}= -j\omega A_\theta \unitsym(\theta) = j\omega \sin(\theta) A_z \unitsym{\theta}
\tag{\label{equation-66}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Note that this result is the portion of the first term of $\eqref{equation-64}$ that is transverse to $\unitvec{r}$ because $-j\omega \mathbf{A} = -j\omega(-A_z \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta} + A_z \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r})$. This property holds in general and is not restricted to line sources.

The ratio of the radiation field components in $\eqref{equation-65}$ and $\eqref{equation-66}$ is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_\theta = \frac{\omega\mu}{\beta} H_\phi = \frac{\omega\mu}{\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon'}} H_\phi = \eta H_\phi
\tag{\label{equation-67}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon'}$ is the intrinsic impedance of the medium. Thus, the radiation fields are orthogonal to each other and to the direction of propagation $\unitvec{r}$ and their magnitudes are related by $\eqref{equation-67}$. These are the familiar properties of plane waves. They also hold for the general form of a *transverse electromagnetic (TEM) wave* that has both the electric and magnetic fields transverse to the direction of propagation. In general, radiation from a finite antenna is a special case of a TEM wave, called a "spherical wave", that propagates radially outward from the antenna and the radiation fields have no radial components. Spherical wave behaviour is also characterized by the $e^{-j\beta r}{4\pi r}$ factor in the field expressions. The $e^{-j\beta r}$ phase factor indicates a traveling wave propagating radially outward from the origin and the $r^{-1}$ decay leads to constant power flow just as with the ideal dipole. Generally, the radiation fields of all antennas of finite extent display this dependence with distance from the antenna.

Although the radiated wavefront is spherical, at sufficiently large distances the local curvature is negligible, so the fields appear locally as plane waves. This is the sense in which far-field radiation may be regarded as plane-wave-like.

<MathBox title="Uniform line source" boxType="example">
A uniform line source has a constant current $I_0$ along its extent. The current of a $z$-directed uniform line source  centered at the origin is

$$
  I(z') = \begin{cases}
    I_0,\quad& x' = 0,\; y' = 0, |z'| \leq \frac{L}{2} \\
    0,\quad& \text{elsewhere}
  \end{cases}
$$

where $L$ is the length of the line source. The vector potential is

$$
\begin{align*}
  A_z =& \mu \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} I_0 e^{j\beta z' \cos(\theta)} \;\d z' \\
  =& \mu \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \int_0 \left(\frac{e^{j\beta (L/2)\cos(\theta)} - e^{-j\beta (L/2) \cos(\theta)}}{j\beta\cos(\theta)} \right) \\
  =& \mu \frac{I_0 L e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \frac{\sin[(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)]}{(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)}
\end{align*}
$$

and the electric field is

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \mathbf{E} =& j\omega \sin(\theta) A_z \unitsym{\theta} \\
  =& \frac{j\omega\mu I_0 L e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \frac{\sin[(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)]}{(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)} \unitsym{\theta}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-88}}
\end{equation*}
$$
</MathBox>

### Far-field coditions and field regions

For general sources the far-field approximation relies on the parallel-ray assumption, which expresses the distance between and observation point and a source point as

$$
\begin{align*}
  R =& r - r' \cos(\alpha) \\
  =& r - r' \frac{\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{r}'}{rr'} \\
  =& r - \unitvec{r} \cdot \mathbf{r}''
\end{align*}
$$

The far-field distance (Rayleigh distance), $r_\text{ff}$, is defined as the radial distance at which the phase error introduced by neglecting the quadratic term in $\eqref{equation-58}$ is small. This is typically taken as $\pi/8$ radians which corresponds to sixteenth of a wavelength

$$
  \frac{2\pi}{/\lambda}\frac{\lambda}{16} = \frac{\pi}{8}
$$

The far-field region is $r \geq r_\text{ff}$.

For a line source of length $D$, the maximum value of the quadratic term in $\eqref{equation-68}$ occurs for $z' = D/2$ and $\theta = \pi/2$. The far-field distance $r_\text{ff}$ is found by equating the maximum quadratic contribution to a sixteenth of a wavelength, or $(D/2)^2 /2r_\text{ff} = \lambda/16$. Solving for $r_\text{ff}$ gives

$$
  r_\text{ff} = \frac{2D^2}{\lambda}
$$

This far-field condition is not sufficiently large when the antenna is electrically small, for which its physical size is small compared to the wavelength.

The region interior to the far field, called the near field, is divided into the reactive near field where the reactive fields dominate over the radiation (real-valued) fields and the radiating near field where the radiation fields dominate over the reactive fields.

For the ideal dipole, the radiated-reactive power density ratio is $|S_r^\text{ff} / S_r^\text{nf}| = (\beta r)^3$. The magnitudes of the radiated and reactive power densities are equal when $\beta r = 1$ or $r = \lambda/2\pi$, which is the radius of the radiansphere. For tue boundary value $\beta r = 10$, the radiated power density is $1000$ times ($30 \unit{dB}$) greather than the reactive power density. Thus $r = 10/\beta = 1.6\lambda$, forms the far-field boundary for the ideal dipole, and corresponds to the point where the wave impedance converges to the intrinsic impedance of free space $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\epsilon}$. A more conservative condition or $r = 5\lambda$ aries based on the error in magnitude of the $E_\theta$ in $\eqref{equation-70}$ being $30 \unit{dB}$. These results also apply to any electrically small antenna, which is defined as fitting inside a sphere of radius $a$ equal to or less that $\lambda/2\pi$.

For a line source, the reactive-radiating near field boundary is $r = 0.62 \sqrt{D^3/\lambda}$ and the far-field starts at $2D^2 / \lambda$. An additional far-field distance condition is that $r$ is greater than $5D$, which is based on the maximum error in distance to the far field being $10%$.

The radiated fields (i.e., in the far field) are found by summing up all contributions due to tiny subdivisions of the current distributions (i.e., integrating). The resulting radiation pattern is independent of distance from the antenna, $r$, and varies only with angle around the antenna. This result is a consequence of approximating rays from parts of the source as being parallel. Then distance in the far field is irrelevant and only angles matter. A further general principle for all antennas is that in the far field the only field components present are transverse to the radial direction

### Normalized field and power patterns

The radiation pattern of an antenna describes the variation of the radiated electric field as a function of direction on a spherical surface centered at the antenna. Since the radial distance $r$ is held constant in this description, the field pattern depends only on the angular coordinates $(\theta, \phi)$.

It is convenient to normalize the field pattern so that its maximum magnitude is unity. For a $z$-directed source that produces only a $\theta$-polarized electric field component $E_\theta$ the normalized field pattern is defined as

$$
  F(\theta, \phi) := \frac{E_\theta (\theta)}{\max_{\theta, \phi} |E_\theta (\theta, \phi)|}
$$

where $\max_{\theta, \phi} (E_\theta)$ is the maximum magnitude of $E_\theta$ over the observation sphere of radius $r$. In this case, the pattern is relative to linear polarization in the $\theta$-orientation.

In general, $E_\theta$ can be complex-valued. Since only the relative phase is of interest in pattern analysis, the phase at the normalization point is usually set to zero.

In general, a normalized field pattern can be written as the product

$$
  F(\theta, \phi) = g(\theta, \phi) f(\theta, \phi)
$$

where 
- $g(\theta, \phi)$ is the element factor, i.e. the field pattern of a single infinitesimal source element
- $f(\theta, \phi)$ is the pattern factor, determined by the spatial distribution of the source elements. 

For a $z$-directed infinitesimal current element is

$$
  g(\theta) = \sin(\theta)
$$

which represents the projection of the current element into the observation direction. The $\sin(\theta)$ dependence captures the fact that no radiation is observed along the axis of the element.

The pattern factor $f(\theta, \phi)$ represents the integrated effect of ration contributions from the current distribution, which can be treated as being made up of many current elements. The pattern value in a specific diresction is the found by summing the parallel rays from each from each current element to the far field with the magnitude and phase of each included. The radiation integral $\eqref{equation-}$ sums the far-field contributions from the current elements and when normalized yields the pattern factor.

The radiation integral encodes both magnitude variations (due to current amplitude) and phase variations (due to current phase and path-length differences). Interference of these contributions produces lobes in the radiation pattern.

The main lobe corresponds to the direction of maximum radiation intensity. This occurs when the contributions from the current distribution arrive most nearly in-phase at the far-field point. For a uniformly phased source, this typically corresponds to the direction normal to the antenna aperture.

Frequently, the radiation pattern is expressed in terms of power density rather than field amplitude. The general normalized power pattern is defined as

$$
  P(\theta, \phi) := |F(\theta, \phi)|^2
$$

This corresponds to the angular variation of the radial component of the time-averaged Poynting vector, normalized to its maximum value.

Radiation patterns are commonly expressed in decibels. The field and power patterns are the same in decibels, i.e.

$$
  P(\theta, \phi)_\text{dB} = |F(\theta,\phi)|_\text{dB}
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

In decibels, the field pattern is given by

$$
  |F(\theta, \phi)|_\text{dB} = 20 \log_{10} |F(\theta, \phi)|
$$

and the power pattern and is

$$
\begin{align*}
  P(\theta, \phi)_\text{dB} =& 10 \log_{10} P(\theta, \phi) \\
  =& 10 \log_{10} |F(\theta, \phi)|^2 \\
  =& 20 \log_{20} |F(\theta, \phi)|^2
\end{align*}
$$

This shows that

$$
  P(\theta, \phi)_\text{dB} = |F(\theta,\phi)|_\text{dB}
$$
</details>

<MathBox title="Normalized power pattern for an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
From $\eqref{equation-48}$, the normal field pattern of an ideal dipole is

$$
\begin{align*}
  F(\theta) =& \frac{(I\Delta z/4\pi)j\omega\mu (e^{-j\beta r} / r) \sin(\theta)}{(I\Delta z/4\pi)j\omega\mu (e^{-j\beta r} / r)} \\
  =& \sin(\theta)
\end{align*}
$$

and the normalized power pattern is

$$
  P(\theta) = |F(\theta)|^2 = \sin^2 (\theta)
$$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Normalized power pattern for a uniform line source" boxType="example">
From $\eqref{equation-88}$, the normal field pattern of uniform line source of length $L$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  F(\theta) =& \sin(\theta) \frac{\sin[(\beta L) \cos\theta]}
  =& \sin(\theta)
\end{align*}
$$

Written in the form $F(\theta) = g(\theta) f(\theta)$, the element factor is

$$
  g(\theta) = \sin(\theta)
$$

while the pattern factor is

$$
  f(\theta) = \frac{\sin[(\beta L) \cos\theta]}
$$

The normalized power pattern is

$$
  P(\theta) = |F(\theta)|^2 = \left(\sin(\theta) \frac{\sin[(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)]}{(\beta L/2) \cos(\theta)} \right)^2

### Radiation pattern parameters

The main beam (or main lobe or major lobe) is the lobe containing the direction of maximum radiation. There is also usually a series of lobes smaller than the main lobe. Any lobe other than the main lobe is called a minor lobe. Minor lobes are composed of side lobes and back lobes. Back lobes are directly opposite the main lobe, or sometimes they are taken to be the lobes in the half-space opposite the main lobe. The term side lobe is sometimes reserved for those minor lobes near the main lobe, but is most often taken to be synonymous with minor lobe; we will use the latter convention.

A measure of how well the power is concentrated into the main lobe is the (relative) side lobe level, which is the ratio of the pattern value of a side lobe peak to the pattern value of the main lobe. The largest side lobe level for the whole pattern is the maximum (relative) side lobe level, frequently abbreviated as SLL. In decibels, it is given by

$$
  \operatorname{SLL}_\text{dB} = 20 \log_{10} \frac{|F(\operatorname{SLL})}{F(\max)}
$$

where $|F(\max)|$ is the maximum value of the pattern magnitude and $|F(\operatorname{SLL})|$ is the pattern value of the maximum of the highest side lobe magnitude.

The width of the main beam is quantified through half-power beamwidth (HP), which is the angular separation of the points where the main beam of the power pattern equals one half of the maximum value

$$
  \operatorname{HP} = |\theta_\text{HP left} - \theta_\text{HP right}|
$$

where $\theta_\text{HP left}$ and $\theta_\text{HP right}$ are points to the left and right of the main beam maximum for which the normalized power pattern has a value of one-half. On the field pattern $|F(\theta)|$, these points correspond to the value $1/\sqrt{2}$. In three dimensions, the radiation pattern major lobe becomes a solid object and the half-power contour is a continuous curve. If this curve is essentially elliptical, the pattern cuts that contain the major and minor axes of the ellipse determine what the IEEE defines as the principal half-power beamwidths.

Antennas are often referred to by the type of pattern they produce. An isotropic antenna, which is hypothetical, radiates equally in all directions giving a constant radiation pattern. An omnidirectional antenna produces a pattern that is constant in
one plane.

We often refer to a directive antenna as being broadside or endfire. A broadside antenna is one for which the main beam maximum is in a direction normal to the plane containing the antenna. An endfire antenna is one for which the main beam is in the plane containing the antenna. A type of broadside pattern is a fan beam, and a type of endfire pattern is a pencil beam.

## Directivity and gain

The directivity of an antenna describes how much it concentrates energy in one direction in preference to radiation in other directions. If the antenna is $100%$ efficient, the directivity is equal to the gain of the antenna.

From $\eqref{equation-71}$ the power radiated by an antenne is

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  P =& \iint \mathbf{S} \cdot \d\mathbf{s} = \frac{1}{2} \Re \iint (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*) \cdot \d\mathbf{s} \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \Re \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi (E_\theta H_\phi^* - E_\theta H_\phi^*) r^2 \sin(\theta) \;\d\theta \d\phi
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-72}}
\end{equation*}
$$

From the plane wave relation $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\eta} \unitvec{r} \times \mathbf{E}$ it follows that

$$
  H_\phi = \frac{E_\theta}{\eta},\quad H_\theta = -\frac{E_\phi}{\eta}
$$

Inserting these into $\eqref{equation-72}$ yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P = \frac{1}{2\eta} \iint (|E_\theta|^2 + |E_\phi|^2) r^2 \;\d\Omega
\tag{\label{equation-73}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\d\Omega = \sin(\theta) \;\d\theta \d\phi$ is an infinitesimal angle element. The integral can be evaluated over any surface enclosing the antenna, but for simplicity a spherical surface centered on the origin is usually used. Since the magnitude variations of the radiation fields are $1/r$, it is convenient to introduce radiation intensity, defined as


$$
\begin{equation*}
  U(\theta,\phi) = \frac{1}{2} \Re(\mathbf{E} \times\mathbf{H}^*) \cdot r^2 \unitvec{r} = S(\theta, \phi) r^2
\tag{\label{equation-74}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Radiation intensity is the power radiated in a given direction per unit solid angle and has units of watts per square radian, also called steradian (sr). Radiation intensity can be expressed as

$$
  U(\theta, \phi) = U_m |F(\theta, \phi)|^2
$$

where $U_m = U(\theta_\text{max}, \phi_\text{max})$ is the maximum radiation intensity and $|F(\theta, \phi)|^2$ is the power pattern normalized to a maximum value of unity in the direction $(\theta_\text{max}, \phi_\text{max})$. The total power radiated is obtained by integrating the radiation intensity over all angles around the antenna

$$
\begin{align*}
  P =& \iint U(\theta, \phi) \;\d\Omega \\
  =& U_m \iint |F(\theta, \phi)|^2 \;\d\Omega \tag{\label{equation-76}}
\end{align*}
$$

In particular for a hypothetical isotropic source with constant intensity $\bar{U}$, the total power is

$$
  P = \bar{U} \iint \;\d\Omega = 4\pi \bar{U}
$$

For nonisotropic sources, the radiation intensity is not constant throughout space, but an average power per steradian can be defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{U} = \frac{1}{4\pi} \iint U(\theta, \phi) \;\d\Omega = \frac{P}{4\pi}
\tag{\label{equation-79}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The average radiation intensity $\bar{U}$ equals the radiation intensity $U(\theta,\phi)$ that an isotropic source with the same input power $P$ would radiate.

<MathBox title="Radiation intensity of an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
Using $eqref{equation-74}$ with $\eqref{equation-47}$ and $\eqref{equation-48}$ we find

$$
  U(\theta, \phi) = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \right)^2 \beta\omega\mu \sin^2 (\theta)
$$

so that

$$
  U_m = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \right)^2 \beta\omega\mu 
$$

From $\eqref{equation-50}$, the average radiation intensity of an ideal dipole is

$$
\begin{align*}
  \bar{U} =& \frac{P}{4\pi} = \frac{(\beta\omega\mu/ 12\pi) (I\Delta z)^2}{4\pi} \\
  =& \frac{1}{3} \left(\frac{I\Delta z}{4\pi} \right)^2 \beta\omega\mu \\
  =& \frac{2}{3} U_m \tag{\label{equation-89}}
\end{align*}
$$
</MathBox>

### Directivitity

Directivity is defined as the ratio of the radiation intensity in a certain direction to the average radiation intensity. The reference direction is usually taken to be that of the maximum radiation, giving

$$
\begin{equation*}
  D = \frac{U_m}{\bar{U}} = \frac{U_m}{P/(4\pi)}
\tag{\label{equation-75}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Unless otherwise stated, directivity will be assime to be this maximum directivity value. If both the numerator and denominator are divided by $\eqref{equation-75}$ becomes a ratio of power densities at distance $r$ from the antenna

$$
\begin{align*}
  D =& \frac{U/r^2}{\bar{U}/r^2} \\
  =& \frac{\frac{1}{2} \max[\Re(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*) \cdot\unitvec{r}]}{P/4\pi r^2}
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, directivity has two interpretations, as a ratio of maximum to average radiation intensities and as a ratio of maximum to average power densities. Directivity can be tied more directly to the pattern function. First, we define the beam solid angle

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \Omega_A = \iint |F(\theta, \phi)|^2 \;\d\Omega
\tag{\label{equation-77}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The beam solid angle of the actual radiation pattern has the same solid angle as constant radiation of a level equal to the maximum value radiation intensity over all the beam area, or

$$
  P = U_m \Omega_A
$$

This also follows from $\eqref{equation-76}$ and $\eqref{equation-77}$. Substituting $\eqref{equation-}$ in $\eqref{equation-75}$ with $\eqref{equation-77}$ gives

$$
\begin{equation*}
  D = \frac{4\pi}{\Omega_A}
\tag{\label{equation-78}}
\end{equation*}
$$

These results show that directivity is entirely determined by the pattern shape; it is independent of the details of the antenna hardware. Directivity as a function of pattern angle is expressed simply as the directitiy multiplied by the power pattern

$$
  D(\theta,\phi) = D|F(\theta,\phi)|^2
$$

In the case of isotropic power distribution, the radiation intensity would have a maximum value equal to its average value, that is $U_m = \bar{U}$ or $\Omega_A = 4\pi$. Thus, the directivity of this isotropic pattern is unity. The distribution of radiation intensity $U(\theta, \phi)$ for an actual antenne has a maximum radiation density in the direction $(\theta_\text{max}, \phi_\text{max})$ of $U_m = D\bar{U}$ and an average radiation intensity of $\bar{U} = P/4\pi$. There is $D$ times as much power density in the direction $(\theta_\text{max}, \phi_\text{max})$ as there would be if the same total power were radiated by an isotropic source. Thus, by directing the radiated power $P$ in a preferred direction (by the maximum radiation direction) the radiation intensity is increased in that direction by a factor of $D$ over what it would be if the same radiated power had been isotropically radiated.

<MathBox title="Directivity of an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
The directivity if an ideal dipole is easily found using $\eqref{equation-75}$ with $eqref{equation-89}$

$$
  D = \frac{U_m}{\bar{U}} = \frac{U_m}{\frac{3}{2} U_m} = \frac{3}{2}
$$

The directivity can also be calculated directly by finding the beam solid angle

$$
\begin{align*}
  \Omega_A =& \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi |\sin(\theta)|^2 \sin(\theta) \;\d\theta \;\d\phi \\
  =&\frac{8\pi}{3} 
\end{align*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-78}$, we find

$$
  D = \frac{4\pi}{\Omega_A} = \frac{4\pi}{8\pi /3} = \frac{3}{2}
$$
</MathBox>

### Gain

When an antenna is used in a system (say as a transmitting antenna) we are interested in how efficiently the antenna transforms available power at its input terminals into radiated power, as well as its directive properties. To quantify this, gain is defined as $4\pi$ times the ratio of the maximum radiation intensity to the net power accepted by the antenna from the connected transmitter, or

$$
\begin{equation*}
  G = \frac{4\pi U_m}{P_m}
\tag{\label{equation-80}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This is a power quantity and is sometimes referred to as power gain. This definition does not include losses due to mismatches of impedance or polarization.

Gain can be expressed as a function of angle by including the power pattern as $G(\theta, \phi) = G|F(\theta, \phi)|^2$. Gain can also be give as a value in a specific direction. If no direction is specified and the gain value is not given as a function of $\theta$ and $\phi$, it is assumed to be the maximum gain.

Comparing the gain formula $\eqref{equation-80}$ with the directivity formula $\eqref{equation-75}$, the only difference is the power value used. Directivity can be interpreted as the gain an antenna would have if all input power appeared as radiated power. Gin includes the fact that real antennas do not behave in this fashion and some of teh input power is lost on the antenna. The portion of input power $P_\text{in}$ that does no appear as radiated power is absorbed on the antenna and nearby structures. The radiation efficiency, $e_r$, of an antenna is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  e_r = \frac{P}{P_\text{in}} \in [0,1]
\tag{\label{equation-87}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Many antennas are highly efficient, with radiation efficiency close to $1$. However, electrically small antenna are very inefficient. Substituting $P = e_r P_\text{in}$ in $\eqref{equation-75}$ we obtain

$$
  G = e_r D
$$

Thus, the gain of an antenna is equal to its purely directional characteristic of directivity multiplied by radiation efficiency. The term absolute gain, which is synonymous with gain, is sometimes used.

### Units

Because gain is a power ratio it can be calculated in decibels as follows

$$
  G_\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10} G
$$

Similar for directivity

$$
  D_\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10} G
$$

Frequently gain is used to describe the performance of the antenna relative to some standard reference antenna. This relative gain is defined as the ratio of the maximum radiation intensity from the antenna $U_m$ to the maximum radiation intensity from a reference antenna $U_{m, \text{ref}}$ with the same input power

$$
\begin{equation*}
  G_\text{ref} = \frac{U_m}{U_{m, \text{ref}}}
\tag{\label{equation-81}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This is a convenient definition from a measurement standpoint. The formal definition of gain employs a lossless isotropic antenna as a reference antenna. This can be shown that noting that the lossless isotropic reference antenna has a maximum radiation density of $P_\text{in} / 4\pi$ because all of its input power is radiated, and substituting this into $\eqref{equation-81}$ for $U_{m,\text{ref}}$ leads to $\eqref{equation-80}$.

It is common at frequencies below $1 \unit{GHz}$ to quote gain values relative to that of a half-wave dipole because a half-wave dipole is the usual reference antenna used in gain measurements at these frequencies. Gain relative to a half-wave dipole carries the units of $\unit{dBd}$ and is related to gain relative to the normal implied reference of a lossless, isotropic antenna as

$$
  G_\text{dBd} = G_\text{dB} - 2.15
$$

The unit of gain is sometimes denoted as $\tunit{dBi}$ instead of just $\tunit{dB}$ to emphasize that an isotropic antenna is used as the reference.

## Antenna impedance

The primary function of a transmitting antenna is to convert a bound wave to an unbound (i.e. radiated) wave, and vice versa for a receiving antenna. Whereas the transmission line connected to an antenna binds the wave and prevents it from radiating, the antenna itself should enable radio waves to leave the structure. The antenna is an interface between wave phenomena on and beyond the antenna to the connecting circuit hardware. The antenna input terminals from the interface point and the circuit parameter of impedance is used to characterize the input to the antenna. The *input impedance* of an antenna (or simply *antenna impedance*) will be affected by other antennas or objects that are nearby, but the discussion here assumes an isolated antenna.

Antenna impedance is import to the transfer of power from a transmitter to an antenna or from an antenna to a receiver. For example, to maximize the power transferred from a receiving antenna, the receiver impedance should be a conjugate match to the antenna impedance (equal resistances, equal magnitude and opposite sign reactances).

As with conventional circuits, antenna impedance is composed of real and imaginary parts

$$
\begin{equation*}
  Z_A = R_A + jX_A
\tag{\label{equation-82}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Due to reciprocity, the impedance of an antenna is identical for receiving and transmitting operation. The input resistance $R_A$ represents dissipation which occurs in two ways. Power that leaves the antenna and never return is one part of the dissipation. There are also ohmic losses just as in a lumped resistor. Electrically small antennas can have significant ohmic losses but other antennas usually have ohmic losses that are small compared to their radiation dissipation. The input reactance, $X_A$, represents power stored in the near fields of the antenna.

The averave power dissipated in an antenna is

$$
  P_\text{in} = \frac{1}{2} R_A |I_A|^2
$$

where $I_A$ is the current at the input terminals. Note that a factor of $1/2$ is present because current $I_A$ is the peak value in time waveform. Separating the dissipated power into radiative and ohmic losses gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  P_\text{in} =& P + P_o \\
  \frac{1}{2} R_A |I_A|^2 =& \frac{1}{2} R_r |I_A|^2 + \frac{1}{2} R_o |I_A|^2 \tag{\label{equation-83}}
\end{align*}
$$

where we define the radiation resistance of an antenna referred to the input terminal as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_r = \frac{2P}{|I_A|^2}
\tag{\label{equation-85}}
\end{equation*}
$$

It follows from $\eqref{equation-83}$ that

$$
  R_A = R_r + R_o
$$

where $R_o$ is the resistance associated with ohmic losses that inlude the directly drive part of the antenna as well as losses in other portions of the antenna structure such as a ground plan. Ohmic resistance of an antenna is defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_o = \frac{2P_o}{|I_A|^2} = \frac{2(P_\text{in} - P)}{|I_A|^2}
\tag{\label{equation-92}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The radiated power is found using $\eqref{equation-84}$

$$
  P = \frac{1}{2} \iint (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*) \cdot \d\mathbf{s}
$$

$P$ is real-valued because the power density $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*$ is real-valued in the far-field.

Radiation resistance can be defined relative to the current at any point on the antenna, but we reserve $R_r$ for radiation resistance referred to the input terminals. Radiation resistance relative to the maximum current $I_m$ that occurs on the antenna $R_{rm}$ is obtained by using $I_m$ in place of $I_A$ in $\eqref{equation-85}$.

### Ideal and short dipoles

<MathBox title="Impedance of an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
Using $\eqref{equation-50}$, the radiation resistance of an ideal dipole of length $\Delta z \ll \lambda$ and input current $I_A = I$ is

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_r =& \frac{2P}{|I_A|^2} = \frac{2}{I^2}{\omega\mu\beta}{12\pi}(I\Delta z)^2 \\
  =& \frac{\sqrt{\mu\omega}\sqrt{\mu}\sqrt{\varepsilon}}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}6\pi} \beta (\Delta z)^2 \\
  =& \eta \frac{\beta^2}{6\pi} (\Delta z)^2 = \eta\frac{2}{3} \pi \left(\frac{\Delta z}{\lambda}\right)^2
\end{align*}
$$

For free space $\eta = 120\pi \unit{\Omega}$, such that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_r = 80\pi^2 \left(\frac{\Delta z}{\lambda} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega}
\tag{\label{equation-90}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Except for low frequencies, the skin depth $\delta = \sqrt{2/\omega\mu\sigma}$ is much smaller than the conductor radius, such that the ohmic resistance for an antenna of length $L$ that carries an axially uniform current is directly proportional to the wire length and inversely proportional to the wire circumference

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_o \approx \frac{L}{2\pi a} R_s
\tag{\label{equation-93}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $a$ is the wire radius and $R_s$ is the surface resistance given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_s = \sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu}{2\sigma}}
\tag{\label{equation-121}}
\end{equation}
$$
</MathBox>

The ideal dipole has a uniform current. In reality, the current on a straight wire antenna must smoothly go to zero at the wire ends. The current distribution on a center-fed wire dipole of length $\Delta z \ll\lambda$, called a *short dipole*, is approximately triangular in shape. If end loading such as a with metal plates is added to the short dipole, the radial current reduces to zero at the of the plates, giving a nearly uniform current on the vertical portion of the dipole, which permits the use of the ideal dipole model.

Since the short dipole also satisfies $\Delta z \ll\lambda$, it shares the sinusoidal radiation pattern as the ideal dipose. In addition, the ideal dipole and short dipole will have the same directivity of $1.5$ because pattern shape completely determines directivity.

The triangular current distribution of the short dipole leads to and equivalent length that is one-half that of its physical length. This is because the equivalent length is proportional to the area under the current versus distance curves, which follows from the radiation integral $\eqref{equation-91}$ with $\exp(j\beta z' \cos(\theta)) \approx 1$ for short dipoles. The radiated fields are, in turn, proportional to this equivalent length. Because the radiation resistance is proportional to the integral of the far-zone electric field squared and the patterns of the ideal and short dipolles are the same, the radiation resistance are proportional to the equivalent lenghts squared. The area of the triangle-shape current on the short dipole is one-half that of the uniform curent shape, so the radiation resistance one fourth that of the ideal dipole. Dividing $\eqref{equation-90}$ by $4$ results in the following radiation resistance for the short dipole:

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_r = 20\pi^2 \left(\frac{\Delta z}{\lambda} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega}
\tag{\label{equation-95}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The ohmic resistance for the short dipole is less than that of the ideal dipole. The ohmic resistance of a short dipole is found by fist determining the power dissipation from ohmic losses, which at any point along the antenna is propotional to the current squared. In fact, in general the total power dissipated is evaluated by integrating the current squared over the wire antenna, which together with $\eqref{equation-92}$ yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_o =& \frac{2P_o}{|I_A|^2} \\
  =& \frac{1}{|I_A|^2} \frac{R_s}{2\pi a} \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} |I(z)|^2 \;\d z \tag{\label{equation-94}}
\end{align*}
$$

This can be shown to reduce to $\eqref{equation-93}$ for a uniform current of length $L = \Delta z$. The short dipole triangular current can be written as a function of position along the wire as

$$
  I(z) = I_A \left(1 - \frac{2|z|}{\Delta z} \right),\, |z| \leq \frac{\Delta z}{2}
$$

Using this in $\eqref{equation-94}$ yields the ohmic resistance

$$
  R_o = \frac{\Delta z}{2\pi a} \frac{R_s}{3}
$$

The ohmic resistance for the short dipole is therefore one-third that for an ideal dipole of the same length $\Delta z$ given in $\eqref{equation-93}$. Since the radiation resistance for the short dipole is one-fourth that of an ideal dipose, the radiation resistance is decreased more relative to the ohmic resistance, and thus the efficiency is lower for the short dipole than is it for an ideal dipole of the same length.

Antennas that are electrically small have a large input reactance, in addition to a small radiation resistance. For example, the short dipole has a capacitive reactance, whereas an electrically small loop antenna has an inductive reactance. The reactance of a short dipole is approximated by

$$
  X_A = -\frac{120}{\pi\frac{\Delta z}{\lambda}} \left[\ln\left(\frac{\Delta z}{2a} \right) - 1 \right] \unit{\Omega}
$$

This gives a large capacitive reactance for very short dipoles. The total input impedance of the short dipole is $R_r + R_o + jX_A$.

## Radiation efficiency

In general, an efficiency factor is the ratio of wanted power to total power supplied. The radiation efficiency $e_r$ of an antenna, defined in $\eqref{equation-87}$ is the ratio of radiated power to the net power accepted by the antenna

$$
\begin{equation*}
  e_r = \frac{P}{P_\text{in}} = \frac{P}{P + P_o}
\tag{\label{equation-86}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where
- $P$ is radiated power
- $P_o$ is power dissipated in ohmic losses on the antenna
- $P_\text{in} = P + P_o$ is input power accepted by the antenna

We can derive an expression for the radiation efficiency of an antenna totally in terms of resistances by substituting $\eqref{equation-83}$ and $\eqref{equation-84}$ into $\eqref{equation-86}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  e_r =& \frac{\frac{1}{2} R_r |I_A|^2}{\frac{1}{2} R_r |I_A|^2 + \frac{1}{2}R_o |I_A|^2} \\
  =& \frac{R_r}{R_r + R_o} = \frac{R_r}{R_A}
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, the radiation resistance relative to the total input resistance gives the radiation efficiency (often referred to just as efficiency). This fundamental equation contains the obvious result that for a fixed radiation resistance as the ohmic resistance increases, the efficiency decreases. For many antennas radiation efficiency is nearly $100%$, but electrically small antennas often have very low radiation efficiency. Low radiation efficiency is acceptable for receiving and for low-power transmitting applications. However, radiation efficiency must be as close to $100%$ as possible in high-power transmitting antennas for two reasons: the ohmic losses cost money in power consumption and the heat generated can possibly damage or destroy the antenna.

In addition to reduction in radiation efficiency, antenna ohmic losses have another undesirable effect. As with any resistive element in an electrical system, ohmic losses on antennas are noise sources. For receiving applications above $1 \text{MHz}$ when the signal is low, internal noise can swamp out a signal. Receiving antennas also pick up external noise. For frequencies around $1 \text{MHz}$ and below, external noise, mainly due to lightning, is significant and always present. This external noise picked up by the antenna via the radiation resistance is usually larger than the internal noise and antenna loss can be tolerated.

## Antenna polarization

A finite-sized source radiates a spherical wavefront. However, over a small observation region the wavefront becomes nearly planar. Such an approximation leads to the notion of a plane wave, whose electric and magnetic fields lie in a plane transverse to the propagation direction. 

The polarization of a plane wave is the trajectory traced by the instantaneous electric field at a fixed spatial point as a function of time. For a completely polarized wave, this trajectory is an ellipse. In contrast, unpolarized or partially polarized waves (e.g., due to random noise processes) do not yield deterministic elliptical trajectories. The temporal and spatial variations of the magnetic field are analogous to those of the electric field, except that the magnetic field is orthogonal both to the electric field and to the propagation direction, ensuring transversality.

The polarization ellipse admits several important limiting cases:
- **Linear polarization:** The electric field vector oscillates along a fixed line, corresponding to a degenerate ellipse with infinite axial ratio. Examples include the radiation from an ideal linear dipole or a uniform line current.
- **Circular polarization:** The electric field vector has constant magnitude and rotates uniformly in a circle. At radian frequency $\omega$, this rotation may occur in one of two senses:
    - **Left-hand circular polarization (LHCP):** Looking in the direction of wave propagation, the field vector rotates clockwise.
    - **Right-hand circular polarization (RHCP):** Looking in the direction of wave propagation, the field vector rotates counterclockwise.
- **Elliptical polarization:** The most general case, where the electric field vector traces out an ellipse with finite axial ratio

A helix antenna, for example, produces circular polarization, with the handedness determined by the sense of the winding.

Consider a monochromatic plane wave propagating in the $z$-direction, with instantaneous electric field

$$
  \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = E_x (t) \unitvec{x} + E_y (t) \unitvec{y}
$$

Let the field components be harmonic with angular frequency $\omega$

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_x (t) =& E_1 \cos(\omega t) \\
  E_y (t) =& E_2 \cos(\omega t + \delta) \unitvec{y}
\end{align*}
$$

where $E_1, E_2 \geq 0$ are the field amplitudes along the $x$- and $y$-axes, and $\delta\in (-\pi, \pi]$ is the relative phase. If the components are in-phase ($\delta = 0$), the net vector is linearly polarized. The orientation of the linear polarization depends on the relative values of $E_1$ and $E_2$
- $E_1 = 0$ gives vertical polarization
- $E_2 = 0$ gives horizontal polarization
- $E_1 = E_2$ gives linear polarization at $45\deg$ with respect to the axes

The axial ratio (AR) of the polarization ellipse is defined as the ratio of the magnitudes of the major to the minor axes:

$$
  |\mathrm{AR}| := \frac{E_\text{major}}{E_\text{minor}},\; 1 \leq |\mathrm{AR}| \leq \infty
$$

In particular,
- $\mathrm{AR} = \infty$ corresponds to linear polarization.
- $|\mathrm{AR}| = 1$ corresponds to circular polarization.
- Finite $|\mathrm{AR}|$ corresponds to elliptical polarization.

The ellipticity angle $\epsilon$ is defined by

$$
  \epsilon = \cot^{-1} (-\mathrm{AR}) \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4} \right]
$$

so that $\epsilon = 0$ yields linear polarization, while $\epsilon = \pm \pi/4$ yields circular polarization. The sign of $\mathrm{AR}$ encodes the handedness: 
- $\mathrm{AR} > 0$: right-hand polarization
- $\mathrm{AR} < 0$: left-hand polarization

Axial ratio is often expressed in decibels as $\mathrm{AR}_\text{dB} = 20 \log_{10}(|\mathrm{AR}|)$. 

The tilt angle $\tau$ specifies the orientation of the ellipse relative to the $x$-axis, defined as the angle between the $x$-axis and the major axis of the ellipsis.

The phasor representation of the electric field is

$$
  \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = E_1 \unitvec{x} + E_2 e^{j\delta}
$$

which can be normalized as

$$
  \mathbf{E} = |E| \left(\cos(\gamma) \unitvec{x} + \sin(\gamma) e^{j\delta} \unitvec{y} \right)
$$

where

$$
\begin{align*}
  |E| =& \sqrt{E_1^2 + E_2^2} \\
  \gamma =& \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{E_2}{E_1} \right) \in \left[0, \frac{\pi}{2} \right]
\end{align*}
$$

The unit vector

$$
  \unitvec{e} = \cos(\gamma) \unitvec{x} + \sin(\gamma) e^{j\delta} \unitvec{y}
$$

encodes the polarization state.  Thus, $\gamma$ and $\delta$ uniquely characterizes the polarization. The two parametrizations $(\epsilon, \tau)$ and $(\gamma, \delta)$ are equivalent. The transformation between these angles are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \gamma =& \frac{1}{2} \cos^{-1} \left[\cos(2\epsilon) \cos(2\tau)\right] \\
  \delta =& \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{\tan(2\epsilon)}{\sin(2\tau)}\right)
\end{align*}
$$

## Image theory

The electromagnetic environment surrounding an antenna influences its radiation characteristics, most notably its radiation pattern and input impedance. The object most frequently encountered in practice is a ground plane. An idealized ground plane is assumed to be planar, infinite in extent, and perfectly conducting. Although no physical ground plane exactly satisfies these assumptions, they often provide an excellent approximation.

The assumption of perfect conductivity is generally very accurate for highly conductive materials such as copper or aluminum, whose surface impedance is negligible at radio frequencies. Likewise, if the surface irregularities are small compared with the wavelength ($\ll \lambda$), the assumption of a perfectly planar surface introduces only minor errors. The most restrictive assumption is that of infinite extent. Nevertheless, when the dimensions of the conducting surface are large compared with both the wavelength and the antenna dimensions, a finite metal sheet or wire-mesh ground system often approximates an infinite ground plane with sufficient accuracy.

When an antenna operates in the presence of a perfect ground plane, the field observed at any point above the plane may be interpreted as the superposition of a direct field radiated by the antenna and a reflected field produced by the conducting surface. The reflected field satisfies Snell's law of reflection and can be represented mathematically by an equivalent image source. This construction is known as the method of images.

The justification for the image method follows from the uniqueness theorem for solutions of Maxwell's equations. If two electromagnetic field configurations satisfy the same governing equations, possess the same sources in a specified region, and satisfy identical boundary conditions on the enclosing surfaces, then the solutions must be identical throughout that region.

### Ideal dipole

Consider an ideal dipole located above a perfect conducting plane $PP'$ and oriented perpendicular to the plane. Since ground planes are typically horizontal, this configuration is commonly referred to as a vertical dipole above a perfect ground plane. Our objective is to determine the electric and magnetic fields in the half-space above $PP'$.

Using the method of images, the physical problem consisting of a dipole above a perfectly conducting plane may be replaced by an equivalent problem in free space in which an image dipole is placed the same distance below the plane as the original dipole is above it. For a dipole oriented normal to the conducting plane, the image dipole has the same orientation and current amplitude as the original dipole.

To verify that this image configuration satisfies the boundary conditions, consider the electric field of an ideal dipole. From $\ref{equation-46}$, the radial and polar components are proportional to

$$
  E_r \propto \cos\theta \quad E_\theta \propto \sin\theta
$$

where $\theta$ is measured from the dipole axis.

Let $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ denote the angles subtended at a point on the plane $PP'$ by the physical dipole and its image, respectively. By symmetry,

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \theta_1 + \theta_2 = \pi
\tag{\label{equation-97}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Since the observation point is equidistant from both sources and the source amplitudes are identical, the radial field components may be written as

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_{r1} =& C \cos(\theta_1) \\
  E_{r2} =& C \cos(\tehta_2)
\end{align*}
$$

The amplitude factor $C$ is the same for each field component since the amplitude of the sources is the same and points on the boundary are equidistant from the current elements. Using $\eqref{equation-97}$, we find

$$
\begin{equation*}
  E_{r1} = C \cos(\pi - \theta_2) = -C\cos(\theta_2) = -E_{r2}
\tag{\label{equation-96}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Consequently

$$
  E_{r1} + E_{r2} = 0
$$

showing that the radial components cancel everywhere on the plane $PP'$.

Similarly, the polar components are

$$
\begin{align*}
  E_{\theta1} =& D \sin(\theta_1) \\
  E_{\theta2} =& D \sin(\theta_2)
\end{align*}
$$

where $D$ is a common amplitude factor. Using $\eqref{equation-97}$, we obtain

$$
  E_{\theta1} = D \sin(\pi - \theta_2) = D \sin(\theta_2) = E_{\theta2}
$$

At the conducting plane, the $\theta$-components are directed normal to the surface, whereas the radial components lie tangentially to the surface. Consequently, the tangential electric field on $PP'$ is

$$
  (E_{r1} + E_{r2}) \unitvec{r} = \mathbf{0}
$$

This satisfies the boundary condition for a perfect electric conductor,

$$
  \mathbf{\hat n}\times\mathbf E = \mathbf 0
$$

where $\unitvec{n}$ is the unit normal to the conducting surface.

Since the image configuration satisfies the same boundary condition as the conducting plane and contains the same physical source in the region above (PP'), the uniqueness theorem guarantees that the fields in the upper half-space are identical for the physical and image problems. Therefore, the electromagnetic fields above a perfect ground plane may be obtained by replacing the conducting plane with the appropriate image source and summing the free-space fields of the original source and its image.

More generally, the image of an arbitrarily oriented current element may be obtained by decomposing the current into components perpendicular and parallel to the conducting plane. The perpendicular component is imaged with the same orientation, while the parallel component is imaged with reversed orientation. The image of an arbitrary current distribution is then obtained by applying this procedure to each differential current element and forming the vector sum of the resulting image currents.

## Electromagnetic duality

Many antenna configurations possess a dual structure whose electromagnetic solution may be obtained directly from a known solution through the principle of electromagnetic duality. The concept is analogous to duality in circuit theory, where voltages and currents, series and parallel connections, and inductances and capacitances are interchanged.

<MathBox title="Dual cicuits" boxType="example">
Consider a simple circuit of a voltage source applied to a series connection of a resistor $R$ and an inductor $L$. The dial circuit is a current source $I (=) V$ applied to the parallel combination of conductance $G (=) R$ and capacitance $C (=) L$. Since the circuits are duals, the solutions are duals.

The serial circuit can be described by the mesh equation

$$
  V = RI + j\omega LI
$$

The dual of this mesh equation is a node equation obtained by replacing $V$ by $I$, $R$ by $G$ and $L$ by $C$. The node equation for the dual parellel network is then

$$
  I = GV + j\omega CV
$$
</MathBox>

Consider first an electromagnetic system containing an electric current source with current density $\mathbf{J}_1$ embedded in a linear medium characterized by constitutive parameters $(\varepsilon_1, \mu_1, \sigma_1)$. In phasor form, Maxwell's curl equations for this system are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\mathbf{E}_1 =& -j\omega\mu_1 \mathbf{H}_1 \tag{\label{equation-101}} \\
  \nabla\times\mathbf{H}_1 =& -j\omega\varepsilon'_1 \mathbf{E}_1 + \mathbf{J}_1 \tag{\label{equation-102}}
\end{align*}
$$

where $\varepsilon'_1 = \varepsilon_1 - j\sigma_1/\omega$ is the complex permittivity of the medium.

Now consider a fictitious magnetic-current source with mangetic current density $\mathbf{M}_2$ embedded in a linear medium characterized by $(\varepsilon_2, \mu_2, \sigma_2)$ present. Maxwell's equations generalized to include magnetic current become

$$
\begin{align*}
  \nabla\times\mathbf{H}_2 =& j\omega\varepsilon'_2 \mathbf{E}_2 \tag{\label{equation-99}} \\
  \nabla\times\mathbf{E}_2 =& -j\omega\mu_2 \mathbf{H}_2 - \mathbf{M}_2 \tag{\label{equation-100}}
\end{align*}
$$

where $\varepsilon'_2 = \varepsilon_2 - j\sigma_2 /\omega$ is the complex permittivity of the medium. 

The electric and magnetic systems are dual if the transformations below map one set of Maxwell equations into the other:
- $\mathbf{M}_2 \leftrightarrow \mathbf{J}_1$
- $\varepsilon'_2 \leftrightarrow \mu_1$
- $\mu_2 \leftrightarrow \varepsilon'_1$
- $\mathbf{E}_2 \leftrightarrow -\mathbf{H}_1$
- $\mathbf{H}_2 \leftrightarrow \mathbf{E}_1$

Substituting $\mathbf{E}_2 = -\mathbf{H}_1$ and $\mathbf{H}_2 = \mathbf{E}_1$ into $\eqref{equation-99}$ and $\eqref{equation-100}$ yield

$$
\begin{align*}
 \nabla\times\mathbf{E}_1 =& j\omega\mu_1 (-\mathbf{H}_1) \\
 \nabla\times (-\mathbf{H}_1) =& -j\omega\epsilon'_1 \mathbf{E}_1 - \mathbf{J}_1
\end{align*}
$$

which are precisely the equations of the electric system $\eqref{equation-101}$ and $\eqref{equation-102}$. This shows that Maxwell's equations are invariant under the duality transformations. Consequently, if the sources of two systems are duals, 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{M}_2 (=) \mathbf{J}_1
\tag{\label{equation-105}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and the material parameters are duals

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mu_2 (=) \varepsilon'_1,\quad \varepsilon'_2 (=) \mu_1
\tag{\label{equation-104}}
\end{equation*}
$$

then the fields of system $2$ can be obtained from those of system $1$ by the substitutions

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{E}_2 (=) -\mathbf{H}_1,\quad \mathbf{H}_2 (=) \mathbf{E}_1
\tag{\label{equation-106}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Here the symbol $(=)$ indicates a duality substitution rather than an algebraic equality.

If magnetic conduction were allowed, a a magnetic conductivity $\sigma_m$ could be introduced through the complex permeability $\mu' = \mu - j(\sigma_m /\omega)$, which is the exact dual of the complex permeability $\varepsilon' = \varepsilon - j\sigma/\omega$. 

### Small current loop

The electromagnetic field of a small current loop may be obtained by applying duality to the field of an ideal electric dipole.

A small loop carrying electric current $I$ is equivalent to an ideal magnetic dipole. Let the dual magnetic-current element have magnetic current $I_m$ and lenght $\Delta z$. The source duality required by $\eqref{equation-105}$ is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  I_m (=) I_e
\tag{\label{equation-107}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $I_e$ is the current of an ideal electric dipole of length $\Delta z$. 

Since both systems are embedded in the same homogeneous medium, there are no material interfaces and hence no boundary conditions to transform. The duality substitutions of $\eqref{equation-104}$ therefore reduce to

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \mu_2 =& \mu (=) \varepsilon'_1 = \varepsilon \\
  \varepsilon'_2 = \varepsilon (=) \mu_1 = \mu
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-108}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The fields of an ideal electric dipole are

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{E}_1 =& E_{\theta 1} \unitsym{\theta} + E_{r1} \unitvec{r} \\
  \mathbf{H}_1 =& H_{\phi 1} \unitsym{\phi}
\end{align*}
$$

The fields of the dual magnetic dipole are then found from the duality transformation $\eqref{equation-106}$ as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{E}_2 (=) -\mathbf{H}_1 = -H_{\phi 1} \unitsym{\phi} \tag{\label{equation-109}} \\
  \mathbf{H}_2 (=) \mathbf{E}_1 = E_{\theta 1} \unitsym{\theta} + E_{r1} \unitvec{r} \tag{\label{equation-110}}
\end{align*}
$$

Using $\eqref{equation-107}$ and $\eqref{equation-108}$ in the ideal electric field expressions $\eqref{equation-45}$ and $\eqref{equation-46}$ together with $\eqref{equation-109}$ and $\eqref{equation-110}$ gives

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{E}_2 = \frac{I_m \Delta z}{4\pi} j\beta \left(1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} \right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\theta) \unitvec{\phi}
\tag{\label{equation-118}}
\end{equation*}
$$

and

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split*}
  \mathbf{H}_2 =& \frac{I_m \Delta z}{4\pi} j\omega\varepsilon \left(1 + \frac{1}{j\beta r} + \frac{1}{(j\beta r)^2} \right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \sin(\theta) \\
  &+ \frac{I_m \Delta z}{2\pi} j\omega\varepsilon \left(\frac{1}{j\beta r} + \frac{1}{(j\beta r)^2} \right) \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{r} \cos(\theta) \unitvec{r}
\end{split*}
\tag{\label{equation-119}}
\end{equation*}
$$

These are the complete near- and far-field expressions of an ideal magnetic dipole and, by equivalence, of a small current loop. In the radiation zone $\beta \gg 1$, only the terms proportional to $r^{-1}$ are retained, giving

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{E}_2 =& -I_m \Delta z j\beta \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\phi} \tag{\label{equation-115}} \\
  \mathbf{H}_2 =& I_m \Delta z j\omega\varepsilon \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta} \tag{\label{equation-116}}
\end{align*}
$$

## Electrically small dipoles

An antenna whose dimensions are small compared to the wavelength at the frequency of operation is an electrically small antenna. How much smaller than a wavelength an electrically small antenna must be depends on the application, but generally is taken to be on the order of a tenth of a wavelength in extent or less. Electrical size and physical size can be quite different. An antenna operating at low frequencies can be physically large but electrically small, i.e., a small fraction of a wavelength in extent.

The simplest practical electrically small antenna is the short dipole, which can be realized as a wire with a feed point in the center. The current distribution of the short dipole is nearly triangular in shape. This is because the current distribution on thin wire antennas ($\text{diameter} \ll \lambda$) is approximately sinusoidal and also must be zero at the wire ends. Because the arms of the short dipole are a fraction of a wavelength long, only a small portion of the sine wave current appears on the arm and is therefore nearly linear.

The decreasing current toward the wire ends requires that charges peel off and appear on the wire surface. Because the input current is changing sinusoidally with time, the current and charge distributions on the dipole do also. This charge accumulation leads to a displacement current density $j\omega\varepsilon\mathbf{E}$ in the space surrounding the dipole. The displacement current density, in turn, gives rise to an electromagnetic wave that propagates outward from the source. Displacement current in space couples a transmittingantenna to a receiving antenna, much as a conduction current provides coupling between lumped elements in a circuit. The radiation pattern of all forms of the electrically small dipole (with its radiating portion along the $z$-axis) is $\sin(\theta)$.

In the ideal dipole, all charge accumulates at the ends of the antenna. In fact, the ideal dipole can be analyzed as either a uniform current or two point charges oscillating at radian frequency $\omega$. The charge dipole model shows that charge accumulates at the ends of the antenna, leading to a higher radiation resistance. In fact, the ideal dipole radiation resistance $\eqref{equation-90}$ is four times that of the short dipole $\eqref{equation-95}$.

The input reactance of a short dipole is capacitive. This can be seen by visulizing the antenna as an open-circuited transmission line. When the distance from the end of the antenna to the feed point is much less than a quarter wavelenght, the input impedance is capacitive, because from transmission line theory the impedance a distance $s$ from an open-circuit termination is $-jZ_0 \cos(\beta s)$. Simple transmission line theory only gives qualitive results when radiation is present.

### Loaded short dipoles

The larger radiation resistance associated with the uniform current of the ideal dipole can be realized in practice by providing a mechanism for charge accumulation at the wire ends. One method of accomplishing this is to place metal plates at the ends of the wire. This is called a capacitor-plate antenna, or top-hat-loaded dipole antenna. If $\Delta z \ll\lambda$, the radial currents on the plates produce fields that almost cancel in the far field, since the currents are opposite-directed and the phase difference due to separation is small $(\beta\Delta z \ll 2\pi$). If, in addition, $\Delta z \ll \Delta r$, the plates provide for charge storage such that the current on the wire is constant. The capacitor-plate antenna then closely approximates the uniform current ideal dipole model. Frequently in practice, radial wires are used for the top loading in place of the the solid plate.

Another small antenna used to approximate the ideal dipole is the transmission line loaded antenna. The results of transmission line theory can be used to determine the current distribution. The current is essentially sinusoidal along the wire with a zero at the ends. If $\Delta z \\ L$, the horizontal wires provide an effective place for the charge to be stored and the current on the vertical section is nearly constant. Then radiation comes from a short section over which the current is nearly constant and the antenna approximates an ideal dipole.

The monopole form of the transmission line loaded dipole is called the inverted-L antenna (ILA). The small ILA has a total wire length that is much less than a wavelength. The reactance of the ILA antenna can be reduced by adding more horizontal wires.  As more horizontal wires are added, the reactance is further reduced and the structure approaches that of the capacitor plate antenna.

## Half-wave dipoles

The half-wave dipole is realized with a straight wire fed in the center. The simple and accurate model is that of a linear current whose amplitude distribution is sinusoidal with a maximum at the center. As a resonant antenna, it has zero input reactance as well as a desirable input resistance. The input impedance of an infinitely this dipole of exactly one-half wavelength is

$$
  Z_A = 73 + j42.5 \unit{\Omega}
$$

Tuning of a wire half-wave dipole antenna is accomplished by trimming the wire ends until a zero input reactance is achieved, with more shortening required for thicker wire diameter. The tuned half-wave dipole then has an input impedance of about $70 + j0\unit{\Omega}$. Thus, the half-wave dipole has the advantages of being naturally well-matched in impedance to common devices. Additionally, it has a radiation efficiency very close to $100\%$ for common wire materials.

The sinusoidal current distribution for the half-wave dipole along and centered on the $z$-axis is given by

$$
  I(z) = I_m \sin\left[\beta\left(\frac{\lambda}{4} - |z| \right) \right],\, |z| \leq \frac{\lambda}{4}
$$

where $\beta = 2\pi/\lambda$. This equation yields the required zero current condition at the ends of the dipole where $z = \pm\lambda / 4$ and a maximum value $I_m$ at the center ($z = 0$). The radiated electric field pattern is given by

$$
  E_\theta = j\omega\mu \frac{2I_m}{\beta} \frac{e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \frac{\cos[(\pi/2)\cos(\theta)]}{\sin^2 (\theta)}
$$

where we identify the element factor $g(\theta) = \sin(\theta)$ and the normalized pattern factor

$$
  f(\theta) = \frac{\cos[(\theta/2) \cos(\theta)]}{\sin^2 (\theta)}
$$

Both $\g(\theta)$ and $f(\theta)$ have a maximum of unity for $\theta = \pi/2$. The complete far-field pattern, normalized to unity maximum is

$$
  F(\theta) = g(\theta) f(\theta) = \frac{\cos[(\pi/2) \cos(\theta)]}{\sin(\theta)}
$$

which is independent of $\phi$ due to azimuthal symmetry. Because of this, the radiation is uniform (in the $H$-plane) around the dipole.

The length of the half-wave dipole is large enough electrically that radiation contributions from current elements along its extent will have significant phase variation in the far field as a function of angle around the antenna due to the difference in path length from the current elements to the far field. In the broadside direction $(\theta = \pi/2)$ all path lengths are equal and there is full phasor addition and a pattern maximum. For other directions, there is partial cancellation due to phase differences between current elements that are as large as $180\deg$ from the dipole ends in the endfire directions ($\theta = 0,\pi$). The major influence on the endfire direction is the element factor $f(\theta) = \sin(\theta)$, which is zero caused by the vanishing projected area of the antenna in end view. As the dipole length is increased beyond a wavelength, the current element contributions in the far field are increasingly out of phase, leading to partial or total cancellation in the far field in the broadside direction and reinforcement in off-broadside directions, forming multiple lobes in the $E$-plane. This explains why practical center-fed wire antennas are less than a wavelength long.

## Monopoles

A monopole is a dipole that has been divided in half at its center feed point and fed against a ground plane. The currents and charges on a monopole are the same as on the upper half of its dipole counterpart, but the terminal voltage is only half that of the dipole. The voltage is half because the gap width of the input terminals is half that of the dipole, and the same electric field over half the distance gives half the voltage. The input impedance for a monopole is therefore half that of its dipole counterpart, or

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z_{A, \text{mono}} =& \frac{V_{A, \text{mono}}}{I_{A, \text{mono}}} \\
  =& \frac{(1/2) V_{A, \text{dipole}}}{I_{A, \text{dipole}}} = \frac{1}{2} Z_{A, \text{dipole}}
\end{align*}
$$

This is easily demonstrated for the radiation resistance. Because the fields only extend over a hemisphere, the power radiated is only half that of a dipole with the same current. Therefore, the radiation resistance of a monopole is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_{r, \text{mono}} =& \frac{P_\text{mono}}{(1/2) |I_{A, \text{mono}}|^2} \\
  =& \frac{(1/2) P_\text{dipole}}{(1/2) |I_{A, \text{dipole}}|^2} = \frac{1}{2} R_{r, \text{dipole}}
\end{align*}
$$

From $\eqref{equation-95}$, the radiation resistance of a monople is expressed

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_{r, \text{mono}} = 40\pi^2 \left(\frac{h}{\lambda} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega},\; h\ll\lambda
\tag{\label{equation-98}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $h$ is the length of the monopole and $\Delta z = 2h$.

The radiation pattern of a monopole above a perfect ground plane is the same as that of a dipole similarly positioned in free space since the fields above the image plane are the same. Therefore, a monopole fed against a perfect ground plane radiates one-half the total power of a similar dipole in free space because the power is distributed in the same fashion but only over half as much space. As a result, the beam solid angle of a monopole above a perfect ground plane is one-half that of a similar dipole in free space, leading to a doubling of the directivity

$$
\begin{align*}
  D_\text{mono} =& \frac{4}{\Omega_{A, \text{mono}}} \\
  =& \frac{4\pi}{(1/2) \Omega_{A, \text{dipole}}} = 2D_\text{dipole}
\end{align*}
$$

This can be shown in another way. If a dipole in free space has a maximum radiation intensity of $U_m$, a monopole of half the length above a perfect ground plane with the same current will have the same value $U_m$ because the fields are the same. The total radiated power from the dipole is $P$, so the power radiated from the monopole is $P/2$. From $\eqref{equation-75}$ the directivity for the two antennas is

$$
  D_\text{mono} = \frac{U_m}{(1/2) P/(4\pi)} =  2D_\text{dipole}
$$

The directivity increase does not come from an increase in the radiation intensity (and, hence, field intensity) but rather from a decrease in average radiation intensity. This, in turn, comes about because only half the power radiated by a dipole is radiated by a monopole.

The directivity of a quarter-wave monopole is twice that of a half-wave dipole in free space

$$
  D_{\lambda/4-\text{mono}} = 2(1.64) = 3.28 = 5.16 \unit{dB}
$$

The input impedance of an infinitesimally this quarter-wave monopole is

$$
\begin{align*}
  Z_{A, \lambda/4-\text{mono}} =& \frac{1}{2} (72 + j42.5) \unit{Omega} \\
  =& 36 + j21.3 \unit{\Omega}
\end{align*}
$$

For a top-loaded monopole antenna, the radiation resistance may be obtained by comparing the effective current moment of the monopole with that of a short dipole. Since the radiated power is proportional to the square of the current moment, the radiation resistance is proportional to the square of the area under the current-distribution curve $I(z)$. Assuming a sinusoidal current distribution,

$$
  I(z)= I_0\sin (\beta(h-z)), \; |z| \leq h
$$

the current moment is

$$
  \int_0^h I(z) \;\d z = \frac{I_0}{\beta}(1-\cos(\beta h))
$$

For a top-loaded monopole, however, the current is approximately uniform over the loading structure, and the resulting effective current moment can be expressed in terms of the feed current $I_0$ as

$$
  \frac{I_0}{\beta} (1 + \cos(\beta h)).
$$

Substituting this into $\eqref{equation-98}$

$$
  R_r = 40\pi^2 \left(\frac{h}{\lambda} \right)^2 (1 + \cos(\beta h))^2
$$

For a quarter-wave monopole, $\beta h = \pi/2$ and $\cos(\beta h) = 0$ and this reduces to $\eqref{equation-}$, which holds for a short monopole less than $0.15\lambda$ with zero current on top.

## Small loop antennas

A closed loop current whose maximum dimension is much smaller than the wavelength is called a *small loop antenna*. In this regime, which is typically less than a tenth of a wavelength, the current can be assumed uniform in amplitude and phase around the loop, and the loop behaves as an elementary magnetic dipole.

A fundamental result is that, in the electrically small limit, the radiated field depends only on the loop area and no on the detailed shape of the loop. Consequently, it is sufficient to consider a square loop of side length $\ell$, lying in the $xy$-plane and centered at the origin. The loop carries a uniform time-harmonic current $I$.

Each side of the loop is electrically short ($\ell\ll\lambda$) and can therefore be modeled as an ideal dipole. The pair of sides parallel to the $x$-axis produces an $x$-directed vector potential

$$
\begin{equation*}
  A_x = \frac{\mu I \ell}{4\pi} \left(\frac{e^{-j\beta R_1}}{R_1} - \frac{e^{-j\beta R_3}}{R_3} \right)
\tag{\label{equation-111}}
\end{equation*}
$$

while the pair of sides parallel to the $y$-axis produces the vector potential

$$
\begin{equation*}
  A_y = \frac{\mu I \ell}{4\pi} \left(\frac{e^{-j\beta R_2}}{R_2} - \frac{e^{-j\beta R_4}}{R_4} \right)
\tag{\label{equation-112}}
\end{equation*}
$$

In the radiation zone ($r \gg\ell$), amplitude variations across the loop can be neglected, so that $R_i \approx r$ for $i=1,\dots,4$ in the denominators. The phase differences, however, must be retained. Using the standard far-field approximation

$$
  R_i = r - \unitvec{r}\cdot \mathbf{r}_i
$$

where $\mathbf{r}_i$ denotes the position of the corresponding current element, we obtain

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_1 =& r + \frac{\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \\
  R_2 =& r - \frac{\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi) \\
  R_3 =& r - \frac{\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \\
  R_4 =& r - \frac{\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi)
\end{align*}
$$

Subtituting these into the vector potentials $\eqref{equation-111}$ and $\eqref{equation-112}$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  A_x =& \frac{\mu I\ell e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \left[\exp\left(-j\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \right) - \exp\left(j\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \right) \right] \\
  A_y =& \frac{\mu I\ell e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \left[\exp\left(-j\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi) \right) - \exp\left(j\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi) \right) \right]
\end{align*}
$$

Using

$
  e^{-jx} - e^{jx} = -2j \sin(x)
$

yields

$$
\begin{align*}
  A_x =& -2j \frac{\mu I\ell e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin\left(\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \right) \\
  A_y =& 2j \frac{\mu I\ell e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin\left(\frac{\beta\ell}{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi) \right)
\end{align*}
$$

Since the loop is electrically small compared to a wavelength

$$
  \beta\ell = \frac{2\pi\ell}{\lambda} \ll 1
$$ 

and therefore $\sin(x) \approx x$. Retaining only the leading-order term gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  A_x =& -j \frac{\mu I e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \beta\ell^{2} \sin(\theta) \sin(\phi) \\
  A_y =& -j \frac{\mu I e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \beta\ell^{2} \sin(\theta) \cos(\phi)
\end{align*}
$$

The total vector potential therefore becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf{A} =& A_x \unitvec{x} + A_y \unitvec{y} \\
  =& j\beta\ell^2 \frac{\mu I e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) (-\sin(\phi)\unitvec{x} + \cos(\phi) \unitvec{y})
\end{align*}
$$

The term in the parenthesis is exactly the unit vector $\unitsym{\phi}$, so

$$
  \mathbf{A} = j\beta S \frac{\mu I e^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\phi}
$$

where $S = \ell^2$ is the area of the loop. Because $\mathbf{A}$ is purely transverse in the radiation zone, the electric field is $-j\omega\mathbf{A}$, giving

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{E} = \eta\beta^2 S \frac{Ie^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\phi}
\tag{\labal{equation-113}}
\end{equation*}
$$

since $\omega\mu\beta = \omega^2 \mu\sqrt{\mu s} = \sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon} \omega^2 \mu\varepsilon = \eta\beta^2$. The radiation magnetic field is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\eta} \unitvec{r} \times\mathbf{E} = -\beta^2 S \frac{Ie^{-j\beta r}}{4\pi r} \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta}
\tag{\label{equation-114}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The complete field expressions for a small loop of magnetic moment $\mathbf{m} = IS$ are found from $\eqref{equation-118}$ and $\eqref{equation-119}$ using $\eqref{equation-117}$. Comparing $\eqref{equation-113}$ and $\eqref{equation-114}$ to the magnetic dipole radiation fields $\eqref{equation-115}$ and $\eqref{equation-116}$, we find that

$$
\begin{equation*}
  I_m \Delta z = j\omega\mu IS
\tag{\label{equation-117}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Equivalently, the small loop is characterized by the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m} = IS \unitvec{z}$. Thus, to leading order in $\beta\ell$, the electromagnetic fields of an electrically small loop depend only on its magnetic moment $IS$ and are independent of the detailed shape of the loop. Furthermore, the radiation pattern is identical to that of an infinitesimal electric dipole, differing only by the interchange of the electric and magnetic field roles dictated by electromagntic duality.

The small loop antenna, independent of its shape, has an omnidirectional torus-shaped radiation pattern, which equals that of an ideal electric dipole. The horizontal small loop (in the $xy$-plane) and vertical ($z$-direction) short dipole both have uniform radiation in the horizontal plane, but the loop provides horizontal polarization ($E_\phi$), whereas the dipole is vertically polarized ($E_\theta$).

### Antenna impedance

The impedance of a small loop antenna is quite different from its ideal dipole dual. Whereas the ideal dipole is capacitive, the small loop is inductive. 

The radiation resistance is found by calculating the power radiated using the small loop radiation fields with $\eqref{equation-72}$, which yields

$$
  P = 10I^2 (\beta^2 S)^2
$$

The radiation resistance is then

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_r =& \frac{2P}{I^2} = 20(\beta^2 S)^2 \\
  \approx& 31,200 \left(\frac{S}{\lambda^2} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega}
\end{align*}
$$

The radiation resistance of a loop antenna can be increased significantly by using multiple turns. The magnetic moment of an $N$ turn loop is $NIS$, where is $S$ is the area of single turn. The radiation resistance is then

$$
\begin{align*}
  R_r =& 20(\beta^2 NS)^2 \\
  \approx& 31,200 \left(\frac{NS}{\lambda^2} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega} \tag{\label{equation-120}}
\end{align*}
$$

which depends on the square of the number of turns, $N^$.

Provided the total wire length of the multitum loop is small compared to a wavelength, the current remains nearly constant over the wire length and previous small loop analysis applies so that the pattern is the same as a single turn loop.

Another common way to enhance the radiation resistance of a loop antenna is to wind the turns around a ferrite core forming the ferrite rod antenna (also called a loop-stick antenna or ferrite-core multiturn loop) which is used up to VHF frequencies, where usually losses become excessive.

The phase velocity is less than that of the ferrite core because it is of finite extent. Thus, the relative permeability of the bulk ferrite material is replaced by an effective (relative) permeability $\mu_\text{eff}$ and

$$
  \beta = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0} \sqrt{\mu_\text{eff}}
$$

Modifying $\eqref{equation-120}$ to include this effect gives the radiation resistance for a ferrite rod antenna as

$$
  R_r \approx 31,200 \left(N\mu_\text{eff} \frac{S}{\lambda^2} \right)^2 \unit{\Omega}
$$

The effective permeability depends not only on the permeability of the core material but also on the core geometry as a function of the relative core length (length-to-diameter ratio), $R$, in the following way

$$
  \mu_\text{eff} = \frac{\mu_r}{1 + D(\mu_r - 1)}
$$

where $D = 0.37 \unit{R^{-1.44}}$ is the demagnetization factor. This equation reveals that $\mu_\text{eff}$ is always less than $\mu_r$ but for longer/thinner cores (larger $R$ values) then $\mu_\text{eff}$ approaches $\mu_r$. This expression assumes the core is much longer than the winding on it. For other cases $\mu_\text{eff}$is reduced, reaching $0.7 D$ for a core equal in length to the winding.

Receivers for AM broadcast radio around $1 \unit{MHz}$ are a popular application for the ferrite rod antenna with typical values in a wide range around $50$ for $\mu_\text{eff}$ and $10$ for $R$. Ferrites being lossy eliminates them for use in transmitters.

Small loop antennas also have ohmic resistance. For a rectangular loop of wire by $\ell_1$ by $\ell_2$, the ohmic resistance of the wire is given approximately by

$$
  R_w = \frac{2\ell_1 \ell_2}{\pi(2a)^2} R_s \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{(\ell_1 /2a)^2 - 1}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{(\ell_2 /2a)^2 - 1}} \right)
$$

where $a$ is the wire radius and $R_s$ is the surface resistance $\eqref{equation-121}$. If $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ are much larger than $a$, then the ohmic resistance reduces to

$$
  R_w = \frac{2(\ell_1 + \ell_2)}{2\pi a} R_s
$$

This formula can be generalized to loops of arbitrary shape as follows

$$
  R_w = \frac{L_m}{2\pi a} R_s
$$

where $L_m$ is the mean length of the wire loop, i.e. the perimeter. For a circular loop this becomes

$$
  R_w = \frac{2\pi b}{2\pi a} R_s = \frac{b}{a}R_s
$$

where $b$ is the mean loop radius and $a$ is the wire radius.

The small loop antenna is inherently inductive. The inductance of a small by $\ell_1$ by $\ell_2$ rectangular loop is given by

$$
  L = \frac{\mu}{\pi} \left[\ell_2 \cosh^{-1} \left(\frac{\ell_1}{2a} + \ell_1 \cosh^{-1} \left(\frac{\ell_2}{2a} \right) \right) \right]
$$

For a small circular loop of radius $b$, the inductance for $a \ll b$ is

$$
  L = \mu b \left[\ln\left(\frac{8b}{a} \right) - 2 \right]
$$

The loss and inductance of an $N$-turn loop is factored by $N^2$. Ferrite rod antennas will also have losses in the ferrite core, but often they are smaller than coil losses.

Radiation resistance decreases much faster with decreasing frequency for a small loop ($R_f \propto f^{-4}$) than for a short dipole ($R_r \propto f^{-2}$). Multiturn loops are ised to increase radiation resistance. Wire losses can be reduced by decreasing the number of turns in a loop and using a ferrite core to maintain radiation resistance. In practice, a variable capacitor placed in parallel with the loop is used to tune out inductance.

## Two element arrays

The overall radiation characteristics of an antenna system can be controlled combining the fields radiated by multiple antenna elements. Such a collection radiating elements is called an antenna array, or simply an array. The simplest nontrivial example is the two-element array, which serves as the foundation for understanding more general array configurations.

On an introductory level, antenna arrays can be modelled in terms of idealized radiators known isotropic point sources (or simply isotropic radiators). An isotropic radiator is a hypothetical antenna that radiates equal power density in all directions in three-dimensional space. Equivalently, its radiation intensity is independent of direction. Although no physical antenna posesses this property, the isotropic radiator provides a convenient mathematical model that isolates the effects of array geometry and excitation from the directional characteristics of the individual antenna elements.

For an array composed of isotropic elements, the total radiated field is obtained by superposing the contributions from all elements. Since the fields are time-harmonic, this superposition is performed using complex phasors that account for both amplitude and phase. The resulting angular dependence of the field is determined entirely by the relative positions and excitations of the elements and is known as the array factor.

The term "factor" reflects an important property of antenna arrays. When the individual elements are not isotropic, the total radiation pattern can often by expressed as the product of two terms

$$
  \text{total pattern} = \text{element pattern} \times \text{array factor}
$$

This relationship, known as the pattern multiplication principle, allows the effects of the element radiation characteristics and the array geometry to be analyzed separately. The array factor describes the directional behavior arising solely from the spatial arrangement and excitation of the elements, while the element pattern accounts for the radiation properties of the individual antennas.

For definiteness, the discussion is presented from a transmitting perspective. However, by virtue of the reciprocity theorem, the directional characteristics of an antenna array are identical in transmission and reception. Consequently, every radiation pattern derived here also describes the corresponding receiving pattern of the array.

<MathBox title="Two isotropic point sources with equal amplitudes and phace, and one-half wavelength spacing" boxType="example">
Consider two isotropic point sources located on the $z$-axis and separated by a distance $d = \lambda/2$. The sources are excited with identical currents, so that their radiated fields have equal amplitudes and equal phases. The resulting radiation pattern in the far field can be found as follows.

Along the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the sources (the broadside direction, corresponding to the $x$-axis), the distances from the observation point to the two sources are equal. Consequently, the waves arrive with identical implitudes and phases. The fields therefore add constructively, and the resulting field amplitude is twice that produces by either source alone.

The situtation is different along the array axis ($z$-axis). Consider an observer located in the $+z$-direction. In the ar field, the wave radiated by the source at $z = -d/2$ travels an additional distance $d$ relative to the wave radiated by the source at $z = d/2$. This additional path length produces a phase difference

$$
  \Delta\phi = \beta d
$$

where $\beta = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the phase constant. Since $d = \lambda/2$, we get

$$
  \Delta\phi = \beta\frac{\lambda}{2} = \pi
$$

Thus, the two waves arrive $180^\deg$ out of phase. Because their amplitudes are equal, complete destructive interference occurs, and the total field vanishes in the $+z$-direction. By symmetry, the same cancellation occurs in the $-z$-direction.

Between the broadside and axial directions, the path-length difference varies continuously with observation angle. Consequently, the relative phase between the two contributions also varies continuously from $0$ in the broadside direction to $\pi$ in the axial directions. The resulting field magnitude there changes smoothly between its maximum value and zero.

The three-dimensional radiation pattern is rotationally symmetric about the array axis and has the shape of a torus, resembling the pattern of an infinitesimal electric dipole.

**Array factor**

Let the two isotropic sources be located at $z = \pm d/2$. For an observation point in the far field at polar angle $\theta$ (measured from the $+z$-direction), the far-field path difference associated with the source position is $\delta r = d \cos(\theta)$. Relative to the array center, the phase shifts of the two sources are therefore $\pm\beta (d/2) \cos(\theta)$. The array factor is obtained by summing the corresponding phasors

$$
\begin{align*}
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) =& \exp\left(-j\beta \frac{d}{2}\cos(\theta) \right) + \exp\left(j\beta \frac{d}{2}\cos(\theta) \right\right) \\
  =& 2 \cos\left(\beta\frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) \right) \tag{\label{equation-122}}
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting $d = \lambda/2$ gives

$$
  \beta\frac{d}{2} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\frac{\lambda}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

so that $\eqref{equation-121}$ can be written

$$
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) = 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \cos(\theta) \right)
$$

The maximum of the array factor occurs when $\cos(\theta) = 0$ or $\theta = \pi/2$ corresponding to the broadside direction. The array factor vanishes along the array axis for $\theta = 0, \pi$. This is the characteristic radiation pattern of a two-element broadside array with one-half wavelength spacing and equal in-phase excitation.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Two isotropic point sources with equal amplitudes, a $\pi$ phase difference, and one-half wavelength spacing" boxType="example" tag="1">
Consider two isotropic point sources located on the $z$-axis and separated by a distance $d = \lambda/2$. The sources are excited with equal current amplitudes and a relative excitation phase difference of $\pi$. In other words, the two sources radiate fields of equal amplitude but opposite phase. The resulting radiation pattern in the far field can be found as follows.

Along the perpendicular bisector of the array (the broadside direction, corresponding to the $x$-axis), the distances from the observation point to the two sources are equal. Consequently, the propagation phases of the two waves are identical. Since the sources themselves are excited $pi$ radians out of phase, the two fields arrive with equal amplitudes and opposite phases, producing complete destructive interference. Hence, the radiatedfield vanishes in the broadside direction.

Along the array axis in the $\pm z$-directions, the propagation path lengths differ by the element spacing $d$. For an observer in the $+z$-direction, the wave radiated by the source at $z = -d/2$ travels an additional distance $\delta r = d$ relative to the wave radiated by the source at $z = +d/2$. This additional path introduces a propagation phase shift

$$
  \beta d = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} {\lambda}{d} = \pi
$$

The propagation phase difference exactly compensates for the $\pi$ excitation phase difference between the sources, yielding a zero net phase difference. Thus, the two waves arrive in phase and interfere constuctively. By symmetry, the same argument applies in the $-z$-direction, so the array exhibits a maxima along both directions of the array axis.

As the observation angle varies from the broadside direction to the array axis, the propagation phase changes continuously, producing a smooth transition from complete destructive interference to complete constructive interference. The resulting three-dimensional radiation pattern is rotationally symmetric about the $z$-axis and has the characteristic dumbbell shape of an end-fire array.

**Array factor**

Let the two isotropic sources be located at $z = \pm d/2$. The far-field phase associated with the source location is $\pm\beta (d/2) \cos(\theta)$. Since the two sources are excited $\pi$ out-of-phase, one excitation acquires an additional phase factor of $e^{j\pi} = -1$. The array factor is therefore

$$
\begin{align*}
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) =& -\exp\left(-j\beta \frac{d}{2}\cos(\theta) \right) + \exp\left(j\beta \frac{d}{2}\cos(\theta) \right) \\
  =& 2j \sin\left(\beta\frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) \right) \tag{\label{equation-123}}
\end{align*}
$$

The imaginary factor $j$ represents a constant phase shift and has no effect on the radiation pattern, since the pattern depends only on hte magnitude of the array factor.

Substituting $d = \lambda/2$ gives

$$
  \beta\frac{d}{2} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\frac{\lambda}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

so that $\eqref{equation-123}$ can be written

$$
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) = 2j\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \cos(\theta) \right)
$$

The corresponding normalized array factor is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  f(\theta) = \left| \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \cos(\theta)\right) \right|
\tag{\label{equation-125}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The array factor has a maximum when $\cos(\theta) = 1$ or $\theta = 0, \pi$, corresponding to the end-fire directions. Moreover, the array factor vanishes when $\theta = \pi/2$ corresponding to the broadsise direction.
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="Two isotropic point sources with equal amplitude, a $\pi/2$ phase difference, and one-quarter wavelength spacing" boxType="example">
Consider two isotropic point sources located on the $z$-axis at positions $z = \pm d/2$, where the element spacing is $d = \lambda/4$. The sources are excited with equal current amplitudes and a relative excitation phase difference of $\pi/2$.

To describe the resulting radiation pattern, consider the first end-fire direction ($\theta = 0$, corresponding to the $+z$-direction). The wave radiated by the source at $z = -d/2$ travels an additional distance $d = \lambda/4$ before reaching the observer, introducing a propagation phase delay

$$
  \beta d = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \frac{\lambda}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

Since the second source also lags the first by an excitation phase of $\pi/2$, the propagation delay exactly compensates for the excitation phase difference. The two waves therefore arrive at the observer in phase and interfere constructively, producing a maximum in the $+z$ direction.

In the opposite end-fire direction ($\theta = \pi$), the propagation difference has the opposite sign. The total relative phase between the waves is therefore

$$
  -\frac{\pi}{2} + \frac{\pi}{2} = \pi
$$

so the fields arrive with equal amplitudes and opposite phases. Complete destructive interference occurs, producing a null in the $-z$-direction.

Between the two end-fire directions, the propagation phase varies continuously with the observation angle, causing the field magnitude to vary smoothly between its maximum and minimum values. The resulting radiation pattern is ssymmetric about the array axis and the characteristic cardioid shape. Cardioid patterns are widely used in acoustics, particularly for directional microphones, because they provide high sensitivity in one direction while strongly suppressing signals arriving from the opposite direction.

**Array factor**

The far-field phase associated with the source location is $\pm\beta (d/2) \cos(\theta)$. Since the two sources are excited $\pi/2$ out-of-phase, one excitation acquires an additional phase factor of $e^{j\pi/2}$. The array factor is therefore

$$
\begin{align*}
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) =& \exp\left(-j\beta \frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) \right) + \exp\left(-j \frac{\pi}{2} \right) \exp\left(j\beta \frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) \right) \\
  =& \exp\left(-j \frac{\pi}{4} \right) \exp[\exp\left(-j \left[\beta\frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) - \frac{\pi}{4} \right] \right) + \exp\left(j \left[\beta\frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) - \frac{\pi}{4} \right] \right) \right] \\
  =& \exp\left(-j\frac{\pi}{4} \right) 2\cos\left(\beta \frac{d}{2} \cos(\theta) - \frac{\pi}{4} \right) \tag{\label{equation-124}}
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting $d = \lambda/4$ gives

$$
  \beta \frac{d}{2} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\frac{\lambda}{8} = \frac{\pi}{4}
$$

so that $\eqref{equation-124}$ becomes

$$
  \operatorname{AF}(\theta) = 2 e^{-j\pi/2} \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} [\cos(\theta) - 1] \right)
$$

The constant phase factor $e^{-j\pi/4}$ has no effect on the radiation pattern, since only the magnitude of the array factor is physically significant. Normalizing by its value yields

$$
  f(\theta) = \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} [\cos(\theta) - 1] \right)
$$

which satisfies
- $f(0) = 1$ (maximum)
- $f(\pi/2) = \cos(\pi/4) = 1/\sqrt{2}$
- $f(\pi) = 0$
</MathBox>

<MathBox title="A half-wave dipole, one-quarter wavelength in front of a ground plane" boxType="example">
Let $\lambda$ denote wavelength. Consider a half-wave dipole oriented parallel to a ground plane and located at a height $h = \lambda/4$ above the surface. The dipole axis is taken to be parallel to the $x$-axis, while the ground plane coincides with the plane $z = 0$.

Using image theory, the ground plane can be replaced by an image dipole located at a distance $h$ below the plane. Since the electric dipole is parallel to the conducting surface, the image current has the same magnitude as the source current by in reversed direction. Consequently, the image excitation differs from that of the source by a phase of $\pi$. The original problem is therefore equivalent, in the region $z > 0$, to a two-element array of parallel half-wave dipoles separated by $d = 2h = \lambda/2$ with equal excitation amplitudes and a relative phase difference of $\pi$. This precisely the array configuration analyzed in Example $\ref{example-1}$, and the corresponding normalized array factor is therefore given by $\eqref{equation-125}$. The element is a half-wave dipole, but not oriented along the $z$-axis, but along the $x$-axis. The normalized element pattern is given by

$$
  g(\theta, \phi) = \frac{\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \sin(\theta) \right) \cos(\phi)}{\sqrt{1 - \sin^2 (\theta) \cos^2 (\phase)}}
$$

By the pattern multiplication principle, the normalized array pattern is

$$
\begin{align*}
  F(\theta, \phi) =& g(\theta, \phi) f(\theta) \\
  =& \frac{\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \sin(\theta) \right) \cos(\phi)}{\sqrt{1 - \sin^2 (\theta) \cos^2 (\phi)}} \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \cos(\theta) \right) \tag{\label{equation-126}}
\end{align*}
$$

The array factor introduces complete destructive interference in the lower half-space, while the element pattern determines the angular variation within the radiating half-space. Together, they produce a unidirectional radiation pattern directed away from the conducting plane. Thus, a single dipole placed above a ground plane exhibits the directional characteristics of a two-element array.

The principal-plane beamwidths are unequal because the element pattern is anisotropic. In particular, the $E$-plane beamwidth is narrower than the $H$-plane beamwidth, with typical values
- $\operatorname{HP}_\text{E} = 72^\deg$
- $\operatorname{HP}_\text{H} = 120^\deg$

The directivity $D$ is obtained from the normalized power pattern by computing the beam solid angle

$$
  \Omega_A = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi |F(\theta, \phi)|^2 \sin(\theta) \;\d\theta \;\d\phi
$$

and then using $D = 4\pi$. Evaluating the integral gives

$$
  D \approx 5.62 \approx 7.5 \unit{dBi}
$$

This represents a substantial increase over the directivity of an isolated half-wave dipole

$$
  D \approx 1.64 \approx 2.15 \unit{dBi}
$$

because the image dipole produces constructive interference in the forward hemisphere while the conducting plane supresses radiation into the lower half-space. The resulting field distribution is therefore identical to that of a properly phased two-element array, even though only a single physical antenna is present.
</MathBox>

# Receiving properties of antennas

A receiving antenna converts converts incident electromagnetic radiation into terminal voltage and currect at the connected transmission line. The received power can be equivalently expressed in terms of the incident electric field intensity or the incident power density.

In the incident field approach, the receiving antenna converts the incident electric field $\mathbf{E}_i$ to an open-circuit voltage $V_A$ characterized by the effective length $\mathbf{h}(\theta, \phi)$ through the relation

$$
  V_a = \mathbf{E}_i \cdot \mathbf{h}^*
$$

This expression can also be derived from basic electromagnetics using reciprocity. In terms of the polarization mismatch angle $\alpha_p$ between $\mathbf{E}_i$ and $\mathbf{h}$, the output voltage can be written

$$
  V_A = \norm{\mathbf{E}_i} \cdot \norm{\mathbf{h}} \cos(\alpha_p)
$$

Thus, maximum output voltage occurs when the wave arrives from a direction of peak receive antenna pattern response and is matched to the polarization of the receive antenna.

For a linearly polarized plane wave with electric field amplitude $E_i$, the effective length is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  h = \frac{V_A}{E_i}
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

<MathBox title="Effective length of an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
The radiation electric field of an ideal dipole is given by

$$
  \mathbf{E} = i\frac{\omega\mu I}{4\pi} \frac{e^{-i\beta r}}{r} \Delta z \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta}
$$

Since $\mathbf{h}$ contains information on the size of the antenna and the angular dependence of the radiation pattern, we can define 

$$
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbf{h} := \Delta z \sin(\theta) \unitsym{\theta}
\tag{\label{equation-6}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Note that the dimension of $\mathbf{h}$ is length, and for the ideal dipole, the effective length is the same as the projection of the physical length viewed from angle $\theta$. This is not, however, true in general. Polarization information for the wave and antenna are contained in $\mathbf{E}_i$ and $\mathbf{h}$, respectively.
</MathBox>

In the power density approach, the receiving antenna converts the incident power density $S$ into received power by the "collecting area" of the receiving antenna called *maximum effective aperture* $A_\text{em}$ as expressed by the defining relation

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_\text{rm} = S A_\text{em}
\tag{\label{equation-3}}
\end{equation*}
$$

The maximum available received power $P_\text{rm}$ is attained when the antenna is oriented for peak response, is polarization-matched to the wave, and is impedance-matched to its load.

We can now combine the two antenna-wave interaction relations. A receive antenna can be described by the circuit given in Figure 
$\ref{figure-1}$. Let the antenna impedance be $Z_A = R_A + jX_A$ and load impedance be $Z_L = R_L + jX_L$. By $\ref{figure-1}$, the received power is given by

$$
  P_\text{rm} = \frac{1}{2} |I_A|^2 R_L = \frac{1}{2} \frac{|V_A|^2}{(R_A + R_L)^2 + (X_A + X_L)^2} R_L
$$

Maximum power will be received when the load impedance is conjugate-matched to the antenna impedance

$$
  Z_L = R_L + j X_L = R_A - j X_A
$$

For a conjugate match, this reduces to

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_\text{rm} = \frac{1}{8} \frac{|V_A|^2}{R_A}
\tag{\label{equation-4}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Using the voltage for the case of a linearly polarized antenna from $\eqref{equation-2}$ for simplicity in $\eqref{equation-4}$ and equating to $\eqref{equation-3}$ yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  P_\text{rm} =& \frac{1}{8}\frac{|V_A|^2}{R_A} \\
  =& \frac{1}{8} \frac{|E_i h|^2}{R_A} = SA_\text{em} \\
  =& \frac{1}{2} \frac{|E_i|^2}{\eta} A_\text{em}
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-5}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the power density expression for the incoming wave is

$$
  S = \frac{1}{2} |\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*| = \frac{1}{2}{|E_i|^2}{\eta}
$$

Solving $\eqref{equation-5}$ for $h$ results in

$$
  h = 2\sqrt{\frac{R_A A_\text{em}}{\eta}}
$$

In practice, effective length is used is association with linear antennas such as dipoles and effective aperture is used with aperture antennas such as parabolic reflectors.

<MathBox title="Maximum effective aperture of an ideal dipole" boxType="example">
The maximum effective aperture for the ideal dipole is found using $\eqref{equation-4}$ and $\eqref{equation-5}$ with $\eqref{equation-6}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  A_\text{em} =& \frac{P_\text{rm}}{S} = \frac{\frac{1}{8} \frac{|V_A|^2}{R_r}}{\frac{1}{2}\frac{|E_i|^2}{\eta}} \\
  =& \frac{1}{4} \frac{\eta}{R_r} {|E_i|^2 (\Delta z)^2}{|E_i|^2} \\
  =& \frac{1}{4} \frac{\eta}{R_r} (\Delta z)^2 \\
  =& \frac{1}{4} \frac{\eta (\Delta z)^2}{\eta \frac{2}{3} \pi \left(\frac{\Delta z}{\lambda}\right)^2} \\
  =& \frac{3}{8\pi} \lambda^2
\end{split}
\tag{\label{equation-7}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This result is counterintuitive because it shows the the maximum effective aperture of an ideal dipole is independent of its length $\Delta z$ (as long as $\Delta z \ll \lambda$). However, the dipole length cannot be reduced arbitrarily without penalty. Here $R_r$ is proportional to $(\Delta z/\lambda)^2$ so that even though $A_\text{em}$ remains constant as the dipole is shortened, its radiation resistance decreases rapidly and the maximum effective aperture will probably not be realized because of the difficulty in achieving the required conjugate impedance match of the receiver to the antenna.
</MathBox>

Another quantity characterizing a receiving antenna is *antenna factor* $K$, defined as

$$
\begin{equation*}
  K = \frac{E_i}{V_L}
\tag{\label{equation-8}}
\end{equation*}
$$

which gives the output voltage $V_L$ across a load. It includes impedance mismatches but not transmission line losses. If a high-impedance load is used, the voltage in $\eqref{equation-8}$ is an open-circuit voltage and equals the inverse of the effective length in $\eqref{equation-2}$, i.e. $K = h^{-1}$.

# Antenna noise

Any material in the environment surrounding an antenna radiate electromagnetic waves, as dictated by blackbody radiation. The antenna collects this noisy radiation through its antenna pattern. Because thermal noise is incoherent, the contributions from different directions add in power rather than in field amplitude, producing a noise contribution at the antenna output in addition to the desired signal.

From noise theory, the available noise power $P_\text{N}$ delivered by a noise resistor at temperature $T$ in a bandwidth $\Delta f$ is

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_\text{N} = kT\Delta T
\tag{\label{equation-10}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ JK}^{-1}$ is Boltzmann's constant. This is known as *Nyquist noise* or *Johnson noise*.

The antenna noise contribution is computed by determining the antenna temperature $T_A$. This is not the physical temperature of the antenna itself, but rather an equivalent noise temperature defined such that the noise power available at the antenna output is the same as if the antenna were connected to a matched thermal source at temperature $T_A$. Thus, $T_A$ characterizes the effective noise environment as seen through the antenna pattern.

The antenna temperature is computed by averaging the environment temperature distribution $T(\theta, \phi)$, weighted by the normalized antenna power pattern $P_n (\theta, \phi)$

$$
\begin{equation*}
  T_A = \frac{1}{\Omega_A} \int_0^\pi \int_0^{2\pi} T(\theta, \phi) P_n (\theta, \phi) \;\d\Omega
\tag{\label{equation-9}}
\end{equation*}
$$

Expectedly, if the scene is of constant temperature $T_o$ over all angles, then $T_A = T_o$. In this csae the antenna is completely surrounded by noise of temperature $T_o$ and its output antenna temperature equals $T_o$ independent of the antenna patttern shape. Consider the case of a discrete source of small solid angular extent $\Omega_s$ with constant temperature $T_s$ and observed with an antenna beam directed toward its center that is broad compared to the source. Then $P_n (\theta, \phi) \approx 1$ over the source and $\eqref{equation-9}$ reduces to

$$
  T_A = \frac{\Omega_s}{T_A} T_s
$$

For a circularly symmetric source or circularly symmetric antenna beam, the solid angles $\Omega_s$ and $\Omega_A$ are proportional to the angular extent of the source squared and $\text{HP}^2$, respectively.

The environmental temperature distribution $T(\theta, \phi)$ generally consists of two components: ground noise and sky noise. The noise temperature of ground in most situations is well approximated by $290 \text{ K}$, but is much less for reflecting surfaces and smooth surfaces at or near grazing incidence angles.

Unlike ground noise, sky noise is a strong function of frequency and is made up of atmospheric, cosmic and manmande noise.
- Below about $10 \text{ MHz}$ external noise is very strong and usually exceeds the internal noise of a receiver, so antenna efficiency and directivity have litte effect on signal-to-noise ratio.
- From $10$ to $100 \text{ MHz}$, natural noise is a combination of atmospheric and cosmic noise.
- From $100 \text{ MHz}$ to $1 \text{ GHz}$, cosmic noise dominates.
- The band between $1$ and $10 \text{ GHz}$ is the lowest noise regime and noise increases with decreasing elevation angle due to the finite heigh of the atmosphere with the path length through the atmosphere becoming lower for elevation angles. This makes this frequency region desireable for many wireleess applications. Also evident in this band is the irreducible sky background temperature of $3K$, which is theorized to be the residual effect of big bang.
- Above $10 \text{ GHz}$ atmospheric noise again dominates, increasing with frequency due to water vapor and hydrometer absorption, which vary with season and location. Atmospheric gases also are noise sources and include strong, broad spectral lines.

The total system noise power is the sum of the antenna output noise and the input receiver noise power. The noise temperatures add also and the total system noise temperature is found as

$$
  T_\text{sys} = T_A + T_r
$$

where $T_r$ is the receiver input noise temperature.

## Carrier-to-noise ratio (CNR)

In most communications and active remote-sensing systems, the noise power must be sufficiently below the signal power for proper operation. In these cases, the system is evaluated using *carrier-to-noise ratio* (CNR), whic is the ratio of the signal power to the system noise power.

$$
  \mathrm{CNR} = \frac{P_D}{P_{\text{N, sys}}}
$$

where
- $P_D$ is the carrier power delivered from the antenna $[\text{W}]$
- $P_{\text{N, sys}}$ is system noise power $[\text{W}]$

The parameter closely related to CNR is signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) which uses the signal power after demodulation.

## Radiometry

A radiometer is a receiving system used to measure noise in passive remote-sensing applications. It pick up noise from "hot" objects and can form an image by scanning through a noise scene, usually with a narrow-beam antenna. Extraterrestial noise is quantified in a unit called a *jansky* ($= 10^{-26} \text{ Wm}^{-2} \text{Hz}^{-1})$ in honor of Karl Jansky (1905-1950), who first measured extraterrestial noise during and accidental radio astronomy observation in $1933$.

# Radar

If the distance between the antenna and the target in the radar channel is sufficiently large, then both objects lie in each other's far field. The far field is the region in which the radiated or received field can be approximated by a locally plane wave. For an antenna with aperture of maximum geometric extent $A$, the far field distance of its radiated field is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
  R_{\text{FF}} = \frac{2A}{\lambda}
\tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\lambda$ denotes the free-space wavelength.

## Radar equation

The radar equation relates the range of the target, the transmitted power and the characteristics of the targets to the received power.

### Point targets

When deriving the radar equation for point targets, it is assumed that there is exactly one target in the radar channel between the transmit and receive antennas. Consider a radar system with a single transmitting antenna radiating a power $P_\text{tx}$. For an isotropic antenna, 

If  is radiated by the transmit antenna and is distributed isotropically on a spherical surface expanding at the speed of light $c_0$ around the antenna. This leads to the isotropic power density

$$
  S_{\text{tx}, i} = \frac{P_\text{tx}}{4\pi r^2}
$$

Since real antennas do not radiate isotropically, but are focused with the gain $G_\text{tx}$ in the main beam direction, the power density is

$$
  S_\text{tx} = \frac{P_\text{tx} G_\text{tx}}{4\pi r^2}
$$

The target is assumed to be in the main beam direction of the antenna. The wave propagates from the transmitter to the target. This range between transmitter and target is $R$. The actual power density at the target is therefore

$$
  S_\text{tx} = \frac{P_\text{tx} G_\text{tx}}{4\pi R^2}
$$

This power is scattered back from the target object. The property of the target that describes this backscatter is called the radar-cross section (RCS) $\sigma_\text{rcs}$ and has the unit of an area. The backscattered power is given by

$$
  P_s = S_\text{tx} \sigma_\text{rcs}
$$

This backscattering now again leads to a wave that propagates spherically from the target in all spatial directions. This results in the power density

$$
  S_s = \frac{P_s}{4\pi R_s^2} = \frac{P_\text{tx} G_\text{tx}}{4\pi R^2} \sigma_\text{rcs} \frac{1}{4\pi R_s^2}
$$

of the backscattered wave at a range $R_s$ from the target object. If the transmitter and the receiver are at the same location, the power denisty with $R_s = R$ at the receiver is

$$
  S_s = \frac{P_\text{tx} G_\text{tx}}{(4\pi R^2)^2} \sigma_\text{rcs}
$$

The waves with this power density are received by the receive antenna. With the effective antenna are $A_\text{e}$, the following applies to the received power $P_\text{rx}$

$$
  P_\text{tx} = A_\text{e, rx} S_s
$$

Adding the gain of the receive antenna

$$
  G_\text{rx} = \frac{4\pi A_\text{e, rx}}{\lambda^2}
$$

the receive power becomes

$$
\begin{equation*}
  P_\text{rx} = P_\text{tx} \frac{G_\text{tx} G_\text{rx} \lambda^2}{(4\pi)^3 R^4} \sigma_\text{rcs}
\tag{\label{equation-2}}
\end{equation*}
$$

This equation is known as the radar equation for point targets. The minimum received power $P_\text{rx, min}$ that can still be processed is often known for radar sensors. This allows for the maximum range $R_\text{max}$ of a radar to be calculateada for a given transmit power

$$
  R_\text{max} = \sqrt[4]{P_\text{tx} \frac{G_\text{tx} G_\text{rx} \lambda^2}{(4\pi)^3 P_\text{rx, min}}}
$$

As can be seen, the receive power of a radar sensor decreases with the fourth power of the range $R$ between the target and the sensor. Doubling the range leads to a reduction in receive power by a factor of $16$. This is due to the two spherical wave, which propagate between the transmitter and the target with $1/R^2$ each and a product in $\eqref{equation-2}$ ultimately lead to $1/R^4$. The resulting strong attenuation of the signal in the channel between the radar sensor and the target means that a radar sensor must be able to process very weak signals. If very different ranges occur in an application, the radar channel is highly dynamic. The dynamics of the radar channel is the ratio of the maximum received power from targets at close range to the minimum received power for targets at maximum range.
