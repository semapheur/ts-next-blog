# Mathematical foundation

## Dirac notation (bra-ket)

- A <b>ket</b> $|v\rangle$ denotes a vector representing the state of a quantum system
- A <b>bra</b> $\langle f|$ denotes a linear form $f : V \mapsto \mathbb{C}$ and is a covector to $|f\rangle$
- A <b>bra-ket</b> $\langle f | v\rangle$ denotes an innerproduct

Bra-ket properties
- Conjugate symmetry: $\langle \psi | \phi \rangle = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle^*$
- Linearity in the second argument: $\langle \psi | a_1\phi_1 + a_2 \phi_2 \rangle = a_1\langle \psi | \phi_1 \rangle + a_2\langle \psi | \phi_2 \rangle$
- Anti-linearity in the first argument: $\langle a_1\psi_1 + a_2\psi_2 | \phi \rangle = a_1^*\langle \psi_1 | \phi \rangle + a_2^*\langle \psi_2 | \phi \rangle$
- Positive definitive: $\langle \psi | \psi \rangle \geq 0$ and $\langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 0 \iff |\psi\rangle = 0$
- Triangle inequality: $\sqrt{\langle \psi + \phi | \psi + \phi \rangle} \leq \sqrt{\langle \psi | \psi \rangle} + \sqrt{\langle \phi | \phi \rangle}$
- Schwarz inequality: $\left[\langle \psi | \phi \rangle\right]^2 \leq \left[\langle \psi | \psi \rangle \right] \left[\langle \phi | \phi \rangle \right]$
- Orthogonality: $\langle \psi | \phi \rangle = 0$ iff $\psi$ and $\phi$ are orthogonal
- Hermitian conjugate/adjoint: 
$$
\begin{align*}
  |\psi\rangle^\dagger &= \langle\psi| \\
  \langle\phi|^\dagger &= |\phi\rangle
\end{align*}
$$

## Linear operators

A linear operator is a mapping between two vector spaces that is preserved under vector addition and scalar multiplication

- Additivity: 
  - $\hat{A}\left( | \phi_1 \rangle + | \phi_2 \rangle \right) = \hat{A} | \phi_1 \rangle + \hat{A} | \phi_2 \rangle$
    - $\left( \langle \psi_1 | + \langle \psi_2 | \right)\hat{A} = \langle \psi_1 | \hat{A} + \langle \psi_2 | \hat{A}$
- Scalar multiplication
  - $\hat{A} | a\phi \rangle = a\hat{A} | \phi \rangle$
  - $ \left( \langle \psi | a \right) \hat{A} = a \langle \psi | \hat{A}$

The outer product $| \psi \rangle \langle \phi |$ is a linear operator.

Operator properties
- Anticommutative: $\hat{A}\hat{B} \neq \hat{B}\hat{A}$
- Associative: $\hat{A}\hat{B}\hat{C} = \hat{A}\left(\hat{B}\hat{C}\right) = (\hat{A}\hat{B})\hat{C}$
- Power property: $\left(\hat{A}\right)^n \left( \hat{A} \right)^m = \left(\hat{A}\right)^{n + m}$
- Inner product: $\langle \psi | \hat{A} | \phi \rangle = \langle \psi | \phi' \rangle \in \mathbb{C}$
- Expectation: $\langle \hat{A} \rangle = \frac{\langle \psi | \hat{A} | \phi \rangle}{\langle \psi | \psi \rangle}$

### Hermitian conjugate/adjoint

The Hermitian conjugate of an operator $\hat{A}$ is defined as 

$$
  \langle \varphi | \hat{A} \psi\rangle = \langle \hat{A}^\dagger \varphi | \psi \rangle
$$ 

or alternatively 

$$
  \langle \varphi | \hat{A} | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{A}^\dagger | \varphi \rangle^*
$$

Hermitian conjugate properties
- $\left( \hat{A}^\dagger \right)^\dagger = \hat{A}$
- $\left(\hat{A} + \hat{B}\right)^\dagger = \hat{A}^\dagger + \hat{B}^\dagger$
- $\left(\hat{A}\hat{B} \right)^\dagger = \hat{B}^\dagger\hat{A}^\dagger$
- $\left(\hat{A}|\psi\rangle\right)^\dagger = \langle\psi|\hat{A}^\dagger$
- $\left(|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|\right)^\dagger = |\phi\rangle\langle\psi|$
- $\langle a\hat{A}\psi| = a^* \langle\psi|\hat{A}^\dagger$

An operator is Hermitian (self-adjoint) if it is its own Hermitian conjugate

$$
  \hat{A} = \hat{A}^\dagger
$$

An operator is anti-Hermitian if it is the negative of its own Hermitian conjugate

$$
  \hat{A} = -\hat{A}^\dagger
$$

An operator is unitary if its Hermitian conjugate equals its inverse

$$
  \hat{A}^\dagger = \hat{A}^{-1}
$$
    
### Eigenvalues

The eigenvalue of an operator $\hat{A}$ is a scalar $\lambda$ satisfying

$$
  \hat{A} |\psi\rangle = \lambda |\psi\rangle
$$

where $|\psi\rangle$ is an eigenvector of $\hat{A}$ corresponding to $\lambda$. The collection of all of eigenvalues is called the spectrum of the operator $\hat{A}$. An operator with distinct eigenvalues is said to have a non-degenerate specturm. If two or more eigenvalues coincide, the spectrum becomes degenerate.

For a Hermitian operator $\hat{A}$, the eigenvalue equation satisfies

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \langle \psi_i | \hat{A} \psi_j \rangle &= \langle \hat{A} \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle \\
    \lambda_i^* \langle \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle &= \lambda \langle \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle
\end{aligned}\\
  \implies \left( \lambda_j^* - \lambda_j \right) \langle \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle = 0
$$

If $i = j$, then $\lambda_i = \lambda_i^*$ so that the eigenvalue is real. If $i \neq j$, then $\langle \psi_i | \psi_j \rangle = 0$ which shows that the eigenvectors are orthogonal.

The eigenvectors of a Hermitian operator are complete, meaning that they form an orthogonal basis. Any wavefunction $\psi$ can therefore be expanded in terms of the eigenvalues of a given operator $\hat{A}$

$$
  \psi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i^\infty a_i \phi_i (\mathbf{x})
$$

for some complex coefficients $a_n$. If the eigenvectors are normalized, forming an orthonormal basis, these coefficients are given by

$$
  \langle \phi_i | \psi \rangle = \sum_j a_j \langle \phi_i | \psi \rangle = \sum_j a_j \delta_{ij} = a_i
$$

The norm of a wavefunction is

$$
  \lVert \psi(\mathbf{x}) \rVert^2 = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = \left\langle \sum_i a_i \phi_i \middle| \sum_j a_j \phi_j  \right\rangle = \sum_i \sum_j a_i^* a_j \langle \phi_i | \phi_j \rangle = \sum_i \sum_j a_i^* a_j \delta_{ij} = \sum_i \left| a_i \right|^2
$$

### Commutator

The commutator of two operators $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$ is written

$$
  \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right] \equiv \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}
$$

while the anticommutator is written 

$$
  \left\{ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right\} \equiv \hat{A}\hat{B} + \hat{B}\hat{A}
$$

Commutator properties
- Self-commution: $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{A} \right] = 0$
- Antisymmetry: $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right] = -\left[ \hat{B}, \hat{A} \right]$
- Linearity: $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} + \hat{C} \right] = \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right] + \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{C} \right]$
- Hermitian conjugable: $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B}  \right]^\dagger = \left[ \hat{A}^\dagger, \hat{B}^\dagger \right]$
- Distributivity:
$$
\begin{align*}
  \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B}\hat{C} \right] &= \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B}  \right]\hat{C} + \hat{B}\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{C}  \right] \\
  \left[ \hat{A}\hat{B}, \hat{C} \right] &= \hat{A}\left[ \hat{B}, \hat{C} \right] + \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{C} \right]\hat{B}
\end{align*}
$$
- Jacobi identity: $\left[ \hat{A}, \left[ \hat{B}, \hat{C} \right] \right] + \left[ \hat{B}, \left[ \hat{C}, \hat{A} \right] \right] + \left[ \hat{C}, \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right] \right] = 0$
- Operators commute with scalars
- If $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$ Hermitian operators then
  * $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right]$ is anti-Hermitian
  * $ \left\{ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right\}$ is Hermitian
    
Two operators $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$ are said to commute if $\left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right] = 0$. Two commuting operators share the same eigenvectors. Suppose $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$ commute with common eigenvectors

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \hat{A}\phi_i (\mathbf{x}) = \lambda_i \phi_i (\mathbf{x}
\end{aligned}\quad
\begin{aligned}
  \hat{B}\phi_i (\mathbf{x}) = \mu_i \phi_i (\mathbf{x}
\end{aligned}
$$

then any function $\phi$ can be expanded in terms of these eigenstates $\psi = \sum_i a_i \phi_i$ giving

$$
  \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right]\psi = \sum_i a_i \left[ \hat{A}, \hat{B} \right]\phi_i = \sum_i a_i \left(\lambda_i \mu_i - \mu_i \lambda_i \right)\phi_i = 0 
$$

# Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics

## Postulates

1. Description of the state of a system: The state of an isolated physical system is represented, at a fixed time $t$, by a state vector $|\psi\rangle$ belonging to a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H} = L^2(M)$ of square normalisable functions, called the state space.
2. Description of physical quantities: Every measurable physical quantity $\mathcal{A}$ is described by a Hermitian operator $\hat{A}$ acting in the state space $\mathcal{H}$. This operator is an observable, meaning that its eigenvectors form a basis for $\mathcal{H}$.
3. Measurement of physical quantities: The result of measuring a physical quantity $\mathcal{A}$ must be one of the eigenvalues of the corresponding observable $\hat{A}$.
4. When the physical quantity $\mathcal{A}$ is measured on a system in a normalized state $|\psi\rangle$, the probability of obtaining an eigenvalue of the corresponding observable $\mathcal{A}$ is given by amplitude squared of the appropriate wave function.
5. Effect of measurement on the state: If the measurement of the physical quantity $\mathcal{A}$ on the system in the state $|\psi\rangle$ gives the eigenvector $a_n$, then the state of the system immediately after the measurement is the normalized projection of $|\psi\rangle$ on the eigensubspace associated with $a_n$

## Wave function

The state of a quantum particle is described by a complex-valued wavefunction $| \psi \rangle \equiv \psi(\mathbf{x}, t)$. The mod square of the wavefunction represents the probability density for the particle's position

$$
  P(\mathbf{x}, t) = |\psi(\mathbf{x}, t)|^2
$$

This is known as the Born rule. The probabilistic interpretation requires the wavefunction to be a square normalisable function

$$
  \int |\psi(\mathbf{x}, t)|^2 \mathrm{d}^3 x = \int \psi^* \psi \,\mathrm{d}^3x = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle < \infty
$$

The set of square normalisable functions forms the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H} \in L^2 (M)$, which is a vector space. This means that if $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ are possible states of the system, then so too is

$$
  \psi_3 (\mathbf{x}, t) = \alpha \psi_1 (\mathbf{x}, t) + \beta \psi_2 (\mathbf{x}, t) \quad \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C} 
$$

which is known as the principle of superposition.

## Schrödinger equation

The Schrödinger equation describes the time evolution of a state vector $\left|\psi(\mathbf{x}, t)\right\rangle$

$$
  i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi \rangle = \hat{H}|\psi(t)\rangle
$$

where $\hat{H}$ is the Hamiltonian. For a non-relativistic system the Hamiltonian is given by

$$
  \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(x)
$$

### Conservation of probability

The Schrödinger equation ensures that probability is conserved for a quantum system. The partial time derivative of the probability density is

$$
  \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} = \psi^* \frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial\psi^*}{\partial t}\psi
$$

Applying the Schrödinger equation on the partial time derivatives gives

$$
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} &= -\psi* \frac{i}{\hbar}\left( -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V \right)\psi + \psi* \frac{i}\psi \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V \right)\psi^* \\
  &= \frac{i\hbar}{2m}\left( \psi^* \nabla^2 \psi - \psi\nabla^2 \psi^* \right) + \frac{i}{\hbar}V\left(\psi\psi* - \psi*\psi \right) \\
  &= \frac{i\hbar}{2m}\nabla \cdot \left( \psi^* \nabla \psi - \psi\nabla \psi^* \right) \\
  &\equiv -\mathbf{J}
\end{align*}
$$

This can be rewritten as the continuity equation

$$
  \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0
$$

## Observables

In quantum mechanics, all observables are represented by Hermitian operators on the Hilbert space

- Momentum operator
$$
  \hat{\mathbf{p}} = -i\hbar \nabla
$$
- Angular momentum operator
$$
  \hat{\mathbf{L}} = -i\hbar \times \nabla
$$
- Hamiltonian (energy operator)
$$
  \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V\left(\hat{\mathbf{x}} \right)
$$

## Measurement

The possible measurements of an observable $\hat{O}$ in a quantum system lies in its spectrum of eigenvalues. The eigenvalues of $\hat{O}$ is given by

$$
  \hat{O}\phi_i = \lambda_i \phi_i
$$

The wavefunction $\psi$ of quantum system can thus be decomposed in terms of the orthonormal eigenstates of $\hat{O}$

$$
  \psi (\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i a_i \phi_i (\mathbf{x}) \quad \sum_i \left| a_i \right|^2 = 1
$$

Assuming that the spectrum is non-degenerate, the probability that the measurement yields the eigenstate $\lambda_i$ is given by the Born rule

$$
  P(\lambda_i) = \left| a_i \right|^2
$$

After the measurement, the wavefunction discontinuously collapses into the eigenvector $\phi_i$ corresponding to the measured eigenstate $\lambda_i$

$$
  \phi(\mathbf{x}) \rightarrow \phi_i (\mathbf{x})
$$

When the spectrum is degenerate, the probability is given by

$$
  P(\lambda) = \sum_{i|\lambda_i = \lambda} \left| a_i \right|^2
$$

in which case, the wavefunction collapses to

$$
  \psi(\mathbf{x}) \rightarrow C \sum_{i|\lambda_i = \lambda} a_i \phi_n
$$

where $C$ is the appropriate normalization factor.

### Expectation values

The expectation value of an observable $\hat{O}$ in a system of state $\psi$ is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left\langle \hat{O} \right\rangle_\psi = \left\langle \psi | \hat{O} \psi \right\rangle &= \left\langle \sum_i a_i \phi_i \middle| \sum_h a_j \hat{O} \phi_j \right\rangle \\
  &= \sum_i \sum_j a_i^* a_j \lambda_j \left\langle \phi_i | \phi_j \right\rangle \\
  &= \sum_i \sum_j a_i^* a_j \lambda_j \delta_{ij} \\
  &= \sum_i \left| a_i \right|^2 \lambda_i
\end{align*}
$$

## Heisenberg uncertainty principle

The uncertainty of an observable $\hat{O}$ in a state $\psi$ is denoted $\Delta_\psi O$ and is defined as

$$
\begin{align*}
  \left(\Delta_\psi O \right)^2 &= \left\langle \left( \hat{O} - \left\langle \hat{O} \right\rangle_\psi \right)^2 \right\rangle_\psi \\
  &= \left\langle \hat{O}^2 - 2\left\langle \hat{O} \right\rangle_\psi\hat{O} + \left\langle \hat{O} \right\rangle_\psi^2 \right\rangle_\psi \\
  &= \left\langle \hat{O}^2 \right\rangle_\psi - \left\langle \hat{O} \right\rangle_\psi^2 \\
  &= \left\langle \psi | \hat{O}^2 \psi \right\rangle - \left\langle \psi | \hat{O}\psi \right\rangle^2
\end{align*}
$$

The uncertainty represents the standard deviation of the state's probability distribution.

The Heisenberg uncertainty principle states that 

$$
  \Delta_\psi x \Delta_\psi p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}
$$

The principle can be derived by consider the one-parameter family of states defined by

$$
  \Psi_s (\mathbf{x}) = \left( \hat{p} - is\hat{x} \right)\psi(\mathbf{x}) \quad s \in \mathbf{R}
$$

with norm given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  0 \leq \left\langle \Psi_s | \Psi_s \right\rangle &= \left\langle \left( \hat{p} - is\hat{x} \right)\psi | \left( \hat{p} - is\hat{x} \right)\psi \right\rangle \\
  &= \left\langle \psi | \left( \hat{p} + is\hat{x} \right)\left( \hat{p} - is\hat{x} \right)\psi \right\rangle \\
  &= \left\langle \psi | \left( \hat{p}^2 + is\left[\hat{x}, \hat{p} \right] s^2\hat{x}^2 \right)\psi \right\rangle \\
  &= \left\langle \psi | \left( \hat{p}^2 - s\hbar + s^2\hat{x}^2 \right)\psi \right\rangle \\
  &= \left(\Delta_\psi p \right)^2 - s\hbar + s^2\left(\Delta_\psi x \right)^2
\end{align*}
$$

where the commutation $\left[\hat{x}, \hat{p} \right] = i\hbar$ is used. To satisfy the inequality, the derived quadratic equation in $s$

$$
  \left(\Delta_\psi p \right)^2 - s\hbar + s^2\left(\Delta_\psi x \right)^2 \geq 0
$$

has either one or no roots. This is only true if the discriminant is non-positive, i.e.

$$
\begin{gather*}
  4\left(\Delta_\psi x \right)^2\left(\Delta_\psi p \right)^2 \geq \hbar^2 \\
  \Delta_\psi x \Delta_\psi p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}
\end{gather*}
$$

which is the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

# Quantum particle in one dimension

## Free particle

For a particle moving in the absence of a potential, the time independent Schrödinger equation is the eigenvalue equation

$$
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 \psi}{\mathrm{d}x^2} = E\psi
$$

The solutions are 

$$
  \psi(x) = e^{ikx} \quad \forall k \in \mathbb{R}
$$

where $|k| = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$ is the wavenumber. The energy states are given by

$$
  E = \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2m}
$$

In the classical case, the energy equals the particle's kinetic energy $E_\textrm{classic} = \frac{p^2}{2m}$. This suggests that momentum in the quantum case is given by $p = \hbar k$. In terms of wavelength, the momentum can be expressed

$$
  |p| = \frac{2\pi\hbar}{\lambda}
$$

where $\lambda$ is the de Broglie wavelength of the particle. The wavefunction $\psi$ is not normalisable, meaning that $\psi$ cannot be interpreted as the state of the particle.

### Infinite potential well 

The infinite potential well is used as a plot device to model a spatially trapped quantum particle. This is achieved by setting

$$
  V(x) = \begin{cases} 0 &\quad 0 < x < L \\ \infty &\quad \textrm{otherwise} \end{cases}
$$

Inside the well, the time-independent Schrödinger equation is

$$
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 \psi}{\mathrm{d}x^2} = E\psi
$$

with boundary conditions $\psi(0) = \psi(L) = 0$. The general solution is given by

$$
  \psi(x) = Ae^{ikx} + Be^{ikx}
$$

which is the superposition of two states, with momentums $p_\pm = \pm \hbar k$. This configuration is thus similar to that of standing waves, bouncing backwards and forwards inside the well.

The boundary condition $\psi(0) = 0$ implies $B = -A$, so that the wavefunction must be of the form

$$
  \psi(x) = A\left(e^{ikx} - e^{-ikx} \right) = 2iA \sin kx
$$

When normalized, the wavefunction becomes

$$
  \psi(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin kx
$$

The boundary conditon $\psi(L) = 0$ implies that

$$
  \sin kL = 0 \implies k = \frac{\pi n}{L} \, , n \in \mathbb{Z}^+
$$

The energy states are given by

$$
  E_n = \frac{\left( \hbar \pi n \right)^2}{2m L^2}
$$

### Gaussian wavepacket

The time dependent Schrödinger equation for a free particle reads

$$
  i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}
$$

This is solved by the separable solution

$$
  \psi_k (x, t) = e^{iE_k t/\hbar}e^{ikx} \quad \forall k \in \mathbb{R}
$$

Although $\psi_k$ itself is non-normalisable, a valid solution can be formed from superpositions of $\psi_k$ with the Fourier transform

$$
  \psi(x, t) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty A(k) \psi_k (x,t) \mathrm{d}k
$$

An appropriate function for $A(k)$ is the Gaussian distribution

$$
  A(k) = \exp\left[-\frac{\left( k - k_0 \right)^2}{2\sigma} + \frac{k_0^2}{2\sigma} \right]
$$

The resulting wavefunction becomes

$$
\begin{align*}
  \psi(x, t) &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{k^2 - 2kk_0}{2\sigma} - i\frac{\hbar t k^2}{2m} + ikx \right)\mathrm{d}k \\
  &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp\left[ -\frac{\alpha}{2}\left(k - \frac{\beta}{\alpha} \right)^2 + \frac{\beta^2}{2\alpha} \right] \mathrm{d}k
\end{align*}
$$

with

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \alpha = \frac{1}{\sigma} + \frac{i\hbar t}{m}
\end{aligned}\quad
\begin{aligned}
  \beta = \frac{k_0}{\sigma} + ix
\end{aligned}\quad
$$

integrating gives

$$
  \psi(x, t) = \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\alpha (t)}}\exp\left[ -\frac{1}{2\alpha}\left( x - \frac{ik_0}{\sigma} \right)^2 \right]
$$

which is known as a Gaussian wavepacket. The probability density is given by

$$
\begin{align*}
  P(x, t) = |\psi(x, t)|^2 &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left[-\frac{1}{2\alpha}\left( x - ik_0/\sigma \right)^2 -\frac{1}{2\alpha^*}\left( x + ik_0/\sigma \right)^2 \right] \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2|\alpha|^2} \left[ \alpha^* \left( x - ik_0/\sigma \right)^2 + \alpha \left( x + ik_0/\sigma \right)^2 \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2|\alpha|^2} \left[ \left(\alpha + \alpha^* \right) \left( x^2 - \frac{k_0^2}{\sigma^2} \right) + i2\left(\alpha - \alpha^* \right)k_0 x  \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2|\alpha|^2} \left[ \frac{2}{\sigma} \left( x^2 - \frac{k_0^2}{\sigma^2} \right) - \frac{2}{\sigma}\frac{2\hbar k_0 t}{m} x \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \left[ \left( x^2 - \frac{k_0^2}{\sigma^2} \right) - 2\frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m} x \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \left[ \left( x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m}\right)^2 - \frac{k_0^2}{\sigma^2} - \left(\frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m}\right)^2 \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \left[ \left( x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m}\right)^2 - k_0^2 \left( \frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\hbar^2 t^2}{m^2}\right) \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C}{|\alpha|}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \left[ \left( x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m}\right)^2 - k_0^2 |\alpha|^2 \right]\right\} \\
  &= \frac{2\pi C e^{k_0^2/\sigma}}{|\alpha|}\exp\left[ -\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \left(x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m} \right)^2 \right]
\end{align*}
$$

where $C = \frac{e^{-k_0^2/\sigma}}{\sqrt{4\sigma\pi^3}}$ is the normalisation coefficient. Because $\alpha$ increases with time, the wavepacket spreads out, and because the spatial dependence is of the form $x - vt$ with $v = \frac{\hbar k_0}{m}$, it also shifts with time. This can be shown more rigorously by finding the expected value for position

$$
\begin{align*}
  \langle x \rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma|\alpha|^2}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty x \exp\left[-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2}\left( x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m} \right)^2 \right] \mathrm{d}x \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma|\alpha|^2}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \left(\tilde{x} + \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m} \right) \exp\left( -\frac{\tilde{x}^2}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \right)\mathrm{d}\tilde{x} \\
  &= \frac{\hbar k_0}{m}t
\end{align*}
$$

This suggests that the momentum is $p = mv = \hbar k_0$. This can be show by calculating the expected value for the momentum operator $p = -i\hbar\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \langle p \rangle &= -i\hbar \int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathrm{d}x \, \frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi|^2}{\mathrm{d}x} = -i\hbar \int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathrm{d}x \, \psi^* \frac{\mathrm{d}\psi}{\mathrm{d}x} \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma|\alpha|^2}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp\left[ -\frac{1}{2a^*} \left(x + ik_0/2 \right) \right] \cdot -i\hbar \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} \exp\left[ -\frac{1}{2a} \left(x - ik_0/2 \right) \right] \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma|\alpha|^2}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{i\hbar}{\alpha} \left(x - \frac{ik_0}{\sigma} \right) \exp\left[-\frac{1}{\sigma|\alpha|^2}\left( x - \frac{\hbar k_0 t}{m} \right)^2 \right] \mathrm{d}x \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma|\alpha|^2}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{i\hbar k_0}{\alpha} \left(\frac{\tilde{x}}{k_0} + \frac{\hbar t}{m} - \frac{i}{\sigma} \right) \exp\left( -\frac{\tilde{x}^2}{\sigma|\alpha|^2} \right)\mathrm{d}\tilde{x} \\
  &= \hbar k_0
\end{align*}
$$

## Harmonic oscillator

The harmonic oscillator describes a particle subject to a quadratic potential $V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$. With this potential, the Hamiltonian becomes

$$
  \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\mathrm{d}^2}{\mathrm{d}x^2} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2
$$

Almost any system subject to small perturbations about a minimum can be approximated by a Hamiltionian of this form through Taylor expansion. The harmonic oscillator is therefore an important system in quantum mechanics.


The time independent Shrödinger equation takes the form

$$
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\mathrm{d}^2\psi}{\mathrm{d}x^2} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2 \psi = E\psi
$$

The equation can be simplified by defining

$$
\begin{aligned}
  y = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}}x
\end{aligned}\quad
\begin{aligned}
  \tilde{E} = \frac{2E}{\hbar\omega}
\end{aligned}\quad
$$

reducing the equation to

$$
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^2\psi}{\mathrm{d}y^2} - y^2 \psi = -\tilde{E}\psi
$$

An apparant solution is the Gaussian wavefunction $\psi(y) = e^{-y^2/2}$ with derivatives $\psi' = -y\psi$ and $\psi'' = y^2\psi - \psi$. This wavefunction obeys the Schrödinger equation with energy $\tilde{E} = 1$.

As $y \to \infty$, the $\tilde{E}\psi$ term becomes negligable. This shows that all normalisable solutions should fall as $\psi \sim e^{-y^2/2}$. A general solution therefore takes the form

$$
  \psi(y) = h(y)e^{-y/2}
$$

where $h(y)$ is a polynomial. The derivatives are $\psi' = (h' - hy)e^{-y^2/2}$ and $\psi'' = \left(h'' - 2h'y + hy^2 - h \right)e^{-y^2/2}$. Plugging into the Schrödinger equation gives

$$
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 h}{\mathrm{d}y^2} - 2y \frac{\mathrm{d}h}{\mathrm{d}y} + \left(\tilde{E} - 1 \right)h = 0
$$

Assuming a general solution of the form

$$
  h(y) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n y^n
$$

and inserting it into the equation

$$
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{n=0}^\infty n(n-1) a_n y^{n-2} - 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty n a_n y^n \left(\tilde{E} - 1 \right) \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n y^n = 0 \\
  \sum_{n=0} \left[ (n + 2)(n + 1)a_{n+2} - 2na_n - \left(\tilde{E} - 1 \right)a_n \right] y = 0
\end{align*}
$$

This results in the recursive relation

$$
  a_{n+2} = \frac{2n - \tilde{E} + 1}{(n + 2)(n + 1)}a_n
$$

If the series does not terminate, then the recursion relation approximates to $a_{p+2} \approx 2a_p / p$ as $p \to \infty$. This results in an exponentially growing power series like

$$
  e^{y^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{y^{2p}}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n y^n \quad b_n = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{(p/2)!} &\quad \textrm{for even }p \\ 0 &\quad \textrm{for odd }p \end{cases}
$$

The recursive relation for this non-normalisable exponential function is

$$
  b_{n+2} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n/2)!}{(n/2 + 1)!}b_n = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n/2 + 1}b_n = \frac{2}{n}b_n
$$

The power series must therefore terminate for some $n$ to be normalisable. This then requires $2n - \tilde{E} + 1 = 0$, which yields the energy spectrum for the harmonic oscillator

$$
\begin{gather*}
  \tilde{E}_n = 1 + 2n \\
  E_n = \hbar\omega\left(\frac{1}{2} + n\right)
\end{gather*}
$$

With these energy states, the polynomial component $h(y)$ of the wavefunctions are the Hermite polynomials.
