# Newtonian mechanics

## Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion of particles. In this context a *particle* is an idealized point-like object of zero extent. Thus, the particle's position is the only relevant information when describing its motion. In this regard, its size and shape can be fully neglected.

The motion of a particle is described relative to a *reference frame*, i.e. a coordinate system with a given origin and basis vectors (axes), which we are free to choose at our convenience. Using Cartesian coordinates in three dimensional space ($\R^n$) with origin $\mathbf{0} = (0,0,0)$ basis $\Set{\hat{\mathbf{x}}, \hat{\mathbf{y}}, \hat{\mathbf{z}}}$ the position of a particle is a vector ($3$-tuple)

$$
	\mathbf{r} = (x,y,z) = x\hat{\mathbf{x}} + y\hat{\mathbf{y}} + z\hat{\mathbf{z}}
$$

which points radially from the origin. The magnitude of the position vector gives its distance from the origin

$$
	|\mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r}} = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}
$$

In motion, the now time-dependent position vector traces out a *trajectory* in the form of a parametric curve $\mathbf{r}:[0,\infty)\to\R^3$ given by

$$
	\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + y(t)\hat{\mathbf{y}} + z(t)\hat{\mathbf{z}}
$$

Taking the time derivative of the trajectory gives a tangent vector describing the direction of the motion, called the velocity of the particle

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{v}(t) =& \frac{\d}{\d t} \mathbf{r}(t) = \frac{\d}{\d t}x(t)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \frac{\d}{\d t}y(t)\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \frac{\d}{\d t}z(t)\hat{\mathbf{z}} \\
	=& \dot{x}\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \dot{y}\hat{\mathbf{y}} +  \dot{z}\hat{\mathbf{z}} = (\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}) = \dot{\mathbf{r}}
\end{align*}
$$

The magnitude of the velocity gives the particle's speed

$$
	v(t) = |\mathbf{v}(t)| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}(t)\cdot \mathbf{v}(t)}
$$

Taking the time derivative of the velocity gives the acceleration of the particle

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{a}(t) =& \frac{\d}{\d t} \mathbf{v}(t) = \frac{\d^2}{\d t^2}\mathbf{r}^2(t) \\
	=& \frac{\d^2}{\d t^2}x(t)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \frac{\d^2}{\d t^2}y(t)\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \frac{\d^2}{\d t^2}z(t)\hat{\mathbf{z}} \\
	=& \ddot{x}\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \ddot{y}\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \ddot{z}\hat{\mathbf{z}} = (\ddot{x}, \ddot{y}, \ddot{z}) = \dot{\mathbf{v}} = \ddot{\mathbf{r}}
\end{align*}
$$

### Polar coordinates

In polar coordinates, the basis vectors in terms of the Cartesian basis are

$$
	\begin{bmatrix} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \\ \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\phi & \sin\phi  \\ -\sin\phi & \cos\phi \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \hat{\mathbf{x}} \\ \hat{\mathbf{y}} \end{bmatrix}
$$

The time derivatives of the basis vectors are

$$
\begin{aligned}
	\frac{\d\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{\d t} =& \frac{\d}{\d t}\left(\cos\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \right) \\
	=& \dot{\phi}\left(-\sin\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\right) \\
	=& \dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}
\end{aligned} \quad
\begin{aligned}
	\frac{\d\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}}{\d t} =& \frac{\d}{\d t}\left(-\sin\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \right) \\
	=& -\dot{\phi}\left(\cos\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\right) \\
	=& -\dot{\phi}\hat{\mathbf{r}}
\end{aligned}\\
$$

In polar coordinates the position vector is

$$
	\mathbf{R} = r \hat{\mathbf{r}} = r\left(\cos\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\phi \,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\right)
$$

The velocity is

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{v} &\equiv \dot{\mathbf{R}} = \frac{\d}{\d t} r \hat{\mathbf{r}} = \dot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\frac{\d\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{\d t} \\
	=& \dot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}
\end{align*}
$$

The acceleration is

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{a} &\equiv \ddot{\mathbf{R}} = \frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \dot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \right) \\
	=& \ddot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \dot{r}\frac{\d\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{\d t} + \dot{r}\dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + r\left(\ddot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} + \dot{\phi} \frac{\d\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}}{\d t} \right) \\
	=& \left(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\phi}^2 \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \left(2\dot{r}\dot{\phi} + r\ddot{\phi} \right)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \\
	=& \left(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\phi}^2 \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\left(2r\dot{r}\dot{\phi} + r^2\ddot{\phi} \right)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \\
	=& \left(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\phi}^2 \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( r^2\dot{\phi} \right)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}
\end{align*}
$$

## Newton's laws of motion

Newtonian mechanics is derived from Newton's tree laws of motion describing the relation of the motion of a particle and the forces acting upon it:
1. **(Law of inertia):** A body remains at rest, or in motion at a constant speed in a straight line, unless acted upon by a force.
2. **(Law of acceleration):** The net force on a body is proportional to it acceleration, or equivalently directly proporional to its rate of change in linear momentum.
3. **(Law of action and reaction):** Every action (force applied) has an equal but opposite reaction (equal force applied in the opposite direction).

Newton's second law describes a direct proportionality between the rate of change of a particle's linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ and the applied force

$$
	\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}, \dot{\mathbf{r}}) = \dot{\mathbf{p}}
$$

For particles with constant mass, i.e. $\dot{m} = 0$, the equation can be restated in terms of the particle's acceleration

$$
	\mathbf{F} = m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}
$$

In the absence of a force acting on a particle, the second law tells that its linear momentum is constant.

Newton's first law can be read as a special case of the second law since when $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{0}$ then $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$. However, the first law entails a more fundamental principle stating the existence of inertial frames, which are frames at rest or moving with constant velocity. In general, Newton's laws of motion are only valid in inertial frames as motion in non-inertial frames introduces fictitious forces, also called inertial forces, such as the Coriolis force and the centrifugal force.

### Non-inertial reference system

Let $S$ and $\tilde{S}$ be two reference frames separated by a displacement $\mathbf{d}$, and suppose $\tilde{S}$ is moving relative to $S$. The transformation between position vector is

$$
	\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(t) = \mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{d}(t)
$$

If $S$ is an inertial frame, then Newton's second law gives $\mathbf{F} = m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}$. In $\tilde{S}$, the equation of motion becomes

$$
	m\ddot{\tilde{\mathbf{r}}} = m\left(\ddot{\mathbf{r}} - \ddot{\mathbf{d}}\right) = \mathbf{F} - m\ddot{\mathbf{d}}
$$

This shows that Newton's equation is preserved only if $\dot{\mathbf{d}}$ is constant, meaning that $\tilde{S}$ is an inertial frame.

#### Leibnizian transformation

In Leibnizian spacetime, Newton's second law is not preserved as applying a spatial transformation $\mathbf{r} \mapsto \mathbf{R}(t)\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{a}(t)$, where $\mathbf{R}:\R\to\mathrm{SO}_3 (\R)$ and $\mathbf{a}(t):\R\to\R^3$ gives

$$
\begin{align*}
	m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} =& m\frac{\d^2}{\d t^2} [\mathbf{R}(t)\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{a}(t)] \\
	=& m\left[\ddot{\mathbf{R}}(t)\mathbf{x} + 2\dot{\mathbf{R}}(t) + \mathbf{R}(t)\ddot{\mathbf{r}} + \ddot{\mathbf{a}}(t)\right]
\end{align*}
$$

The path of an object under no external forces is thus given by

$$
	\ddot{\mathbf{r}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{R}^{-1}(t)\ddot{\mathbf{R}}(t)\mathbf{r}}_{\text{centrifugal inertial force}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{R}^{-1}(t)\ddot{\mathbf{a}}(t)}_{\text{linear inertial force}} + \underbrace{2\mathbf{R}^{-1}\dot{\mathbf{R}}(t)\dot{\mathbf{r}}}_{\text{coriolis inertial force}} = 0
$$

### Variable-mass systems

For a system with variable mass, i.e. $\dot{m} \neq 0$, the usual form of Newton's second form $\mathbf{F} = m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}$ is no longer valid. There are two canonical cases to consider, whether a system losses or gains mass. In the first case, consider a system having momemtum $\mathbf{p}_0 = m\mathbf{v}$ at time $t$. Suppose after a short interval $\delta t$, the system merges with a small particle having linear momentum $\mathbf{p}'(t) = (\Delta m) \mathbf{u}$ along $\mathbf{p}_0$, where $\Delta m = m(t) - m(t + \delta t)$. The total momentum of the system now becomes

$$
	\mathbf{p}(t + \delta t) = \mathbf{p}_0 (t + \delta t) + \mathbf{p}'(t + \delta t)
$$

where, applying first-order Taylor series expansion

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{p}_0(t + \delta t) =& m(t + \delta t)\mathbf{v}(t + \delta t) \\
	=& \left(m(t) + \frac{\d m}{\d t} \delta t + O(\delta t^2) \right) \left(\mathbf{v} + \frac{\d\mathbf{v}}{\d t}\delta t + O(\delta t^2) \right) \\
	=& m(t)\mathbf{v}(t) + \left( \mathbf{v}\frac{\d m}{\d t} + m\frac{\d\mathbf{v}}{\d t} \right) + O(\delta t^2)
\end{align*}
$$

and

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{p}'(t + \delta) =& [m(t) - m(t + \delta)][\mathbf{v}(t) - \mathbf{u}] \\
	=& \left[m(t) - \left(m(t) + \frac{\d m}{\d t} \delta t + O(\delta t^2) \right) \right][\mathbf{v}(t) - \mathbf{u}] \\
	=& - \frac{\d m}{\d t} \delta t [\mathbf{v}(t) - \mathbf{u}] + O(\delta t^2)
\end{align*}
$$

Adding the terms we get

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{p}(t + \delta t) = \mathbf{p}(t) + \left( m(t)\frac{\mathbf{d}\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{d}t} + \mathbf{u}\frac{\mathbf{d}m}{\mathbf{d}t} \right) + O(\delta t^2)
\end{align*}
$$

Applying Newton's second law $\mathbf{F} = \dot{\mathbf{p}}$ we get

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{F} =& \lim_{\delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{p}(t + \delta t) - \mathbf{p}(t)}{\delta t} \\
	=& m\frac{\d\mathbf{v}}{\d t} + \mathbf{u}\frac{\d m}{\d t} \\
	=& m\dot{\mathbf{v}} + \mathbf{u}\dot{m}
\end{align*}
$$

which is known as the *Tsiolkovsky rocket equation*.

## Torque

Torque is rotational equivalent of linear force. The torque acting on a particle is defined as

$$
	\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}
$$

The angular momentum of a particle at position $r$ is

$$
	\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{r} \times m \dot{\mathbf{r}}
$$

The rate of change of the angular moment equals torque

$$
	\frac{\d\mathbf{L}}{\d t} = m \left(\mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} + \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} \right) = \mathbf{r} \times m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = \boldsymbol{\tau}
$$

In the absence of a torque acting on a particle, its angular momentum is conserved.

## Work

The total work done on a particle moving from $\mathbf{r}(a)$ to $\mathbf{r}(b)$ is the line integral

$$
	W_{ab} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F} \cdot \d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}\;\d t
$$

The line integral equals the total change in the particles kinetic energy $T = \frac{m}{2}\dot{r}^2$

$$
\begin{align*}
	W_{ab} =& \int_a^b m \frac{\d}{\d t}\dot{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}\d t \\
	=& \int_a^b \frac{m}{2} \frac{\d}{\d t} \left(\dot{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}\right) \d t \\
	=& \int_a^b \frac{\d T}{\d t} \d t \\
	=& T(b) - T(a)
\end{align*}
$$

If the line integral is independent of the particle's trajectory, the gradient theory gives that $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla V(\mathbf{r})$, where $V$ is the potential energy of the particle. This implies that the work equals the total change in potential energy

$$
	W_{ab} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F} \cdot \d\mathbf{r} = -\int_a^b \nabla V \cdot \d\mathbf{r} = V(a) - V(b)
$$

### Conservation of energy

Since the total work both equals change in the particle's kinetic energy and potential energy

$$
\begin{gather*}
	T(b) - T(a) = V(a) - V(b) \\
	T(a) + V(a) = T(b) + T(a)
\end{gather*}
$$

meaning that the total mechanical $E = T + V$ stays constant. Alternatively, it can be shown that the time derivative of $E$ vanishes

$$
	\frac{\d E}{\d t} = \frac{m}{2} \frac{\d\dot{r}^2}{\d t} + \frac{\d V(\mathbf{r})}{\d t} = m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} + \nabla V \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{F} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} - \mathbf{F} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} = 0
$$

## Centre of mass

The centre of mass of an $n$-body system is at a position $\mathbf{R}$ defined by

$$
	\mathbf{R} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_i m_i \mathbf{r_i}
$$

where $M = \sum_i m_i$. By Newton's third law, the centre of mass moves with a uniform velocity, as can be shown with evaluating the equation of motion for the centre of mass

$$
\begin{align*}
	M \ddot{\mathbf{R}} & \sum_i m_i \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_i = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \\
	=& \sum_i \sum_{j\neq i} m_j \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_j = \sum_i \sum_{j\neq i} \mathbf{F}_{ij} \, ,\quad \mathbf{F}_{ij} = -\mathbf{F}_{ji} \\
	=& \mathbf{0} \\
	\implies \mathbf{R}(t) =& \mathbf{R}(0) + \dot{\mathbf{R}}(0)t
\end{align*}
$$

It is therefore convenient to shift the origin of the reference frame to the centre of mass by substituting

$$
	\mathbf{r}'_i = \mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}
$$

The total linear momentum follows the motion of the centre of mass

$$
	\mathbf{P} = \sum_i \mathbf{p}_i \sum_i m_i \dot{\mathbf{r}} = \frac{\d}{\d t} \sum_i m_i \mathbf{r}_i = \frac{\d}{\d t} M \mathbf{R} = M\dot{\mathbf{R}}
$$

If the origin of the inertial frame is the centre of mass, then $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{0}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{0}$. This implies that $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{0}$, meaning that the total momentum of the system vanishes in this case.

## Rotational inertia

The rotational inertia of a system of $n$ particles with mass $m_i$ and displacement $\mathbf{r}_i$, rotating about a specified axis is

$$
	I = \sum_i^n m_i |\mathbf{r}_i|^2
$$

For a continuous mass body with density $\rho:\R^3 \to (0,\infty)$ and volume $V$, the moment of inertia is

$$
	I = \iiint_V \rho(\mathbf{r})|\mathbf{r}|^2 \;\d V
$$

### Angular momentum

Consider a rigid body with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. The position, velocities and accceleration of the body's particles relative to a reference point $\mathbf{R}$ are

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i = \mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R} \\
	\mathbf{v}_i = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}) + \dot{\mathbf{R}} = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i + \dot{\mathbf{R}} \\
	\mathbf{a}_i = \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}) + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \left[\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}) \right] + \ddot{\mathbf{R}}
\end{gather*}
$$

where $\mathbf{r}_i$ are the positions relative to the body's centre of mass. The angular momentum is

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{L} =& \sum_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \mathbf{p}_i = \sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \mathbf{v}_i \\
	=& \sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \left( \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i + \dot{\mathbf{R}}  \right) \\
	=& \sum_i m_i \left[(\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i)\boldsymbol{\omega} - (\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega})\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right] + \left(\sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right) \times \dot{\mathbf{R}} \\
	=& \sum_i m_i \bar{r}_i^2 \boldsymbol{\omega} + \left(\sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right) \times \dot{\mathbf{R}} \\
	=& I_C \boldsymbol{\omega} + \left(\sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right) \times \dot{\mathbf{R}}
\end{align*}
$$

Setting the centre of mass as the reference point simplifies the angular momentum to

$$
	\mathbf{L} = I_C \boldsymbol{\omega}
$$

### Kinetic energy

The kinetic energy of a rigid body is

$$
\begin{align*}
	T =& \frac{1}{2}\sum_i m_i \mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{v}_i \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}\sum_i m_i \left(\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}} + \dot{\mathbf{R}} \right) \cdot \left(\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}} + \dot{\mathbf{R}} \right) \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}\left[\sum_i m_i (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] + \left[\sum_i m_i \dot{\mathbf{R}} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] + \frac{1}{2}\left[\sum_i m_i \dot{\mathbf{R}} + \dot{\mathbf{R}}  \right] \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}\left[\sum_i m_i \bar{r}_i^2 \omega^2 - (\boldsymbol{\omega} \cdot \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i )^2 \right] + \left[\sum_i m_i \dot{\mathbf{R}} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] + \frac{1}{2}M\dot{R}^2 \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}I_C \omega^2 + \left[\sum_i m_i \dot{\mathbf{R}} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] + \frac{1}{2}M\dot{R}^2
\end{align*}
$$

Setting the centre of mass as the reference point simplifies the kinetic energy to

$$
	T = \frac{1}{2}I_C \omega^2 + \frac{1}{2}M\dot{R}^2
$$

### Torque

The torque of a rigid body is (assuming inertial reference frame with $\ddot{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{0}$)

$$
\begin{align*}
	\boldsymbol{\tau} =& \sum_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \mathbf{F}_i = \sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \mathbf{a}_i \\
	=& \sum_i m_i \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \left[\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega}  \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) + \ddot{\mathbf{R}} \right] \\
	=& \sum_i m_i \left[(\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i)\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} - (\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}})\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right] + \sum_i m_i \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \left[ \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] \\
	=& I_C \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \sum_i m_i \left[(\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i)\boldsymbol{\omega} - (\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{\omega})\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \right] \\
	=& I_C \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times I_C \boldsymbol{\omega}
\end{align*}
$$

The calculation utilizes the Jacobi identity

$$
	\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times \left[\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \left[\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \bar{\mathbf{r}}_i) \right] = 0
$$

### Parallel axis theorem

The rotational inertia of an object about an axis that is parallell to an axis through the object's centre of mass is

$$
	I = I_C + Md^2
$$

where $d$ is the distance between the two parallel axes.

### Inertia tensor

The inertia tensor describes all the rotational inertia of an object about different axes of rotation.

$$
	I_{ij} = \sum_k^N m_k \left(\norm{ \mathbf{r}_k}^2 \delta_{ij} - x_i^{(k)}x_j^{(k)} \right)
$$

For a distributed mass

$$
	\mathbf{I} = \iiint_\mathcal{V} \rho(x, y, z)\left(\norm{ \mathbf{r}_k}^2 \mathbf{E}_3 - \mathbf{r} \otimes \mathbf{r} \right)\d x\;\d y\;\d z
$$

### Virial theorem

For a system of $N$ particles bound by a conservative force, the time-averaged total kinetic energy is related to the forces acting on the particles by

$$
	\braket{T} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^N \braket{\mathbf{F}_k \cdot \mathbf{r}_k }
$$

where $\mathrm{F}_k$ is the force on $k$ particle, located at position $\mathbf{r}_k$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

The virial theorem is obtained by considering the scalar quantity

$$
	G := \sum_i \mathbf{p}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i
$$

The total time derivative of $G$ is

$$
	\frac{\d G}{\d t} = \sum_i \mathbf{p} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i + \sum_i \dot{\mathbf{p}}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i
$$

The first term is simply twice the total kinetic energy $T$, and by Netwon's 2nd law, we have $\dot{\mathbf{p}}_i = \mathbf{F}_i$. Thus

$$
	\frac{\d G}{\d t} = 2T + \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i
$$

Averaging over a time interval $\Delta t$, we have

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{1}{\Delta t} \int_0^{\Delta t} \frac{\d G}{\d t} \;\d t =& 2\braket{T} + \sum_i \braket{\mathbf{F}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i} \\
	=& \frac{1}{\Delta t} [G(\Delta t) - G(0)] \tag{\label{equation-1}}
\end{align*}
$$

If the values of $\mathbf{p}_i$ and $\mathbf{r}_i$ are such that $G$ is finite at all times, then we can always choose $\Delta t$ to be large enough that the right-hand side of $\eqref{equation-1}$ is vanishingly small, so that

$$
	\braket{T} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^N \braket{\mathbf{F}_k \cdot \mathbf{r}_k }
$$

in the limit $\Delta t \to \infty$.
</details>

If the forces are interparticle central forces, then

$$
\begin{align*}
	\sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i =& \sum_i \sum_j \mathbf{F}_{ji} \cdot \mathbf{r}_i,\; \mathbf{F}_{ii} = 0 \\
	=& \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \mathbf{F}_{ji} \cdot \mathbf{r}_{ij}
\end{align*}
$$

where the last equality follows from Newton's 3rd law. If the central forces are also conservative, so that $\mathbf{F}_{ji} = -\nabla_{ij} U_{ij} (r_{ij})$, then

$$
	\sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \cdot \mathbf{r}_i = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial U_{ij}}{\partial r_{ij}} r_{ij}
$$

Finally, if the central potential is a power law with $U_{ij} (r_{ij}) = A_{ij} r_{ij}^\alpha$ for some $\alpha\in\Z_-$, then

$$
\begin{align*}
	\sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \cdot\mathbf{r}_i =& -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} \alpha r_{ij}^{\alpha - 1} r_{ij} \\
	=& -\frac{\alpha}{2} \sum_{i,j} U_{ij} (r_{ij}) = -\alpha U
\end{align*}
$$

where $U$ is the total potential energy of the system. Thus, for inverse-square law, conservative, central forces the virial theorem becomes

$$
	\braket{T} = -\frac{1}{2}\braket{U}
$$

## Projectile motion

Consider a projectile with mass $m$ subject that is launched in the $xy$-plane at an angle $\theta$ relative to the $x$-axis, and with the following initial values.

- position: $\mathbf{r}_0 = x_0\hat{\mathbf{x}} + y_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}$
- velocity: $\mathbf{v}_0 = v_0 \cos(\theta)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + v_0 \sin(\theta)\hat{\mathbf{y}}$

Neglecting drag, the net force acting on the projectile is the gravitational force

$$
	\mathbf{F} = -mg\hat{\mathbf{y}}
$$

The equation of motion for the projectile in $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$-direction is

$$
	m\ddot{y}(t) = -mg \iff \ddot{y} = -g
$$

Assuming that the gravitational field is uniform, the gravitational acceleration $g$ is constant. Hence the kinetic equations for object are

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{v}(t) =& v_0\cos(\theta)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + (v_0\sin(\theta) - gt)\hat{\mathbf{y}} \\
	\mathbf{r}(t) =& [x_0 + v_0\cos(\theta)t]\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \left[y_0 + v_0\sin(\theta) t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\right]\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\end{align*} 
$$

Eliminating $t$ from the position component functions gives

$$
	y(x) = \tan(\theta)x - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2(\theta)}x^2
$$

### Drag forces

Drag is a friction force resisting the motion of an object moving in a fluid. Assuming an isotropic fluid, we expect that the magnitude of the drag force should only depend on the relative speed $v$ of the object. If the object is symmetrical, we expect the direction of the force to be opposite of motion, i.e.

$$
	\mathbf{F}_{\d} = -f(v)\hat{\mathbf{v}}
$$

Under these assumption, the general form of the drag force is a Taylor series expansion

$$
	F_{\d} = \sum_{i=1}^\infty k_0 \left(\frac{v}{v_s}\right)^i
$$

where $v_s$ is the speed of sound. For low speeds, $v\ll v_s$, the drag force approximates to its linear and quadratic term

$$
	f(v) = bv + kv^2
$$

At low speeds, the fluid tends to flow smoothly in what is known as laminar flow. In this regime, the linear drag term dominates. At higher speeds, the fluid flow becomes turbulent and the quadratric drag dominates.

### Projectile motion with linear drag

For spherical objects, linear drag is given by Stokes' law

$$
	F_{\d} = 6\pi\eta r v
$$

where
- $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity
- $r$ is the radius of the object
- $v$ is the flow velocity relative to the object

Setting $b = 6\pi\eta r$, the net force acting on the projectile is, noting that $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{F} =& -b|\mathbf{v}| \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} - mg\hat{\mathbf{y}} \\
	=& -b\mathbf{v} - mg\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\end{align*}
$$

The equations of motions in $x$ and $y$ directions take the form of coupled differential equations

$$
\begin{align*}
	m\ddot{x} =& -b\dot{x} \\
	m\ddot{y} =& k\dot{y} - mg
\end{align*}
$$

The $x$-equation can be transformed into a first order ODE by substituting $v_x = \dot{x}$. The resulting equation can be solved by separation

$$
\begin{align*}
	\dot{v}_x =& -\frac{b}{m}v_x \\
	\int \frac{\d v_x}{v_x} =&  \int-\frac{b}{m}\;\d t \\
	\ln v_x =& -\frac{b}{m}t + A \\
\end{align*}
$$

The initial condition $v_x(t=0) = v_0\cos(\theta)$ gives $A = \ln (v_0\cos\theta)$.

$$
\begin{align*}
	\ln v_x =& \ln (v_0\cos\theta) - \frac{b}{m}t \\
	\ln\frac{v_x}{v_0\cos\theta} =& -\frac{b}{m}t \\
	\frac{v_x}{v_0\cos\theta} =& e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \\
	v_x(t) =& v_0\cos(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}
\end{align*}
$$

Integrating again gives the $x$-position

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{\d x}{\d t} =& v_0\cos(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \\
	\int \d x =& \int v_0\cos(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\;\d t \\
	x =& -\frac{m}{b}v_0\cos(\theta)e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} + B
\end{align*}
$$

The initial condition $x(t=0) = x_0$ gives $B = x_0 + \frac{m}{b}v_0$.

In $y$ direction, the projectile will keep accelerating until reaching its terminal speed $v_\infty = \lim_{t\to\infty} \dot{y}$ when the drag and gravitational forces even out

$$
	mg = bv_\infty
$$

Inserting this into the $y$ equation and substituting $v_y = \dot{y}$ gives the first order ODE

$$
	\dot{v}_y = -\frac{b}{m}(v_y - v_\infty)
$$

The equation can be further simplified by substituting $u = (v_y - v_\infty)$, noting that $\dot{u} = \dot{v}_y$

$$
\dot{u} = -\frac{b}{m}u
$$

which is solved in the same way as the $x$-equation, noting that $u_0 = v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty$

$$
\begin{align*}
	u(t) =& u_0 e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \\
	v_y(t) - v_\infty =& (v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \\
	v_y (t) =& v_\infty\left(1 -  e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\right) + v_0\sin(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}
\end{align*}
$$

Integrating again gives the $y$-position

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{\d y}{\d t} =& v_\infty + (v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty)e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \\
	\int\d y =& \int \left[ v_\infty + (v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty)e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \right]\d t \\
	y =& v_\infty t - \frac{m}{b}(v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty)e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} + C
\end{align*}
$$

The initial condition $y(t=0) = y_0$ gives

$$
	C = y_0 + \frac{m}{b}(v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty)
$$

Putting it all together, the kinematic equations are

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{v}(t) =& v_0\cos(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\hat{\mathbf{x}} \\
	& + \left[v_\infty\left(1 -  e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\right) + v_0\sin(\theta) e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\right]\hat{\mathbf{y}} \\
	\mathbf{r}(t) =& \left[x_0 + \frac{m}{b}v_0\cos(\theta)\left(1 - e^{-\frac{b}{m}t} \right)\right]\hat{\mathbf{x}} \\
	& + \left[y_0 + v_\infty t - \frac{m}{b}(v_0\sin\theta - v_\infty)\left(1 - e^{-\frac{b}{m}t}\right)\right]\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\end{align*}
$$

### Projectile motion with quadratic drag 

Quadratic drag is given by

$$
	F_{\d} = \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 c_{\d} A
$$

where
- $\rho$ is the mass density of the fluid
- $v$ is the flow velocity relative to the object
- $A$ is the cross sectional area
- $c_{\d}$ is the drag coefficient

Setting $k = \frac{1}{2}\rho c_{\d} A$, the net force acting on the projectile is

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{F} =& -k|\mathbf{v}|^2 \hat{\mathbf{v}} + mg\hat{\mathbf{y}} \\
	=& -k|\mathbf{v}|\mathbf{v} - mg\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\end{align*}
$$

The equations of motions in $x$ and $y$ directions take the form of coupled differential equations

$$
\begin{align*}
	m\ddot{x} =& -k\sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2}\dot{x} \\
	m\ddot{y} =& k\sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2}\dot{y} - mg
\end{align*}
$$

The equations can be solved numerically. An analytical solution can be found for a purely vertical motion in which case the equations of motion reduce to

$$
	m\ddot{y} = k\dot{y}^2 - mg
$$

At equilibrium, the projectile reaches its terminal velocity $v_\infty = \lim_{t\to\infty} v_y (t)$ which found by solving

$$
\begin{gather*}
	mg - \frac{1}{2}\rho v_\infty^2 A c_{\d} = 0 \\
	v_\infty = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{\rho c_{\d}A}} = \sqrt{\frac{mg}{k}}
\end{gather*}
$$

The equation can be transformed into a first order ODE by substituting $v_y(t) = \dot{y}$

$$
\begin{align*}
	\ddot{y} =& \frac{k}{m}\dot{y}^2 - g \\
	\frac{\ddot{y}}{-g + \frac{k}{m}\dot{y}^2} =& 1 \\
	\frac{\ddot{y}}{1 - \frac{k}{mg}\dot{y}^2} =& -g \\
	\frac{\dot{v}_y}{1 - \left(v_y/v_\infty\right)^2} =& -g \\
	\frac{\d v_y}{1 - \left(v_y/v_\infty\right)^2} =& -g\;\d t
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting $u = \frac{v_y}{v_\infty}$ and splitting the left integrand

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{1}{1 - u^2} =& \frac{1}{(1 + u)(1 - u)} \\
	=& \frac{1}{2(1 + u)} + \frac{1}{2(1 - u)}
\end{align*}
$$

The left integral can now be solved

$$
\begin{align*}
	\int \left( \frac{1}{2(1 + u)} + \frac{1}{2(1 - u)} \right)\;\d u =& \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+u) - \frac{1}{2}\ln(1 - u) \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}\ln\left(\frac{1 + u}{1 - u} \right)
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting into the differential equation 

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{1}{2}\ln\left(\frac{1+u}{1-u} \right) =& -\frac{gt}{v_\infty} + A \\
	\frac{1+u}{1-u} =& Be^{-2gt/v_\infty} \\
	1 + u =& Be^{-2gt/v_\infty}(1 - u) \\
	u =& \frac{Be^{-2gt/v_\infty} - 1}{Be^{-2gt/v_\infty} + 1} \\
	v_y(t) =& v_\infty \frac{Be^{-2gt/v_\infty} - 1}{Be^{-2gt/v_\infty} + 1}
\end{align*}
$$

It remains to solve for the integration constant $B$, assuming initial velocity $v_y (0) = -v_0$

$$
\begin{gather*}
	-v_0 = v_\infty \frac{B-1}{B+2} \\
	B = \frac{v_\infty + v_0}{v_\infty - v_0}
\end{gather*}
$$

If the object starts from rest $v_0 = 0$ then $B = 1$, and the velocity simplifies to

$$
	v_y (t) = v_\infty \frac{e^{-2gt/v_\infty} - 1}{e^{-2gt/v_\infty} + 1} = v_\infty \tanh{\frac{gt}{v_\infty}}
$$

Taking the derivative and integrating gives the kinematic equations

$$
\begin{align*}
	a_y (t) =& g\left[1 - \tanh^2\left( \frac{gt}{v_\infty} \right)\right] \\
	v_y (t) =& v_\infty \frac{e^{-2gt/v_\infty} - 1}{e^{-2gt/v_\infty} + 1} = v_\infty \tanh{\frac{gt}{v_\infty}} \\
	y(t) =& y_0 - \frac{v_\infty}{gt}\ln\left[\cosh\left(\frac{gt}{v_\infty} \right) \right]
\end{align*}
$$

## Motion of a simple planar pendulum

Consider a pendulum consisting of an object with mass $m$ attached to a rigid massless rod of length $l$. In polar coordinates, the objects' acceleration is

$$
	\mathbf{a} = \left(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2 \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( r^2\dot{\theta} \right)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}
$$

The net force acting on the mass is $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{T} + m\mathbf{g}$ where $\mathbf{T} = -T\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ is the tension. In polar coordinates, the net force is written out

$$
	\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{T} + m\mathbf{g} = \left(-T + mg\cos\theta \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} - mg\sin\theta \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
$$

Newton's equations of motion become

$$
\begin{split}
		m\left(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2\right) = -T + mg\cos\theta
\end{split}\quad
\begin{split}
		\frac{1}{r}\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( r^2\dot{\theta} \right) = -g\sin\theta
\end{split}\quad
$$

The equations simplify since $r = l$ is constant

$$
\begin{split}
		T = m\left(l\dot{\theta}^2 + g\cos\theta \right)
\end{split}\quad
\begin{split}
		l\ddot{\theta} = -g\sin\theta
\end{split}\quad
$$

The angular equation can be written as

$$
	\ddot{\theta} + \omega^2\sin\theta = 0
$$

where $\omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}$. Multiplying the equation with $\dot{\theta}$ gives

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\ddot{\theta}\dot{\theta} + \omega^2\dot{\theta}\sin\theta = 0 \\
	\frac{1}{2}\frac{\d}{\d t}\dot{\theta}^2 - \omega^2\frac{\d}{\d t}\cos\theta = 0 \\
	\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 - \omega^2\cos\theta \right) = 0
	\implies \frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 - \omega^2\cos\theta = \varepsilon
\end{gather*}
$$

where $\varepsilon$ is a constant. The resulting first order differential equation represents the pendulum's energy as

$$
	E = T + V = ml^2\left(\frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 - \omega^2\cos\theta  \right) = ml^2\varepsilon
$$

Here $-\omega^2 \cos\theta \equiv \nu(\theta)$ represents reduced potential energy and $\frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 \equiv \epsilon - \nu(\theta)$ represents reduced kinetic energy.

Whenever $\epsilon < \omega^2$, the pendulum ocillates between two well-defined amplitude limits $\theta = \pm \theta_0$ in a potential well. This amplitude is determined by setting $\dot{\theta} = 0$ (zero kinetic energy), so that

$$
	\epsilon = \nu(\theta_0) = -\omega^2 \cos\theta_0
$$

This results in the first-order differential equation

$$
\begin{align*}
	\frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 =& \omega^2 \left(\cos\theta - \cos\theta_0 \right) \\
	\dot{\theta} =& \pm \sqrt{2}\omega\sqrt{\cos\theta - \cos\theta_0} \\
	t =& \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\omega} \int \frac{\d\theta}{\sqrt{\cos\theta - \cos\theta_0}} + C
\end{align*}
$$

The period of the pendulum is

$$
	P = \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}\omega} \int_0^{\theta_0} \frac{\d\theta}{\sqrt{\cos\theta - \cos\theta_0}}
$$

The integral can be put in standard form with the change of variable

$$
	z = \frac{\sin\frac{1}{2}\theta}{\sin\frac{1}{2}\theta_0}
$$

and the parameter $s = \sin\frac{1}{2}\theta_0$. Algebraic manipulation gives

$$
	\frac{\d z}{\d\theta} = \frac{\sqrt{1 - s^2z^2}}{2s} \, ,\quad \sqrt{\cos\theta - \cos\theta_0} = \sqrt{2s}\sqrt{1 - z^2}
$$

Substituting into the integral

$$
	P = \frac{4}{\omega} \int_0^1 \frac{\d z}{\sqrt{\left( 1 - z^2 \right)\left(1 - s^2z^2 \right)}} = \frac{4}{\omega}K(s)
$$

where $K(s)$ is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind expressed as the power series of Legendre polynomials

$$
	K(s) = \frac{\pi}{2} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left[ P_{2n}(0) \right]^2 s^{2n}
$$

When the oscillations are small so that $\theta_0 \ll 1 \implies s^2 \ll 1$, the factor $\left(1 - s^2z^2 \right)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ can be expanded into a Taylor series about $s=0$.

With this expansion, the elliptic integral becomes

$$
	K(s)
$$

### Small oscillation approximation

In case of small oscillations, $\sin\theta \cong \theta$ and the angular differential eqution simplifies to

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\ddot{\theta} + \omega^2 = 0 \\
	\omega(t) = \theta_0 \cos(\omega t + \delta)
\end{gather*}
$$

## Motion of a compound pendulum

## Kepler's problem

A system of bodies is subjected to mutual gravitational forces. In a two-body system, the force acting on body $1$ due to the gravity of body $2$ is:

$$
	\mathbf{F}_{12} = -G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \hat{\mathbf{r}}
$$

where $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2$

Gravity field

$$
	\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{r}) = -G\frac{m}{r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla \left( G\frac{m}{r} \right) = -\nabla V(r)
$$

Newton's equations of motion for the two bodies are

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_1 = -Gm_2 \frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2} \\
	\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_2 = Gm_1 \frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}
\end{gather*}
$$

In terms of the system's centre of mass, $M\mathbf{R} = m_1\mathbf{r}_1 + m_2\mathbf{r}_2$, the positon vectors become

$$
	\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{R} + \frac{m_2}{M}\mathbf{r} \,,\quad \mathbf{r}_2 = \mathbf{R} - \frac{m_1}{M}\mathbf{r}
$$

The system's centre of mass moves uniformly

$$
	M\ddot{\mathbf{R}} = m_1\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_1 + m_2\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_2 = \mathbf{0} \\
$$

and defines the origin of an inertial frame. With $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{0}$, the position vectors reduce to

$$
	\mathbf{r}_1 = \frac{m_2}{M}\mathbf{r} \,,\quad \mathbf{r}_2 = -\frac{m_1}{M}\mathbf{r}
$$

while the motion of the relative position $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2$ is determined by

$$
	\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -GM \frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2} \tag{1}
$$

This reduces the two-body problem to a simpler effective one-body problem. In polar coordinates the equations are

$$
	\left( \ddot{r} - r\dot{\phi}^2 \right)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( r^2\dot{\phi} \right)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} = - GM\frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2} \tag{1}
$$

### Conservation of angular momentum

The angular equation gives the conservation statement

$$
	\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( r^2\dot{\phi} \right) = 0
$$

This implies conservation of angular momentum by noting

$$
	\mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} = r\hat{\mathbf{r}} \times \left( \dot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\right) = r^2\dot{\phi}\left(\hat{\mathbf{r}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \right) \equiv \mathbf{h}
$$

The vector $\mathbf{h}$ expresses a reduced angular moment vector because

$$
\begin{align*}
	\mathbf{L} =& m_1\mathbf{r}_1 \times \dot{\mathbf{r}_1} + m_1\mathbf{r}_1 \times \dot{\mathbf{r}_1} \\
	=& \frac{m_1 m_2}{M} \mathbf{h}
\end{align*}
$$

and hence the angular momentum is conserved.This implies planar motion, because $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{h} = \mathbf{r}\cdot\left(\mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} \right) = 0$, showing that $\mathbf{r}$ is always orhogonal to $\mathbf{h}$.

Conservation of angular momentum can alternatively be shown by noting that (from eq. $1$)

$$
	\mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -GM\frac{\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{r}}{r^3} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Furthermore, we have

$$
	\mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} + \mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \frac{\d}{\d t}\left(\mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} \right) \equiv \frac{\d\mathbf{h}}{\d t} = \mathbf{0}
$$

meaning that $\mathbf{h}$ is constant.

### Kepler's second law

Kepler's second law states that the position vector of a planet orbiting the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times. During an infinitisimal time interval $\d t$, the position vector moves by an angle $\d\phi$ and sweeps out an area approximated by the triangle $\d A = \frac{1}{2}r\left(r \d\phi \right) = \frac{1}{2}r^2 \d\phi$. The change in the swept-out area is therefore

$$
	\frac{\d A}{\d t} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\dot{\phi} = \frac{1}{2}h
$$

where $h$ is constant as previously shown.

### Conservation of energy

With the substitution $\dot{\phi} = \frac{h}{r^2}$, eq. $1$ becomes

$$
	\ddot{r} + \frac{GM}{r^2} - \frac{h}{r^3} = 0 \tag{2}
$$

Multiplying the equation with $\dot{r}$ and noting that

$$
\begin{align*}
	\ddot{r}\dot{r} =& \frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{r}^2 \right) \\
	\frac{GM}{r^2}\dot{r} =& -\frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \frac{GM}{r} \right) \\
	-\frac{h^2}{r^3}\dot{r} =& \frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \frac{h^2}{2r^2} \right)
\end{align*}
$$

gives the conservation statement

$$
	\frac{\d}{\d t}\left(\frac{1}{2}\dot{r}^2 - \frac{GM}{r} + \frac{h^2}{2r^2} \right) = 0
$$

The conserved measure expresses reduced total energy $\varepsilon$ and can be written as

$$
	\frac{1}{2}\dot{r}^2 + \nu(r) = \varepsilon \tag{3}
$$

with $\nu(r) = -\frac{GM}{r} + \frac{h^2}{2r^2}$.

The total energy is

$$
\begin{align*}
	E =& T + V \\
	=& \frac{1}{2}m_1 \left| \dot{\mathbf{r}}_1 \right|^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 \left| \dot{\mathbf{r}}_2 \right|^2 - \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r} \\
	=& \frac{m_1 m_2}{M}\left(\frac{1}{2}\left| \dot{\mathbf{r}} \right|^2 - \frac{GM}{r} \right)
\end{align*}
$$

Noting that $|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 = | \dot{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}|^2 = \dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot{\phi}^2 = \dot{r}^2 + \frac{h^2}{r^2}$, gives

$$
\begin{align*}
	E =& \frac{m_1 m_2}{M}\left(\frac{1}{2}\dot{r}^2 - \frac{GM}{r} + \frac{h^2}{2r^2} \right) \\
	=& \frac{m_1 m_2}{M}\varepsilon
\end{align*}
$$

Eq. $3$ describes the orbit of the system, where the shape is governed by $\varepsilon$:

- When $\varepsilon < 0$, the motion is bounded between two turning points $(r_a, r_b)$ forming an elliptical shape. When $\varepsilon = \nu_{min} < 0$, the motion bounded at a fixed position $r_0$, giving a circular orbit
- When $\varepsilon > 0$, the motion takes place only to the right of a single turning point. The particle starts at $r = \infty$ with a radial velocity given by $\frac{1}{2}\dot{r}^2 = \varepsilon$ and proceeds to the turning point, forming a hyperbolic shape
- When $\varepsilon = 0$, the particle starts at $r = \infty$ with zero radial velocity and proceeds to a turning point, form a parabolic shape.

### Shape of orbits

The time-dependency can be eliminated from eq. $2$ by transforming it in terms of $r(\phi)$, noting

$$
	\frac{\d r}{\d t} = \frac{\d r}{\d\phi} \frac{\d\phi}{\d t} = \frac{h}{r^2}\frac{\d r}{\d\phi}
$$

We then have

$$
\begin{align*}
	\ddot{r} =& \frac{\d}{\d t}\left( \frac{h}{r^2}\frac{\d r}{\d\phi} \right) \\
	=& -\frac{2h}{r^3}\dot{r}\frac{\d r}{\d\phi} + \frac{h}{r^2}\frac{\d^2r}{\d\phi^2}\dot{\phi} \\
	=& -\frac{2h^2}{r^5}\left( \frac{\d r}{\d\phi} \right)^2 + \frac{h^2}{r^4}\frac{\d^2r}{\d\phi^2}
\end{align*}
$$

Inserting into eq. $2$

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\frac{h^2}{r^4}\frac{\d^2r}{\d\phi^2} - \frac{2h^2}{r^5}\left( \frac{\d r}{\d\phi} \right)^2 + \frac{GM}{r^2} - \frac{h^2}{r^3} = 0 \\
	-\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\d^2r}{\d\phi^2} + \frac{2}{r^3}\left( \frac{\d r}{\d\phi} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{r} = \frac{GM}{h^2} \tag{4}
\end{gather*}
$$

This is a second-order nonlinear differential equation for $r(\phi)$. It can be linearized by substituting $u = \frac{1}{r}$ so that

$$
\begin{gather*}
	\frac{\d r}{\d\phi} = -\frac{1}{u^2}\frac{\d u}{\d\phi} \\
	\frac{\d^2r}{\d\phi^2} = \frac{2}{u^3}\left( \frac{\d u}{\d\phi} \right)^2 - \frac{1}{u^2}\frac{\d^2u}{\d\phi^2}
\end{gather*}
$$

Inserting into eq. $4$

$$
\begin{gather*}
	-\frac{2}{u} \left( \frac{\d u}{\d\phi} \right)^2 +\frac{\d^2u}{\d\phi^2} + \frac{2}{u} \left( \frac{\d u}{\d\phi} \right)^2 + u = \frac{GM}{h^2} \\
	\frac{\d^2u}{\d\phi^2} + u = \frac{GM}{h^2} \\
	\frac{\d^2u}{\d\phi^2} + v = 0 \, ,\quad v = u - \frac{GM}{h^2}
\end{gather*}
$$

The solution is $v = u_0 \cos\left(\phi - \phi_0 \right)$ giving

$$
\begin{align*}
	u =& \frac{GM}{h^2} + u_0 \cos\left(\phi - \phi_0 \right) \\
	&\equiv \frac{GM}{h^2} \left[ 1 + e \cos\left( \phi - \phi_0 \right) \right] \\
	&\equiv \frac{1}{p}\left[ 1 + e \cos\left( \phi - \phi_0 \right) \right]
\end{align*}
$$

where $u_0 = \frac{GMe}{h^2}$. The final result for $r(\phi)$ is

$$
	r(\phi) = \frac{p}{1 + e\cos\phi}
$$

which takes the form a parametric equation for conic sections with semi-latus rectum $p$ and eccentricity $e$. The eccentricity can be related to the reduced energy $\varepsilon$, noting that

$$
	\dot{r}(\phi) = \frac{ep\sin\phi}{\left( 1 + e\cos\phi \right)^2 \dot{\phi}} = \frac{e}{p}r^2 \dot{\phi}\sin\phi = \frac{e}{p}h\sin\phi
$$

giving

$$
\begin{align*}
	\varepsilon =& \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{e}{p}\sin\phi\right)^2 - \frac{GM}{p}(1 + e\cos\phi) + \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{h}{p}(1 + \cos\phi)\right]^2 \\
	=& \frac{GM}{2p}\left[ e^2 \sin^2\phi - 2(1 + e\cos\phi) + (1 + e\cos\phi)^2 \right] \\
	=& -\frac{GM}{2p}\left(1 - e^2\right)
\end{align*}
$$

The time-dependency of the motion is determined by the differential equation $\dot{\phi} = \frac{h}{r^2}$ that can be written as

$$
	\dot{\phi} = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{p^3}}\left(1 + e\cos\phi \right)^2
$$

The integral form is

$$
	t = \sqrt{\frac{p^3}{GM}}\int \frac{\d\phi}{\left( 1 + e\cos\phi \right)^2} + C
$$

The integral can be solved for bound orbits $e < 1$, giving the period

$$
\begin{align*}
	P =& 2\sqrt{\frac{p^3}{GM}}\int_0^\pi \frac{\d\phi}{\left( 1 + e\cos\phi \right)^2} \\
	=& 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{1}{GM}\left(\frac{p}{1 - e^2}\right)^3} \\
	=& 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{a^3}{GM}}
\end{align*}
$$

where $a = \frac{p}{1 - e^2}$ is the semi-major axis. This shows that $P^2 \propto a^3$, which is the general statement of Kepler's third law.
